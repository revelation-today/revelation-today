## Everything made new

Revelation 21 opens with one of the most beautiful promises in all of Scripture: "I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea" (Revelation 21:1). "New" here doesn't just mean recently made — it means a different quality of existence altogether: permanent, not temporary, fulfilling promises God had made for centuries (Isaiah 65:17-25).

"No longer any sea" matters more than it might first appear. Throughout Scripture, the sea represents the place evil originates from — the beast itself rises from the sea in Revelation 13, and ancient sea monsters like Leviathan and Rahab symbolize chaos and threat. If there is no more sea, there is no longer any place left for evil to hide or oppress God's people. It has been entirely removed.

## The bride, coming down

"I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband" (Revelation 21:2). This draws deeply on Isaiah's picture of Jerusalem herself: dressed beautifully, given a new name, entering an intimate, joyful relationship with God (Isaiah 61:10-62:5). And notice, comparing this verse with verses 10-11 later in the chapter: the bride's beautiful dress turns out to be nothing less than God's own glory — and God's glory, as Exodus 33 shows us, is fundamentally his mercy and compassion. The church, the bride, is called to reflect that same mercy.

"Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them" (Revelation 21:3) — the fulfillment of a promise running all the way back through the Pentateuch, through Ezekiel's vision of God returning to his temple, into the New Testament, and finally realized here in full: God, personally and permanently present with his people, not at a distance, not through an intermediary, but face to face.

"He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain" (Revelation 21:4). Every grief we have ever carried — every loss, every fear, every real and painful memory of oppression, exile, or violence — finds its ending here, personally addressed by God's own hand.

## A cube like the Holy of Holies

When we look at the city's shape, something remarkable appears: it's a cube (Revelation 21:16). In the entire Old Testament, only one space shares this shape — the Holy of Holies, the innermost chamber of the temple (2 Chronicles 3:8), where God's presence dwelt and which only the high priest could enter, once a year, at tremendous risk. That space existed precisely because there was still distance between God and his people — the farther you stood from the Holy of Holies, the farther you were from God's presence.

In the New Jerusalem, the entire city is that same shape — meaning the whole city is the Holy of Holies now, and every believer stands equally, fully close to God. The access once reserved for the high priest alone, on pain of death if approached wrongly, is now the ordinary inheritance of every single believer. That is the ultimate reversal of every system, ancient or modern, that ever excluded ordinary people from God's presence, keeping it for an elite few.

## The counterpoint to Babylon

The New Jerusalem is deliberately drawn as the opposite, point by point, of Babylon the harlot from our earlier session. Babylon's wealth came through exploitation and idolatry; Jerusalem's riches are God's own gift, freely given. Babylon caused suffering; Jerusalem removes it entirely. Babylon collapsed in a single hour, without warning; Jerusalem stands forever, secure. Everything the harlot only pretended to offer — permanence, glory, security — the New Jerusalem actually delivers, without the deception or the cost paid by the vulnerable.

## Every nation, welcomed

"The nations will walk by its light, and the kings of the earth will bring their splendor into it" (Revelation 21:24). This echoes Isaiah's picture of the nations streaming to Jerusalem's light, bringing their wealth in acknowledgment that God's blessing rests there. In both Isaiah and Revelation, what the nations bring is not something they already possessed independently — it is something they now offer back to God, no longer as the currency of Babylon's false economy, but as an act of worship. Israel's calling — beginning with Abraham, promised to bless all nations — finally reaches its completion here: every tribe, tongue, people, and nation gathered, without losing what makes each nation distinctly itself, all now serving the one true God together.

## Living water and the tree of life

The chapter closes on a river of life flowing from God's throne, and the tree of life, once lost at Eden, now freely accessible to all (Revelation 22:1-2). The whole story of Scripture, from the garden onward, has been the story of God relentlessly restoring what was lost at the fall — and here, finally, it's restored completely. "To the thirsty I will give water without cost from the spring of the water of life" (Revelation 21:6) — free, abundant, permanent.

## Closing

This is where the whole story of Revelation, and indeed the whole story of Scripture, has been heading: not escape from the world, but the world itself, and God's people within it, finally and fully made new. Every tear wiped away personally. Every barrier between us and God's presence removed. Every nation gathered, distinct and honored, worshiping together. This is our actual eternal home — not a vague, distant idea, but a specific, promised reality, described in careful, deliberate detail because God wants us to hold onto it.

What griefs are you carrying that you can trust God to personally address in that day? What would it change about how you live now, to remember that this — not Babylon's glamor, not any earthly system's promises — is your real and permanent home?
