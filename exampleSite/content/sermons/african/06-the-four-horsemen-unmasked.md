## Four riders, one puzzle

Revelation 6 introduces four apocalyptic horsemen, unleashed as the Lamb opens the first four seals: war, violence, famine, and death riding out across the earth. Three of them are clearly destructive. But the first rider — the one on the white horse — causes real debate, because on the surface, he almost looks like good news.

## The text

"I looked, and there before me was a white horse! Its rider held a bow, and he was given a crown, and he rode out as a conqueror bent on conquest" (Revelation 6:2).

A white horse. A crown. Conquest. Doesn't that sound like Jesus? Later in Revelation, a rider on a white horse with many crowns really is Jesus, returning in victory (Revelation 19:11). White is consistently a positive color throughout the book. Could this first horseman be Christ himself, or the spread of the gospel, going out ahead of the coming troubles?

## Why that reading doesn't hold

Look closer, and the case falls apart. In Ezekiel, God strikes the bow out of the hands of Gog and Magog — marking the bow as the weapon of God's end-time enemy, not his ally (Ezekiel 39:3). The demonic locusts of Revelation 9:7 also wear crowns — so a crown alone doesn't prove divine origin. "Conquering" elsewhere in Revelation describes the beast conquering the saints (Revelation 11:7, 13:7) — not a positive action.

More importantly: the four horsemen function as a single unit, just as Zechariah's four horses and chariots form one unit (Zechariah 1:8-15; 6:1-8), and just as Revelation's seals, trumpets, and bowls each move in matching groups of four. Either all four horsemen are evil, or none of them are — you can't split this rider off from the other three.

And Jesus' own teaching about the last days, given in Mark 13, Matthew 24, and Luke 21, opens with exactly this order: first, a warning about deception ("many will come in my name" and lead many astray), then war, then famine. That's the same sequence as our four horsemen. The first horseman is deception disguised as victory — a false Christ, a counterfeit gospel of conquest without the cross.

## The unveiling

So here is the picture: the first horseman looks like a triumphant Christ-figure — crowned, conquering, riding a white horse — but he is secretly the devil's own gospel campaign, launched the moment the real Lamb is revealed as worthy to open the scroll (Revelation 5). Deception comes first, then it escalates: war (persecution of those who won't buy the deception), then economic injustice (the third horseman's scales, where luxury goods are protected from price shocks while the poor starve — an eight-to-sixteen-times markup on basic grain while wine and oil remain untouched), and finally death and its full harvest. That's why the next scene is the cry of those under the altar: "How long?" (Revelation 6:9-11). Once the deception, violence, and injustice have run their course, that cry makes complete sense.

## This is the prosperity gospel's oldest ancestor

Here is where this passage speaks with unusual precision into our own moment. The first horseman's whole appeal is that he looks like Christ but isn't — he promises victory, security, and conquest without ever going through the cross. That is exactly the shape of every message that offers breakthrough, prosperity, and triumph as the immediate, guaranteed inheritance of faith, without the cost, the suffering, or the patient endurance the rest of Revelation insists belongs to genuine discipleship.

This matters more here than in many other places in the world, because in a large number of our fastest-growing church movements, health-and-wealth teaching isn't a fringe position — it's often close to the mainstream. The first horseman is Revelation's own warning against exactly that message: a counterfeit Christ, riding in triumph, offering what looks like blessing but ends, several verses later, in the same devastation the other three horsemen bring. The real Jesus, revealed one chapter earlier, wins through the cross — a slaughtered Lamb, not a conquering rider offering an easy victory.

## Closing

However attractive the devil's offer looks — and it is built to look attractive, even to resemble something genuinely of God — following it ends in ruin, not blessing. Real wisdom does not come from being dazzled by a triumphant appearance. It comes from testing every promise of victory against the pattern already shown us in Revelation 5: real triumph, God's way, goes through the cross, not around it.

Where have you heard a gospel of guaranteed victory that skips the cross? What would it look like to test every message of breakthrough and blessing against the Lamb who was slain, rather than the rider who merely looks like him?
