## A word before we begin

Before we open the text, a word directly to whoever is in this room. Some of you reading or hearing this live somewhere in relative peace. Others of you may be in the Sahel, in Nigeria, in Sudan, or in another place where what this session describes is not ancient history or abstract theology — it is this month, this week, maybe today. This session is written with both of you in mind. What follows is not a lesson to study from a safe distance. It is, for some of us, a description of our actual lives.

## Counting God's army

Revelation 7 opens with an angel sealing 144,000 servants of God — 12,000 from each tribe of Israel — before the trumpet judgments begin. This echoes Ezekiel 9, where an angel marks the righteous before judgment falls, sparing them from what's coming. The sealing is both protection and a call to stay faithful, undiluted by compromise.

Notice which tribe is named first: Judah, the tribe Jesus comes from (Revelation 7:5). And notice the counting itself: it's framed exactly like a military census, the way Israel's army was counted in the wilderness. These sound like soldiers, ready for battle.

But then comes the same twist we've already seen twice in this book — the Lion of Judah announced in chapter 5 turns out to be a slaughtered Lamb; and here, the 144,000 counted like an army turn out, when John actually looks, to be "a great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language, standing before the throne and before the Lamb" (Revelation 7:9). What John hears is soldiers. What he sees is worshipers. That is the church — sealed, safe, and drawn from every nation, not confined to ethnic Israel.

These worshipers are described as coming "out of the great tribulation" (Revelation 7:14), robes washed white in the blood of the Lamb, now comforted, with every tear wiped away — already living, in some sense, "the not yet" ahead of time.

## What is the Great Tribulation, really?

Here's where a great deal of confusion gets cleared up. "The Great Tribulation" is not a short, future seven-year crisis reserved for one final generation. The term comes from Daniel 12:1 — "There will be a time of distress such as has not happened from the beginning of nations until then. But at that time your people... will be delivered" — a passage Jesus himself quotes directly in Matthew 24:21 concerning the fall of Jerusalem. Daniel's own context was the suffering of God's faithful under a specific pagan oppressor, meant to purify and distinguish the genuinely faithful from those who would compromise.

Revelation picks up this same term and stretches it across the entire period between Jesus' first and second coming. Satan, defeated and cast down at Christ's first coming (Revelation 12:7-9), knows his time is short and is ruthless because of it — attacking Israel first, then the church, throughout this whole age. That means the church's suffering, from the first century until the day Christ returns, is what Scripture means by the Great Tribulation. It is not a future event we are waiting to enter. The church has been living in it since Pentecost. Paul and Peter both experienced it. We are, right now, still in it.

## Why this matters here, more than almost anywhere

For readers in comfortable circumstances, this teaching can feel abstract — an interesting theological point about timing and terminology. For many of us, it isn't abstract at all. If you are currently facing threats to your church, your community, or your family because of your faith — in the Sahel, in Nigeria, in Sudan, or anywhere else this is a daily, lived reality — this passage is not describing something distant. It is describing you, right now, in real time.

And here is what Revelation actually offers you, precisely because it recognizes your situation is real and not merely historical: you are not forgotten, not overlooked, not left to face this alone. You are sealed. You are counted — not lost in some vague, uncounted suffering, but personally known and marked as God's own, exactly the way Ezekiel's angel marked the righteous before judgment fell. And the ending is not in doubt: an uncountable multitude from every nation, robes washed white, standing before the throne, every tear personally wiped away by God himself (Revelation 7:17).

## Not abandonment — companionship and eventual comfort

Notice something easy to miss: this vision doesn't promise the tribulation will simply stop happening to God's people. It promises that those who endure it come out the other side into comfort, into the presence of God, into complete vindication. The path runs through the suffering, not around it — exactly as it did for Jesus himself. But it does not run through it alone, and it does not run through it forever.

If you are in a season of real, active danger for your faith right now, hear this plainly: your suffering is seen, your endurance is not wasted, and the comfort promised here is not a metaphor. "Never again will they hunger; never again will they thirst... For the Lamb at the center of the throne will be their shepherd; he will lead them to springs of living water. And God will wipe away every tear from their eyes" (Revelation 7:16-17).

## Closing

The 144,000 counted as an army turn out to be an uncountable multitude of worshipers. The Great Tribulation is not a future crisis to brace for — it is the whole span of the church's life between Christ's two comings, a span some of us are living through with unusual, painful directness right now. And the promise standing at the end of it is not vague comfort but a specific, personal one: sealed, safe, seen, and one day gathered before the throne with every tear wiped away by God's own hand.

If you are currently facing real danger for your faith, what would it mean this week to know you are sealed and counted, not forgotten? If you are not currently in that kind of danger, how might you stand with, pray for, and materially support those in this room, or elsewhere in the body of Christ, who are?
