## Why worship, and why now?

Revelation 4 opens onto one of the most breathtaking scenes in all of Scripture: an open door in heaven, a throne, and unceasing worship surrounding it. Every plague, every judgment, every trumpet and bowl that follows in this book flows out of this one scene. Before anything else happens, John is shown worship. Why?

Because worship is the answer to every problem the seven churches of chapters 2-3 were facing. Wrong doctrine leading to wrong behavior — as in Pergamon and Thyatira. Wrong self-evaluation leading to wrong action — as in Sardis and Laodicea. Wrong priorities leading to a hollowed-out faith — as in Ephesus. Overwhelming circumstances tempting believers to give up — as in Smyrna and Philadelphia. For every one of these, the throne room is the cure. The more clearly we know and worship God as he actually is, the less any false teaching can move us; the more we see ourselves rightly in relation to him, the more our priorities fall into place; and the more we trust him, the less easily we're shaken, because we've anchored ourselves to something unshakable.

This is not an abstract point for many of us. In movements across our continent where worship is loud, physical, expectant, and central to the life of the church — where we don't merely discuss God but actively encounter him in gathered praise — Revelation 4 isn't a strange or foreign scene. It is, in many ways, a description of what already happens most Sundays. That instinct is right, and it deserves to be affirmed, not corrected.

## What John sees

John's first impression is God's overwhelming glory — so much that it can't be looked at directly, only described through reflected images: jasper, carnelian, a rainbow like an emerald encircling the throne. There's a detail worth pausing on here. When Moses asked to see God's glory, he wasn't shown God's face — he was shown God's mercy (Exodus 33:18-20). That means God's glory can rightly be described as his mercy. Every judgment that follows in this book, then, should be read through that lens: not vindictiveness, but mercy working itself out even in judgment.

The rainbow recalls God's covenant with Noah — a promise with no conditions attached, a pure gift (Genesis 9:8-11). Its presence here signals that whatever plagues are coming, they are limited, held within God's covenant faithfulness, not unleashed without restraint.

And then there's the sea of glass before the throne (Revelation 4:6) — calm, crystal, motionless. Throughout Scripture, the sea is the place evil comes from, the place of chaos and threat (Psalm 74; Isaiah 57:20). Here it is frozen solid, subdued, powerless. The message: whatever chaos and evil still churns in the world below, in God's own throne room it has already been stilled.

## Creation and covenant together

Around the throne stand four living creatures — lion, ox, man, eagle — representing the whole of creation (echoing Ezekiel 1), and twenty-four elders, echoing both the twenty-four priestly divisions David established for temple worship and the twelve tribes plus twelve apostles together. This is a striking detail: Israel and the church, pictured as one worshiping body, despite every tension between them elsewhere in the book. At its very core, the church's identity is defined by worship — not activity, not doctrine alone, not organizational structure, but worship first.

## An honest word about spiritual warfare

Because this vision draws so directly on the language of thrones, principalities, and cosmic realities, it's tempting — especially where our traditions already have a rich vocabulary for spiritual warfare — to read chapter 4 primarily as a battle scene, a strategy session before the fight. That instinct isn't wrong to have; Revelation absolutely assumes an unseen spiritual reality behind visible events. But notice what actually happens in this chapter: no battle plans, no binding of territorial spirits, no naming of ancestral strongholds. Just worship. The entire spiritual battle described in the rest of Revelation is won and unfolds from a throne room where the only activity is uninterrupted adoration. If we import a more elaborate spiritual-warfare system onto this text than it actually gives us, we risk missing its real, simpler, more radical claim: worship itself is the decisive spiritual act. Everything else in the book flows from it.

## The answer for every struggling church

Whatever the seven churches were dealing with — compromise, complacency, persecution, false teaching — Revelation's answer for every single one of them is the same: come back to the throne room. Not because worship magically fixes external circumstances, but because worship rightly orders everything else. When God is truly first, everything — priorities, fears, ambitions, even our understanding of ourselves — falls into its right place.

## Closing

The letters to the seven churches expose real problems in real congregations. But Revelation's answer to all of them isn't a program or a strategy. It's a summons back to the throne room — to worship God as he actually is, in his overwhelming glory and his covenant mercy, and to let that worship reorder everything else in our lives. This is why the book of Revelation, so often read as a book of fear, actually begins — and ends — in worship.

Ask yourself: does your worship life actually reorder your priorities, or has it become one more activity alongside everything else competing for your devotion? What would it look like to trust that worship itself — not strategy, not our own spiritual effort — is the church's real weapon?
