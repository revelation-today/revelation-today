## A good life — guaranteed

Imagine someone promising you this: security, prosperity, cultural flourishing, law and order, a life free from war on your doorstep. And all they ask in return is that you publicly acknowledge who you owe it all to.

Sounds like a fair deal, doesn't it? That was the exact offer the first readers of Revelation lived under. It had a name: the Pax Romana, the "Roman Peace." And we live in a strikingly similar world — Rome, the emperor, and the temple just wear different names now.

## What was the Pax Romana?

From about 27 BC into the late second century AD, the Roman Empire experienced a long stretch of relative stability: few civil wars, a strong and unified empire, real prosperity for many, and a flourishing of art and culture. At the same time, this was a period of sporadic persecution of Christians under emperors like Nero and Domitian, even though the first truly empire-wide, systematic persecution didn't begin until Emperor Decius's edict in 250 AD.

Underneath these conditions sat a coherent, almost theological story: the gods have chosen Rome. The emperor is their instrument, their representative on earth. Every blessing — security, peace, justice, wealth, fertility — flows through him to those who submit to his rule. That is why the emperor deserved worship, loyalty, and titles equal to a god.

This message wasn't tucked away in the background. It was everywhere: on coins, in statues, in processions, at the games, even toasted at shared meals. Cities in Asia Minor — precisely where the seven churches of Revelation stood — competed with each other to build temples to the emperor, hoping to win his favor.

And here's the uncomfortable truth underneath it: this "golden age" was purchased with violence, oppression, and pacification by force. It was golden mainly for those who fell in line.

## The believer's problem

For Christians inside this system, there was a genuine dilemma. Jesus was their emperor — King of kings — not Caesar. They trusted him for provision, not the state. And they simply could not join in giving the emperor the divine honor demanded of them.

Jesus himself had already said it plainly: "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve both God and money" (Matthew 6:24). That left believers with three paths.

First, give up entirely — deny the faith to belong. Second, stand entirely — live the faith fully, risking social exclusion, financial ruin, imprisonment, even death. Third, and most tempting of all: conform outwardly, stay faithful inwardly. Confess Caesar with the mouth, keep Jesus in the heart. Comfortable — and deeply compromised.

John, the writer of Revelation, describes himself this way: "I, John, your brother and companion in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus, was on the island of Patmos because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus" (Revelation 1:9). He is writing from exile — because he refused to play along. The whole book he writes is a kind of counter-propaganda: showing who the true emperor really is, who the true savior really is, and that the Pax Romana's shining surface will collapse in the end.

## Our own Pax Romana

Here is where this touches us directly. The temptation the first Christians faced didn't stay in the ancient world — it just changed clothes.

Across our own continent, in our own cities and villages, we have our own version of a "golden age" on offer: financial breakthrough, political favor, social respectability, technological progress. None of that is bad in itself — we can be grateful for it. But the Pax Romana's question was never "is it good?" It was always: **who do you actually credit for it, and what does that demand of you?**

A few modern faces of that same question, right here in this room. Prosperity as savior — when our sense of security rests on our bank balance, our harvest, our business, our connections, rather than on God, the "emperor" has simply changed his name to money. Public approval as temple — nobody demands incense before a statue of Caesar anymore, but the pressure to publicly affirm whatever the crowd, the clan, or the movement demands — even when your heart isn't fully behind it — can feel remarkably similar to bowing before the emperor. National or tribal pride as faith — when loyalty to nation, party, or ethnic group becomes so entangled with our faith that it's hard to say which one we actually serve first. And comfortable compromise — church on Sunday, business as usual with whatever power structure runs the rest of the week, never questioned.

Exactly as in ancient Asia Minor, it was easy to conform outwardly. It still is. The difference is that for most of us, conformity rarely costs our life. It just costs our whole devotion — and that makes it more dangerous, not less, because it's so easy to miss.

## What Revelation offers instead

The remarkable thing about Revelation is that it doesn't call believers primarily to withdraw from the world. It calls them to **clear-eyed worship**. Before anything else happens in the book, John sees, in chapters 4 and 5, a throne room where God and the Lamb are worshiped without reservation. That is the answer to every Pax Romana, ancient or modern: not escape, but the question of who truly sits on the throne of your life.

And Revelation is honest: the third way — outward conformity, inward faithfulness — is never presented as a workable solution. It is unmasked for what it is: half-heartedness. That is exactly what Jesus later says to the church in Laodicea — lukewarm, neither hot nor cold.

## Closing

Believers in first-century Asia Minor had to ask themselves who they truly served: the emperor, who promised security and prosperity, or the crucified and risen Jesus, who promised hardship first and eternal life after. That is the same question in front of us today, dressed differently.

Some questions to carry with you: Where do you actually expect the solution to your problems to come from — God, or your income, your connections, your health, your nation? Where do you say something publicly that you don't fully believe inwardly, just to belong? If your convictions cost you something real — professionally, socially, within your family — would you still hold to them? What would that actually look like?

The Pax Romana is long gone. The temptation it represented never left — it just wears new colors. The good news of Revelation is that the true King is already on the throne. He does not ask for lip service; he invites us into real, whole-hearted worship. And this King has already won — not through force, but through self-giving love.
