## The most important session in this series

Of everything Revelation addresses, this is the one that speaks most directly and most personally into many of our churches today. In a region where health-and-wealth teaching is, in many places, not a fringe position but close to the mainstream of independent and Pentecostal-charismatic Christianity, Revelation's harlot Babylon material is aimed with striking precision at exactly this territory. This isn't an attack on anyone in this room. It's an invitation to test what we've been taught to expect from God against what Scripture actually promises.

## A crowded chapter, one clear picture

Revelation 17 introduces a lot of characters at once: a great harlot, a scarlet beast with seven heads and ten horns, seven kings, ten more kings, and finally Jesus himself. It can feel confusing, but every piece serves one purpose: unveiling the devil's entire system, piece by piece.

The beast in this chapter closely mirrors the first beast of chapter 13 — same seven heads and ten horns, same claim to being unrivaled, same blasphemous name. It represents imperial military and political power. The seven hills the woman sits on identified Rome unmistakably to the first readers — this was never a prophecy about some future pope or nation, but a description of the empire actually ruling them at the time.

The harlot herself sits on the beast, dressed in the very same materials as the Old Testament high priest — the offering for the tabernacle, the ephod, the breastplate — but where the priest's forehead-plate read "Holy to the Lord," hers reads "Babylon the great, the mother of harlots" (Revelation 17:5). She is a corrupted picture of what should have been holy. The parallel goes further: the same judgment prescribed for a priest's daughter who became a prostitute — death by fire (Leviticus 21:9) — falls on her exactly (Revelation 18:8).

## What the harlot actually represents

The beast is political and military power. The harlot is everything that dresses that power up and makes it attractive: the economy, culture, ideology, public celebration — coins, holidays, impressive temple worship, a whole way of daily life that doesn't look evil at all. Together, they form Satan's rival gospel: security bought through fear of violence, wealth through the exploitation of the weak or the exclusion of anyone who won't comply, a sense of superiority, and the silencing of any inconvenient voice.

This is drawn from a long Old Testament pattern. Judah, Tyre, Nineveh, Israel itself — all were, at different points, called a "harlot" for exactly this pattern: a covenant relationship with God traded for wealth, security, and idolatry. The harlot in Revelation isn't a brand-new idea. She's what a covenant relationship with God looks like once it's turned toward idol worship instead — and the specific idol in view here is wealth and security purchased by compromising with an oppressive system.

## The direct collision

Let's name this plainly, because Revelation does. The harlot's core offer is exactly what health-and-wealth teaching offers: wealth and security as the visible proof of divine favor, blessing detached from covenant faithfulness, glamor built on a foundation the text is careful to describe as violence, injustice, and idolatry, however elegantly it's dressed up.

Look at her cargo list in chapter 18 — gold, jewels, fine linen, spices, and then, right alongside all of it, without any separation in the list: "human beings sold as slaves" (Revelation 18:13). People, treated as one more commodity next to cinnamon and cattle. That's what happens wherever wealth and security become the measure of God's blessing: people themselves eventually become expendable in service of the system.

She sees herself as untouchable: "I sit enthroned as queen. I am not a widow, and I will never mourn" (Revelation 18:7). That's the language of absolute, permanent security through wealth — precisely the confidence prosperity teaching offers as the mark of true faith. And her fall comes without warning, in a single hour (Revelation 18:10, 17, 19) — because anything built on shifting worldly power, rather than on God, collapses without notice the moment its supporting alliances change.

## Whose security are you actually buying?

The beast's number, 666, deserves a word here too, because it's often mishandled as a hidden code for decoding modern politics. It isn't. Read plainly, in context, it's Revelation's own verdict: measured against the real Trinity — Father, Son, and Spirit — the devil's imitation trinity fails, fails, and fails again. The dragon's power is only ever borrowed, and only for a limited time. The first beast's death and resurrection are a fake. The second beast rules by fear, not invitation. Three sixes: three failing grades.

And the number carries one more loaded reference: it appears written out exactly as "666" in only one other place in the whole Bible — the account of Solomon's income, "666 talents of gold" a year (1 Kings 10:14), positioned right at the peak of his life, immediately before the account of his catastrophic fall into compromise and idolatry (1 Kings 11). Solomon asked God for wisdom, received wealth on top of it as a gift — and then let the wealth itself slowly outrank his relationship with God, with 666 quietly marking the turning point. The warning could not be sharper: the danger isn't wealth itself. It's wealth become the thing we actually trust, the substitute for the God who gave it.

That is the whole question Revelation asks through both the beast and the harlot: whose security are you actually buying, and what are you willing to trade for it?

## Why she falls

Here is one of Revelation's strangest, most instructive turns: the very kings who built her up, who depended on her, are the ones who turn and destroy her (Revelation 17:16), and then weep over her ruin (Revelation 18:9). Loyalty built on mutual advantage always eventually breaks, because everyone in that system is ultimately looking out for their own interest first. God does not change. Systems built on shifting human loyalty always will.

## A pastoral word, not a condemnation

None of this makes wealth, trade, business success, or economic growth inherently evil. Scripture never says that. The question Revelation actually presses is narrower and more searching: what do we do when the economy crumbles, when the harvest fails, when the promised breakthrough doesn't come? Do we quietly conclude that God has failed us — or that we simply didn't have enough faith — because our theology told us faithfulness always produces visible prosperity? That is the precise moment we need to ask whether we've unknowingly moved into Babylon's territory, mistaking her glamor for God's blessing.

The call, both to the church addressed in Revelation and to us, is the same: "Come out of her, my people" (Revelation 18:4) — not out of business, not out of ambition, not out of hope for a better life, but out of the lie that wealth and ease are the true measure of God's favor, and the false gospel that promises them without the cross.

## Closing: the real prosperity

Revelation doesn't end with the harlot's fall. It ends, three chapters later, with the New Jerusalem — a city where God himself lives permanently among his people, where there is no more death, mourning, or pain, where every tear is personally wiped away. That is the real, lasting security Revelation actually promises — not wealth as proof of favor now, but God's own presence, forever, freely given to "the thirsty... without cost" (Revelation 21:6). That is what real, permanent prosperity looks like, and it's better than anything Babylon ever offered.

Where have you, even unknowingly, measured God's favor by your bank balance or your circumstances rather than his presence? What would it look like this week to trade the promise of the harlot for the promise of the New Jerusalem?
