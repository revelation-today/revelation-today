## A Christmas story you haven't heard

You know the familiar Christmas story from Luke — shepherds, a manger, wise travelers following a star. It's beautiful, and we've heard it so many times we barely hear it anymore. Today, let's hear another Christmas story — this one from Revelation 12.

## The scene

"A great sign appeared in heaven: a woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet and a crown of twelve stars on her head. She was pregnant and cried out in pain as she was about to give birth. Then another sign appeared in heaven: an enormous red dragon with seven heads and ten horns and seven crowns on its heads... The dragon stood in front of the woman who was about to give birth, so that it might devour her child the moment he was born. She gave birth to a son, a male child, who 'will rule all the nations with an iron scepter.' And her child was snatched up to God and to his throne" (Revelation 12:1-5).

Picture the scene without knowing how it ends: a woman about to give birth, utterly vulnerable, facing an enormous, furious dragon whose only goal is to devour her child the instant he's born. By every human measure, this child has no chance. And yet — the child is snatched away, safe, to God's own throne. The dragon fails completely.

## Who is who

The dragon is named plainly a few verses later: "that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan" (Revelation 12:9) — language that immediately recalls the serpent in Eden.

The child is identified by one detail: he will rule with an iron scepter, language drawn from Psalm 2 (the nations rebel, God warns them to fear his Son) and Isaiah 11:4 (the coming Messiah strikes the earth with the rod of his mouth). Jesus himself claims this title in Revelation 2:27. This is Jesus, and this is the story of his birth.

Who, then, is the woman clothed with sun, moon, and twelve stars? Three candidates exist: Mary, who literally gave birth to Jesus; Eve, promised that her offspring would crush the serpent (Genesis 3:15); or Israel, promised to give birth to the Messiah (Isaiah 66:7). Mary seems the obvious answer, but nowhere does Scripture connect Mary to sun, moon, and stars — that association came later, from church tradition, not from the Bible itself. The closer match is Joseph's dream in Genesis 37:9-10, where the sun, moon, and eleven stars represent his parents and eleven brothers — Joseph himself making the twelfth. Those twelve are, in effect, the twelve tribes of Israel. The woman is Israel.

## The story, told again

So the story is this: Israel — the woman — was promised she would give birth to the Messiah, and that promise came due precisely when Israel was under crushing pressure, occupied and oppressed by Rome, with every rebellion against Rome brutally crushed. Into that pressure, Jesus was born — and was rescued when Herod tried to kill him as an infant. As his ministry began, the devil tried again to destroy him through temptation, and failed again. Jesus overcomes every attack, even death on the cross, and is raised and taken up to heaven.

This is the same Christmas story we already know — but told with the emotional weight laid bare: the glory of the woman alongside her helplessness, the fear for her child, the dragon's rage and determination, and the stunning reversal at the end.

## The pattern for the whole book

Revelation never explains exactly how Jesus overcame — it simply shows, again and again, that the one who looks helpless is the one who actually wins. The two churches with no complaint against them at all, Smyrna and Philadelphia, are also the two in the worst outward circumstances. The Lion of Judah, expected to conquer by force, turns out to be a slaughtered Lamb (Revelation 5:5-6). The two witnesses appear utterly defeated, yet are the ones actually triumphant (Revelation 11:7-12) — set against the dragon and beast of chapter 13, who look unstoppable but end in total disaster.

## Naming — and containing — the spiritual conflict

This chapter puts cosmic spiritual conflict right at the center of the gospel story, and that will feel immediately at home to many of us — we already carry a strong sense that the world is not merely material, that real spiritual forces are at work behind visible events. That instinct is exactly right, and Revelation 12 confirms it directly.

But notice carefully what the text actually claims, and what it doesn't. It names one dragon, one child, one woman, one specific historical conflict — Rome's oppression of Israel, into which Jesus was born and through which he triumphed. It does not describe territorial spirits assigned to specific regions, generational curses passed down bloodlines, or a detailed map of demonic hierarchy. Those frameworks may feel natural to layer onto this passage, especially where our traditions already have well-developed language for that kind of spiritual warfare — but doing so risks obscuring the chapter's own, sharper claim: there is exactly one decisive battle in cosmic history, it was already fought and won at the cross and resurrection, and the outcome is already settled. We are not still waiting to find out who wins.

## Closing

This Christmas story ends the way ours should too: what looks weak triumphs, and what looks unstoppable falls. The dragon already lost. Whatever pressure, opposition, or apparent helplessness you're facing, the pattern of this chapter — and the pattern of the whole book — says the same thing: God's power is most fully revealed exactly where it looks like it should have failed.

Where in your life does the dragon look unstoppable right now? What would it mean to trust that the decisive battle is already over, even while the struggle continues?
