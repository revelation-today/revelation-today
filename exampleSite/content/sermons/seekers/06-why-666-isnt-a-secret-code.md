## Opening Hook

If there's one thing almost everybody has heard about Revelation, whether or not they've ever opened a Bible, it's "666, the number of the beast." It shows up on horror movie posters, heavy metal album covers, and in nervous jokes about barcodes and microchips. For decades, people have tried to "crack" this number — running a president's name through some numbering scheme, decoding a product code, or reinterpreting it in a new language whenever the last attempt didn't fit. If you've ever wondered whether 666 is a secret code pointing at someone specific today, you're in very good historical company — plenty of serious people have tried that exact exercise. Tonight, let's look at why that approach almost always misses the point, and what the number was actually saying.

## Core Teaching

The number appears in Revelation 13:18, right after the description of the two beasts we looked at last time: "This calls for wisdom. Let the person who has insight calculate the number of the beast, for it is the number of a man. That number is 666."

The most common method people have used to "solve" this is called **gematria** — an ancient practice of assigning numerical values to letters, so that a name can be added up into a number. The trouble is, gematria is flexible almost to the point of meaninglessness: switch the language (Hebrew, Greek, Latin, English), try a nickname or a title instead of a birth name, or just read "666" as a triple-six instead of a sum — and you can make an enormous number of names across history "fit." Historically, this method has "identified" the beast as various popes, various political leaders, and — famously — even a twentieth-century American president, once you allow for a middle name and count each name's letters. A lock that opens with almost any key isn't actually telling you anything. That's the core problem with number-hunting as a strategy: it says more about the person doing the calculating than about the text.

So what should we do instead? Go back to the first readers and ask what 666 would have meant to them, using clues already inside the Bible itself.

One approach looks at how numbers function elsewhere in Scripture. The number seven regularly signals completeness — tied to God, who completed creation in seven days. The number six, one short of seven, signals incompleteness or human limitation — humanity was created on the sixth day. Some readers of Revelation have suggested we can see this play out across all three figures of Satan's counterfeit trinity from last session — thinking of the count "6, 6, 6" as picturing failure on the very points where the counterfeit needed to succeed: the dragon's power is only ever borrowed and temporary, not eternal like God's; the first beast's "resurrection" isn't a real death and return to life, unlike Jesus's; the second beast operates through fear and coercion, not the Spirit's invitation. It's worth being honest that this reading works better as an illustration of the number's meaning than as something the text spells out explicitly — but as an illustration, it makes a real point: however impressive this system looks, it keeps falling short, three times over, of the reality it's imitating.

A second, more historically grounded approach connects "the number of a man" to a specific first-century figure using the gematria method in the way ancient readers themselves would have used it — not modern guesswork, but the actual number-values of letters in the actual language of the time. Read that way, the number matches the name of the Emperor Nero strikingly well. Nero was the first emperor to actively persecute Christians, and after his death, persistent rumors claimed he wasn't really dead and would return with an army from the East — an idea that let later writers use "Nero" almost as a symbol for "the persecuting power of Rome, however it returns." That fits Revelation's larger pattern: a real historical figure, in the first readers' own lifetime, standing for a recurring type of threat.

A third connection is worth noting for what it warns against, rather than what it decodes. In the Old Testament, King Solomon — famous for wisdom, wealth, and building God's temple — receives exactly 666 talents of gold in a single year, right at the height of his success, just before the account of his tragic downfall into idolatry and compromise. It's placed there deliberately: a warning that wealth and success, unchecked, can quietly pull someone away from God even after decades of faithfulness.

Taken together, these threads point in one consistent direction: 666 isn't a puzzle waiting for you to plug in the name of a modern celebrity, dictator, or piece of technology. It's a verdict — a way of saying that the devil's whole showy system, no matter how impressive or threatening it looks, is a spectacular failure by God's own standard, and a warning against being seduced by the same things that tripped up Solomon: counterfeit security, borrowed power, and wealth mistaken for wisdom.

## So What

Here's an honest, gentler way to hold this: it's understandable that a mysterious number attached to "evil" invites speculation — mystery is genuinely intriguing, and wanting to make sense of a scary world by finding the villain is a very human impulse. But chasing 666 through today's headlines misses what the text was actually built to do: warn its readers (then and now) not to be seduced by systems that promise security and power through fear, force, or wealth instead of love. That's a far more useful question to sit with than "whose name adds up to 666 this week."

## Discussion Questions

1. Had you heard theories about "666" before tonight? Where did you first encounter them, and did they make sense to you at the time?
2. Why do you think number-decoding theories about Revelation keep resurfacing generation after generation, even though the "match" always seems to need updating?
3. If 666 is really a warning against counterfeit security, power, and wealth, where do you see similar counterfeits offered to us today?
