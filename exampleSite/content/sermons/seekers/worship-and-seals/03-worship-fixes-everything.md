## Opening Hook

Quick question: if you had to guess what sits at the actual center of the book of Revelation — the thing everything else revolves around — what would you guess? Disaster? Judgment? The end of the world? Here's the surprise: it's none of those. At the literal structural center of the book, right after the letters to the seven churches and right before any plague or judgment begins, John doesn't describe a war. He describes a worship service.

## Core Teaching

First, a quick word on what "worship" even means, since it can sound like church jargon. Worship, at its simplest, is giving your attention, honor, and trust to whatever you consider most valuable or most in control of your life. Everybody worships something — the question is only what, or who.

In Revelation chapters 4 and 5, John is given a vision of a throne room in heaven. Around the throne are strange, vivid figures — four living creatures (representing all of creation), twenty-four elders (representing God's people, from the Old Testament tribes of Israel all the way through to Jesus's apostles), and countless angels — and all of them are caught up in continuous, awestruck worship of the one seated on the throne. Nothing has happened yet in the story. No plague, no battle. Just this: overwhelming worship, first.

Why does this matter so much? Because if you look back at the seven churches John had just finished writing to (which we'll spend a whole session on next time), you find them struggling with a strikingly wide range of problems: some had drifted into wrong belief, some had grown deaf and numb to their own condition, some had let other priorities quietly crowd out what mattered most, some were simply exhausted and tempted to give up under real pressure. Different problems, different churches — but the throne room offers the same answer to every single one of them.

Here's why worship works as the answer to all of it:

- The more clearly we see God as he actually is, the less we're fooled by false versions of him or persuasive alternatives.
- When we worship God as he truly is, we see ourselves more honestly too — including where we've fooled ourselves.
- When God is genuinely first, everything else falls into its proper place. When he isn't, everything else gets subtly warped trying to fill that spot.
- When our trust is anchored in something truly unshakable, we're not so easily shaken by hard circumstances.

There's a detail in this vision worth pausing on: a rainbow encircles the throne. Any reader familiar with the Old Testament would recognize that image instantly — it's the sign God gave Noah as a promise never again to destroy the earth by flood, a promise with no conditions attached. Placing that rainbow around God's throne, right before a book full of plagues and judgments, is a signal: read everything that follows through the lens of mercy, not vindictiveness. God's glory, elsewhere in Scripture, is explicitly connected to his mercy and compassion — so that's the light in which the rest of the book should be read.

Every disaster, every plague, every judgment scene that follows in Revelation flows outward from this throne room. That's the book's real claim: history isn't chaotic or directionless. It radiates from a center of worship, mercy, and control that never wavers, no matter how out of control events on the ground may look.

## So What

If worship really is the answer to wrong belief, self-deception, misplaced priorities, and exhaustion under pressure, that's worth sitting with — whether or not you'd call yourself a Christian yet. Ask yourself honestly: what currently sits on the "throne" of your life — what do you give your ultimate attention, trust, and loyalty to? And does it deserve that place?

## Discussion Questions

1. Before this session, would you have guessed that "worship" is the actual center of Revelation, rather than judgment or disaster? Why or why not?
2. What (or who) genuinely holds first place in your attention and trust right now — and how did it get there?
3. Does the idea that mercy frames everything that follows in Revelation (the rainbow around the throne) change how you'd expect the rest of the book to read?
