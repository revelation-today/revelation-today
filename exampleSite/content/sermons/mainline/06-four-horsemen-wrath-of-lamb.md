# The Four Horsemen Unmasked / The Wrath of the Lamb

## Four riders, one puzzle

Revelation 6 introduces four horsemen — riders bringing conquest, war, famine, and death
across the earth. Commentators have argued for centuries about whether the first rider, on
the white horse, is a positive figure — Christ or the gospel going out — or whether he
belongs with the other three as an agent of evil. The text repays close attention here, and
the confusion turns out to be the point.

## Reading the four together

Old Testament background helps immediately. Zechariah describes four horses of different
colors patrolling the earth (1:8–15), and later four chariots doing the same (6:1–8);
Ezekiel names four plagues of God together — sword, famine, wild beasts, and pestilence
(14:12–23) — closely tracking what these four horsemen do. All four riders in Revelation
are summoned; none acts independently. Each is given an instrument to act with. Yet only
the first is explicitly commanded to a task — "to conquer" — while the others simply carry
out war, famine, and death without needing separate instruction, as though their nature
were already given.

There are real arguments on both sides for reading the first horseman positively: a
messianic king conquering with a bow appears in Psalm 45, applied to Christ in Hebrews 1;
Revelation itself later shows Christ on a white horse with many crowns (19:11) and
someone "like a son of man" with a sickle (14:14); white is elsewhere a positive color in
the book. But the case against is stronger on close reading. God strikes the bow from the
hand of Gog, the end-time enemy, in Ezekiel 39:3 — identifying the bow itself as the
weapon of God's opponent. Crowns in Revelation are not exclusive to Christ; the demonic
locusts of chapter 9 wear them too. "Conquering" elsewhere in the book describes the beast
overpowering the saints (11:7; 13:7) — the opposite of a triumphant gospel campaign. And
structurally, the four horses form a single unit, just as they do in Zechariah, just as the
first four trumpets and first four bowls each form their own unit later in the book — a
strong argument that whatever these four are, they stand or fall together.

The resolution: the first horseman is evil masquerading as good — as Christ himself. That
reading fits its position in the narrative precisely. The chapter immediately before this
one revealed Jesus as the only one worthy to open the scroll (chapter 5); now, in
response, the devil's true nature is revealed by contrast — moving from a counterfeit
promise of conquest, to war, to engineered economic injustice (the third horseman's
scales, weighing out inflated grain prices while sparing luxury oil and wine — an image of
crushing the poor while the wealthy are untouched), to death and Hades trailing behind. No
wonder the next scene shows the martyrs crying out, "How long?" (6:9–11). The point is not
subtle once seen: the devil's offer will always resemble something good, right up until it
delivers exactly the opposite.

## The wrath of the Lamb

The sixth seal brings cosmic upheaval, and the people of the earth cry out to the
mountains: "Fall on us and hide us... from the wrath of the Lamb!" (6:16). The phrase
appears only this once in the whole Bible, and it is worth noticing precisely what it
does and does not describe. It is not primarily a description of the Lamb's action — it
describes how terrified people perceive him, having just watched the devastation the four
horsemen (masquerading as Christ) left behind. They assume Jesus will act the same way. He
will not. His judgment is real and severe — final separation from God — but it does not
take the form of the horsemen's devastating violence.

The Greek word behind "wrath" here, *orge*, describes a long, slow build-up of pressure
rather than a sudden outburst — closer to the biblical picture of Adam and Eve hiding from
God after the Fall (Genesis 3:8), or Israel, ashamed of its idolatry, hiding from God's
face in Hosea 10:6–8, than to a temperamental deity losing his patience. People hide
because they finally recognize what they have done — not because an angry God is lashing
out. And crucially, only the people's terror is described here; the actual response of
Christ himself is never narrated in this scene at all.

## Why wrath is not a foreign category to grace

Confessional theology has never treated the wrath of God and the grace of God as
competing attributes needing to be balanced against each other or explained away. Both are
held together in the doctrine of the atonement itself: at the cross, God's wrath against
sin and God's mercy toward the sinner meet in the same event, borne by the same Christ.
Revelation's wrath of the Lamb is not evidence of a different, harsher God than the one
revealed at Calvary — it is the same God, still just, refusing forever to pretend that
oppression, violence, and exploitation do not matter. Those who have never lived under
real tyranny, slavery, or systemic violence can find the wrath of God difficult to
stomach in the abstract. Throughout most of history, most people have known at least one
of those realities firsthand. When God acts on behalf of the oppressed, that action is not
arbitrary; it is justice finally arriving. We are responsible for the suffering we cause,
and its consequences are not erased by a single prayer of convenience — God wants hearts of
stone to actually change, which is precisely what repentance, rightly preached, calls for.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- Why does it matter that the first horseman looks appealing before it is unmasked? Where
  do you see counterfeit versions of Christ's promises operating today?
- How does understanding *orge* as slow-building pressure, not a sudden outburst, change
  how you preach or teach about the wrath of God?
- How does holding wrath and grace together at the cross help you resist both a sentimental
  God who never judges and a capricious God who is simply angry?
- Where in the world today would you expect God's slow-building justice to eventually
  break in — and where do you need patience while it does?
