# The Unholy Trinity

## A counterfeit built to deceive, not just to destroy

Revelation 13 introduces the dragon and two beasts — one from the sea, one from the
land — and the mark, 666. Popular imagination has run wild with this chapter for
centuries, but read closely against its Old Testament background, it says something
specific and sobering: Satan's great strategy is not simply violence. It is imitation.

## Old Testament roots

The description draws on several recognizable sources. Job 40–41 describes Behemoth and
Leviathan, monstrous beasts of land and sea, invincible to human strength but mere
playthings before God (Psalm 104:26). The first beast's composite appearance — like a
leopard, with a bear's feet and a lion's mouth, ten horns, seven heads — deliberately
combines all four beasts of Daniel 7, which together represent the succession of pagan
empires opposing God's kingdom. And the second beast's demand that all worship an image
or be killed directly echoes Daniel 3, Nebuchadnezzar's golden statue and fiery furnace —
a story that itself ends with Daniel's friends rescued and the pagan king forced to praise
the true God. That detail matters: every Old Testament source behind this chapter's
imagery ends in the beast-figure's defeat. Revelation borrows the villains and keeps the
ending.

## The false trinity

The chapter's real subject becomes clear when you notice the pattern the dragon and two
beasts form together: the dragon gives all his authority to the first beast, just as the
Father gives all authority to the Son (Philippians 2:9–11); the first beast appears to die
and be resurrected, just as Christ died and rose; every knee bows to the first beast, as
every knee bows to Christ; the second beast has two horns like a lamb, and speaks with the
authority of the first beast to make everyone worship him, just as the Spirit speaks with
Christ's authority and points all worship toward him. This is a deliberate, three-part
counterfeit of the Trinity — dragon in place of Father, wounded-and-risen beast in place of
Son, deceptive lamb-voiced beast in place of Spirit.

This same three-part pattern of deception recurs across the whole book — the four
horsemen (deceiver, then war, then economic injustice, then death summarizing all three);
the dragon and two beasts here in chapter 13; and later the beast, false prophet, and
harlot in chapters 17–18. It is one playbook, shown three times from different angles, and
every single time it ends the same way: defeated.

## Why this matters theologically

The point of naming this counterfeit so precisely is not merely descriptive. It is
diagnostic. From the outside, the false trinity and the true one can look remarkably
alike — two horns like a lamb, a claim to victory, a claim to worship. The difference is in
the voice: the second beast "spoke like a dragon" (13:11). Only those who know the true
Shepherd's voice can tell the difference, "for his sheep know his voice" (John 10:4). And
in Hebrew thought, hearing rightly always implies obedience, not mere recognition — which
means only a church that stays attentive and submitted to the actual voice of Christ, not
merely the appearance of victory or power, can reliably distinguish the two.

That has a sharp edge for the church itself. Whenever the church resorts to fear,
coercion, manipulation, or violence to accomplish its ends — even in the name of good
outcomes — it is not following the Trinity it claims to worship. It is, in that moment,
following the pattern of the beast. The false trinity offers real, recognizable goods:
visible power so that faith isn't mocked as foolish, an easy and total spread of belief
without the discomfort of rejection, dramatic signs to settle doubt, decisive relief from
suffering, a God who simply crushes evil outright, a world that submits to your values
without a fight. None of those longings are themselves shameful. What's counterfeit is the
method: the beast gives nothing out of love, only out of the desire to intimidate; it
does not build up the church, it attacks it; it does not invite, it coerces. There is no
"setting aside love for a greater good," because love is the greater good (Matthew
22:36–39) — and no genuine victory, ancient or modern, is ever purchased at the cost of it.

## A note on miracles and power

None of this means every experience of divine power or victory should be viewed with
suspicion, as if strength itself were evidence of counterfeit. The devil would be doing a
poor job of imitation if nothing about the counterfeit resembled the real thing. God and
the church are victorious too — genuinely, and sometimes visibly — just by a different
route. What Satan cannot copy, no matter how convincing the imitation otherwise, is God's
signature: self-giving love, freely given rather than coerced.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- Where have you seen the church tempted to adopt fear, coercion, or manipulation to
  accomplish good ends? What made it tempting in the moment?
- The false trinity offers real goods — power, certainty, relief from suffering — through
  a corrupted method. Which of those longings do you feel most acutely right now?
- "Only his sheep know his voice." What has helped you learn to recognize Christ's actual
  voice, as opposed to a counterfeit dressed in familiar language?
- How does seeing this same three-part deception pattern recur three times across
  Revelation (horsemen, beasts, harlot) change how alert you are to it in daily life?
