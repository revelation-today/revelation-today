# God's Covenant: A Story of Amazing Grace

## Vocabulary you already own

If you were catechized in a Lutheran or Reformed congregation, "covenant theology" is not
a new framework this session is importing — it is very likely the framework you were
taught to read the whole Bible through in the first place. Reformed covenant theology
speaks of the covenant of grace running from Genesis 3 through Christ; Lutheran teaching,
while more cautious about building an entire covenantal system, still confesses that God's
promises to Israel and his promise in Christ are one continuous, gracious act of God
toward an undeserving people. This session simply puts the ancient Near Eastern furniture
back into the room so you can see how strikingly that grace was displayed at Sinai and
even earlier — and how directly it sets up everything Revelation later assumes about
God's relationship to his people.

## How ancient covenants actually worked

In the ancient world, belonging to a family meant survival — provision, protection,
identity. If you weren't born into the right family, Scripture recognizes four doors in:
marriage, birth, adoption, and covenant. Historically there were two kinds of covenants.
One was between equals — a mutual alliance, its members called brothers. The other was
between the strong and the weak: a covenant of conquest, in which the stronger party was
called master or father, and the weaker one slave or son. This second type matters most
for understanding Israel's relationship to God, because it was the standard pattern
whenever one nation conquered another. The stronger nation held authority; the weaker
nation paid tribute — but the stronger nation was in turn *obligated* to defend the weaker
one, because the weaker party was now, in effect, family. Joshua's covenant with the
deceptive Gibeonites (Joshua 9–10) shows exactly this dynamic in practice: once the
covenant existed, Joshua was bound to defend them even though he didn't want to.

The phrase "to make a covenant" literally means "to cut a covenant" — language drawn from
the ceremony itself. Animals were cut in half and laid in two rows, and the weaker party
walked between the pieces: a vivid, deliberate act of intimidation showing exactly what
would happen to him if he broke his word.

## The covenant with Abraham — and the reversal

Genesis 15 follows this same ancient pattern of sacrifice and implied threat — with one
staggering difference. It is not Abraham, the weaker party, who walks between the divided
animals. It is God himself, appearing as fire, who passes between the pieces. God takes
Abraham's place under the covenant curse before Abraham has even broken a single term.
That is why, later, when Abraham does break the covenant — his impregnation of Hagar
being the clearest instance — it is ultimately God's own Son who bears the curse instead
of Abraham's son. This is the "amazing grace" of the covenant: God binds himself to a
curse he did not deserve, on behalf of a partner who could not keep the terms, long before
Calvary makes that arrangement visible in history.

## The covenant with Israel — a standard treaty, upended

Ancient conquest covenants followed a standard five-part form: a preamble naming the
greater party in grand terms; a prologue recounting the greater party's past kindness, to
encourage willing acceptance; the demands themselves; blessings for obedience and curses
for disobedience; and witnesses called to guarantee the covenant would be kept. This exact
structure appears in the covenant at Sinai. The preamble: "I am the LORD your God."  The
prologue: "who brought you out of Egypt, out of the land of slavery" — look at what I've
already done for you. The demands begin with the first commandment: have no other gods.
Blessings and curses follow in Deuteronomy 28, escalating gradually through Leviticus 26's
repeated "if you still do not listen" — each stage designed to give Israel another chance
to reconsider. The witnesses called were heaven and earth themselves (Deuteronomy 30:19).

Crucially — and this is the heart of what makes this a covenant of grace rather than mere
law — the covenant's terms were never impossible, unlike so many earthly treaties designed
to set the weaker party up to fail. Israel could have kept the covenant. It chose not to.
And through it all, God calls himself Israel's Father — a father who, even while bound by
covenant terms, remains merciful. Scripture captures that combination in the rich Hebrew
word *hesed*, usually translated "covenant loyalty" or "steadfast love" — a word broad
enough to mean love, goodness, mercy, and faithfulness all at once, depending on context.

## Why this matters for reading Revelation

Once you see the covenant structure clearly, Revelation's logic becomes far less
mysterious. The letters to the seven churches follow exactly this covenant pattern:
commendation (prologue — recounting the church's own faithfulness), demand (repent, hold
fast), and blessing-or-curse (a promise to the one who overcomes, a warning to the one who
does not). The plagues that follow are not random cruelty; they are the covenant curses of
Leviticus 26 and Deuteronomy 28 escalating exactly as they did for Israel — measured,
repeated, giving space for repentance at every stage, precisely because that is how the God
of the covenant has always dealt with his people. And the whole book culminates in a
marriage — the covenant's oldest metaphor for God's bond with his people — where the Bride
is finally, fully home with the Lamb who bore the covenant curse for her.

## Amazing grace, indeed

This is the story confessional Christians sing every time they sing "Amazing Grace," and
confess every time they recite that Christ bore what we deserved. Revelation is not a
detour from that story into some separate genre of speculative future-telling. It is that
same covenant story reaching its climax — grace that took the curse upon itself at
Calvary, applied now to a people who, like Israel, cannot claim to have earned it.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- How does seeing the ancient covenant ceremony of Genesis 15 — God alone walking between
  the pieces — change how you read the doctrine of Christ bearing our curse?
- Where in the letters to the seven churches (Revelation 2–3) do you see the classic
  covenant pattern of prologue, demand, and blessing/curse at work?
- *Hesed* — steadfast covenant love — is hard to translate in one English word. Where have
  you experienced it, and where do you find it hardest to extend to others?
- If Israel's covenant terms were genuinely keepable and Israel simply chose not to keep
  them, what does that say about the nature of sin — and about the necessity of grace?
