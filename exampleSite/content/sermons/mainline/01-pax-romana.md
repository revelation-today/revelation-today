# Living Under Pax Romana: The World John Wrote To

## A deal too good to refuse

Imagine someone offers you this deal: guaranteed security, a growing economy, the rule of
law, freedom from war on your own soil. All you need to do in return is publicly
acknowledge who is responsible for it all — a small, costless gesture of loyalty. Most of
us would take that deal without much hesitation. That deal had a name in the first
century: Pax Romana, the "Roman Peace." It is the world Revelation was written into, and
understanding it is not background reading to get through before the "real" content
starts. It is the historical key that unlocks nearly every image in the book.

## What the Pax Romana actually was

From roughly 27 BC into the late second century AD, Rome enjoyed an extended stretch of
internal stability: few civil wars, a strong and unified empire, real prosperity for a
good portion of the population, and a flourishing of art and culture — set against a
backdrop of sporadic, localized persecution of Christians under emperors like Nero and
Domitian. Systematic, empire-wide persecution did not actually begin until the third
century, under Decius. Before that, the pressure on Christians was real but uneven —
which, as we'll see, made it in some ways more dangerous, not less.

Underneath these conditions sat something closer to a theology than a policy. The gods
had chosen Rome. The emperor was their agent and representative on earth. Every blessing
worth having — security, peace, justice, fertility, wealth — flowed through submission to
Rome's rule. Therefore the emperor himself was worthy of worship, with titles equal to a
god's. This was not a fringe idea confined to court flatterers. It was broadcast
constantly, everywhere: on coinage, in public statuary, at festivals and games, in shared
meals where a toast to the emperor's health was simply what one did. Cities in Asia
Minor — precisely where the seven churches of Revelation stood — competed with each other
to build temples to the emperor, hoping to win his favor or a break on taxes.

## The dilemma this created for Christians

Jesus, not the emperor, was the true king for the Christians of Asia Minor — and the very
word for the gospel message, *euangelion*, was Rome's own term for the proclamation of a
new emperor's reign. That word choice alone functioned as a quiet act of political
defiance. Christians looked to Jesus, not Caesar, for provision. Jesus himself had put the
choice in the starkest possible terms: "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve
God and money" (Matthew 6:24).

That left three real options. Renounce the faith outright and blend in. Live it out fully
and risk social exclusion, economic ruin, imprisonment, or death. Or find a middle path —
confess Caesar with the mouth while keeping Jesus in the heart secretly, outwardly
compliant, inwardly faithful. That third option — the comfortable compromise — is exactly
the temptation Revelation keeps circling back to. The whole book functions as
counter-propaganda: a rival account of who really secures your life, who actually deserves
your worship, and what the "golden age" cost the people for whom it was not golden at all.

John himself describes his own situation in exactly these terms: "I, John, your brother
and companion in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus,
was on the island of Patmos because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus"
(Revelation 1:9). He is writing from exile, imposed precisely because he would not take
the comfortable third option.

## Why this matters for a confessional reader

This is a book that assumes total allegiance and offers no safe middle ground — an
either/or structure that will feel familiar to anyone catechized on the First
Commandment. Luther's Large Catechism famously defines a "god" as "that to which we look
for all good and in which we find refuge in every time of need" — whatever holds that
place in your life functions as your god, whether or not you ever call it one. That is
precisely the diagnostic question Revelation is asking of the seven churches, and it is
the same question the Reformers asked of every human heart: not "do you believe the right
doctrine" in the abstract, but "who or what do you actually trust for your daily bread and
your ultimate security?" Revelation names Rome's answer as idolatry dressed up as
patriotism and good citizenship. It invites you to ask the same question of your own
context.

## Our own Pax Romana

The temptation the first Christians faced did not end with Rome. It only changed clothes.
Most of us in the modern West live inside our own version of a golden age: relative
peace, material comfort, the rule of law, personal freedom, rapid technological progress.
None of that is bad in itself — gratitude for ordinary stability and prosperity is right
and good. But the question Pax Romana posed was never "is this good?" It was always: who
gets the credit, and what does that credit demand of you?

A few modern faces of that same question: Financial security as savior — when our sense
of safety hangs on a bank balance or a career trajectory rather than on God's providence.
Public approval as the new temple — nobody asks you to burn incense to Caesar anymore, but
the pressure to affirm the "correct" opinion publicly, at work or online or among friends,
can feel remarkably similar to the pressure to confess the emperor as lord. You don't have
to believe it; you just have to say it loudly enough. National pride blurred with faith —
some Christians today fuse "what makes my country great" so tightly with their faith that
it becomes hard to say which one they are actually serving. And plain comfort as
compromise — the same third way is alive and well: Christian on Sunday, formed by whatever
system governs the rest of the week, without ever really examining it.

## What Revelation offers instead

The striking thing about Revelation is that its answer to all this is not first a call to
withdraw from the world. It is a call to right worship. Before any plague or judgment
unfolds, John is shown a throne room in chapters 4 and 5 where God and the Lamb are
worshiped without ceasing. That is the book's answer to every Pax Romana, ancient or
modern: not escape, but the question of who actually sits on the throne of your life. And
Revelation is honest that the comfortable middle way — outwardly compliant, inwardly
faithful — is never presented as a viable solution. It is unmasked for what it is:
half-heartedness. That is exactly the charge Jesus later brings against the church in
Laodicea — lukewarm, neither hot nor cold.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- Where is our own "Pax Romana" articulated today, and what happens when someone declines to affirm it?
- Who or what do you actually expect to solve your problems — God, or your bank account, your career, your health, your country?
- How do you react when your convictions collide with what is socially or professionally expedient?
- Where do you pretend, publicly or privately, rather than say what you actually believe? What would change if you stopped?
- If your convictions cost you something real this year — professionally, socially, in your own family — would you still hold them? What would that look like in practice?

Pax Romana is long gone. The temptation it embodied has not disappeared; it has only
changed its wardrobe. The good news Revelation proclaims is that the true King already sits
on the throne. He does not ask for lip service. He invites us into whole, undivided worship —
and he has already won, not through force, but through self-giving love.
