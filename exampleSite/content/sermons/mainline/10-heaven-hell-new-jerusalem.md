# Heaven and Hell / The New Jerusalem: Our Eternal Home

## Not a reward, not a punishment — a relationship

Popular imagination pictures heaven as a reward for good behavior and hell as punishment
for bad behavior. Revelation describes something more precise, and in some ways more
sobering: both destinies are the natural, inevitable outcome of one thing — total
communion with God, or total absence of him. Chapter 7 comforts the saints; chapter 21
describes the New Jerusalem at length; both converge on the same single point: heaven is
defined by fellowship with God, nothing added and nothing else needed.

## The shape of the city

Chapter 21 opens with the same literary technique used earlier in the book — John hears
one thing and sees another, as when the "lion of Judah" turns out to be a slaughtered
lamb (5:5–6). Here John hears of the bride, then sees her as a city — the New Jerusalem
(21:9–10). The mismatch is deliberate: a woman and a city are not literally the same
thing, which signals we are reading symbol pointing to spiritual reality, not literal
architecture — while still insisting on deep continuity with Israel's own story and hopes.

The chapter's structure moves from a first impression of the city, to its measurements, to
its material, to its internal features, and finally to the river and tree of life at its
center — deliberately answering, point for point, Isaiah's vision of Jerusalem restored
(Isaiah 60). "New" here describes not merely recent origin but a change in quality: the
first heaven and earth were temporary; this one is permanent, and in it many Old Testament
promises reach their intended fulfillment (Isaiah 65:17–25). There is no longer any
sea — significant because the sea, throughout Scripture, is the place associated with
chaos, evil, and death (Revelation 13:1; Psalm 74:13–14). Its total absence means there is
no longer anywhere for evil to hide or oppress the faithful.

## The cube, and what it means

The city is measured as a perfect cube (21:16) — and the only object in the entire Old
Testament with that exact shape is the Holy of Holies (2 Chronicles 3:8), the innermost
chamber of the temple where God's presence dwelt so intensely that only the high priest
could enter, and only once a year, at tremendous risk. That detail carries the whole
chapter's theological weight: in the New Jerusalem, every believer stands in the space
once reserved for the high priest alone. There is no longer a "closer" or "farther" from
God's presence — everyone stands equally, fully near him. That is the ultimate reversal
of every barrier, every veil, every "you may go this far and no further" that marked the
old covenant's approach to God — access to the Father, purchased fully by the Son, now
extended to all his people without qualification or hierarchy.

Twelve gates recall the twelve tribes; twelve foundations recall the twelve apostles —
Israel and the church again pictured as one people, one city, one worshiping body,
exactly as this series has traced from chapter 4 onward. The city's dimensions roughly
match the size of the Roman world known to John's first readers — a way of saying: there
is room enough here for everyone willing to come in.

## What hell actually is

If heaven is total communion with God, hell — described here as being cast outside the
city, into the lake of fire, the "second death" — is simply the natural consequence of its
total absence. Every good thing we treasure and rely on has its ultimate source in
God: hope, joy, peace, purpose, forgiveness. We experience these now, in this life,
because this is the season in which God is drawing us toward himself. That season will
end. What remains without him is not a set of externally imposed torments so much as the
unraveling that necessarily follows when the source of every good gift is finally and
permanently absent — a downward spiral of bitterness, hopelessness, and fear with nothing
left to arrest it.

God does not force fellowship on anyone. If someone finally, fully refuses him, he lets
them go — but the consequence of that refusal is a life entirely without him, and
therefore without any of his gifts, which is precisely what constitutes hell. Fear and
manipulation, this series has already noted, are the beast's tools, not the church's
(Revelation 13). The church's calling is not to threaten people into faith but to bear
faithful testimony and invite people into the same fellowship the New Jerusalem describes
in full.

## A vision worth living toward now

This chapter is not merely a description of the far future; it is the goal the whole
present age is moving toward, and the church already lives as an outpost of it. The
promises made to each of the seven struggling churches in chapters 2–3 — the tree of life,
a place as a pillar in God's temple, a name written in the book of life, a share in
Christ's own reign — are all fulfilled here, in this final vision. What was promised to a
handful of small, pressured, first-century congregations turns out, in the end, to be
promised to the whole company of the redeemed, drawn from every nation, secure forever in
unbroken fellowship with the God who made them.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- How does understanding hell as the natural consequence of God's absence, rather than an
  externally imposed torture, shape how you talk about it with someone who is grieving or
  doubting?
- The cube shape of the New Jerusalem means every believer now has access once reserved
  for the high priest alone. What does that reversal mean for how you approach God in
  prayer today?
- Every promise made to the struggling seven churches is fulfilled in this final vision.
  Which of those promises (Revelation 2–3) speaks most directly to a struggle you are
  facing right now?
- "There is room enough for everyone willing to come in." How does that shape your sense
  of urgency, or hope, for people in your life who have not yet turned to Christ?
