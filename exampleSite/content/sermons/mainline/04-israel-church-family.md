# Israel, the Church, and the Family of God

## A controversial-sounding question that isn't actually controversial

Is the church greater than Israel? Has the church replaced Israel? Will Israel make some
separate national comeback at the end of time, apart from the church? Each question
assumes a controversy that, examined closely, does not actually exist in the text. The
church is not a rival to Israel and did not replace it. The church is Israel, expanded —
the same elect people of God now including believing Gentiles alongside believing Jews,
united in the Messiah who was always the true Israelite Israel needed.

## The mystery of the Gentiles

Paul names this directly in Ephesians: "This mystery is that through the gospel the
Gentiles are heirs together with Israel, members together of one body, and sharers
together in the promise in Christ Jesus" (Ephesians 3:6). The "mystery" here is not some
hidden secret trivia — it is an almost unbelievable act of God: Gentiles now share fully
in Israel's heritage, body, and promises. Jesus is the substance of the New Covenant, and
in him Jews and Gentiles together make up end-time Israel — a people defined not by
ethnicity but by belonging to Jesus. As Paul puts it elsewhere, the covenant given through
Moses functioned as an intermediary step between the promise to Abraham and its fulfillment
in the church (Galatians 3:23–28).

Nearly every title the Old Testament gives to Israel is now applied to the church, not as
a replacement but as the same elect people reaching its intended, expanded form: beloved of
the Lord, God's son, circumcised in heart, God's temple, the bride of God, a kingdom of
priests, God's vineyard, God's treasured possession, the light to the nations promised in
Isaiah. Peter applies "kingdom of priests" to the church directly (1 Peter 2:9), and
Revelation's own opening does the same (Revelation 1:5–6). The prophecy of Joel about the
Spirit poured out on Israel is fulfilled at Pentecost — and that outpouring immediately
extends, without controversy, to Gentile households like Cornelius's (Acts 10–11),
exactly as Isaiah had promised: Israel would become a light to the nations, drawing them
in rather than excluding them.

## Jews in Revelation

The tension between the early church and the Jewish community was real and painful. Jews
were tolerated by Rome because of their ancient standing; Christians, viewed initially as
simply another Jewish sect, threatened that toleration by refusing Rome's demands and
converting others. Some Jewish communities responded by distancing themselves from
Christians before Roman authorities — precisely the pressure behind Revelation's sharp
language about "the synagogue of Satan" in the letters to Smyrna and Philadelphia (2:9;
3:9). That language addresses local Jewish opponents actively persecuting the church in
those specific cities — it is a family dispute over who the true heirs of Abraham's
promise are, not a blanket verdict on Jewish ethnicity as such. When Philadelphia's
opponents are said to eventually "bow down" before the church, the point is not
institutional submission but that they come to acknowledge Jesus.

Revelation never turns its argument against ethnic Israel as such — its entire concern,
throughout, is a person's relationship to Jesus. That is precisely because the church is
part of Israel and cannot cut off its own roots, even where it is under attack from some
who reject Jesus. The unity is made structurally explicit: the twenty-four elders around
the throne in chapter 4 represent the twelve tribes and the twelve apostles together, and
both sets of twelve names are engraved side by side on the gates and foundations of the
New Jerusalem (21:12,14). Heaven itself is never pictured as some placeless, generic
realm — it is pictured as Jerusalem, the New Jerusalem, Israel's own capital transfigured
and filled to overflowing with people from every nation.

## Holding both truths together

The challenge for the first-century church — and, in different form, for us — is to hold
two things together that pull in opposite directions: being part of Israel, and at times
being opposed by Israel. That tension has ongoing relevance for how confessional
Christians think about our relationship with the Jewish people today, and it is worth
naming plainly: this reading gives no warrant whatsoever for supersessionist contempt or
for treating Jewish people as rejected. Quite the opposite — the whole argument depends on
the church's identity being bound up with, not detached from, Israel's story and promises.

## Why this matters for how we read the rest of the book

Once this is established, a great deal of Revelation's imagery stops looking like a
different subject altogether and starts looking like exactly what your tradition has
always confessed about the church: one people of God, gathered from every nation, tribe,
and tongue, standing in continuity with Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and the prophets — not a
parenthesis inserted into God's plan for ethnic Israel, and not a separate group awaiting
a future national program of its own. The 144,000 sealed from the twelve tribes in chapter
7 and the innumerable multitude from every nation in the very same scene are not two
different groups — they are the same church, described first in covenantal, then in
universal terms. This is one of the reasons this series reads Revelation's Israel language
as fulfilled in the church rather than reserved for a future, separate national fulfillment
apart from Christ.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- Where have you heard "replacement theology" used as an accusation? How does the language
  of expansion and fulfillment, rather than replacement, change that conversation?
- The twenty-four elders represent the twelve tribes and twelve apostles together. What
  does that image suggest about how the church should think of its Jewish roots?
- How should the church's identity as "one Israel, expanded" shape its posture toward the
  Jewish people today?
- Where in your own congregation's practice do you see (or fail to see) this unity of Jew
  and Gentile as one body actually lived out?
