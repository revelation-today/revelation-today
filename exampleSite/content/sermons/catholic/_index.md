# Welcome, Friends in the Catholic Tradition

Revelation was written for a Church that gathered to worship, that told its story through
symbol and sacrament, and that expected faithfulness to cost something. That is not a foreign
world to Catholic readers — it may be the most native soil this book has anywhere. This series
adapts the wider "Revelation Today" material for a Catholic and liturgical context, not to
correct it and not to defend it, but because so much of it already speaks your language.

## Where this book feels like home

Three things stand out immediately. First, Revelation is saturated with liturgy. The throne
room of chapters 4 and 5 — the Sanctus ("Holy, holy, holy"), the assembly of the redeemed, the
Lamb who was slain now standing at the center of worship, incense rising with the prayers of
the saints — reads like a description of the Mass before the Mass existed, and Catholic
worship reads like a continuation of that same throne-room scene. Second, this book rewards
the kind of patient, communal, tradition-shaped reading Catholic scholarship has practiced for
two thousand years. The Church Fathers debated Revelation's images long before modern
"prophecy charts" existed, and their instinct — caution about pinning symbols to a single
future event, attention to the whole of Scripture, trust that the Church herself carries
insight across generations — is close kin to the symbolic, first-century-anchored reading this
series argues for throughout. Third, Revelation's vision of a Church that spans every nation,
tongue, and tribe, gathered as one Bride around one Lamb, maps naturally onto a Catholic
imagination already trained to think of the Church as one body across time and geography.

## Where we'll think together

A few places call for a bit more care, and we name them honestly rather than skip past them.
Revelation itself doesn't address purgatory one way or the other — it describes final,
definite destinies, so we'll flag that as a question this book simply isn't answering, not one
it's answering differently than the Church teaches. Revelation 12's woman "clothed with the
sun" has long invited both a reading as Israel/the people of God — the reading this series
argues from the text — and an additional, beloved Marian reading well established in Catholic
devotion; we'll present both without forcing a choice between them. And because many Catholics
have Protestant neighbors, coworkers, or family members who talk about "the rapture" and
being "left behind," one session simply explains where that idea came from and how Revelation
actually reads — less an urgent correction than useful context for a conversation you may
already have had at a family dinner.

## What's inside

Fourteen topics, each with a full adult session and a matching kids' session, drawn from the
same source material used across the whole "Revelation Today" project. Use them in order or
pull individual sessions as needed for a class, a small group, or a homily series.
