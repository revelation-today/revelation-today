# The New Jerusalem: Our Eternal Home

## Opening: A woman who is also a city

Revelation 21 opens with a puzzle John has trained us to expect by now. Earlier in the book, he hears one thing and sees another — he hears about a conquering Lion (Revelation 5:5) and turns to see a slain Lamb (Revelation 5:6); he hears the number of the sealed, 144,000, an army (Revelation 7:4), and turns to see an uncountable worshiping multitude from every nation (Revelation 7:9). The pattern trains us to read carefully: what John sees interprets what he hears. Here, John hears about "the bride, the wife of the Lamb" (Revelation 21:9) — and turns to see the New Jerusalem, a city, coming down out of heaven (Revelation 21:10). A woman and a city are not literally the same thing, and that mismatch is the point: John is signaling symbolic description rooted in real biblical imagery, not a literal architectural blueprint. Once we know that, the rest of the chapter opens up rather than confuses us.

## New, permanently

Revelation 21:1-8 introduces the New Jerusalem in miniature; nearly everything there gets developed at length later (21:9-22:5). Start with the word "new." It doesn't mean merely recent — it means a change in quality. The old heaven and earth were never meant to be permanent; the new heaven and new earth are. This is the fulfillment many Old Testament promises were reaching for, especially Isaiah 65:17-25's vision of new heavens and a new earth where weeping is no more.

"No more sea" (Revelation 21:1) sounds strange to modern ears until we remember what the sea meant throughout Scripture: the place evil comes from — Leviathan, Rahab, kings pictured as sea monsters — and, alongside Hades, a place associated with the dead. "No more sea" isn't a comment on beach vacations; it's a declaration that there is no longer any place left for evil to hide or oppress God's people.

"The holy city... prepared as a bride adorned for her husband" (Revelation 21:2) draws directly on Isaiah's picture of Jerusalem dressed beautifully, entering an intimate covenant with the God who rejoices over her (Isaiah 61-62). Compare 21:2 with the fuller description in 21:10-11, and something beautiful emerges: the bride's "beautiful dress" turns out to be the glory of God himself, radiating from the city. And God's glory, elsewhere in Scripture, is described above all as his mercy and compassion (Exodus 33) — which means the Church, the Bride, is called to be clothed in that same mercy, reflecting it to the world even now.

"God's dwelling place is now among the people" (Revelation 21:3) fulfills the ancient promise of Leviticus 26:11-12, taken up by Ezekiel's vision of God returning to the temple, and extended even to the nations in Zechariah 2:10-11. "He will wipe away every tear" (Revelation 21:4) fulfills Isaiah's repeated promises that death, mourning, and sighing will flee away (Isaiah 25:8; 35:10; 51:11). And "I am making all things new" (Revelation 21:5) fulfills Isaiah 43:18-19 — a renewal already begun in Jesus, in whom "the old things have passed away; new things have come" (2 Corinthians 5:17). "The water of life, without cost" (Revelation 21:6) fulfills Isaiah 49:10, and finds its fulfillment already in Jesus' promise to the woman at the well (John 4) and to every believer (John 7:38).

## Promises kept

Revelation 21:7 and its surrounding verses gather up the promises Jesus made to the seven churches back in chapters 2-3 and show them fulfilled. Ephesus was promised the tree of life (Revelation 2:7) — it reappears in 22:2, available to all. Philadelphia was promised a place as a pillar in God's temple, with God's name written on it (Revelation 3:12) — fulfilled in being part of the New Jerusalem itself, God's name inscribed on his people. Smyrna was promised exclusion from the second death (Revelation 2:11) — matched by Revelation 21:8. Thyatira and Laodicea were promised a share in Christ's own rule (Revelation 2:26-27; 3:21) — fulfilled as the redeemed reign with him.

Two of these connections are worth holding a little more loosely, in fairness to the text. Sardis was promised white robes (Revelation 3:5); Revelation 21:2 describes the bride's dress, but that's not quite the same image, so the better parallel is Revelation 19:8, where the bride is given "fine linen, bright and pure," explicitly identified as "the righteous deeds of the saints." And Pergamon was promised a "white amulet" or "stone" with a new name (Revelation 2:17); the New Jerusalem's jewels and foundation stones (Revelation 21:11, 18-21) echo this only loosely, by way of theme — stones, brilliance — rather than as a precise one-to-one fulfillment. Scripture doesn't need every connection forced into a perfect match to be trustworthy; some threads are meant simply to resonate.

The list of the excluded in Revelation 21:8 — the cowardly, murderers, the sexually immoral, sorcerers, idolaters, liars — is the direct counterpart to the overcomers' list, and many of these particular sins describe not only unbelievers but compromised believers inside the church, exactly as we saw at Pergamon and Thyatira. Nearly all of them, traced back far enough, are forms of idolatry: loving something in place of God.

## The cube, and the access it opens

The New Jerusalem's shape is a perfect cube (Revelation 21:16) — and as we saw in our last session, the only object in the Old Testament with that exact shape is the Holy of Holies (2 Chronicles 3:8). The Holy of Holies marked a real limitation: God dwelt there, and the farther a worshiper stood from it, the farther they were from him. Access belonged to one man, the high priest, one day a year. In the New Jerusalem, that limitation is gone entirely: everyone stands equally, unrestrictedly close to God. The whole city is the Holy of Holies now.

It's worth noting, too, that the city's vast size — on a scale comparable to the whole Hellenistic world, the Roman Empire itself — isn't meant as a literal capacity calculation, as if John were doing math to prove there's exactly enough room for a certain headcount. Read alongside the numbers twelve and its multiples running throughout this passage (twelve gates, twelve foundations, twelve thousand stadia), the city's scale functions symbolically, like those numbers do: a sign of totality and completeness, God's house sized to the fullness of his family, not an architectural footprint to be measured.

## This access, already given: the Eucharist

Here is where this session's imagery becomes something more than future hope — it becomes present reality, and it's worth pausing on this longer, because it's one of the most beautiful connections Revelation offers a Catholic reader. The entire point of the cube, of the Holy of Holies made universal, is that unrestricted nearness to God — once rationed to one priest, one day a year — has become the ordinary inheritance of the redeemed. That is precisely the claim the Church makes about the Eucharist, every single day.

At every Mass, we do not merely commemorate a distant future reality or wait passively for it. We receive, really and truly, the presence of God himself, drawing nearer to us than the high priest ever dared draw to the Ark. Ancient Israel could scarcely have imagined what happens at an ordinary parish altar on an ordinary weekday: God, present, given, received. The Catechism calls the Eucharist a "pledge of future glory" — and that phrase captures exactly what's happening here. It isn't a metaphor borrowed loosely from Revelation 21-22; it's the same reality, arriving early. The fullness of New Jerusalem intimacy — the whole city as Holy of Holies, God dwelling among his people without wall or veil or restriction — has its down payment, its genuine "already," every time we walk forward at Communion. We are not people straining to picture something far off. We are people who have already tasted, in the Eucharist, the very nearness Revelation describes as our final home. What awaits us in fullness, we already hold, however partially, in our hands and on our tongues.

## Materials, streets, and glory restored

Gold reflects God's own glory; jasper and the other jewels recall the high priest's breastpiece (Exodus 28), where each stone stood for a tribe of Israel. Here, twelve foundation stones stand for the twelve apostles instead — fitting, since believers themselves are called "living stones" being built into God's house (1 Peter 2:5). Even the city's street is significant: it's the same word used for the street where the two witnesses' bodies once lay shamefully exposed (Revelation 11:8). What was once a place of public shame becomes, in the New Jerusalem, a place of gold and glory — shame permanently overturned.

There is no temple in the city (Revelation 21:22), "for the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb are its temple." The whole city has become what the Holy of Holies used to be: nothing set apart any longer, because nothing needs to be. And the nations stream in, bringing their glory and honor (Revelation 21:24-26), echoing Isaiah 60 — not glory they possessed on their own, but glory now given back to God, the nations drawn in through Christ's fulfillment of the calling first given to Abraham: to bless all the families of the earth. Not every nation enters, though — Revelation 21:27 still closes the gate to whatever is unclean. Only those who believe come in.

## The river and the tree

The closing image (Revelation 22:1-5) brings us full circle: a river of living water flows from the throne of God and the Lamb — water throughout Scripture closely tied to the Holy Spirit, from the new heart promised in Ezekiel, to being "born of water and Spirit," to the living water Jesus offers. The tree of life returns, accessible now to all, healing the nations. We are back in Eden, with the fall fully undone. And the name once worn only by the high priest on his forehead (Exodus 28:36-38) now marks every believer — God's own character imprinted not on a priestly class alone, but on his whole redeemed people.

## Living this

We are not merely waiting for the New Jerusalem. We are already, in the Eucharist, tasting the very thing it describes: unrestricted nearness to God. That should change how we approach the altar — not out of routine, but with the wonder of someone who has just been let into the Holy of Holies.

## Closing

The New Jerusalem is not a fantasy about someday. It's the fullest unfolding of something already begun — begun in Christ's death and resurrection, begun in our baptism, and renewed at every Mass where we receive the God who has made his home among us. What Revelation shows us as our eternal future, the Eucharist lets us already hold.

## For preparation

**Discussion questions:**
1. How does it change your experience of Mass to think of the Eucharist as a real, present "down payment" on the New Jerusalem's promise of unrestricted access to God?
2. What do you make of the deliberate mismatch between "hearing" a bride and "seeing" a city? Why might John want us to notice symbolic language at work here?
3. Which of the overcomer promises (tree of life, a name written, exclusion from the second death, reigning with Christ) means the most to you personally, and why?
4. How does seeing the city's size as symbolic (totality) rather than a literal headcount change how you read Revelation's use of numbers elsewhere?
