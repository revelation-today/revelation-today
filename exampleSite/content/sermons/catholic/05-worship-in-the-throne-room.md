# Worship in the Throne Room

## Opening: You've Already Been Here

Here is a strange claim, and this session is going to ask you to take it seriously: you have
already stood in the throne room of Revelation 4 and 5. Maybe last Sunday. Maybe this morning.
Every time you have gone to Mass, sung "Holy, Holy, Holy," and watched a priest lift up bread and
wine while the assembly around you bowed its heart toward something not fully visible — you were
not merely reenacting John's vision. You were, in a sense the Church has always taken with full
seriousness, joining it.

That is not a poetic flourish tacked onto this session. It is close to the plain claim these two
chapters make, and it is why Revelation 4-5 may be the single strongest bridge between this
ancient, strange book and ordinary Catholic life. Don't rush past it. Let's slow down and look at
what John actually saw.

## The Answer Before the Question

It helps to remember where we've come from. Chapters 2-3 are seven hard, specific letters to
seven real churches, and their problems sort into a few clear piles: wrong teaching leading
congregations into wrong behavior (Pergamum, Thyatira); wrong self-assessment leading to
complacency or false confidence (Sardis, Laodicea); distraction and misplaced priorities
draining away first love (Ephesus); and sheer hard circumstances tempting people to quit
(Smyrna, Philadelphia).

Revelation's answer to every one of these, without exception, is worship. Know God truly as he
is, and false teaching loses its grip. See God rightly enthroned, and you finally see yourself
rightly too — neither the false confidence of Laodicea nor the despair of a church that thinks
it's finished. Put God first in unclouded worship, and misplaced priorities have nowhere left to
hide. Find something genuinely unshakeable to hold onto, and the hardest circumstances stop being
able to shake you loose.

So chapter 4 opens with a door standing open in heaven — and that detail is not incidental. The
promise of an "open door" was made to Philadelphia and implied to Laodicea; here, the door is
already open, an invitation already extended. Look for these deliberate echoes as we go: the
white clothing promised to Sardis and Laodicea reappears on the twenty-four elders. The thrones
and crowns promised to Laodicea, Smyrna, and Philadelphia reappear as thrones and crowns already
worn around God's throne. The "seven spirits of God" named to Sardis blaze again before the
throne and shine in the Lamb's eyes. The authority promised to the overcomer in Thyatira is the
very authority the Lamb receives when he takes the scroll. Every promise made to a struggling
church in chapters 2-3 is shown, here, already true and already occupied. This is not merely a
future hope dangled in front of tired congregations. It is the church's own truest reality,
pulled back like a curtain — a picture to grow into, not just wait for.

## What John Actually Sees

John's first impression is glory, and glory so overwhelming that it can't be looked at directly
— only its reflected brilliance, like jasper and carnelian, like an emerald rainbow encircling the
throne. It's worth remembering Moses on the mountain, asking to see God's glory face to face
(Exodus 33:18-20). God's answer was not a blinding vision but this: "I will make all my goodness
pass before you... I will be gracious to whom I will be gracious, and will show mercy on whom I
will show mercy." God's glory and God's mercy are, at the deepest level, the same reality wearing
two names. Keep that close at hand for everything that follows in Revelation — every seal, every
trumpet, every bowl of judgment poured out later in this book is poured out by the One whose
glory is mercy.

The rainbow around the throne confirms it. It recalls God's covenant with Noah — a promise never
again to destroy the earth by flood, asked for by no one, earned by no one, given freely (Genesis
9:8-11). Judgment is coming in this book, and it is real. But it happens under a rainbow. It is
bounded, limited, wrapped in a prior promise of mercy that never gets revoked.

Then the "sea of glass," calm as crystal, spread before the throne. Scripture elsewhere pictures
the sea as the home of chaos and menace — the restless nations, the untamed deep (Psalm 74; Isaiah
57:20). Here that same sea lies still, solid, harmless, congealed into something you could stand
on. It is a picture of evil finally rendered powerless in God's presence. The saints in this book
will still walk through real trial — but they walk through it with God directly present, the
chaos beneath their feet subdued rather than raging.

Around the throne stand four living creatures — lion, ox, human face, eagle, echoing Ezekiel's
vision — representing the whole created order paying homage: ruler of the wild animals, ruler of
the tame, ruler of creation, ruler of the skies. All creation is in the room. And twenty-four
elders sit enthroned in white, wearing gold crowns. The number recalls the twenty-four priestly
divisions and twenty-four worship-teams King David established for the Temple (1 Chronicles
24-26) — but it also lands, unmistakably, on twelve tribes plus twelve apostles: Israel and the
Church, together, as one worshiping body. Whatever historical tension exists between them
elsewhere, here they kneel side by side. At its very foundation, this vision defines "church" not
as an institution or a movement but as an assembly gathered to worship.

This entire scene, in fact, closely parallels Daniel 7: a throne, the Ancient of Days seated on
it, fire before the throne, thousands of attendants, books opened, a messianic figure approaching
to receive authority and dominion over every people, nation, and tongue, the seer's distress, an
attendant explaining the vision, and the saints ultimately given a share in the reign. John is
drawing directly on Daniel's language — and on Isaiah's throne-room call (Isaiah 6) and the
divine council scene before Ahab's death (1 Kings 22) — to say plainly: this is that same throne,
now fully and finally revealed.

## The Lamb Joins the Worship

Chapter 5 turns the volume up further. A Lamb, looking as though slain, takes his place at the
very center — and the worship that has, until now, gone entirely to the One on the throne now
turns, without hesitation or qualification, to include him. "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain, to
receive power and wealth and wisdom and might and honor and glory and blessing!" Scripture is
consistently jealous about worship — only God may rightly receive it. That heaven worships the
Lamb exactly as it worships the One on the throne is Revelation's own quiet, overwhelming
argument that the Lamb is God.

## The Mass: Not an Imitation, but a Continuation

Here is where this session asks you to hear something you may have said a thousand times as if
for the first time. "Holy, Holy, Holy Lord God of hosts" — the Sanctus, sung or spoken at every
Mass — is drawn directly from the cry of the four living creatures before God's throne (Revelation
4:8), itself echoing Isaiah's seraphim. "Lamb of God, you take away the sins of the world, have
mercy on us" — the Agnus Dei — names Christ in exactly the terms Revelation 5 does: the Lamb who
was slain, worthy of worship, present at the center of the gathered assembly.

This is not decoration borrowed from a vivid book. The Catechism describes the Eucharistic
liturgy as a real participation in the heavenly liturgy already being celebrated in the
Jerusalem above — the very liturgy John was shown. An assembly gathered around an altar; incense
rising; a multitude giving glory with one voice; the slain Lamb present at the center, not merely
remembered but really there. When you stand at Mass and sing the Sanctus, you are not staging a
tribute to Revelation 4. You are doing, in real time, what the four living creatures never stop
doing. "Worship in the throne room" is not only heaven's future — it is a present reality you were
invited into the last time you walked through the doors of your parish.

## Closing

The seven churches were struggling with false teaching, false self-image, misplaced priorities,
and battered courage — and the answer to all of it was the same: look up, and worship rightly.
That door is still open. The same throne, the same Lamb, the same "Holy, Holy, Holy" is available
to you this coming Sunday, and every Sunday after that. Go and take your place in that assembly
with new eyes — you are not watching from a distance. You are already in the room.

## For Preparation

**Discussion questions:**
1. Which of the seven churches' struggles — wrong teaching, wrong self-image, wrong priorities,
   or battered courage — do you recognize most in your own life right now? How might deeper
   worship actually address it, rather than just distract from it?
2. Read Exodus 33:18-20 alongside Revelation 4. How does it change the judgments later in
   Revelation to know that God's glory and God's mercy are, at bottom, the same thing?
3. This week, attend Mass (or recall your last Mass) paying deliberate attention to the Sanctus
   and the Agnus Dei. What changes when you consciously hear them as Revelation 4-5's own words,
   not just familiar liturgical text?
4. The twenty-four elders bring together priestly Israel and apostolic Church as one worshiping
   body. Where do you see that unity lived out — or still needing to be lived into — in the
   Church today?

**Song/hymn suggestions:** "Holy, Holy, Holy! Lord God Almighty"; "Holy God, We Praise Thy Name";
"Ye Watchers and Ye Holy Ones" (with its refrain "Alleluia!" echoing the heavenly chorus);
consider simply attending Mass this week with this session fresh in mind, listening for the
Sanctus and the Agnus Dei as if hearing Revelation 4-5 read aloud — because, in a real sense, you
are.
