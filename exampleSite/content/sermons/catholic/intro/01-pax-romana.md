# Pax Romana

## Opening: A Deal Too Good to Refuse

Imagine someone offered you this deal: guaranteed safety, a growing economy, freedom from war
on your own soil, a stable legal system that actually works — and the only thing asked in
return is that you say, publicly, who deserves the credit for all of it. No fine print. Just one
word of acknowledgment.

Most of us would take that deal without blinking. Two thousand years ago, that deal had a name:
the Pax Romana. It is the world the book of Revelation was written into — not background color,
but the very pressure the book exists to answer.

## What the "Roman Peace" Actually Was

From roughly 27 BC into the late second century AD, Rome enjoyed an extended stretch of relative
internal stability: few civil wars, a strong and unified empire, real prosperity for a good
portion of the population, and a flourishing of art and culture. Beneath the surface, though,
this "golden age" rested on violence and forced pacification — golden mainly for those who
complied.

Underneath the peace sat something closer to a theology than a policy. The gods, Rome taught,
had chosen Rome. The emperor was their agent on earth. Every blessing worth having — security,
peace, justice, wealth, fertility — flowed through submission to Rome's rule. Therefore the
emperor himself deserved worship and titles equal to a god's.

This wasn't a fringe idea confined to court flatterers. It was broadcast everywhere: in poetry
like Virgil's *Aeneid*, in processions and public games, in statues and coinage, in shared meals
where a toast to the emperor's health was simply what one did. It was inescapable — present at
work, at market, at the family table. Cities across Asia Minor, exactly where the seven churches
of Revelation stood, competed to build temples to the emperor, hoping to win his favor or a tax
break in return.

Persecution of Christians under this system was real but, for a long while, sporadic and
localized — under Nero, Domitian, Trajan. It would be more than a century into this "peace"
before persecution became truly systematic and empire-wide, under the emperor Decius in 250 AD.
For most of Revelation's first readers, the daily pressure was not a single dramatic edict
demanding their lives. It was something quieter and, in its own way, harder to resist: constant
social and economic pressure to simply go along.

## The Dilemma This Created for Christians

Christians in Asia Minor believed Jesus, not the emperor, was their true king. Tellingly, the
very word for their message — *euangelion*, "gospel" — was Rome's own term for the proclamation
of a new emperor's reign. The Gospels themselves were written, in a real sense, as a rival
announcement: a new King has taken the throne, and it isn't the one on the coins. Christians
trusted Jesus, not Caesar, for their daily provision (see
{{% bible val="Matthew 6:25-34" link="mat:6,25-34" lang="en" %}}), and certain features of civic
life — corporate meals and rites bound up with honoring the emperor and the gods — were simply
incompatible with worshiping one God alone. For refusing to blend in, Christians were branded
intolerant.

That left three real options. Deny the faith outright and disappear into the crowd — an option
Jesus had already ruled out: "{{% bible val="No one can serve two masters" link="mat:6,24" lang="en" %}}" (Matthew 6:24). Live the faith fully and risk social exclusion, economic ruin,
imprisonment, or death. Or find a middle path: confess Caesar with the lips while keeping Jesus
in the heart — outwardly compliant, inwardly faithful, the "reasonable" choice that costs nothing
visible.

Revelation treats that third option nowhere as acceptable. It is exactly what Jesus condemns in
his letter to Laodicea later in the book — lukewarm compromise, neither cold nor hot, about to be
spit out. John himself models the second option rather than the third: he tells us plainly he
was exiled to Patmos "because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus" (Revelation 1:9). He
paid the price rather than take the easier road. The whole of Revelation is counter-propaganda
aimed at the Pax Romana's version of the good life — a rival account of who really secures your
existence, and who actually deserves your worship.

## A Book We Already Half-Know How to Answer

Catholic worship offers more than commentary on this history — it offers a living, weekly answer
to it. Every Sunday, the Mass gathers the Church around the Lamb who was slain, present on the
altar, worthy of worship in a way no Caesar ever was. A life ordered around the Mass and the
sacraments is, itself, a standing act of resistance to whatever modern "Caesar" competes for our
ultimate loyalty. Showing up to kneel before the Lamb each week is already the answer Revelation
points toward.

## Where Is Our Own "Pax Romana"?

Nobody today is asked to burn incense before a statue of Caesar. But the same underlying
question — who or what actually secures your life? — has not gone away. It has just changed
clothes.

Prosperity and security can become a modern Caesar: trusting a bank balance, a career, or
consumption for the kind of safety only God can actually give. Social approval can become a
modern temple: the quiet pressure to publicly affirm the "right" opinion at work, online, or
among friends — no one demanding incense, but everyone reading the room the same way Ephesus or
Smyrna once did. Nationalism can blur, almost imperceptibly, with faith, until loyalty to a
nation and loyalty to Christ start to feel like the same thing.

Compromise rarely arrives as one dramatic demand. It creeps in gradually — a small accommodation
here, a comfortable silence there — until, like a frog in slowly warming water, we no longer
notice who is really "Caesar" in our life.

Revelation's answer to all of this is not withdrawal from the world. It is clear worship. The
throne-room scene of chapters 4 and 5 — all creation, every voice, bowing before God and the
Lamb — is the model: right worship reorders everything else in its proper place.

## Questions for Reflection

Where do you find yourself trusting something other than God for your sense of security — a
paycheck, a title, the approval of people whose opinion matters too much to you? Where have you
taken the "third way" recently — said or implied something in public that didn't match what you
actually believe, just to keep the peace? If your convictions cost you something real this
year — socially, professionally, within your own family — would you still hold them?

## Closing

The Pax Romana promised peace, but it was peace built on submission to a false king. The peace
Christ offers costs everything and gives everything in return — a peace the world can neither
grant nor take away. Go from here this week and, in ways large and small, let your life say
plainly whose kingdom you belong to. And may the God who has already secured the victory, not
through Rome's kind of power but through a Lamb who was slain, go with you.

## For Preparation

**Discussion questions:**
1. What made the "third way" — outward conformity while remaining inwardly faithful — so
   tempting for a first-century Christian? What made it, in Revelation's own eyes, unacceptable?
2. Why do you think Revelation names this compromise so sharply in the letter to Laodicea?
3. In what way does the Mass function as a weekly declaration of allegiance to Christ over every
   rival claim on our loyalty?
4. Where do you notice "frog in warm water" compromise happening gradually in your own life or
   the wider culture?

**Song/hymn suggestions:** "Holy God, We Praise Thy Name"; "Crown Him with Many Crowns"; consider
pairing this session with a period of Eucharistic Adoration, letting the throne-room imagery of
Revelation 4-5 inform quiet time before the Blessed Sacrament.
