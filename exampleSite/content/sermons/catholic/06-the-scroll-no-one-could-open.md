# The Scroll No One Could Open

## Opening: The Two Movements You Already Know

Every Mass you have ever attended has the same basic shape. First, Scripture is proclaimed and
opened up — readings, psalm, Gospel, homily — until the Word that seemed distant becomes
something addressed directly to you. Then the assembly moves to the altar, where the sacrifice
of the Lamb is made present and you receive him in communion. Liturgy of the Word, then Liturgy
of the Eucharist. You already know this shape in your bones.

What you may not have noticed is that Revelation 5 tells this same story first. A sealed scroll
that must be opened, and then a slain Lamb who is worshiped at the center of the assembly. The
Mass didn't borrow this structure from nowhere. It is living inside a pattern Revelation shows us
in heaven itself.

## A Document That Means Business

Picture the scene: John, still in the throne room, sees a scroll in the right hand of the One
seated there, sealed with seven seals. In the ancient world, this is exactly how you'd seal
something with real legal weight — a will, a new law, an official decree. An official document
would be witnessed and sealed, often by seven witnesses, with a summary of its contents written
on the outside so no one could tamper with it or open it by accident. The document had no legal
force until it was opened. Opening it wasn't just reading it — it was activating it, putting its
contents into effect.

So when a mighty angel calls out, "Who is worthy to open the scroll and break its seals?" the real
question is not "who is curious enough to peek inside?" It's "who has the right and the power to
set this in motion?" We're not told with certainty what the scroll contains — the prophetic
writings, the whole of Scripture's plan, or simply God's design for how history will unfold are
all reasonable readings, and the text doesn't force us to pick one. What is certain is the stakes:
whoever opens this scroll will rule the world.

And no one can. Not in heaven, not on earth, not under the earth. John does not take this news
calmly. He weeps — hard, real tears — because it looks, for one terrible moment, as if God's plan
for history might simply stall out, unopened and unenacted forever.

## You Hear a Lion, You See a Lamb

Then comes one of the most carefully engineered reversals in all of Scripture. An elder tells
John, "Do not weep; look, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has conquered, so
that he can open the scroll." Every expectation that sentence raises is about to be turned
inside out. John turns to look for a lion — a warrior-king, a conqueror by force, exactly what
every apocalyptic imagination of the ancient world would expect at this climactic moment.

What he sees instead is a Lamb, standing as though it had been slain.

Don't let the familiarity of this image dull it. These are not two different figures, one
disappointing after the other was promised. They are one figure, described two ways: what John
hears is the Lion; what John sees is the Lamb; and the point is that they are the same. The Lamb's
apparent weakness — slain, wounded, vulnerable — is not the opposite of lionlike conquest. It is
what lionlike conquest actually looked like, once and for all, in Jesus Christ. He did not
overcome despite the cross. He overcame by means of it. Every ordinary story of a hero who wins
by superior force gets quietly dismantled here and replaced with something far stranger and far
more powerful: victory through self-giving love, worn on the body as scars rather than trophies.

And this Lamb is worthy — the whole heavenly assembly erupts around him exactly as it does around
the One on the throne: "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom
and might and honor and glory and blessing!" Only God may rightly be worshiped, and heaven does
not hesitate to worship the Lamb. That is Revelation's own quiet but unmistakable claim: this
slain, standing Lamb is God.

## The Two Movements of the Mass, Told in Advance

Here is where these two pieces — the scroll and the Lamb — line up so precisely with the Mass that
it's worth sitting with slowly.

The scroll must be *opened* before its contents take legal effect. That's the first movement:
proclamation that activates. At every Mass, the Liturgy of the Word does exactly this — Scripture
is read aloud, then opened up in the homily, until what could have remained sealed, distant
words on a page become something set loose to act on the assembly gathered to hear it. A homily
that genuinely opens the Word is not decoration around the readings; it is, in its own modest way,
a breaking of seals — God's plan being set into motion in the room, this week, for these people.

And the Lamb, present and worshiped at the center of the assembly, is what makes that opening
possible in the first place. It is the Lamb's worthiness — won through his death — that qualifies
him to break the seals at all. In the same way, it is the Eucharist, the Lamb truly present among
his people, that gives the whole liturgy its center of gravity. The Liturgy of the Word points
toward it; the Liturgy of the Eucharist delivers it. "Lamb of God, you take away the sins of the
world, have mercy on us" is not a separate devotional add-on tacked onto the Mass. It is the
whole congregation, right now, joining the very chorus around the throne that worships this same
slain-yet-standing Lamb.

Two movements, one drama, told twice: once in a heavenly vision to John near the end of the first
century, and once again, every single week, at your own parish altar.

## What Follows the Opening

When the Lamb breaks the seals in chapter 6, what pours out is not random chaos but exposure —
Satan's own ambitions for the world laid bare in the four horsemen: conquest, war, famine, death,
all the counterfeit "salvation" the enemy of souls has always offered. The saints under the altar,
martyred for their witness, cry out "how long?" — a real, unanswered question of the suffering
faithful in every age — and are given white robes, told to wait a little longer. Their prayer is
heard, even before it is fully answered. Then the sixth seal brings the great Day of the Lord, the
final reckoning no one escapes.

And after the interlude of the 144,000 sealed and secure, the seventh seal opens onto something
unexpected: half an hour of silence in heaven. In Jewish tradition, silence in heaven is the
sound of God pausing to hear the prayers of his saints rising up — connecting directly back to
the martyrs' cry in the fifth seal, and setting up the trumpets that follow. Even the silence in
this book is not empty. It is attention.

## Closing

The scroll had to be opened before God's plan could move forward, and only the slain Lamb was
found worthy to open it. That is the shape of history according to Revelation — and it is the
shape of every Mass you will ever attend. The Word is opened. The Lamb is present. Go from here
and let both movements do their full work in you this week: let Scripture actually open something
in you, and let the Lamb, truly present, actually be received rather than merely observed.

## For Preparation

**Discussion questions:**
1. Why does it matter that John *hears* "Lion" but *sees* "Lamb"? What would be lost if
   Revelation simply showed him a lion after all?
2. Think about your last few experiences of the Liturgy of the Word. Has a homily ever felt like
   it genuinely "opened" something for you, the way opening a sealed legal document sets its
   contents into effect? What made the difference?
3. The martyrs under the altar cry "how long?" and are told to wait. Where in your own life or in
   the wider world do you find yourself asking God the same question? How does this passage
   shape how you wait?
4. This week, notice the moment at Mass when the priest holds up the host and says "Behold the
   Lamb of God" and the congregation responds "Lamb of God, you take away the sins of the world."
   What does it add to hear that moment as the direct echo of Revelation 5 it actually is?

**Song/hymn suggestions:** "Behold the Lamb"; "Worthy Is the Lamb" settings used in Eucharistic
Adoration; "Thy Word Is a Lamp Unto My Feet"; consider praying with the readings for an upcoming
Sunday Mass beforehand, then attending with attention specifically to how the homily "opens" the
Word before the Liturgy of the Eucharist begins.
