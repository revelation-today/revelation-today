# The Unholy Trinity

## Opening: The devil's not original — he's a plagiarist

Every great forgery depends on a great original. No one bothers counterfeiting a worthless currency. The fact that something gets copied is itself a kind of tribute to how valuable the real thing is.

Revelation 13 shows us the devil's masterwork of forgery: two beasts, assembled to imitate — feature for feature — the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. It's grotesque. It's also, in its own backwards way, a confession. The devil can't invent anything of his own. All he can do is copy the form of what God does and hollow out the substance.

## Why the dragon needs beasts at all

Revelation 12 ends with the dragon having failed twice — he couldn't devour the child, and he couldn't defeat the woman. Furious, "the dragon was enraged at the woman and went off to wage war against the rest of her offspring — those who keep the commandments of God and hold fast to the testimony of Jesus" (Revelation 12:17). That's the Church. Unable to win directly, the dragon recruits help: two beasts, one from the sea and one from the earth, in chapter 13.

## Ancient monsters, familiar endings

First-century readers would have recognized these beasts instantly, because John is deliberately drawing on stories they already knew — and every one of those stories ends the same way.

Job 40-41 describes Behemoth and Leviathan, land and sea monsters so fearsome that no human being could master them — yet Isaiah 27:1 promises God himself will slay Leviathan, and Psalm 104:26 pictures this same terror of the deep as God's plaything, a toy he made "to frolic" with. Daniel 7 gives us four beasts — a lion, a bear, a leopard, and a fourth beast with ten horns — and Revelation's first beast is a composite of all four at once: a leopard's body, a bear's feet, a lion's mouth, and ten horns. And Daniel 3 gives us the story everyone knew: a golden statue, a command to worship it on pain of death, and the number six built into its very construction — the statue itself standing sixty cubits high and six cubits wide. Every one of these stories — Job, Daniel 7, Daniel 3 — ends with the monster defeated, the tyrant humiliated, and God's people vindicated. Daniel's three friends walk out of the furnace unsinged, and the pagan king ends up praising God.

That's the key that unlocks chapter 13. To a reader who knew these older stories, the beasts of Revelation 13 don't read as "invincible evil." They read as the opening act of stories that always end with the monster losing.

## Two beasts, one strategy

The two beasts work as a team, and the parallel between them is deliberate.

The first beast, from the sea, receives "his power and his throne and great authority" from the dragon (Revelation 13:2). It is struck with a fatal wound — and healed, a grotesque mimicry of Christ's death and resurrection. And the world responds exactly as it should only respond to God: "Who is like the beast? Who can wage war against it?" (Revelation 13:4).

The second beast, from the earth, has "two horns like a lamb" but "spoke like a dragon" (Revelation 13:11). It acts entirely on the first beast's authority. It performs deceptive signs — even fire falling from heaven, echoing Elijah's contest with the prophets of Baal (1 Kings 18). It makes an image of the first beast that can even "speak" — echoing the breath of life God gives dry bones in Ezekiel 37. And it forces universal compliance: a mark on the hand or forehead, without which no one can buy or sell — culminating in "the number of a man… 666" (Revelation 13:18), a number that calls for wisdom to decode, and that connects back to the "six" already built into Daniel's forced-worship story.

## A counterfeit Trinity, feature for feature

Lay the pieces side by side and the imitation becomes unmistakable. The dragon gives all authority to the first beast, exactly as the Father gives all authority to the Son (Philippians 2:9-11). The first beast is killed and "resurrected," mimicking Christ's death and rising. Every knee bows to the first beast, exactly as every knee should bow to Jesus (Philippians 2:10). The first beast receives worship, as Jesus rightly receives worship in Revelation 5:8-14. And the second beast — with horns "like a lamb," speaking with the first beast's delegated authority — mimics the Holy Spirit, who likewise speaks with Christ's authority rather than his own (John 16:13), makes fire fall as proof of divine power (as Elijah's fire proved the true God), and gives an image false "life" (as the Spirit gives the dry bones real life in Ezekiel).

It's Father, Son, and Spirit — copied point for point. The form is all there. Only the substance is missing.

## The appeal is real — and that's exactly the danger

It would be a mistake to treat this "unholy trinity" as merely repulsive. It's seductive, and honesty requires admitting why.

It offers visible, undeniable strength, in place of a faith the world keeps calling foolish (1 Corinthians 1:22-23). It offers a world that finally just accepts the Gospel instead of endlessly rejecting it. It offers signs and wonders on demand, when we're aching for some proof that God is near. It offers healing when our prayers seem to go unanswered for years. It offers a God who visibly, immediately smashes evildoers, instead of a Lord who seems patient to the point of silence. It offers everyone, finally, submitting to what's right.

None of those longings are shameful. They're recognizably Christian longings. That's precisely the danger — the beast doesn't tempt us with something ugly. It tempts us with a shortcut to something good.

## The difference is never the goal — it's the method

Here is where the beast unmasks itself, if we look closely: not in what it wants, but in how it gets it. The first beast doesn't give itself for others out of love — it terrifies. It doesn't act to glorify God — it postures to steal his glory. It doesn't build up the weak — it attacks and consumes them. The second beast doesn't invite anyone freely — it threatens their livelihood and their lives if they refuse.

The beast, in short, stands for everything God is not: manipulation, intimidation, coercion, forced compliance dressed up as devotion. And Scripture is clear that love is never a means to some "greater good" that justifies setting it aside — love simply is the greatest good there is (Matthew 22:36-39). The moment any cause, however righteous-sounding, starts requiring fear or force to advance it, it has stopped being God's cause and started being the beast's method, whatever banner it's marching under.

## Why even the Church can be fooled

Here's the sobering part: Revelation 13:11 tells us the only clue given to expose the second beast is its voice — it "spoke like a dragon." Everything else about it looked like the Lamb: horns like a lamb, on the surface indistinguishable. The counterfeit and the real thing can look remarkably alike from the outside. The only reliable test is the voice.

Jesus said his own sheep "know his voice" (John 10:1-5) — and in the Hebrew way of thinking, truly hearing a voice always meant obeying it, not merely recognizing it intellectually. Which means the only reliable defense the Church has against the beast's counterfeit is not cleverness or vigilance in the abstract, but staying close enough to Christ, in prayer, sacrament, and obedience, that his voice becomes unmistakable and every impostor's voice becomes obviously false by comparison. Whenever the Church — or any of us — reaches for fear, coercion, or manipulation to accomplish even a genuinely good end, we have started speaking the dragon's dialect, regardless of the cause we invoke.

## A closing caution — the answer isn't suspicion of power itself

None of this means every miracle, every display of strength, every triumphant moment is suspect. God is victorious. The Church is victorious. Christ has already won. The devil would be a poor counterfeiter if his fake bore no resemblance at all to the original — of course it looks similar. The difference was never going to be found in power versus weakness.

The one thing the devil cannot copy, no matter how hard he tries, is God's actual signature: self-giving love, all the way to the cross. Everything else about the beast can be imitated. That, he cannot forge.

## Living this

- Where have you been tempted to want God to "just fix it" by force — and has that longing ever led you to justify manipulation or fear in your own life, church, or family, "for a good cause"?
- What does it look like, practically, to stay close enough to Christ's voice — through the sacraments, Scripture, and prayer — that you'd recognize a counterfeit if you heard one?
- Where in the Church's history, or in your own life, has coercion been mistaken for zeal? What would it look like to choose love instead, even when it's slower?

## Closing

The devil is a forger, not a creator. Everything he offers in Revelation 13 is a copy of something real, made ugly by the method used to deliver it. The real Trinity conquers through a Lamb who lays down his life. The unholy trinity conquers through fear. Only one of those is actually good news — and only one of those is actually going to win.

## For preparation

**Discussion questions:**
1. Which of the "appeals" of the unholy trinity (visible power, universal acceptance, signs on demand, instant justice) do you personally find most tempting, and why?
2. How does knowing that Job, Daniel 7, and Daniel 3 all end with the monster's defeat change how you read Revelation 13's picture of "unstoppable" evil?
3. What would it look like, concretely, for a parish or a Christian community to recognize it has started using "the beast's methods" even for a good cause?
4. How do we grow in recognizing Christ's voice as distinct from every counterfeit — practically, in daily prayer and sacramental life?
