# Heaven and Hell: The New Jerusalem

We are not going to rush this session. Of everything in this whole series, this is the material you have waited longest for, and it deserves the most time, not the least. Every session so far has pointed toward an ending. This is the ending. Let's take our time inside it.

## Everything made new

"Then I saw 'a new heaven and a new earth,' for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea" (Rev 21:1). "New" here does not simply mean "recently arrived" — it means a different quality of existence altogether. The old order was temporary; what comes now is permanent, the fulfillment of promises Israel's own prophets had been making for centuries (Isaiah 65:17-25; 66:22).

And notice this detail, easy to skip past: "there was no longer any sea." Throughout Scripture, the sea represents the place evil comes from — the sea monsters of the Psalms, the place from which the beast itself rises in this book (Rev 13:1), the place of the dead. If there is no more sea, there is no longer any place left for evil to hide, threaten, or oppress God's people from. Whatever chaos and danger has surrounded your life and your community's life, this promise says plainly: it will not simply be defeated. It will not exist anymore.

## God himself, dwelling with his people

"Look! God's dwelling-place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God" (Rev 21:3). This is not a new idea suddenly introduced — it is the oldest promise in Scripture, reaching back through the tabernacle in the wilderness (Leviticus 26:11-12), through Ezekiel's vision of God returning to dwell in his temple (Ezekiel 37:27), finally arriving at its full and complete form here. Not God visiting occasionally. Not God present through a building or a mediator. God himself, permanently, among his people, without any further separation.

## Every tear, wiped away by his own hand

"He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away" (Rev 21:4). Sit with the image itself: not simply that tears will stop, but that God himself wipes them away — a personal, tender act, not a distant policy of ending suffering. This fulfills Isaiah's ancient promise that death and tears would flee, and mourning and sighing would be undone completely (Isaiah 25:8; 35:10; 51:11).

We want to say this plainly and without qualification, because this is exactly the promise this community most needs to hear given real weight: whatever grief you carry — for family members lost, for years or generations of suffering, for a homeland or a normal life that has been taken from you — this promise does not ask you to explain it away or minimize it now. It names your tears specifically, and it promises God's own hand will wipe every one of them away.

## Living water, freely given

"To the thirsty I will give water without cost from the spring of the water of life" (Rev 21:6). This fulfills Isaiah's picture of God freely giving water to all who thirst (Isaiah 55:1), and it is fulfilled fully in Jesus himself, who offered this same living water to the woman at the well and to all who believe in him (John 4:10; 7:38). Whatever you have had to pay, whatever cost faithfulness has demanded of you in this life, here is a promise given "without cost" — freely, because Jesus has already paid what needed to be paid.

## The specific promises to Smyrna and Philadelphia, finally fulfilled

Look at what happens to the promises given earlier to the two churches at the center of this whole series. Smyrna was promised to be kept from the "second death" (Rev 2:11) — and here, that promise stands fulfilled: only those "victorious" inherit this new creation, sharing fully in God's presence (Rev 21:7). Philadelphia was promised to become "a pillar in the temple of my God... never again will they leave it," and to have God's own name written on them (Rev 3:12) — and here, in the New Jerusalem, that pillar-promise reaches its full form: permanent presence with God, a name that can never again be stripped away.

If you have felt, reading those earlier letters, that the promises given to poor, pressured, faithful churches were almost too good to be believed, here is Revelation's own answer: they were not exaggerated. This is what they were pointing toward the whole time.

## The shape of the city: a cube, and what that means

The New Jerusalem is described with a peculiar shape: a perfect cube (Rev 21:16). The only object described that way anywhere in the Old Testament is the Holy of Holies (2 Chronicles 3:8) — the innermost room of the temple, so saturated with God's presence that only the high priest could enter, and only once a year, at tremendous personal risk. Here, that most restricted, most exclusive space in all of Scripture becomes the shape of the entire city. Everyone who belongs here stands as close to God as the high priest alone once stood. The furthest boundary of the New Jerusalem is still the Holy of Holies. There is no longer a "close" and a "far" among God's people — everyone stands at the center.

## The twelve gates, the twelve foundations, and reconciliation

The city's twelve gates are named for the twelve tribes of Israel; its twelve foundations, for the twelve apostles (Rev 21:12-14). Jewish believers and Gentile believers, one people, forming one city together — the long, hard history of division between Israel and the nations reaching its final reconciliation. Whatever divisions mark your own community's history — and every community carries some — this is the picture underneath all of it: one city, built on both foundations together, with room enough within it for every nation who comes.

## No temple, because there is no longer any need for one

"I did not see a temple in the city, because the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are its temple" (Rev 21:22). Every earlier stage in this story needed a temple — a designated place where God's presence could be approached. Here, that need disappears entirely, because God himself, directly present everywhere in the city, has become the temple. The barrier that made a "holy place" necessary in the first place — sin, separation, the need for a designated meeting point with a holy God — is simply gone.

## The nations bring their honor, not their wealth

"The nations will walk by its light, and the kings of the earth will bring their splendour into it" (Rev 21:24). This echoes Isaiah's picture of the nations streaming toward Jerusalem's light, but with an important shift: what they bring is not wealth extracted the way Babylon extracted it, through exploitation and idolatry. It is glory and honor freely given, offered because Israel's ancient calling — to be a blessing to all nations, beginning all the way back with Abraham (Genesis 12:2-3) — has finally, fully arrived. Every nation who ever suffered under a Babylon of their own now streams toward this city instead, freely, to give rather than to be taken from.

## Hell: simply, the absence of all of this

What, then, is hell? Revelation does not describe it primarily as a torture chamber built to punish. It describes it, more simply and more soberly, as the absence of everything we have just described: a life without God's presence, and therefore without everything that flows from his presence — hope, peace, forgiveness, joy. Everything good we experience even now has its true source in God; hell is what remains when that source is entirely removed, by the settled choice of those who refuse it, forever. God does not force fellowship on anyone who has finally and fully refused it. But the consequence of that refusal is simply a life left with none of what his presence gives.

## What this means for you, specifically

This is the promise underneath every hard thing this series has named honestly: real poverty, real persecution, real grief, real waiting. None of it is the final word. The final word is a city shaped like the Holy of Holies, with God himself as its temple, wiping every tear from your eyes with his own hand, where the specific promises made to Smyrna and to Philadelphia — kept from the second death, made a permanent pillar, never again to leave — stand completely, finally fulfilled.

**For reflection and prayer together:**

- Which specific tear do you most need God's promise in Revelation 21:4 to reach tonight? Name it, if you're able, in the group.
- What does it mean to you that the New Jerusalem is shaped like the Holy of Holies — that everyone stands as close to God as the high priest alone once did?
- Take real time tonight simply resting in this promise, without rushing to the next practical application. This is the ending. Let it be enough for a while.
