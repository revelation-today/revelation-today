# Welcome

You did not need anyone to explain to you what it costs to belong to Jesus in a place that has already decided who is allowed to belong. You have known that your whole life, or your parents did, or your grandparents did, in a line stretching back further than most of the churches reading this same book of the Bible in more comfortable places. So this series is not going to spend its time convincing you that faithfulness is costly. You already know. Instead, it is going to spend its time on something this book cares about at least as much, and that gets rushed past far too often: the promise that your cost has been seen, that it is not wasted, and that it will be answered.

The book of Revelation was written by a man in exile, to churches who were poor, slandered, squeezed economically, and in real danger of prison and death — for the single, simple reason that they would not say the words the surrounding culture demanded of them. Two of those seven churches, Smyrna and Philadelphia, get no rebuke from Jesus at all. Not because they were strong, or safe, or resourced, but because, in the middle of real pressure, they stayed his. If that is closer to your own church, your own family, or your own story than to a history lecture, then you are exactly who this book was written for, and exactly who this series is written for.

We want to name honestly, and without embarrassment, three things many of you carry.

First, the pressure is not ancient history. It is Tuesday. It may be a government form, a checkpoint, a job you cannot get, a neighborhood you cannot safely walk through with a cross visible, a family member in prison for their faith, or worse. Revelation was not written into a metaphor. It was written into exactly this.

Second, there is a real and constant pull toward quiet, safe invisibility — softening what you say about Jesus at a border crossing, on an asylum form, to a landlord, to keep your family safe or keep a door open to leave. That is not a failure of nerve; it is a real and human weight, and this book speaks directly into it, because the first Christians in Asia Minor faced their own version of exactly that same pull.

Third — and this may be the most important thing this series has to say — long, unanswered suffering can tip from faithful endurance into despair. If you or people you love have carried this weight for years, or for generations, with no resolution in sight, Revelation does not shame you for asking "how long, O Lord?" It puts that very question in the mouths of the martyrs under God's own altar, and it tells you God is not silent in response.

So this series will not spend its energy establishing why faithfulness costs something. It will spend its energy on comfort, on vindication, and on the promise that the Lamb who was slain sees every tear, hears every cry, and is coming to make it right. That is what these ten sessions are for.
