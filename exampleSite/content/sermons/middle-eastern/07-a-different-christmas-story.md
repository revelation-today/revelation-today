# A Different Christmas Story

You know the Christmas story from Luke: shepherds, a baby, a barn, travelers following a star. Revelation tells the same story completely differently — not as a gentle scene, but as a cosmic battle between a helpless woman and an enormous, murderous dragon, in which the child's survival looks, right up until the last moment, entirely impossible.

## The scene

"A great and wondrous sign appeared in heaven: a woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet and a crown of twelve stars on her head. She was pregnant and cried out in pain as she was about to give birth" (Rev 12:1-2). Facing her: "an enormous red dragon with seven heads and ten horns... the dragon stood in front of the woman who was about to give birth, so that it might devour her child the moment he was born" (Rev 12:3-4).

Strip away everything we already know about how this story ends, and look only at what is actually in front of us: a woman in labor, in no condition for a fight, and a massive, furious dragon whose entire purpose is to kill her child before he can draw his first breath. By any honest reading of the odds in that moment, the woman has no chance, and the child has no future.

## The dragon and the child

The dragon is named directly a few verses later: "that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan" (Rev 12:9) — language that reaches straight back to the garden and the fall. This is the same enemy behind every act of persecution, every campaign of violence against God's people, in every century since.

The child is described by one striking detail: he "will rule all the nations with an iron sceptre" (Rev 12:5), language pulled from Psalm 2, where God warns the rebellious kings of the earth to submit to his Son, and from Isaiah's picture of the coming Messiah who strikes the earth with the rod of his mouth (Isaiah 11:4). This is Jesus, and this is the story of his birth — read here as an act of open warfare, not a quiet, domesticated scene.

## The woman: Israel giving birth to her promised Messiah

Who is the woman? The best reading is Israel — not obviously described as a woman at first, but named that way elsewhere in Scripture (as "daughter Zion," Isaiah 62:11), and matched closely by Joseph's dream of the sun, moon, and eleven stars representing his family, with Joseph himself completing the twelve (Genesis 37:9-10). Israel is the nation promised, long before Jesus's birth, that she would bring forth the Messiah — a promise that came due at exactly the moment Israel was crushed under Roman power, with rebellion after rebellion against Rome rising and being violently put down.

It was into that pressure — a subject nation, under occupation, with no visible path to the freedom it had been promised — that the Messiah was actually born. Not into safety. Not into a moment of national strength. Into exactly the kind of vulnerable, occupied, pressured existence many of you know from the inside.

## Rescued, tempted, victorious — and it still didn't look like winning

The child was "snatched up to God and to his throne" (Rev 12:5) — Revelation compresses Jesus's entire life, from birth to ascension, into that single verse. But history fills in what that verse skips over: Herod tried to kill him as an infant (Matthew 2). Satan tried again to destroy him through temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1-11). And in the end, Jesus was killed — publicly, humiliatingly, exactly the way his enemies wanted. If you had watched only the crucifixion, without knowing what came three days later, you would have said: the dragon won. The child is dead. And yet Revelation calls this the child's victory, achieved by being "snatched up" through death and out the other side.

## The pattern this sets for the whole book

This is the single most important thing Revelation 12 does for the rest of the book: it sets the pattern for everything that follows. The two churches that receive no rebuke at all — Smyrna and Philadelphia — are both, on paper, in serious, visible trouble. Laodicea, the church most confident in its own strength and success, receives no praise whatsoever. The lion of Judah, when John finally gets to see him, turns out to be a slaughtered lamb (Rev 5:5-6). The two witnesses, publicly killed and celebrated as defeated by the whole world, are resurrected in front of everyone (Rev 11:7-12). Meanwhile the dragon and the beast, who look invincible and in total control, are heading for total disaster, taking down with them everyone who trusted in their power (Rev 14:6-13).

Over and over, this book insists on the same reversal: the one who looks helpless is the one who actually overcomes, and the one who looks unstoppable is already doomed. This is not wishful thinking bolted onto an otherwise grim story. It is the deep architecture of the whole book, beginning here, with a defenseless woman in labor and a dragon who — for all his size, all his fury, all his apparent power — loses.

## Why this matters for you

If your community's story, like Israel's under Rome, includes long seasons of occupation, violence, and a promise that has not yet been fully realized, this chapter is not asking you to pretend the dragon isn't real or isn't dangerous. He is described here in full, terrifying detail — seven heads, ten horns, a tail that sweeps stars from the sky. The danger is not minimized.

What this chapter insists on, instead, is that the dragon's apparent power in the moment is not the same as final victory. The woman who looked doomed gave birth anyway. The child who looked defenseless rules the nations anyway. And the pattern set here — weakness that turns out to be victory, threat that turns out to be already defeated — is the pattern every other hard scene in this book will follow. When you read what comes next, read it with this chapter underneath it.

**For reflection and prayer together:**

- Where in your own community's history do you see the pattern of this chapter: real danger, real vulnerability, and yet survival and eventual vindication?
- The dragon looked unstoppable in the moment he stood over the woman. Where do the powers arrayed against you today look unstoppable — and what does this chapter say about how that story actually ends?
- How does it change your view of Jesus's birth to see it told here as an act of cosmic warfare rather than only a gentle domestic scene?
