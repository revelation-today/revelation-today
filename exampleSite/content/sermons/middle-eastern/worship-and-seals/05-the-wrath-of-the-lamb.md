# The Wrath of the Lamb

Every session so far has pointed toward this one, whether we said so or not. Smyrna's faithfulness unto death, Philadelphia's patient endurance under real pressure — all of it raises a question that does not go away simply because we trust God: how long? How long does faithfulness under real, ongoing suffering actually have to last before God does something about it? Revelation does not dodge this question. It puts it directly into the mouths of martyrs, under God's own altar, and gives us God's own answer.

## The cry under the altar

As the Lamb opens the fifth seal, John sees "under the altar the souls of those who had been slain because of the word of God and the testimony they had maintained. They called out in a loud voice, 'How long, Sovereign Lord, holy and true, until you judge the inhabitants of the earth and avenge our blood?'" (Rev 6:9-10).

Read that again slowly, because it deserves to be heard exactly as it is written, not softened. These are people who were killed for their faithfulness to Jesus — not symbolically, but killed — and their response, from heaven itself, in the presence of God, is not patient silence. It is a cry. A demand, almost: how long. If you have ever felt that this question was too raw, too impatient, too much like doubt to say out loud in prayer, hear this plainly: it is already in Scripture, spoken by the martyrs themselves, directly to God, and God does not rebuke them for asking it.

## God's answer is not silence

"Then each of them was given a white robe, and they were told to wait a little longer, until the full number of their fellow servants, their brothers and sisters, were killed just as they had been" (Rev 6:11). This is not the answer we might wish for — an immediate end to suffering. But look carefully at what it is not: it is not silence, and it is not a rebuke. God answers. He gives them white robes — a mark of vindication and honor, given now, not deferred until some unspecified future. And he tells them the truth: a little longer, until the full number is complete.

This is a hard word, and it should not be preached as an easy one. But it is also, in its own way, the most direct comfort this book offers a community that has waited generations for justice: your cry has been heard. It reached God. It was not lost, ignored, or dismissed. You are not shouting into an empty sky. Somewhere in heaven, other believers who died before you are already vindicated, already clothed in white, already told that their wait — like yours — is not endless, even if it is not yet over.

## What the four horsemen actually reveal

To understand why this cry comes exactly here, in the sixth chapter, we need to look at what comes just before it: four horsemen, released one at a time as the Lamb opens the first four seals. A rider on a white horse, "bent on conquest" (Rev 6:2); a rider on a red horse who takes peace from the earth (Rev 6:4); a rider on a black horse who inflicts crushing food prices on the poor while luxury goods are protected (Rev 6:5-6); and a rider on a pale horse named Death, with Hades following (Rev 6:8).

The first rider is deliberately unsettling, because on the surface he can look like Christ — riding out with a bow, given a crown, described only as "conquering." But look at what follows him: war, then engineered scarcity that crushes the poor while protecting the comfortable, then death. This is not the pattern of Jesus's kingdom. It is a counterfeit — evil dressed up to look like triumph, making grand promises before its true face is unmasked in the devastation that follows. That is precisely why the very next seal reveals the martyrs' cry: after four seals showing exactly the kind of injustice, deception, and suffering many of you know firsthand — economic squeezing of the poor, violence, death for the faithful — the natural, honest response is "how long?" Revelation does not treat that cry as a problem in the story. It treats it as the point of the story arriving.

## The wrath that is not what people fear

Later in the same chapter, we're told that when the full weight of God's response finally arrives, "the kings of the earth, the princes, the generals, the rich, the mighty, and everyone else... called to the mountains and the rocks, 'Fall on us and hide us... from the wrath of the Lamb!'" (Rev 6:15-16). Notice something important here: this is a description of how the guilty perceive Jesus's justice, not necessarily a description of what that justice will actually look like when it arrives. Their terror comes from their own guilt, the same way Adam and Eve hid from God after the fall, and Israel hid from God in shame over its own unfaithfulness in Hosea. The text shows us their fear. It does not show us God taking pleasure in it.

The word translated "wrath" here — orge in Greek — describes something that has built up slowly over a long time, not a sudden, short-tempered outburst. This is not an impulsive God losing his temper. It is patient, long-building justice, finally acting on behalf of those who have suffered under real oppression for a very long time.

## Wrath as justice for real victims, not abstraction

It is worth being direct about something many comfortable readers of this book never have to reckon with honestly: God's wrath, in Revelation, exists specifically because real people suffer real injustice — economic exploitation, slavery, persecution for their faith or their ethnicity, war, famine engineered by the greed of the powerful. If you have not lived any of that, God's wrath can feel like an uncomfortable, even embarrassing topic to preach. But if you have lived it, or your family has, or your church has, this is not embarrassing. It is exactly what you have been waiting to hear someone say plainly: God sees this. God is not neutral about this. God will act.

This is not a promise that gives license for personal revenge, or for violence carried out in Jesus's name — nothing in the New Testament supports that, and Revelation's own vision of victory, as we will see in the sessions ahead, comes through faithful witness and endurance, not through the church taking up the sword. But it is a promise that the suffering you have endured has not escaped God's notice, and it will not go unanswered forever.

## Hold both truths together

So hold both things Revelation gives us in this chapter, without letting either one cancel the other. First: "how long" is a legitimate, faithful prayer, one that heaven itself is already praying alongside you. Second: God's answer to that prayer is not silence, even when it is not yet the full ending we long for. He sees. He answers. He marks his suffering people with honor now, even in the waiting, and he has promised that the waiting has an end.

**For reflection and prayer together:**

- Have you ever felt that "how long, O Lord?" was too raw or too doubtful a prayer to say out loud? What does it change to know the martyrs prayed exactly that, in heaven, in God's presence?
- Where in your own community's story do you see the pattern of the four horsemen — deception dressed as triumph, followed by real suffering for the vulnerable?
- What would it look like this week to bring your own "how long" honestly to God, trusting that he has already answered it once, and will answer it fully?
