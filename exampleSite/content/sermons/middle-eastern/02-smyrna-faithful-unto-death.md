# Smyrna: Faithful Unto Death

Of all seven letters, this is the one written closest to where many of you already live. So we are not going to spend long explaining Smyrna's situation to you. You already recognize it. What we want to spend our time on is the one thing Jesus says to this church that he says to almost no one else: nothing needs correcting here. Just this — hold on.

## A church under pressure from every direction

Smyrna was a wealthy port city, and its wealth had a cost for the church: to work, you generally needed to belong to a trade guild, and the guilds required participation in idol worship. Christians who would not comply were shut out economically — squeezed, in other words, exactly the way believers in guild-controlled or state-controlled economies are squeezed today when faithfulness closes off a livelihood. On top of that, the letter tells us some in the local Jewish community were slandering the church and were likely behind believers being imprisoned — hence Jesus's sharp phrase, "a synagogue of Satan" (Rev 2:9), not a statement about Jewish people in general, but about a specific local group actively working to harm this specific church.

Poor. Slandered. Facing prison. And here is what makes Smyrna remarkable: of the seven churches, only Smyrna and Philadelphia receive no rebuke from Jesus at all. Not the wealthy, comfortable, well-regarded churches. The one under real financial and physical threat.

## Jesus introduces himself as the one who has already died

Before Jesus says a single word about Smyrna's situation, he introduces himself: "These are the words of him who is the First and the Last, who died and came to life again" (Rev 2:8). Of all the ways Jesus could introduce himself to a suffering, threatened church, he chooses this one: I have already died, and I am alive. He is not asking Smyrna to walk a road he has not already walked and come out the other side of.

"I know your afflictions and your poverty — yet you are rich!" (Rev 2:9). Jesus does not deny the poverty. He does not spiritualize it away or tell them it isn't real. He simply adds a second truth beside it: by the world's accounting, poor; by his accounting, rich. Both are true at once. That is worth sitting with, because it is tempting to think we have to choose between naming real hardship honestly and trusting that we are genuinely rich in Christ. Smyrna shows us we don't have to choose.

## "Do not be afraid of what you are about to suffer"

Then comes the part of this letter that asks the most of its readers: "Do not be afraid of what you are about to suffer. I tell you, the devil will put some of you in prison to test you, and you will suffer persecution for ten days. Be faithful, even to the point of death, and I will give you life as your victor's crown" (Rev 2:10).

Notice what Jesus does not say. He does not say, "this won't happen." He does not say, "I will make sure you're spared." He says: it will happen, and here is what to do when it does — do not be afraid, and be faithful, even to the point of death. This is about as direct as Revelation ever gets, and it is directed at exactly the kind of pressure many of you know from the inside: imprisonment for the faith, a limited but real span of suffering, and the plain fact that some will not survive it.

The "ten days" echoes the story of Daniel, who endured a limited period of testing — eating only vegetables rather than defile himself with food from the king's table — and was vindicated at the end of it (Daniel 1:8-14). The number is not a literal calendar; it signals a bounded trial with an end and a vindication on the other side of it, the same pattern Daniel himself lived through generations earlier under a different pagan empire.

## The crown, and the promise that death does not have the last word

"Be faithful, even to the point of death, and I will give you life as your victor's crown" (Rev 2:10). In the Greco-Roman world, a victor's crown was awarded to a winner — an athlete, a soldier who came home from war having actually won. Jesus takes that image and hands it to a poor, threatened, powerless congregation and says: this is what your faithfulness looks like from where I stand. Not defeat. Victory, crowned.

And then the last word of the letter: "The one who is victorious will not be hurt at all by the second death" (Rev 2:11). Smyrna faced a real, physical first death — imprisonment, possibly execution. Jesus does not promise to prevent that. What he promises is that it cannot touch what matters most: it has no power over the resurrection life he already holds in his own hands, having walked through death and out the other side (Rev 5:5-6; 20:4-6).

This is worth being honest about together, because it is not a small thing to say to people who may lose family members, freedom, or their own lives for the name of Christ. Revelation does not answer that reality with easy comfort. It answers it with the truest comfort there is: the first death is real, and it is not the end of the story, because Jesus has already gone through it and come out alive, and he has promised that those who stay faithful with him will do the same.

## No solving, only enduring — together with him

There is a pattern worth naming plainly: the more we are unable to fix our own situation, the more room there is for Jesus to be the one who carries it. Smyrna was not told to organize its way out of poverty, negotiate its way out of persecution, or find a clever middle path with the guilds and the authorities. It was told to endure, faithfully, together with Jesus. We are not asked to solve every hard situation we find ourselves in. We are asked to endure it, not alone, but with him — the same Jesus who stood among the lampstands in the previous chapter, present in the middle of exactly this kind of pressure.

That said, this endurance is not the same as pretending the weight isn't heavy. Long, unrelieved pressure carries a real danger of tipping into despair, of simply giving up under the weight of it. Revelation does not ignore that risk — we will come back to it directly later in this series. For now, hear what Jesus says to Smyrna as what he says to you: I know. I see it. It is real. And it does not have the final word over you.

## Why Jesus rebukes nothing here

Consider what Jesus does not say to Smyrna. He does not tell them to fight back, to find a safer accommodation with the guilds, to quietly stop identifying so visibly as Christian to avoid the worst of the pressure. Given everything we know about the temptation toward a comfortable "third way" — outward compliance, inward faithfulness — Smyrna's silence on this option is itself the message. They apparently were not taking it. They were simply present, poor, threatened, and faithful. And that, entirely on its own, earned them the one thing almost no other church in Revelation receives: nothing to correct.

**For reflection and prayer together:**

- Where has your community been "poor" in the world's eyes while actually being rich in Christ? Say that out loud to one another tonight.
- What would it mean, practically, to "not be afraid" of a hardship you know is coming, rather than pretending it won't come at all?
- Jesus promises a crown, not a rescue, to Smyrna. How do we hold onto that promise honestly, without minimizing what it costs to receive it?
