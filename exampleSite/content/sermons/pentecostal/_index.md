# Welcome, Friends in the Pentecostal and Charismatic Tradition

Revelation is a book about the Spirit poured out, worship that never stops, and a church
learning to tell the difference between what comes from God and what only looks like it does.
That is not unfamiliar territory for Pentecostal and Charismatic believers — in a lot of ways,
this is your book. This series adapts the wider "Revelation Today" material for your context,
taking your questions about the end times seriously rather than treating them as a problem to
be managed.

## Where this book feels like home

Three things stand out immediately. First, Revelation 4–5 is an unbroken scene of Spirit-filled
worship — living creatures, elders, angels, and the redeemed of every nation crying "Holy, holy,
holy" without pause, falling before the throne, casting down their crowns. That is not a
formal liturgy performed at a distance; it is exactly the kind of full-bodied, uninhibited,
never-ending worship your churches already practice and prize, and Revelation puts it at the
very center of the book — everything that follows, every seal and trumpet and bowl, flows out
of that throne room. Second, you already have a rich, practiced vocabulary for discernment of
spirits — for asking whether a sign, a voice, or a manifestation comes from God or from
something counterfeit. Revelation needs exactly that instinct. Its villains are not just
violent; they are imitators, a dragon and two beasts staging a fake trinity that mimics God's
power, God's resurrection, and God's worship point for point. Reading Revelation well means
using the same discernment you already practice every week. Third, you carry a felt, expectant
hope that Jesus is coming back and that it matters urgently for how we live now — and that
expectancy is exactly the posture Revelation asks of its readers, "the time is near," watchful
and ready.

## Where we'll think together

One thing calls for real care, and we want to name it honestly rather than slide past it. Many
Pentecostal and Charismatic believers have grown up with a specific set of end-times
expectations absorbed from wider Christian pop culture — a pre-tribulation rapture, a seven-year
tribulation, a rebuilt temple, timelines tied to current events. That system, usually called
dispensationalism, is relatively young (it traces to the 1800s) and has good company disagreeing
with it, but it is also deeply familiar and, for many, pastorally important — tied to real hope
about a Lord who keeps his promises. This series offers a different reading, one rooted closely
in the text and its first-century setting, without pretending the question is trivial or that
your prior framework was foolish. Three sessions in particular — on the Great Tribulation, the
Thousand-Year Kingdom, and the capstone session on the rapture and "Left Behind" theology —
engage this directly, early enough to matter and fully enough to be worth taking seriously. We
ask only that you read the text alongside us and weigh the argument on its own terms; thoughtful
Christians land in different places here, and this series will tell you why it lands where it
does rather than simply asserting it.

## What's inside

Twelve topics, each with a full adult session and a matching kids' session, drawn from the same
source material used across the whole "Revelation Today" project. Use them in order — the early
sessions on genre and worship lay groundwork the later, more sensitive sessions depend on — or
pull individual sessions as needed for a class, a small group, or a preaching series.
