# What Kind of Book Is This?

## Why we start here

Before we look at a single seal, trumpet, or bowl, we need to answer a more basic question:
what kind of book are we even holding? That might sound like throat-clearing before the real
sermon starts, but it isn't. How you answer this question will quietly shape every single
session that follows in this series — especially the ones later on about the tribulation, the
thousand-year kingdom, and the rapture. If we get the genre wrong here, we'll misread
everything downstream. So it's worth taking one whole session just to slow down and ask: what
is Revelation, actually?

Many of us came to this book already holding a picture in our minds — a coded newsreel of
future headlines, a puzzle to be cross-referenced with tomorrow's news cycle. That picture is
everywhere in Christian pop culture, and it isn't crazy to have absorbed it; it's the water a
lot of us have been swimming in for years. But it's worth setting that picture down for a
moment and asking the text itself what it claims to be.

## Three genres, all at once

Revelation actually tells us what it is in its opening lines, and it's not one thing — it's
three, layered on top of each other.

**It is a letter.** It opens and closes exactly like the New Testament letters we're used to
reading — a greeting, a blessing, a sign-off. It's addressed to seven specific, real churches
in first-century Asia Minor, named right at the start. That matters more than it might seem.
A letter is written to say something to its actual recipients, about their actual situation.
If we treat the bulk of Revelation as speaking only to a generation two thousand years in our
future, we've skipped past the fact that John addressed this to real congregations who were
supposed to understand it and act on it.

**It is a prophecy.** Not prophecy in the sense of a psychic forecast, but in the Old Testament
sense: a message grounded in God's covenant, meant to comfort the oppressed and confront
injustice, always aimed at changing how God's people live right now. The prophets weren't
mainly fortune-tellers; they were covenant lawyers, calling Israel back to faithfulness. And
here's a detail worth sitting with: sometimes prophecy exists precisely so it *won't* come
true — think of Jonah, furious that Nineveh repented and the judgment he'd announced never
landed. Prophecy aims at response, not just prediction.

**It is an apocalypse.** This is the genre most of us have never encountered anywhere else,
which is exactly why it's so easy to misread. An apocalypse encourages God's people through
crisis by unveiling the spiritual reality behind their present suffering — using vivid,
dualistic, "either/or" imagery (Lamb versus dragon, the seal of God versus the mark of the
beast) that forces a choice with no neutral ground. The nearest thing we have today is a
political cartoon: nobody looks at an editorial cartoon of a dragon devouring a city and thinks
dragons are real or that the cartoonist is predicting an actual dragon attack next Tuesday. We
get the point instantly, without needing to literalize the image. Revelation asks the same kind
of reading from us.

## Why this matters for the rest of the series

Put those three together and here's what falls out: Revelation was written to be understood
and acted on by real churches in real trouble, using vivid symbolic imagery to unveil spiritual
realities and press for a decision — not a literal transcript of a future news broadcast to be
decoded centuries later.

This isn't a swipe at anyone's sincerity. It's simply asking us to let the book tell us what
kind of reading it requires, the same way we'd let a love poem tell us not to read it as a
lab report. And it's worth being honest that this reading choice has real stakes: several
sessions later in this series — on the Great Tribulation, the thousand-year kingdom, and the
rapture — will look different depending on whether we read Revelation as a coded prediction of
our own future headlines or as a first-century letter unveiling a spiritual reality that spans
the whole age between Christ's two comings. We'll take each of those questions seriously and in
turn, on their own textual merits, when we get there. For now, the point is simpler: we're
reading a letter, a prophecy, and an apocalypse, written to be grasped by its first hearers —
and that's actually good news, because it means this book was never sealed away from us. It was
written to be lived.

## An invitation, not a correction

If you've always read Revelation as a literal countdown to future events, this isn't an
accusation that you've been foolish — it's an invitation to look again at what the book itself
claims to be, together, with open Bibles. Many careful, Spirit-filled readers over the centuries
have read it this way; we're simply going to let the text's own self-description guide us as we
walk through it session by session. Come with your questions. We'll take all of them seriously.

## Closing thought

The Spirit who inspired this book didn't hide it in a vault for a distant future generation to
crack open. He gave it to churches under real pressure, so that worship, endurance, and
discernment could carry them through — and so that it could carry us through too. That's the
lens we'll use for everything that follows.
