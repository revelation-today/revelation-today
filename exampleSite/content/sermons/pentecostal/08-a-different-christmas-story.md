# A Different Christmas Story

## A story you've never heard told this way

You know the Christmas story from Luke: shepherds, a manger, wise men following a star. It's
beautiful, and we've heard it so many times we can recite it without feeling it anymore.
Revelation 12 tells the same essential story — the birth of Jesus — but stages it as an all-out
cosmic battle, and once you see it, you won't read Christmas the same way again.

## The scene

"A great sign appeared in heaven: a woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet
and a crown of twelve stars on her head. She was pregnant and cried out in pain as she was about
to give birth. Then another sign appeared in heaven: an enormous red dragon... The dragon stood
in front of the woman who was about to give birth, so that it might devour her child the moment
he was born" (Revelation 12:1-4).

Picture it without knowing the ending: a woman in the most vulnerable moment a human being can
be in, facing a monstrous, furious dragon whose entire purpose is to destroy her child before he
draws his first breath. Every instinct says this child has no chance.

## Who is who

The dragon is named outright a few verses later: "that ancient serpent called the devil, or
Satan" (12:9) — deliberately calling back to the serpent in Eden. The child is identified by a
detail that shows up nowhere else but always points the same direction: he will "rule all the
nations with an iron scepter," language lifted from Psalm 2 (God's Son ruling the rebellious
nations) and Isaiah 11 (the coming Messiah striking the earth with the rod of his mouth), and
which Jesus applies directly to himself in Revelation 2:27. This is Jesus.

The woman takes a little more work. Three candidates fit: Mary, who literally gave birth to
Jesus; Eve, promised that her offspring would crush the serpent (Genesis 3:15); or Israel,
promised to give birth to the Messiah (Isaiah 66:7). Mary is the obvious first guess, but
nowhere else in Scripture is Mary described with sun, moon, and stars. The closer match is
Joseph's dream in Genesis 37:9-10, where the sun, moon, and eleven stars represent his parents
and eleven brothers — Joseph himself making the twelfth. That's the twelve tribes of Israel. The
best reading, then, is Israel herself: the nation promised across centuries to give birth to the
Messiah, now in the agony of labor at the exact moment Rome has her under crushing pressure,
with failed rebellions already littering recent memory.

## The dragon's whole strategy, laid bare

What makes this "Christmas story" different from Luke's isn't new information about the
nativity — it's the vantage point. Revelation 12 shows us the spiritual battle raging behind
the manger scene the whole time. Herod's slaughter of the innocents, the wilderness temptation,
the plots against Jesus throughout his ministry, and finally the cross itself: all one continued
assault from the same dragon, trying every angle to devour the child, or at least stop him. And
every single attempt fails. The chapter compresses Jesus' entire earthly life into a single
verse — "she gave birth... and her child was snatched up to God and to his throne" (12:5) — because
the point isn't the biographical details. The point is that the dragon loses, completely, every
time.

## Winning by looking like you're losing

Here's the pattern this scene sets for the rest of Revelation, and it's worth naming clearly
because it recurs constantly: the one who looks helpless is the one who actually wins. A
pregnant woman about to give birth is about as vulnerable as a human being gets — and she and
her child triumph over an enormous, furious dragon. We already saw this with the Lion who turns
out to be a slaughtered Lamb (chapter 5). We'll see it again with the two witnesses, who appear
totally defeated and then are vindicated in full view of their enemies (chapter 11). And it
shows up in the churches themselves: the only two congregations Jesus has nothing but praise
for, Smyrna and Philadelphia, are the poorest and most pressured of the seven — while Laodicea,
confident in its own wealth and strength, gets no praise at all.

## What this means for us

If your life right now looks more like the woman in labor than the triumphant dragon — under
pressure, out of options, facing something that looks unstoppable — Revelation 12 says that's
not a sign you've been abandoned or that evil has the upper hand. It's the same shape the whole
story of Jesus takes: apparent weakness, real victory. The dragon's fury doesn't mean he's
winning. Revelation 12:12 tells us exactly why he's so vicious in this age: "he is filled with
fury, because he knows his time is short." A defeated enemy lashing out before his final
judgment looks a lot like an enemy who's still powerful. He isn't. He already lost the moment
the child was snatched up to the throne.
