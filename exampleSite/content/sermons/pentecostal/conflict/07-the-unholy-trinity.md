# The Unholy Trinity: The Beast in Its Own Day

## Real, and really about them

Revelation 13 introduces the most infamous cast in the whole book: a dragon and two beasts,
one rising from the sea, one from the earth, marking people on the hand and forehead with the
number 666. This chapter has probably inspired more speculation — matching world leaders,
decoding barcodes, hunting for hidden numbers in headlines — than any other passage in
Scripture. So it's worth grounding ourselves first in what this chapter meant to the people it
was actually written for, before we ask what it means for discernment today.

Revelation is a letter to real first-century churches, and its first readers would have
recognized these figures immediately. "Coming from the sea" meant something foreign — for
churches in Asia Minor, that meant Rome, the emperor, and the governors who arrived across the
water. "Coming from the earth" meant the local, native powers: provincial elites who owed their
status to Roman favor. The dragon is the devil, the power behind both. The first beast is the
emperor and the imperial system itself — indestructible from generation to generation no matter
which particular Caesar dies, since a new one always steps into the role. The second beast is
the machinery that enforced everyday loyalty to that system: local priests and officials
performing processions, building temples, selling meat offered to idols, making sure everyone
kept participating in the civic and religious life that propped the emperor up. It "does not
look evil" — that's the point. It looks like ordinary life, like going along to get along.

## An evil trinity

Here's what makes this chapter matter far beyond first-century Rome: the dragon and the two
beasts are deliberately structured to mimic the true Trinity, point by point.

- The dragon gives the first beast all his authority and his throne, just as the Father gives
  Jesus all authority (Philippians 2:9-11).
- The first beast appears to be fatally wounded and yet lives — a counterfeit resurrection.
- Every knee bows to the first beast, just as every knee will bow to Jesus (Philippians 2:10).
- The second beast has "two horns like a lamb" but speaks like the dragon — imitating the Holy
  Spirit's role of pointing all attention toward the first beast, the way the true Spirit points
  all attention toward Christ (John 16:13).
- The second beast performs great signs, even making fire fall from heaven — echoing Elijah's
  contest on Mount Carmel — and gives breath to an image so convincingly that it appears to
  speak.

From the outside, in other words, the false trinity and the true Trinity can look remarkably
alike. That's not an accident; it's the strategy. And it's exactly why this chapter is such
important material for a community that already takes discernment of spirits seriously: the
danger was never going to be something obviously grotesque. The danger is a counterfeit
convincing enough to be mistaken for the real thing.

## Naming what's genuinely appealing about it

It's worth being honest that the text doesn't present this system as merely repulsive. Look
again at what it offers: astonishing power that silences the world's mockery of faith
(13:4), a gospel the whole world accepts without resistance (13:8), visible signs and wonders
(13:13), apparent healing and life restored to what was dead (13:14-15), and a God who deals
decisively with evildoers instead of letting them run free (13:15). Each of these touches
something we actually long for — not wanting our faith to look foolish, wanting our prayers to
be visibly powerful, wanting healing for people we love, wanting evil finally dealt with.

The problem was never that these desires are wrong. The problem is the method. The first beast
gives his "life" not out of love but to inspire fear. It doesn't build up the church; it attacks
believers and coerces compliance through economic threat and violence — no one can "buy or sell"
without submitting first. That is the tell. God's power always moves through love, even when it
looks weak; the counterfeit always moves through intimidation, even when it looks strong.

## Who can actually tell the difference

The chapter itself asks: how could anyone — even people within the church — be taken in by
something the text so plainly calls demonic? The answer is in the description itself: "it had
two horns like a lamb, but it spoke like a dragon" (13:11). The appearance matches; the voice
doesn't. And Jesus tells us plainly who can catch that mismatch: "my sheep listen to my voice...
they will never follow a stranger" (John 10:4-5, 27). In Hebrew thought, hearing isn't passive —
it means obeying. So the only real safeguard is a church that stays close enough to the real
voice of Jesus, through worship, prayer, and Scripture, to recognize immediately when something
else is speaking with borrowed authority.

That cuts both directions, and it's worth saying plainly: the answer to this chapter is not to
grow suspicious of miracles, signs, healing, or victory as such. God still does all of these
today, and Revelation itself is full of a Lamb who conquers. What the beast can never actually
copy — no matter how convincing the imitation gets — is love that gives itself away rather than
demanding submission through fear. Whenever the church itself resorts to fear, manipulation, or
coercion to grow or to enforce compliance, it isn't following the Lamb, no matter how
spiritually impressive the results look. That, finally, is the test this whole chapter hands
us: not "how powerful does this look," but "does this look like a Lamb who was slain, or a beast
that demands worship through force?"
