# The Four Horsemen Unmasked

## A controversial rider

When the Lamb opens the first four seals, four horsemen ride out — conquest, war, famine, and
death. Three of them are plainly destructive from the moment they appear. But the first rider
has caused more disagreement among careful readers than almost any other figure in Revelation,
because at first glance he looks like he might be good. Working out who he is will sharpen a
skill this tradition already values highly: discernment of spirits — telling what genuinely
comes from God apart from what only wears his colors.

## The case for a good rider

There's a real case to be made that the first horseman is Christ or the gospel going forth:

- Psalm 45 describes a king conquering with a bow, a passage the New Testament applies to
  Jesus (Hebrews 1:8).
- Later in Revelation, someone "like a son of man" rides a cloud with a sickle (14:14), and a
  victorious rider on a white horse wears many crowns (19:11) — both clearly Jesus.
- The word "conquering" is used of Jesus and the saints elsewhere in the book.
- White is consistently a positive color throughout Revelation.
- Unlike the other three riders, this one does nothing explicitly violent himself — he simply
  "conquers," which some read as the spread of the gospel ahead of coming judgment (compare
  Mark 13:10).

## The case against

But the case on the other side is just as strong, and ultimately more persuasive:

- God strikes the bow from the hands of Gog and Magog, the end-time enemy, in Ezekiel 39:3 —
  associating the bow with opposition to God, not with him.
- Jesus isn't the only crowned figure in Revelation; the demonic locusts of chapter 9 wear
  crowns too.
- "Conquering" elsewhere describes the beast overpowering the saints (13:7, 11:7) — not only
  Christ's victory.
- The four horsemen, like the four horses and chariots in Zechariah that stand behind this
  image, function as a single unit. And within Revelation itself, the first four trumpets and
  first four bowls also form matching sets. If the fourth horseman (Death) is evil, and the
  four are meant to be read together the way the source material in Zechariah clearly intends,
  the first three almost certainly are too.
- Jesus' own end-times teaching, in Matthew 24, Mark 13, and Luke 21, opens every time with the
  same warning, in the same order: watch out for deception, then war, then famine — precisely
  the sequence of the first three horsemen.

## The resolution: a counterfeit Christ

Put together, the first horseman isn't Christ at all. He's a counterfeit — someone deliberately
made to resemble Christ's own coming victory, right down to the crown and the white horse,
while bringing exactly the opposite of what Jesus brings. He rides out promising conquest, the
same word used of genuine victory elsewhere in the book, but seven verses later leaves nothing
but war, injustice, and death in his wake. The promise is total; the delivery is devastation.

This should sound familiar to anyone who has spent time thinking carefully about spiritual
discernment. Scripture is blunt about this pattern: "Satan himself masquerades as an angel of
light" (2 Corinthians 11:14). The most dangerous counterfeits are never the obviously ugly
ones — they're the ones dressed convincingly like the real thing. A church that already knows
to test what looks impressive against the character and fruit of the real Jesus is exactly the
church equipped to catch this. The question was never "does this look powerful and victorious?"
The question is "does this actually produce what Jesus produces?"

## Naming the pattern

Zoom out, and this scene is the first appearance of a pattern that recurs across the whole
book: the devil shows up not as one obviously evil figure but as a counterfeit trinity,
mimicking the true one point for point. Here it's four horsemen where the fourth ("Death")
sums up the other three — an echo of a three-in-one structure. Later, in chapter 13, it will be
a dragon and two beasts, one of which performs signs and wonders precisely to make a counterfeit
look convincing. Learning to unmask the first horseman here is practice for unmasking the beasts
later.

And notice where this scene falls: right after Jesus has just been declared the only one worthy
to open the scroll (chapter 5), the devil's counterfeit shows up immediately in response — as
if in answer to the challenge. That's instructive. The appearance of a counterfeit isn't a sign
that something has gone wrong with God's plan; it's often a sign that God's plan has just been
publicly affirmed, and the enemy is scrambling to offer an imitation.

## What this means for us

No matter how convincing the devil's offer looks — victory without a cross, conquest without
cost, power without love — don't take it. The first horseman teaches us to ask a better
question than "does this look like Jesus?" Ask instead: "does this act like Jesus?" Does it
give life or take it? Does it build up the vulnerable or profit from their suffering? True
discernment isn't cynicism toward every powerful or impressive thing we encounter — it's
asking whether what we're seeing actually bears the fruit of the Lamb who was slain, or only
borrows his crown.
