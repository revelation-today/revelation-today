# The First Horseman's Fake Gospel

## A rider who doesn't seem to belong

Revelation 6 gives us four horsemen, and three of them are easy to place. Red brings war. Black
brings famine and the kind of economic injustice where luxury goods stay untouched while bread
becomes unaffordable for the poor. Pale brings death. But the first rider, on the white horse,
has confused readers for centuries — because on the surface, he looks like good news.

{{% bible val="'I looked, and there before me was a white horse! Its rider held a bow, and he was given a crown, and he rode out as a conqueror bent on conquest.'" link="rev:6,2" lang="en" %}}

A crown. A white horse — the same color John uses elsewhere for Jesus himself. A rider who
"conquers," the same word used of the saints' own victory. No obvious violence attached to him,
unlike the riders who follow. It's no wonder some readers have wanted to read this figure as
Christ, or as the advance of the gospel before the hard years begin.

But look closer, and the picture doesn't hold. God strips the bow from the hands of Gog and
Magog, the archetypal end-time enemies, in Ezekiel — the bow is their weapon, not a hero's. The
demonic locust-army later in Revelation also wears "something like crowns." And the word
"conquer" gets used just as often for the beast conquering the saints as for the saints
conquering the beast. Most tellingly, all four horsemen are summoned the same way, respond to
the same call, and belong to the same set — a unity confirmed by the matching four winds, four
trumpets, and four bowls elsewhere in the book. If the last three riders are trouble, the first
one is too. He simply arrives first, and arrives look like a gift.

## The counterfeit that comes before the cost

Here's what resolves the mystery: this is a deceiver dressed as a conqueror, and he rides out
immediately after Revelation 5, where Jesus alone was found worthy to open the scroll. The devil's
answer to that scene is to send out his own rival version — offering victory, security, and
"conquest" with none of the substance behind it. Jesus conquered by dying as a slaughtered Lamb.
This rider conquers by simply showing up with a bow and a crown, no cross required. It is the
oldest trick there is: Scripture's very first bow appears in the hands of Jacob's father-in-law
Esau, but the deeper echo is the very first deception, where the offer to "be like God" required
no obedience and no cost. The white horse promises Christ's outcome — victory, blessing, a crown
— while skipping the one thing that makes Christ's victory real: the cross.

That's why the order matters so much. First comes the promise: conquest, without qualification.
Then comes the sword — persecution of anyone who won't buy in. Then hunger and injustice for the
poor, a kind of economic pressure aimed at nonconformists. And finally, death sums up where the
whole sequence leads. The pattern tracks so closely with the beasts of Revelation 13 — the first
beast makes extravagant promises, the second beast enforces compliance economically — that this
horseman functions as their forerunner. He is the "fake gospel," arriving early, dressed
convincingly, asking nothing of you but belief in the offer.

## Why this matters for prosperity teaching

This is the clearest single image in the whole book for a very old and very human temptation:
the offer of blessing detached from the cross. Health-and-wealth teaching, at its most
sympathetic, comes from a real and reasonable place — people who have known genuine poverty,
genuine sickness, genuine desperation, reaching for a God who promises to provide. That desire
is not the problem. Scripture itself promises that God provides for his people, and there's
nothing spiritual about pretending poverty is automatically holy or that God is indifferent to
suffering.

But the first horseman's promise isn't "God provides." It's "conquest without cost" — a crown
that arrives before the wound, security that requires no cross, a message so close to the real
gospel that a first-century reader could momentarily mistake the horse's color for Christ's own.
That's exactly the shape of any teaching that says enough faith, the right confession, or the
right seed of financial giving guarantees health and wealth as a kind of formula — as though the
Lamb's victory were a technique to be applied rather than a person to be trusted, and as though
his followers were promised a crown with no cost attached to wearing it. Revelation's warning is
not that wealth is inherently evil, or that health is something to avoid wanting. Its warning is
that any offer of blessing that never asks you to take up a cross is not the real thing, no
matter how good the horse looks or how quickly it seems to answer your prayer.

## What to actually watch for

The horsemen don't arrive with warning labels. That's the entire point of the image: the first
rider looks like good news, right up until you notice what rides in right behind him. So the
practical question this passage puts to us is not "do I believe God blesses his people?" — of
course he does — but "what is being asked of me to receive this blessing, and what happens to my
faith in God when the blessing doesn't come?" A gospel that only works when the bank account
grows is not stronger than the real gospel; it's more fragile, because it has nothing to say
when the seal breaks and the second, third, and fourth riders show up, as Revelation promises
they eventually will for everyone, in one form or another.

The comfort in this passage isn't given right away — the martyrs under the altar in the very
next scene are still crying "how long?" But the comfort is coming, and it isn't found in
outrunning the horsemen. It's found in already belonging, before they ever ride, to the Lamb who
was worthy to open the scroll in the first place — the one whose crown genuinely did cost him
everything, and who is trustworthy precisely because it did.
