# 666: Whose Security Are You Actually Buying?

## Let's retire the decoder ring

No number in the Bible has launched more guesswork than 666. Assign letters a numeric value,
tally up a name, and — with enough flexibility about spelling, language, and which of a
person's names you count — you can make almost anyone come out to 666. That flexibility is
exactly the problem. A lock that opens with every key isn't a lock at all, and a method that can
implicate any public figure history offers up isn't telling you anything real about the beast.
Revelation itself tells us where to look instead: not outside the text, hunting through
headlines, but inside it, in a story its first readers already knew how to read.

## An imitation Trinity, graded

Chapter 13 shows us a dragon and two beasts running a deliberate counterfeit of Father, Son, and
Spirit — three players imitating three, right down to a false resurrection and a false spirit of
counsel. The number 666 is best read as their report card, graded against the very things the
real Trinity actually does:

- The dragon claims all authority the way God does — but his power is only ever *given* to him,
  and only for a limited, appointed time. Score: 6.
- The first beast wants credit for a sacrificial death, mimicking the Lamb who was actually
  slain — but his own "death" is faked; only one of his heads was ever struck. Score: 6.
- The second beast wants to counsel and draw people in the way the Spirit does — but he works
  purely by fear and coercion, nothing like invitation. Score: 6.

Three sixes: not a secret code, but a failing grade, three times over, on the three points where
the imitation actually needed to hold up. If you already know what the real thing looks like —
real authority freely held, a real death freely given, a real Spirit who invites rather than
coerces — the counterfeit falls apart in your hands. That's the whole warning: stay in the real
thing, and you cannot be fooled by the fake.

## Three case studies the text points us to

The chapter also nudges us toward three specific stories that flesh out what 666 costs the
people who chase it.

**Daniel's friends in the furnace.** Nebuchadnezzar built a golden statue, deliberately shaped to
proportions built around the number six, and commanded everyone to bow to it or be executed.
Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused, and their answer is worth memorizing: "the God we
serve is able to save us... but even if he does not, we want you to know, Your Majesty, that we
will not serve your gods." That's the real shape of faith under pressure — not a guarantee that
God will always intervene visibly, but a trust that holds regardless. The beast's mark works the
same way as the statue: comply, or lose the ability to buy and sell. Real faithfulness doesn't
require God to always show up in the way we'd script it. It requires us to stay faithful either
way — and in Daniel's story, God does show up, standing with them in the fire itself. We can be
just as sure Jesus stands with us.

**Solomon's 666.** First Kings tells us, almost in passing, that Solomon received exactly 666
talents of gold every year — a suspiciously precise number in a chapter where every other figure
is rounded. It arrives right at the peak of his story, one chapter before his catastrophic
downfall. Solomon had started well: offered wealth and power, he asked God instead for wisdom —
and because he asked for the right thing, God gave him the rest as well. But over time, the gift
quietly outgrew the relationship with the Giver. He came to love what God had given him more
than he loved God, and that, not any single decision, was what finally pulled him away.

**Trajan, the "good emperor."** Under Trajan's rule, Rome reached its greatest extent, funded a
genuine welfare program for the poor, built roads and aqueducts, and staged public games — every
mark of a golden age. He was also the emperor who enforced compliance with the imperial cult and
had the blood of Christians who refused it on his hands. Prosperity and cruelty sat comfortably
together in the same reign. Blessing, even real, generous, well-administered blessing, is not
proof of who is actually being served.

## The direct question for health-and-wealth teaching

Put these three side by side and a single, uncomfortable question emerges: wealth and security,
even when they're real, are not evidence of whose side you're on. Solomon's gold didn't prove
his heart was right with God — it eventually revealed the opposite. Trajan's prosperity didn't
make his empire godly. The devil's whole trinity is a spectacular failure precisely because it
counterfeits the appearance of divine blessing without any of its substance.

This is the direct, honest challenge health-and-wealth teaching needs to sit with: financial
increase is not a scoreboard for spiritual standing, in either direction. It is not proof God is
pleased with you when it comes, and its absence is not proof he isn't. The temptation named here
isn't wanting security or provision — it's the "attractive compromise," telling ourselves we're
still following God while quietly arranging to have the best of both God and the world, exactly
the arrangement that eventually cost Solomon everything that mattered. If a teaching promises
that enough faith or the right giving formula guarantees wealth as proof of favor, it is asking
you to trust the blessing rather than the One who gives it — the very substitution 666 exposes
as a failing grade, however impressive the counterfeit looks on the surface.

## Not afraid of the number, free from what it worships

None of this should leave you anxious about barcodes or microchips — as the underlying study
notes wryly point out, there is no hidden technology left to fear that your phone, your search
history, and your debit card haven't already made possible. The number was never really a puzzle
to be cracked. It's a diagnosis: whatever promises total security while asking you to trade
faithfulness for it will always fail its own test, three times over. The real question 666 puts
in front of us isn't "what does the number mean," but "who do I actually trust when the wealth
comes, and who do I still trust if it doesn't?" Solomon's God, the same God who stood in the
furnace with three faithful friends, is worth trusting either way.
