## Opening: A Deal Too Good to Refuse

Start your group with a simple thought experiment. Imagine someone offered your family this
deal: guaranteed safety, a growing economy, a stable legal system, freedom from major war on
your own soil — and the only thing it asks in return is that you publicly acknowledge who's
responsible for all of it, in a way that costs you nothing to say out loud. Ask the group:
would you take that deal? Most of us instinctively would. Now tell them: that deal had a name
two thousand years ago, and it's the exact world Revelation was written into. Understanding it
is not a history lesson to get through before the "real" content starts — it's the key that
unlocks nearly every symbol in the book.

## The Core Teaching

**What the Pax Romana actually was.** From roughly 27 BC into the late second century AD, Rome
enjoyed an extended period of relative internal stability: few civil wars, a strong and unified
empire, real economic prosperity (at least for a good portion of the population), and a
flourishing of art and culture — set against a backdrop of sporadic, localized persecution of
Christians under emperors like Nero and Domitian. For the full argument and sourcing, see
`expl/background/history/pax-romana-key-to-understand-the-book-of-revelation.md`.

Underneath those conditions sat something closer to a theology than a policy: the gods had
chosen Rome; the emperor was their agent on earth; every blessing worth having — security,
peace, justice, fertility, wealth — flowed through submission to Rome's rule; and therefore the
emperor himself was worthy of worship and titles equal to a god's. This wasn't a fringe idea
confined to court flatterers. It was broadcast constantly and everywhere: on coinage, in public
statuary, at festivals and games, in shared meals where a toast to the emperor's health was
simply what one did. Cities in Asia Minor — precisely where the seven churches of Revelation
stood — competed with each other to build temples to the emperor, hoping to win his favor or a
tax break in return.

**Naming a place where the source material needed a correction.** The accuracy review of this
article flagged the original list of Rome's pro-imperial propagandists — it named the Stoic
philosopher Epictetus alongside the poet Virgil as an example of literary support for the
regime. That's worth correcting plainly: Epictetus was a former slave whose Stoic teaching
centered on inner freedom and indifference to external power, including political power — he
is not a figure who glorified Rome or the emperor cult, and in some ways his philosophy gave
people resources to stay detached from exactly this kind of imperial claim. If you want a
genuine example of pro-Roman propaganda alongside Virgil's Aeneid, look instead to Horace's
Carmen Saeculare, or to the visual program stamped across imperial coinage and monuments like
the Ara Pacis. The corrected point stands: Rome's message was inescapable, delivered through
art, architecture, and public ritual alike — it's just that Epictetus isn't a good example of
who was delivering it.

**The dilemma this created for Christians.** Jesus, not the emperor, was their true king — and
the very word used for the gospel message, *euangelion*, was Rome's own term for the
proclamation of a new emperor's reign. That word choice alone was a quiet act of political
defiance. Christians looked to Jesus, not Caesar, for provision (see {{% bible val="Matthew 6:25-34" link="mat:6,25-34" lang="en" %}}), and some ordinary features of civic life under
Pax Romana — ritual meals with divine toasts, festival practices tied to the imperial cult —
were flatly incompatible with worshiping one God alone. That left three real options: renounce
the faith outright and blend in; live it out fully and risk social exclusion, economic ruin,
imprisonment, or death; or find a middle path — confess Caesar with the mouth while keeping
Jesus in the heart, outwardly compliant, inwardly faithful. Jesus had already ruled out the
comfortable middle: "{{% bible val="No one can serve two masters" link="mat:6,24" lang="en" %}}."

That third option — the "convenient compromise" — is precisely the temptation Revelation keeps
returning to, and it's why the whole book reads like counter-propaganda aimed at Pax Romana's
version of the good life: a rival account of who really secures your life, who really deserves
your worship, and what the "golden age" actually cost the people it wasn't golden for.

**One more layer worth surfacing for a small group with time to go deeper.** The dating of
Revelation matters here. Most scholars place the book's writing under Domitian in the 90s AD —
after the Jerusalem temple's destruction in AD 70 — which means the "temple activities" some
sources describe Jews continuing to practice under Roman toleration would need to mean
something like synagogue worship and dietary law, not literal sacrifices at a temple that no
longer existed. (A minority position dates Revelation earlier, before AD 70, under Nero — worth
flagging as a live scholarly question rather than settling in passing.) Similarly, be precise
about persecution: systematic, empire-wide persecution requiring universal sacrifice
certificates didn't begin until Emperor Decius's edict in 250 AD — not 253, a small but real
date correction from the source article. Before that, persecution under Nero, Domitian, and
Trajan was real but localized and sporadic, which actually sharpens the point: John's first
readers weren't (yet) facing empire-wide extermination. They were facing something more like
low-grade, constant social and economic pressure to conform — arguably the harder temptation to
resist, because it rarely demanded a dramatic, one-time choice.

**Original-language note.** The Greek word for "gospel," *euangelion* (εὐαγγέλιον), was standard
vocabulary for the imperial announcement of a new Caesar's accession or a military victory. When
Mark opens his gospel with "the *euangelion* of Jesus Christ," and when Revelation's whole
structure functions as counter-propaganda to Rome's story, the word itself is already making a
political claim: there is a new emperor, and it isn't the one on the coins.

## A Discussion Question Worth Sitting With

Ask your group directly: where is our own "Pax Romana" today? For congregations comfortable in
Western nations, this question can be more uncomfortable than it first sounds — not because
national stability or prosperity are bad things (they're not; gratitude for them is right), but
because the Pax Romana question was never "is this good?" It was always "who gets the credit,
and what does that credit demand of you?" This isn't an accusation. It's an invitation to notice
something the compromised churches in Revelation — Sardis, Laodicea — never noticed about
themselves until Jesus said it plainly.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. What specific pressures did the "third way" of outward conformity actually involve for a
   first-century Christian in Ephesus or Smyrna — socially, economically, religiously?
2. Why does John's choice of the word *euangelion* function as a political statement, not just
   a religious one?
3. Why does it matter whether Revelation was written before or after AD 70 for how we read its
   comments about Jewish "temple activities"?

**So what for us:**
4. Where do you find yourself expecting security, provision, or your sense of a "good life"
   from something other than God — career, nation, financial cushion, cultural approval?
5. Where have you taken the "third way" recently — said or implied something publicly that
   didn't match what you actually believe, just to avoid friction?
6. If your convictions cost you something real this year — professionally, socially, within
   your own family — would you still hold them? What would that actually look like in practice?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Invite the group to pray honestly, not performatively, about one specific place where comfort
or security has quietly become a stand-in for trust in God. Close by asking Jesus — the true
king who has already secured the victory, not through force but through the cross — to make
that trust real and visible this week, not just in a crisis, but in the ordinary decisions of a
comfortable life.
