## Opening hook

Show your group a picture of a lighthouse or a string of string-lights, or simply ask: "What's the difference between a light that generates its own power and a light plugged into the wall?" Hold that image loosely — we'll come back to it. Tonight's passage (Revelation 1:9-20) is the vision that launches the whole book, and its central question is exactly that: is the church a light source, or a light that depends entirely on something outside itself?

## Core teaching

John identifies himself first, not as an untouchable prophet, but as a fellow sufferer: "your brother and companion in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus" (v. 9), writing from exile on Patmos. Whatever vision follows, it comes to someone already paying the cost of following Jesus — that matters for how we receive it.

The vision itself has three movements: what John sees (vv. 12-16), how he reacts (v. 17a), and how it's explained to him (vv. 17b-20).

**What John sees.** Seven golden lampstands, and "one like a son of man" walking among them. The lampstands almost certainly draw on Zechariah 4, where seven lamps represent the Holy Spirit's presence and power — though at this point in the text their exact identity is still held back, to be revealed later. The central figure combines two Old Testament images that first-century readers of Daniel would have caught immediately:

- **"One like a son of man"** — straight from Daniel 7:13, the figure who approaches the Ancient of Days and receives an everlasting kingdom. Jesus applied this title to himself throughout the Gospels.
- **White hair, eyes like blazing fire, feet like burnished bronze, a voice like rushing waters** — these details actually merge two different Daniel figures. The "white hair" recalls the Ancient of Days (God the Father) from Daniel 7:9. The blazing eyes and bronze feet come from Daniel 10:5-6, describing the angelic messenger who prepares Daniel for his final vision.

**A word of caution on sourcing:** some editions of this teaching cite Daniel 10:7 for that physical description — but 10:7 actually describes the terrified bystanders who didn't see the vision, not the angel's appearance itself. The description of blazing eyes and bronze limbs is in Daniel 10:5-6. Small point, but worth getting right if you go back to the text yourself (see expl/content/vision/the-vision.md).

The effect of merging these images: John's vision of Jesus fuses the Ancient of Days and the "son of man" figure into one — a striking, understated claim about who Jesus is. He also wears something like both priestly and royal clothing, an ambiguity the text seems to leave deliberately open, since Jesus holds both offices.

**John's response.** He collapses, "as though dead" (v. 17) — the same reaction Daniel had centuries earlier when confronted with an angelic vision (Daniel 10:8-19). Overwhelming holiness produces this response consistently across Scripture; it isn't a sign of weak faith but of an accurate read on who's in the room.

**The interpretation.** Jesus reassures John, repeating the identity claims from verse 5 ("I am the First and the Last, the Living One..."), and then explains the mystery: the seven stars are the angels (messengers) of the seven churches, and the seven lampstands are the seven churches themselves (v. 20). Both images share two things in common — both are sources of light, and both are entirely dependent on something outside themselves: the stars only shine while held in Jesus' hand; the lampstand only stays lit while filled with oil, a picture of the Holy Spirit.

That's the whole point of the vision, and arguably of the whole book: **the church exists to be light for the world — but only ever as a light that depends on Jesus, never as a light generating its own power.** Every letter to the seven churches that follows is measured against this one standard.

## Discussion questions

1. Which detail of the vision (the lampstands, the son-of-man figure, the merged Ancient-of-Days imagery, John's collapse) strikes you most, and why?
2. What does it mean that Jesus is described using imagery for *both* God the Father (Daniel 7's Ancient of Days) and the exalted human figure (Daniel 7's son of man)? What does this passage claim about who Jesus is?
3. The lampstand only stays lit while filled with oil — the Holy Spirit. Where in your own life, or your church's life, do you see the difference between "generating your own light" and "staying filled"?
4. John falls down "as though dead" when he sees Jesus in full glory. When was the last time you were struck by the sheer weight of who Jesus actually is, rather than a comfortable, familiar mental picture of him?
5. If every one of the seven letters that follow will measure a church by "are you being light, dependent on Jesus" — what would that measurement say about your own small group or church right now?

## Closing prayer prompt

Ask God to give your group a fresh, undomesticated picture of Jesus this week — not less comforting, but bigger and more overwhelming than the version we've gotten used to. Pray specifically that you would recognize where you've been trying to shine on your own strength, and ask to be refilled by the Spirit rather than running on empty.
