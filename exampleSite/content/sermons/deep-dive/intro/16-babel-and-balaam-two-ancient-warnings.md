## Opening Hook

Ask your group which is scarier for a church: outright persecution, or a really good party?
Most people instinctively say persecution. Tonight's session argues the opposite might be true —
that Scripture actually treats seduction into compromise as the more dangerous threat, using two
very different Old Testament stories that Revelation keeps calling back to by name: Babel and
Balaam.

## The Core Teaching

This session draws on `expl/bible/keyword/the-origin-of-babel.md` and
`expl/bible/keyword/the-story-of-balaam.md` — closing out our Part 0 introduction to Revelation
with two Old Testament warnings the book depends on directly.

**Babel: humanity's attempt to build a name for itself.** After the flood, humanity settles in
one place and undertakes a massive building project with two explicit goals: to make a name for
themselves, and to have a central rallying point so they wouldn't be scattered
({{% bible val="Genesis 11:1-9" link="gen:11,1-9" lang="en" %}}). God's response is almost
mocking in its understatement — He "comes down" to see a tower that was supposed to reach the
heavens, a deliberate irony given how unimpressive it must have looked from His actual vantage
point. Neither of Babel's goals is achieved; the opposite happens on both counts. They wanted a
name for themselves; God instead goes on to make a name out of Abraham. They wanted to avoid
being scattered; scattering is exactly what results when God confuses their language — a rupture
only reversed later, at Pentecost, when the Spirit enables people from every nation to understand
one message in their own tongue.

**The Babel-Daniel-Revelation connection.** When Israel goes into exile centuries later, the text
specifically notes that Babylon stood in the land of Shinar — the very location of the original
tower ({{% bible val="Genesis 11:2" link="gen:11,1-9" lang="en" %}}). That's a deliberate literary
link, not a coincidence: pride is the common thread running through both empires, and God cuts
short both of their reigns — Daniel's Babylon lasted less than a century before Cyrus, whom God
Himself raised up for the purpose, brought it down. Revelation's own Babylon (a major theme we'll
spend real time on much later in this series) shares these same features deliberately: pride, a
swift and sudden fall, and God's people living as exiles within it. Babel isn't simply an ancient
story; it's the pattern name Revelation gives to every human system that tries to build security
and significance apart from God.

**Balaam: the deadlier threat of seduction rather than opposition.** Jesus Himself names this
story directly as a live danger for the church: "{{% bible val="there are some among you who hold to the teaching of Balaam, who taught Balak to entice the Israelites to sin so that they ate food sacrificed to idols and committed sexual immorality" link="rev:2,14" lang="en" %}}." The
background: Israel's military victories leave Moab terrified enough to seek Midianite help,
even though Moab had a standing promise that Israel wouldn't invade them
({{% bible val="Deuteronomy 2:8-9" link="deu:2,8-9" lang="en" %}}) and Midian had every reason to
expect goodwill, given Moses's own marriage into a Midianite priestly family. Fear overrides all
of that. Balak sends for Balaam to curse Israel; God flatly forbids it
({{% bible val="Numbers 22:8-14" link="num:22,8-14" lang="en" %}}); Balaam, remarkably, asks
again anyway, as though repeating the question might change the answer — a small but telling
detail about the danger of asking God the same question over and over hoping for a more
convenient answer. God lets him go this time, with real consequences along the way (the donkey
incident). Balaam ends up blessing Israel exactly as God intends, and Balak sends him away in
fury. The story appears to be over.

**It isn't.** Immediately afterward, the Moabites and Midianites throw a feast for Israel that
results in sexual immorality and idolatry at Baal-Peor
({{% bible val="Numbers 25:1-2" link="num:25,1-2" lang="en" %}}), triggering a plague that kills
about 24,000 Israelites, stopped only by Phinehas's intervention. Only later do we learn the
connection: Balaam, unable to curse Israel directly because God stood with them, engineered this
seduction instead — driving a wedge between God and His people by luring them into compromise,
accomplishing through enticement what he couldn't accomplish through opposition
({{% bible val="Numbers 31:16" link="num:31,16" lang="en" %}}). His own pride brought about his
downfall — he's later killed among the Midianites he'd been secretly staying with
({{% bible val="Numbers 31:8" link="num:31,8" lang="en" %}}) — but it was Moses's own hesitancy in
executing full judgment that contributed to how many Israelites were lost in the meantime. A
small citation correction here: the claim that "Israel kills all the Midianite men" is properly
grounded in {{% bible val="Numbers 31:7" link="num:31,7" lang="en" %}}, not 31:2, which is God's
prior command rather than the report of it being carried out.

**Why Revelation names Balaam specifically, and why it matters for a comfortable audience.**
Revelation addresses the Pergamon church directly with this exact charge (a session we'll spend
real time on later in this series). The pattern Balaam represents — unable to attack God's people
through force, so attacking through seduction into comfortable compromise instead — is precisely
the danger a stable, prosperous, unpersecuted church is most exposed to. It's a much harder
danger to see coming than outright hostility, because it doesn't feel like an attack. It feels
like a party.

## A Question Worth Sitting With

If Balaam's strategy is seduction rather than confrontation, what does that suggest about where
the real spiritual danger lies for a comfortable, prosperous congregation that faces little to no
outward opposition? This isn't a rhetorical trap — it's worth letting your group actually answer
concretely, naming specific "feasts" (cultural, economic, social) that function the same way
Baal-Peor did for Israel: not obviously hostile, even enjoyable, and quietly corrosive to
covenant faithfulness.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Why does God's response to Babel — mockingly "coming down" to see a tower that was supposed
   to reach heaven — matter for how we read the story's tone, not just its plot?
2. Balaam asked God the same question twice, hoping for a different answer the second time. What
   does that detail teach about the danger of repeatedly asking God to bless something He's
   already said no to?
3. Why was seduction (the Baal-Peor feast) more effective against Israel than Balaam's attempted
   curse? What does that suggest about which kinds of threats Scripture treats as most
   dangerous?

**So what for us:**
4. Where do you see people (or churches) today trying to "make a name for themselves" the way
   Babel's builders did — building security, reputation, or identity apart from God?
5. Name a specific "Baal-Peor feast" in your own cultural context — something that doesn't feel
   like an attack on your faith, might even feel enjoyable or harmless, but that quietly draws
   you toward compromise over time.
6. Moses's hesitancy in fully carrying out God's command contributed to more loss of life, not
   less. Where might a hesitance to name and confront compromise directly — in your own life or
   your church — actually be causing more harm than a clearer, earlier response would have?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Ask God for discernment to recognize seduction into compromise for what it is, especially when it
arrives disguised as celebration, opportunity, or simple belonging rather than obvious hostility.
Pray specifically for your group's ability to name your own "Baal-Peor" moments honestly with
each other, and thank God that He remains faithful to His people even when, like Israel at
Baal-Peor, they've genuinely failed Him.

## Closing Note for Part 0

This concludes the sixteen-session introduction to Revelation. Everything from here forward —
the letters to the seven churches, the throne room, the seals and trumpets and bowls, the
harlot and the Bride — builds directly on the foundation these sessions laid: the world John
wrote into, the kind of book this is, its hidden architecture and numerical design, its Old
Testament story of covenant and Israel and family, and the Daniel and Exodus patterns Revelation
assumes its readers already carry with them. Take a session, if your group has room for it, to
simply reflect together on what's shifted in how you expect to read the rest of the book before
moving into Part 1.
