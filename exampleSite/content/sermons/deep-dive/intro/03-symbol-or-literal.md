## Opening Hook

Ask your group: has anyone ever seen a chart mapping the beast of Revelation 13 to a real,
living politician, or a specific barcode system, or a particular world government? Most groups
will have encountered at least one version of this. Don't mock it — the impulse comes from
taking the text seriously, which is exactly right. But ask: what if the text itself gives us
signals about how it wants to be read, and those signals point somewhere different than a
current-events decoder ring? That's what this session is about: learning to read Revelation's
own vocabulary for how it tells us to interpret it.

## The Core Teaching

The fullest version of this argument is in `expl/background/literature/literally-or-symbolic.md`
— worth reading in full if your group wants to trace the Greek argument in detail.

**There's no one-size-fits-all rule.** Something can be symbolic and still literally happen —
the Nile turning to blood in the Exodus plagues was a real, physical event *and* a symbolic
judgment on the Nile as a source of Egyptian life and worship. A literal historical fact can
also become a lasting symbol in its own right: the twelve actual sons of Jacob became the
enduring symbol of God's chosen people. So the question "is this symbol or literal?" is often
the wrong question. The better question is: what is this passage doing, and what clues has the
author left us?

**The Daniel connection.** Revelation deliberately echoes the language that frames
{{% bible val="Daniel 2" link="dan:2,28-45" lang="en" %}} — a vision that pictures empires
symbolically, as a statue destroyed by a stone, itself another symbol. Daniel 2 is the only
prophetic book to use the word "mystery," and that word appears three times in Revelation too:
the mystery of the {{% bible val="church" link="rev:1,20" lang="en" %}}, the mystery of
{{% bible val="God" link="rev:10,7" lang="en" %}}, and the mystery of the
{{% bible val="beast" link="rev:17,5" lang="en" %}} (we'll come back to all three in session 6).
Revelation's opening formula, "to show what things must take place quickly," directly echoes
Daniel's own opening and closing language — a signal that John wants us reading his book the
way we'd read Daniel's: symbolically, not as a literal newsreel.

**The key original-language argument: *deiknumi* and *semaino*.** Revelation 1:1 says God
"made it known (*semaino*) by sending his angel" and gave it "to show (*deiknumi*) his servants
what must soon take place." These two Greek verbs carry real weight.

- ***Semaino*** (σημαίνω) is the same word used in the Greek Old Testament for the interpretation
  of a symbolic vision in Daniel. It appears only five times in the whole New Testament — three
  of those describing, symbolically, the nature of Jesus's death on the cross
  ({{% bible val="John 12:33" link="jhn:12,33" lang="en" %}},
  {{% bible val="18:32" link="jhn:18,32" lang="en" %}},
  {{% bible val="21:19" link="jhn:21,19" lang="en" %}}). Notably, John had a plainer word
  available for simply "announce" or "make known" — *gnorizo* — and didn't use it. That word
  choice is a deliberate signal toward symbolic communication, not a straightforward
  announcement.
- ***Deiknumi*** (δείκνυμι) appears throughout the book paired with visions John "sees" and then
  interprets — the throne room in {{% bible val="Revelation 4:1" link="rev:4,1" lang="en" %}},
  the harlot in {{% bible val="17:1" link="rev:17,1" lang="en" %}}, the New Jerusalem in
  {{% bible val="21:9-10" link="rev:21,9-10" lang="en" %}}. It's vision-language, not
  news-report language.

Taken together, these two verbs point toward a default assumption: read Revelation
symbolically wherever the text isn't clearly speaking of a literal, plain event. And indeed the
book is full of things obviously meant as symbols — a lamb, a dragon, a beast with heads and
horns, a sword coming out of a mouth. Nobody in your group thinks Jesus literally has a sword
protruding from his face; we intuitively read that symbolically already. The invitation is to
extend that same instinct consistently across the book, rather than switching to a literal
reading whenever the symbol seems to map onto something we recognize in our own time.

**A place where the underlying source needed a small correction.** One example in the original
article, connecting the feeding of the 5,000 to "spiritual food," cited
{{% bible val="Matthew 16:5-12" link="mat:16,5-12" lang="en" %}} — but that passage is actually
Jesus's later "leaven of the Pharisees" warning, which merely references the feeding miracles in
passing. The feeding narrative itself, and the "bread of life" theme it develops, is better
found in {{% bible val="Matthew 14:13-21" link="mat:14,13-21" lang="en" %}} or, more fully, in
{{% bible val="John 6" link="jhn:6,1-14" lang="en" %}}. Small correction, but worth modeling for
your group: even careful, well-sourced material benefits from double-checking citations against
the text itself — that habit is part of what this deep-dive format is for.

**Why this matters practically.** If Revelation defaults to symbolic reading anchored in its own
first-century world (Rome, the emperor cult, the seven churches' real struggles), then the
temptation to match its symbols to a modern nation, a modern leader, or a modern technology
isn't just a minor misstep — it runs directly against the grain of the book's own stated
purpose. That doesn't drain Revelation of relevance for us; if anything, it means the message
is more durable, because it isn't tied to guessing which century gets to be "the" fulfillment.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Why does the author's choice of *semaino* over the plainer *gnorizo* function as evidence for
   a symbolic reading?
2. How does the Nile-to-blood plague model the idea that something can be both a real historical
   event and a symbol at the same time?
3. What does it mean that "mystery" occurs in Revelation exactly three times, echoing Daniel 2's
   unique use of the same term?

**So what for us:**
4. Have you ever encountered (or made) a confident claim matching a Revelation symbol to a
   current event or public figure? What happened when that prediction didn't pan out?
5. If the default reading is symbolic-but-historically-anchored, how should that change the way
   you react the next time someone claims to have identified "the" Antichrist in the news?
6. What would it look like for your group to hold Revelation's images with both real conviction
   and real humility — taking them seriously without needing to pin them to a headline?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Ask God for the discernment to read Revelation's symbols the way its first readers would have —
seriously, spiritually, and rooted in the actual text — rather than as a puzzle to be solved
against this week's news. Pray for freedom from the anxiety that newspaper-exegesis often
produces, and for a deeper trust that God's control over history doesn't depend on our correctly
guessing its timeline.
