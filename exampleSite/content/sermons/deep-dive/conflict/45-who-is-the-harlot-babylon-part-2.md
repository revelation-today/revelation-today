## Opening

Ask your group a slightly uncomfortable question to open tonight: if Babylon isn't a place on a map but a pattern of life, is it possible for a "faithful" church — one that would never think of itself as Sardis or Laodicea — to be quietly living inside Babylon right now, without recognizing it? That's exactly the question Revelation 17-18 forces on us, and it's the heart of this session.

## A harlot with a priestly wardrobe

Last session we untangled the crowded cast of Revelation 17 — the beast, the seven hills, the ten kings, the harlot herself identified as Rome. This session digs into what makes her "harlot" imagery work, and why it's aimed at more than just one ancient empire.

The Old Testament background for a city or nation pictured as an unfaithful woman runs deep, most vividly in Jeremiah 2:20-4:30, where Judah herself is called a harlot with "a harlot's forehead" who feels no shame (Jeremiah 3:3), whose robe is scarlet and who decorates herself with gold (Jeremiah 4:30) — almost the exact wardrobe Revelation later gives Babylon. But Revelation adds a sharper twist: the harlot's clothing in chapters 17-18 also echoes the garments of Israel's high priest — the gold, the precious stones, the fine materials of the ephod and breastplate (Exodus 28:15-20). The high priest's gold plate was inscribed "Holy to the Lord" (Exodus 28:36); the harlot's forehead instead reads "Babylon the Great, Mother of Harlots" (Revelation 17:5) — a dark parody of the very inscription meant to mark God's set-apart people. Ezekiel paints the same picture from another angle, picturing Jerusalem herself dressed in those priestly-royal garments and then prostituting herself with them (Ezekiel 16:13-26). The pattern across the Old Testament — Tyre, Nineveh, Israel itself — is consistent: the harlot is what a former covenant relationship with God looks like once it has curdled into idolatry. For the fuller textual argument, see expl/content/harlot/who-is-the-harlot-babylon-part-2.md.

**A word worth knowing:** the Hebrew and Greek behind "harlot" (*zanah*/*porne*) is used throughout the prophets not primarily for sexual sin but for covenant infidelity — turning to other gods, other security, other sources of identity, while still claiming the name of God's people. That's the sense Revelation is drawing on. It's a marriage word before it's a morality word.

## When economics and idolatry intertwine

Revelation 17-18 also insists that Babylon's seduction runs through the economy, not just the temple. The clearest Old Testament parallel is Tyre, the ancient world's great trading port, who "plied her trade with all the kingdoms of the earth" (Isaiah 23:17) — language that, more literally rendered, is the same "fornication" vocabulary applied to Babylon's dealings with "the kings of the earth" in Revelation 17:2. Revelation's own list of Babylon's luxury trade goods (18:12-13) closely tracks Ezekiel's earlier list of Tyre's cargo (Ezekiel 27). By the end of Isaiah's oracle, Tyre's profits from the nations are openly called her "harlot's wages" (Isaiah 23:17-18).

It's worth being precise here rather than treating every Old Testament example as the exact same mechanism. Micah's mention of temple gifts funded by exploitation (Micah 1:7), Nahum's picture of Nineveh's trade-driven seduction of nations (Nahum 3:4), and Jezebel's own political marriage into Israel (2 Kings 9:22) are three genuinely different ways that idolatry and economics/politics get entangled — cultic financing, trade dependency, and royal marriage-diplomacy aren't the same mechanism just because they all end up looking like "prostitution" in prophetic language. What all three do consistently support is a narrower, well-grounded claim: alliances with foreign powers regularly came bundled with foreign religious influence. Jezebel's marriage brought Baal worship into Israel; Judah's economic dependence on foreign powers regularly meant importing their gods and their values along with their money. That's the mechanism Revelation is drawing on when it pictures Babylon's kings "growing rich" (18:9) through their entanglement with her.

## Is Babylon the church gone bad?

Not quite — and this distinction matters. Babylon isn't simply a picture of an apostate church; she's the counterpart to the Bride and New Jerusalem, a whole system in her own right, working alongside the beast to offer a rival gospel: security through fear and violence, wealth through exploiting the weak, a feeling of superiority, and the silencing of any voice that objects (Revelation 13-17, drawn together). But the text also draws real parallels between the harlot and Jezebel, the false teacher who led Thyatira astray from within the church (Revelation 2:20-22) — which suggests a real, pointed warning for church leaders in particular, and for all of us: the church is never immune from sliding into Babylon's system. The call throughout Revelation 18 is "come out of her, my people" (18:4) — not a one-time altar call, but a daily act of stepping back out.

That's the discomfort worth sitting with honestly: it's natural to identify instinctively with the faithful, uncompromised churches of Revelation 2-3 — Smyrna, Philadelphia — rather than Sardis or Laodicea. But Babylon isn't drawn with sharp edges precisely so that the question stays open for every generation of the church: have we, without quite noticing, traded covenant faithfulness for the comfort, wealth, and national belonging Babylon offers?

## Discussion questions

**Understanding the text**
1. What does it mean that the harlot's clothing echoes the Old Testament high priest's garments? What point is that parody making?
2. Look at Isaiah 23 (Tyre) and Revelation 18:12-13 (Babylon's trade goods) side by side. What do the parallels suggest about how Revelation wants us to read Babylon's wealth?
3. Why is it more accurate to say temple gifts, trade dependency, and marriage-alliances are three different (though related) mechanisms, rather than treating them as interchangeable examples of the same thing?

**So what for us**
4. If Babylon is "any nation or system that trades covenant faithfulness for wealth and idolatry," where do you see that trade happening in your own culture — and honestly, in your own life?
5. "Come out of her, my people" (18:4) is presented as an ongoing, daily call rather than a single decision. What would it look like this week to concretely "step out" of some small compromise?
6. It's tempting to assume we're the faithful churches, not the compromised ones. What would it take for your group to ask honestly whether some part of your life together has quietly become comfortable with Babylon's values?

## Closing prayer

Invite the group into a time of honest self-examination rather than only outward-focused prayer tonight. Ask God to show each person one specific place where wealth, comfort, or belonging may have quietly taken precedence over covenant faithfulness, and pray for the courage to "come out" of that, concretely, this week.
