# Welcome to the Deep-Dive Guide

You already know how to sit with a hard passage. If your small group is picking up this
guide, you're probably the kind of group that doesn't want the Cliff's Notes version of
Revelation — you want to open the text, look at the Greek word underneath the English one,
argue a little about what a phrase actually means, and only then talk about what to do with
it. That instinct is exactly what this 52-session track is built for.

This guide follows the same 52-topic tour as "Revelation Today," the master sermon series —
same titles, same chapter references, same order. What's different is the format. Instead of
a 20-25 minute Sunday message aimed at a mixed congregation on their way out the door to lunch,
each session here runs 45-60 minutes and assumes a group willing to slow down: to read the
underlying passage together, to look at one or two original-language word studies, to follow
a citation into the fuller argument, and to sit with a question the text doesn't fully settle
rather than rushing past it. Every session closes with discussion questions split between
"what does the text actually say" and "so what do we do about it" — because both halves matter,
and a small group has room for both in a way a sermon rarely does.

A word about who this is for. This guide is written for the same congregation the master
series serves: Bible-believing, doctrinally serious, Evangelical and Free-Church congregations
in the Western world. If that's you, we want to name upfront both what you bring to this book
and where it may ask something new of you — because Revelation was written for people exactly
like you: people who take Scripture at its word and expect God to keep His promises.

**What you already bring.** This tradition has a strong, healthy instinct to take the Bible
seriously as God's actual word rather than a collection of nice ideas — which matters enormously
for a book like Revelation that keeps insisting it be read, heard, and obeyed, not just admired.
You also tend to love expository depth: verse-by-verse attention, cross-referencing Scripture
with Scripture, wanting to know what a word meant in its original context. Revelation rewards
exactly that kind of patience, maybe more than any other book in the Bible. And this tradition
takes personal allegiance to Jesus with real seriousness — which is, as it turns out, the single
thing Revelation is most interested in.

**Where this guide may stretch you gently.** Many in our churches grew up with a specific,
well-developed system for reading Revelation's end-times material — a system with its own
paperbacks, timelines, and film franchises. This guide reads the book differently, and we'll
explain why as we go, rather than assuming you'll simply take our word for it. We'll also
gently push back on the temptation to match Revelation's symbols to this week's headlines —
a temptation this book's first readers didn't face, because its symbols were written for
*their* world, not ours, even though its message still speaks powerfully into ours. And because
this tradition often enjoys real comfort and national belonging, we'll ask a question
Revelation asks its first readers just as pointedly: who do we actually credit when life is
good, and would we recognize ourselves in a compromised church as easily as we recognize
ourselves in a faithful one? None of this is a critique from outside — it's an invitation to
see this remarkable book more clearly, the same invitation we hope it extends to all of us.

Sessions 1-16 below cover Part 0, the introduction and background material every small group
should walk through before diving into the text of Revelation itself — the world John wrote
into, the kind of book this is, its hidden architecture, and the Old Testament story (covenant,
Israel, Daniel, Eden, Exodus) that Revelation assumes you already know. Take your time here.
Everything that follows depends on it.
