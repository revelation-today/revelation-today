## Opening Hook

Ask your group what they think of when they hear the word "mystery" in a religious context — a
secret code, something spooky, an unsolved puzzle. Now point out: Paul uses the word
differently. When he calls something a "mystery," he almost always means something that was
always true in God's plan but wasn't fully revealed until now — not a puzzle to decode, but a
staggering reality finally coming into view. Revelation uses the word exactly three times, and
tracing all three together reveals one of the book's central structural ideas: a mirrored
triptych of church, God, and the harlot.

## The Core Teaching

This session draws together two source articles:
`expl/background/literature/literally-or-symbolic.md`, which identifies the three "mystery"
references and their connection to Daniel, and
`expl/background/israel/the-church-is-part-of-israel.md`, which unpacks the first of the three
in full.

**Why three mysteries, and why here.** As we saw in session 3, the word "mystery" in Revelation
deliberately echoes {{% bible val="Daniel 2" link="dan:2,28-45" lang="en" %}}, the only prophetic
book to use the term — and Revelation uses it exactly three times: the mystery of the
{{% bible val="church" link="rev:1,20" lang="en" %}}, the mystery of
{{% bible val="God" link="rev:10,7" lang="en" %}}, and the mystery of the
{{% bible val="harlot" link="rev:17,5" lang="en" %}}. These three aren't scattered at random —
together they form a mirrored contrast running through the whole book: the Bride's calling to
faithful worship set against the doom awaiting everyone entangled with Babylon, with God's own
hidden plan as the hinge between them.

**Mystery one: the church.** Paul's language in
{{% bible val="Ephesians 3:3-6" link="eph:3,3-6" lang="en" %}} names the mystery directly:
"through the gospel the Gentiles are heirs together with Israel, members together of one body,
and sharers together in the promise in Christ Jesus." This is not a hidden secret in the sense
of a code — it's an almost unbelievable act of God, barely conceivable to a first-century Jewish
reader: Gentiles now share Israel's full inheritance, on equal footing, in the same body.
Revelation 1:20 identifies this same mystery with the seven lampstands and seven stars — the
churches themselves, Jew and Gentile together, are the mystery made visible. Jesus is the New
Covenant, and in Him both Jews and Gentiles together make up end-time Israel — not a separate,
newly invented entity replacing Israel, but Israel's own promises reaching their intended
destination. Nearly every title the Old Testament gives Israel — beloved, God's son, bride,
kingdom of priests, temple, vineyard, treasured possession — is now applied directly to the
church, term for term. See `expl/background/israel/the-church-is-part-of-israel.md` for the
complete list of these term-transfers, each traced from an Old Testament text to its New
Testament application.

**Mystery two: God.** In {{% bible val="Revelation 10:7" link="rev:10,7" lang="en" %}}, the
"mystery of God" is announced as about to be "finished" at the seventh trumpet — this is God's
own long-hidden plan for the world reaching its completion, the same plan the prophets spoke of
without fully understanding it themselves. We'll return to this mystery in more depth once we
reach chapter 10 later in the series; for now, hold onto the pattern: God has been working out a
plan all along that looks, from the ground, like it's taking far too long — but the mystery
being "finished" means it was never actually delayed.

**Mystery three: the harlot.** In {{% bible val="Revelation 17:5" link="rev:17,5" lang="en" %}},
"Babylon the Great, Mother of Prostitutes" is herself called a mystery — a coded name for a real
system dressed up as a bride but revealed, once unveiled, as a doomed counterfeit. We'll spend
substantial time on her identity much later in the series (the Harlot Babylon sessions); the
point worth planting now is structural: the harlot is deliberately positioned as the dark mirror
of the church. Where the church-mystery is the true Bride made up of a redeemed, worshiping
people, the harlot-mystery is the false bride made up of everyone who traded covenant
faithfulness for wealth, security, and status.

**Why this triptych matters for reading the whole book.** Once you see these three mysteries
together, Revelation's basic architecture becomes visible: God's hidden plan (mystery two) is
what's actually driving history, and it plays out through a stark choice between two communities
— the true Bride (mystery one) and the false bride (mystery three). Every reader of Revelation
is being invited into one of those two identities, not standing outside as a neutral observer.
That's the apocalyptic "no neutral ground" principle from session 2, now given a name and a
shape.

## A Question Worth Sitting With Honestly

If nearly every title once given to ethnic Israel — beloved, son, bride, kingdom of priests — is
now applied to the church including Gentile believers, what does that mean for how the church
today relates to Jewish people and to ongoing questions about Israel? The source material is
careful here: the argument is that the church is part of Israel, grafted into her calling and
promises, not a replacement for her or a rejection of her. That's a meaningful distinction worth
letting your group sit with rather than resolving too quickly in either direction — Revelation's
own harsh language toward some first-century Jewish opponents (session 8 will unpack this
further) is best read as a family dispute over who truly belongs to Israel, not an ethnic
rejection.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Why does it matter that all three "mysteries" use the same specific word, echoing Daniel 2,
   rather than three unrelated ideas that happen to be called mysterious?
2. What does Ephesians 3:3-6 add to your understanding of what "mystery" means biblically —
   versus the common modern sense of an unsolved puzzle?
3. How does seeing the church and the harlot as a deliberate mirrored pair change how you expect
   to read the later chapters about Babylon?

**So what for us:**
4. If the church now carries titles once reserved for Israel alone (bride, kingdom of priests,
   God's own treasured possession), how should that shape the way we see our own ordinary,
   unremarkable local church?
5. The harlot represents trading covenant faithfulness for wealth and security. Where do you see
   that trade being offered to you personally, in ways that look nothing like ancient Babylon on
   the surface?
6. God's hidden plan (the "mystery of God") is described as something that looks delayed but
   isn't. Where in your own life do you need to trust that God's timing isn't actually behind
   schedule?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Thank God for the almost unbelievable mystery that Gentile believers have been brought fully
into Israel's inheritance through Christ. Ask Him to help your group see itself clearly as part
of the true Bride he's describing — and to expose, gently but honestly, any place where your
group or your own heart has quietly started trading covenant faithfulness for the harlot's kind
of security.
