## Opening hook

Ask: "Can a church — or a person's faith — look completely alive on the outside while actually being dead?" Let the question sit uncomfortably. Sardis is the answer Jesus gives: "You have a reputation of being alive, but you are dead" (Revelation 3:1).

## Core teaching

Sardis had the longest history of any of the seven cities and one of the strongest natural defenses — perched high on a mountain ridge, ringed by cliffs, disconnected from neighboring peaks (see expl/content/letters/the-letter-to-the-church-in-sardis.md). That position bred deep civic overconfidence: the city considered itself impregnable. History proved otherwise twice — Sardis was conquered on both occasions by enemies who slipped in through the exact cliff faces everyone assumed were unscalable. Confidence in your own defenses, unchecked, becomes the very blind spot that gets you conquered.

That civic overconfidence appears to have rubbed off on the church. Jesus, approaching "in the full power of the Spirit" and holding "the seven stars" (3:1, echoing chapter 1's vision of Jesus among the churches), gives this congregation almost no praise — only "a few people" are named as faithful (3:4) — and a blunt diagnosis: the church is dead, its deeds "unfinished in the sight of my God" (3:2).

**What killed it.** The core problem is a false sense of security. Just as the city trusted its unassailable position, the church seems to have settled into trusting its own reputation and history rather than staying dependent on the Holy Spirit's actual, ongoing work. The image Jesus uses — "wake up... I have not found your deeds complete" — echoes Ezekiel 37's valley of dry bones, where only the Spirit's breath brings life to what looks like a graveyard. It also recalls James 1:17's reminder that every good and perfect gift comes from God — meaning no work is genuinely "complete" unless God himself is doing it through the people involved, not merely being credited afterward.

Life sustained by reputation, momentum, or history — without ongoing dependence on the Spirit — simply doesn't last. And it can look convincingly alive for a long time before the truth becomes undeniable, the same way a impregnable-looking fortress can stand for centuries before the one crack no one guarded gets found.

**The promise.** White robes — a legal image of acquittal — are promised to those who "have not soiled their clothes" (3:4-5), along with assurance that their names will remain in the Book of Life, meaning their citizenship in God's kingdom is secure. But this promise is explicitly for "the one who is victorious" (3:5) — those who wake up and repent, not the church as it currently stands. This letter, more than any other so far, is meant to function as a shock: a wake-up call to a dying church before what little life remains also fades.

## A note on historical background

The source article dates the earthquake that devastated Sardis to "17 BC." The historical event in view — recorded by the Roman historian Tacitus — actually occurred in **AD 17**, under Emperor Tiberius, not 17 BC. Tiberius funded the city's rebuilding and granted major tax relief, and Sardis later petitioned to honor him with a temple. The point about imperial patronage still stands; only the date needs correcting (BC to AD).

## Discussion questions

1. What are the "cliffs" your own church or small group might be quietly trusting — history, reputation, past faithfulness — instead of active, present dependence on the Spirit?
2. Sardis's civic overconfidence directly shaped its church's spiritual complacency. How much do you think the wider culture around a church shapes its blind spots, even without anyone intending it?
3. "I have not found your deeds complete in the sight of my God" — what's the difference between activity that looks complete to observers and deeds that are actually complete before God?
4. Why do you think a dying church can look convincingly alive for so long before the truth becomes obvious — to itself or to others?
5. What would "waking up" concretely look like for you personally this week, if you're honest about where reputation or routine may have quietly replaced real dependence on God?

## Closing prayer prompt

Ask God to wake up anything in your own life or your church that has settled into comfortable routine mistaken for real life. Pray for the humility to recognize dead patterns before they become undeniable, and for the Spirit's breath to bring back what looks like dry bones.
