## Opening Hook

Describe an old-world mob protection racket: a stronger power offers "protection" to a weaker
one, in exchange for total loyalty and regular payment — break the deal, and the strong power
turns on you. That's uncomfortably close to how ancient international treaties actually worked,
including many of the covenants surrounding Israel's story. Then ask your group: what if, at the
critical moment, the powerful party in a deal like that stepped forward and took the
weaker party's punishment on himself, voluntarily, before any betrayal had even happened? That's
exactly what God does with Abraham in Genesis 15 — and it's the hinge this whole session turns
on.

## The Core Teaching

The full argument is in `expl/background/israel/gods-covenant.md`.

**How ancient covenants actually worked.** There were two basic kinds of ancient treaty. Covenants
between equals functioned like alliances — mutual support, and the members could even be called
"brothers." Covenants of conquest were different: the stronger party was "father" or "master,"
the weaker was "son" or "slave" — but crucially, the stronger party was *obligated* to defend the
weaker one, precisely because the weaker party was now, in effect, family. We see this play out
concretely when {{% bible val="the Gibeonites tricked Joshua into a covenant" link="jos:9" lang="en" %}}: once bound, Joshua was later obligated to defend them against the Amorites even
though he didn't like them ({{% bible val="Joshua 10:1-14" link="jos:10,1-14" lang="en" %}}). A
weaker party in this kind of covenant couldn't make other covenants on the side — you can only
have one father — and breaking the covenant (usually by refusing tribute) meant losing the land
and going into exile.

**Original-language note: "cutting" a covenant.** The Hebrew phrase for making a covenant
literally means "cutting" a covenant — drawn from the sacrificial ritual at the ceremony itself.
Animals were cut in half and arranged in two rows, and the weaker party walked between the
pieces: a vivid, deliberate act of intimidation showing exactly what would happen to him if he
broke his word.

**The stunning reversal with Abraham.** In {{% bible val="Genesis 15" link="gen:15" lang="en" %}},
this exact covenant ritual takes place — sacrifices cut in half, arranged in two rows — but with
one critical difference. It is not Abraham, the weaker party, who walks between the pieces. It
is God Himself, represented by fire, who passes through. God takes on Abraham's place under the
covenant curse before Abraham has even broken anything. So when Abraham later does break the
covenant — by fathering a child through Hagar rather than trusting God's promise — the
consequence doesn't fall on Abraham's line the way ancient covenant law would demand; ultimately
it is God's own Son who dies in the place the curse pointed to. This is the gospel, quietly
embedded in Genesis 15, centuries before Sinai and millennia before the cross.

**The Sinai covenant follows the standard ancient treaty format precisely** — and seeing that
format actually deepens the meaning rather than reducing it to bureaucratic paperwork. Ancient
conquest treaties had five standard parts: a self-glorifying preamble naming the ruler, a
prologue recounting the ruler's past kindness (meant to invite willing loyalty, not just compel
it), a list of demands, blessings-and-curses for keeping or breaking the terms, and witnesses
called to guarantee it. Sinai follows this exactly:
- Preamble: "{{% bible val="I am the Lord your God" link="exo:20,2" lang="en" %}}"
- Prologue: "...who brought you out of Egypt, out of the land of slavery" — look at what I've
  already done for you, and trust there's more to come.
- Demands: beginning with the foundational command to have no other gods
  ({{% bible val="Exodus 20:3-6" link="exo:20,3-6" lang="en" %}}).
- Blessings and curses: {{% bible val="Deuteronomy 28:1-14" link="deu:28,1-14" lang="en" %}} and
  {{% bible val="28:15-68" link="deu:28,15-68" lang="en" %}}, with an escalating series of
  warnings in {{% bible val="Leviticus 26" link="lev:26,1-46" lang="en" %}} — "if you still do
  not listen, I will..." — each stage designed as an invitation to reconsider, not simply a
  countdown to punishment.
- Witnesses: {{% bible val="heaven and earth themselves" link="deu:30,19" lang="en" %}}.

**A detail worth pausing on: the terms were never impossible.** Unlike many ancient covenants
designed to set the weaker party up for inevitable failure and permanent subjugation, God's
covenant terms were genuinely achievable. Israel could have kept them. They simply, repeatedly,
chose not to. That matters for how we read the prophets' harsh language later: it isn't the
disproportionate anger of an impossible-to-please deity, but the real grief of a covenant partner
who kept His own side completely and asked for nothing His people couldn't actually do.

**Original-language note: *hesed*.** Through all of this, God calls Himself the Father of Israel
({{% bible val="Exodus 3:6" link="exo:3,6" lang="en" %}}) — a father who, even while legally
entitled to enforce covenant curses, remains merciful. Scripture captures that combination in the
Hebrew word *hesed* (חֶסֶד), usually translated "covenant loyalty" or "steadfast love," but rich
enough to also carry the sense of goodness, mercy, and faithfulness depending on context. It's
one of the most theologically loaded words in the Old Testament — not sentimental affection, but
committed, active loyalty that keeps showing up even when the covenant partner hasn't earned it.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Walk through the five-part ancient covenant structure (preamble, prologue, demands, blessings/
   curses, witnesses) and match each part to its location in Exodus 20 and Deuteronomy 28. What
   surprises you about how tightly Sinai follows this pattern?
2. Why does it matter that God, not Abraham, walked between the pieces in Genesis 15? What would
   the story mean if it had gone the other way?
3. What does the escalating "if you still do not listen" pattern in Leviticus 26 tell us about
   God's purpose in warning His people before judgment actually lands?

**So what for us:**
4. *Hesed* means committed loyalty that keeps showing up even when it isn't earned. Where have
   you experienced that kind of loyalty from God recently — and where might you need to extend
   it to someone else this week?
5. The covenant terms were never impossible — Israel could have kept them but didn't. Where in
   your own life do you quietly tell yourself that faithfulness is "too hard," when the real
   issue is that you haven't chosen it?
6. God took on the covenant curse Himself, before any betrayal occurred. How does picturing that
   scene in Genesis 15 change how you understand what happened at the cross?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Thank God specifically for His *hesed* — His committed, covenant loyalty that has kept showing
up in your life even in seasons you didn't deserve it. Ask Him to help your group trust that His
covenant demands, however weighty they feel, are never impossible — and to renew your willingness
to actually keep them, not just admire them from a distance.
