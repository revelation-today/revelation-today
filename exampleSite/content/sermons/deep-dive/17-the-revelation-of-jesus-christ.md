## Opening hook

Ask your group: "If you knew for certain Jesus was returning within your lifetime, what would you stop doing tomorrow? What would you start?" Let people actually answer — don't rush past it. Most of us live as though the answer to "is this urgent?" is no. Revelation opens by insisting the answer is yes, and it has been yes for two thousand years. Tonight we're looking at the first eight verses of the book most Christians skip past on the way to the "interesting" chapters — and they turn out to be the key that unlocks everything that follows.

## Core teaching

Revelation opens with a title, not a scene: "The revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place" (Revelation 1:1). Every word here is doing work.

**"Apocalypse."** The Greek word behind "Revelation" is *apokalypsis*. It has picked up baggage in English — decoder rings, secret codes, charts with beasts and timelines. That's not what the word meant to John's first readers. It names a whole genre of Jewish and early Christian literature that uses vivid symbolic vision to unveil a spiritual reality behind ordinary events, precisely so readers will act differently now. See expl/content/vision/setting-the-foundation.md and the background article on apocalyptic genre (expl/background/literature/the-book-of-revelation-how-to-read-it.md) for the fuller case. This is worth sitting with as a group, because it reframes the entire rest of your study: Revelation is not primarily a puzzle to be solved but a summons to be obeyed.

**"Of Jesus Christ."** The Greek genitive here is ambiguous, and — as the source material argues — probably deliberately so. It can mean "the revelation that comes from Jesus" (he's the source) or "the revelation that is about Jesus" (he's the content). Both readings work grammatically, and the best answer may be "both": the revelation comes from Jesus, and its whole purpose is to show us more of him. Whatever else this book does with monsters and bowls and battles, it is fundamentally Christological — every strange image serves to reveal Jesus more fully, not to distract from him.

**"What must soon take place."** This phrase deliberately echoes Daniel 2, where Nebuchadnezzar's dream shows every human kingdom crumbling before God's eternal one. For Daniel, that was still future. John's point is that in Jesus, it has already begun. "Soon" and "the time is near" (v. 3) don't mean the end could arrive at any random moment on a clock — compare how Mark 1:15 uses the same kind of language to describe the kingdom of God as having *already* arrived in Jesus' ministry, not as a stopwatch prediction. It's a statement about the character of this age: it is the last age, urgent and active, and has been since the resurrection.

**The greeting (vv. 4-6).** John writes like a letter, because it is one — sent to seven actual churches in Asia Minor. Jesus is described as "the faithful witness, the firstborn from the dead, and the ruler of the kings of the earth" — language pulled from Psalm 89's description of David's promised son. Believers are already told they are "a kingdom and priests" (v. 6) — not a future promise only, but a present identity we're still learning to live out.

**"Every eye will see him" (v. 7).** This verse braids together Daniel 7's "son of man coming with the clouds" and Zechariah 12's "they will look on me, the one they have pierced, and mourn." In Zechariah, the mourning is Israel's own grief over their king. John extends it to "all peoples on earth." Notably, the text doesn't specify *why* they mourn — the source material argues this is deliberately open: some mourn in repentance, recognizing their Savior; others mourn in dread, recognizing their judge. This isn't a promise of vengeance on those who literally crucified Jesus (most of "all peoples on earth" had no part in that) — it's a global call to reckon with him, one way or another.

## A live question worth naming

The source article's reading of Revelation 1:7 leans toward "the outcome is left open — repentance or judgment, we aren't told which will predominate." That's a real interpretive choice, and not the only one made in the history of the church; some readers hear more judgment emphasis here, others more hope. It's worth discussing which reading your group finds more textually convincing, rather than assuming the ambiguity is resolved.

## Discussion questions

1. What does it change for you to hear "apocalypse" as "urgent unveiling meant to provoke action" rather than "coded forecast of future events"?
2. Read verses 1 and 4-6 aloud. Where do you see evidence that the book is "about Jesus" versus "from Jesus" — or both at once?
3. "The time is near" has been true for two thousand years. Does that weaken the urgency, or does it reveal something about how God defines "soon"? How would you explain that to someone who finds it evasive?
4. Revelation 1:6 says believers are *already* "a kingdom and priests." What would it look like this week to live like that's true, rather than something you're still waiting for?
5. Verse 7 leaves open whether "all peoples" mourn in repentance or dread. Which do you find more textually convincing, and why does it matter pastorally how we read it?
6. If Jesus is both the source and the subject of this revelation, what should that do to how nervous or excited you feel walking into the rest of this book?

## Closing prayer prompt

Pray that your group would receive this book the way it asks to be received — not as a puzzle to master, but as a summons from Jesus that changes how you live this week. Ask God to make "the time is near" feel true again, without either despair or obsession over dates. Thank him that the revelation is, before anything else, more of Jesus himself.
