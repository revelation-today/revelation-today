## Opening Hook

Ask your group to name the classic "hero's journey" pattern from ancient mythology: an
extraordinary baby abandoned or exposed, raised by someone of humble status, whose true identity
is hidden until a dramatic reveal that finally gives him the power he was always destined for
(think Hercules, Oedipus, Romulus and Remus, or Cyrus the Great). Then read Moses's birth story
next to it. It's not just different — it inverts the pattern point for point. That inversion is
the whole key to reading Exodus rightly, and it's foundational for how Revelation later borrows
this same story's imagery.

**The historical backdrop, briefly.** Israel's story likely intersects with the Hyksos period —
Semitic peoples from Canaan who gained control of the eastern Nile Delta by around 1720 BC before
being expelled around 1550 BC. A small correction to the source material: Goshen, where Joseph's
family settled, is properly located in the *northeast* of Egypt (the eastern Delta, near the
Sinai border), not the northwest as originally stated — a correction that actually strengthens
the article's own later point, since it keeps Goshen consistent with the northeastern region
where Pithom, Ramses, and the Israelites' eventual departure route are all also located. After
the Hyksos expulsion, Egypt's determination to prevent a second foreign takeover is what drives
Pharaoh's escalating cruelty toward the Israelites who remained.

**Pharaoh's plan fails at every single point, almost as parody.** He works the Israelites hard to
suppress their numbers; instead they multiply. He orders midwives to kill Hebrew newborns; they
defy him and are blessed with families of their own. He orders babies thrown into the Nile; Moses
is placed into the Nile in a basket — the same Hebrew word used for Noah's Ark — and is saved. He
wants to prevent the rise of a deliverer; Moses is raised inside Pharaoh's own household, nursed
by his own mother, who is paid by the palace to do it. Even the story's structure works against
Pharaoh: he isn't named in the text, treated as a background figure, exactly like the unnamed
gods he represents — while the story's actual heroes are named, and are mostly women, and mostly
non-Egyptian outsiders (Shiphrah and Puah, the midwives, bear Semitic names).

**Moses's story is a deliberate reversal of every ancient hero myth.** Where the typical ancient
hero is abandoned as a baby and found by someone of low status, Moses is hidden by a loving
mother with careful, ongoing protection, and found by Pharaoh's own daughter. Where the typical
hero's identity is concealed until a dramatic late reveal, Moses's identity is known from the
very beginning. Where the typical reveal is the turning point that grants the hero his rightful
power, Moses's revealed identity instead gets him into serious trouble, forcing him to flee for
his life. And where these myths exist to establish the hero's own claim to greatness, Exodus
goes out of its way to show that the point was never Moses's greatness — it was God's provision
and control, working through someone who, by every ordinary measure, doesn't look like hero
material: a man with an uncontrolled temper in his youth, refined over forty years of obscurity
as a shepherd into "the most humble man on earth," someone who forgets to circumcise his own son
(the one condition of the covenant resting on him) and has to be rescued by his own wife.

**The hardening of Pharaoh's heart — a question worth taking seriously.** Is it fair for God to
harden Pharaoh's heart and then punish him for the resulting cruelty? The Hebrew text uses three
different words across the narrative — *chazaq* (to harden or strengthen), *qashah* (to be hard
or severe), and *kabad* (heavy or weighty) — and critically, all three verbs describe both God
hardening Pharaoh's heart *and* Pharaoh hardening his own heart, repeatedly, on his own
initiative. The pattern that emerges: God's hardening didn't force Pharaoh into evil against his
will — it gave Pharaoh the strength to keep resisting rather than folding the moment he witnessed
God's power, which Pharaoh then did, repeatedly, of his own accord. The clearest evidence: during
the plague of hail, Pharaoh first admits he has sinned
({{% bible val="Exodus 9:27" link="exo:9,27" lang="en" %}}) — genuine, if temporary,
acknowledgment — and then, the moment the hail stops,
{{% bible val="sins again and hardens his own heart" link="exo:9,34" lang="en" %}}. This is a
picture of a man given every opportunity to relent and repeatedly choosing not to, not a puppet
with no real agency.

**The plagues: pattern and purpose.** The first nine plagues fall into three groups of three,
each following a consistent rhythm — the first plague in each group begins in the morning with
Pharaoh at the Nile; the last in each group comes without warning. The magicians can imitate the
first two plagues but not the third, and by the sixth are so afflicted themselves they can't even
appear before Pharaoh. Starting with the fourth plague, God begins drawing a clear distinction
between Israel and Egypt — a distinction only Pharaoh had drawn before then. The plagues target
Egypt's gods and its whole national self-understanding
({{% bible val="Exodus 12:12" link="exo:12,12" lang="en" %}}), not ordinary Egyptians
personally, who are repeatedly warned in advance and can take refuge with Israel in Goshen — many
did.

**A caution about specific claims in this article.** The accuracy review flagged several specific
claims about Egyptian mythology in the source material as unverifiable or likely mistaken —
including an invented-sounding myth behind "the finger of God" phrase, and a claimed
locust-specific deity with no basis in standard Egyptology. It also flagged an error in which
deity is "father of the gods" (that title belongs to creator-figures like Atum, Ra, or Nun, not
simply "the Nile god," commonly personified as Hapi) and a mismatch pairing the frog goddess
Heqet with "the Nile god" when she's properly paired with Khnum. Rather than repeating those
specific mythological claims, the safest and still fully compelling version of this teaching
point is the sturdier one: the plagues systematically dismantled Egypt's confidence in its own
national gods and its Pharaoh's supposed divine authority, culminating in the death of the
firstborn — a direct strike at Pharaoh's own line of succession and at animal-associated deities
whose future (represented by their own "firstborn") was now shown to be no future at all. You
don't need a specific, doubtful mythological citation to make that argument; the text itself
carries it.

**One citation correction worth noting:** the claim that Exodus 12:12 supports "there is no time
to wait, only to hurry" actually belongs to
{{% bible val="Exodus 12:11" link="exo:12,11" lang="en" %}} ("eat it in haste, with your belt
fastened") — 12:12 is about judgment on Egypt's gods, a theme correctly cited elsewhere in the
same discussion.

**The Passover itself.** Before the tenth plague, God introduces Passover — protection from
judgment, the origin of the Lord's Supper, and, notably, open to anyone who wanted to join
Israel, not restricted by ethnicity ({{% bible val="Exodus 12:43-49" link="exo:12,43-49" lang="en" %}}). The sign placed on hand and forehead becomes a lasting reminder: God delivered Israel, He
is more powerful than every rival claim to power, and His people must not forget it.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Walk through the ways Moses's birth story deliberately inverts the typical ancient hero
   pattern. Why might the biblical author have constructed the story this way rather than simply
   telling it in the "normal" heroic mode?
2. What does the pattern of Hebrew verbs for "hardening" (used for both God and Pharaoh) suggest
   about the nature of Pharaoh's own responsibility for his choices?
3. Why does the text draw a sharp distinction between plagues 1-3 (no distinction between
   Israelites and Egyptians) and plagues 4-9 (a clear distinction drawn)? What's the significance
   of that shift?

**So what for us:**
4. Moses looked like the least likely candidate for the role God gave him — bad temper, forty
   "wasted" years, a critical lapse in his own covenant obedience. Where do you disqualify
   yourself from something God might actually be calling you to?
5. Pharaoh had real, repeated opportunities to relent and chose hardness each time. Where in your
   own life have you mistaken a temporary, convenient acknowledgment of wrongdoing for actual,
   lasting repentance?
6. Passover was open to anyone who wanted to join Israel, regardless of ethnicity. How does that
   shape the way you think about who belongs at the table in your own church or small group?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Thank God for choosing to work through unlikely people — a man with a temper, a stutter, and a
forty-year detour — rather than obvious heroes, so that His own power, not human strength, gets
the credit. Ask Him to soften anywhere your own heart has hardened after a temporary, convenient
acknowledgment of sin, and to give you Moses's kind of hard-won humility.
