## Opening Hook

Ask your group: has anyone grown up with a chart showing "Israel" and "the Church" as two
separate tracks in God's plan, running in parallel but never quite merging — one on a
this-worldly track, one on a heavenly track? Many in Evangelical circles have encountered some
version of this picture. Tonight we're going to look directly at the New Testament's own
vocabulary and ask a harder, more careful question: is that actually the picture Scripture
paints, or does it describe something more like one family with two groups now fully merged into
it?

## The Core Teaching

This session draws on `expl/background/israel/the-church-is-part-of-israel.md` (the full list of
term-transfers) and `expl/background/israel/israel-and-the-church.md` (the tension with Jews in
Revelation specifically).

**The mystery of the Gentiles, named directly by Paul.** {{% bible val="Ephesians 3:3-6" link="eph:3,3-6" lang="en" %}} states it plainly: "through the gospel the Gentiles are heirs
together with Israel, members together of one body, and sharers together in the promise in
Christ Jesus." Jesus is the New Covenant, and in Him Jews and Gentiles together form one
end-time Israel — not defined by ethnicity or nationality, but by belonging to Jesus. The
covenant with ethnic Israel functioned as an intermediary step between the promise to Abraham
and the church ({{% bible val="Galatians 3:23-28" link="gal:3,23-28" lang="en" %}}), not as a
separate, competing plan running alongside it.

**The evidence: title after title, transferred directly.** This is where the argument gets
concrete rather than abstract. Nearly every major title the Old Testament gives Israel is
reapplied to the church in the New Testament, verse by verse:
- **Beloved of the Lord** — given to Benjamin ({{% bible val="Deut 33:12" link="deu:33,12" lang="en" %}}) and Israel, then applied directly to the Thessalonian believers
  ({{% bible val="1 Thess 1:4" link="1th:1,4" lang="en" %}}), using *ekklesia* — the same word
  the Old Testament uses for Israel gathered to worship.
- **God's son** — Israel as firstborn son at the Exodus
  ({{% bible val="Exodus 4:22-23" link="exo:4,22-23" lang="en" %}}); the church as God's adopted
  children, "all His brothers" ({{% bible val="Hebrews 2:11" link="heb:2,11" lang="en" %}},
  {{% bible val="Galatians 4:4-7" link="gal:4,4-7" lang="en" %}}).
- **Bride** — Israel as God's bride even in unfaithfulness
  ({{% bible val="Isaiah 54:5-6" link="isa:54,5-6" lang="en" %}}); the church as Christ's bride
  ({{% bible val="2 Corinthians 11:2" link="2co:11,2" lang="en" %}},
  {{% bible val="Ephesians 5:25-27" link="eph:5,25-27" lang="en" %}}), and in Revelation the
  bride's identity includes elements of both — apostles
  ({{% bible val="Rev 21:14" link="rev:21,14" lang="en" %}}) and tribes
  ({{% bible val="Rev 21:12" link="rev:21,12" lang="en" %}}) together.
- **Kingdom of priests** — Israel at Sinai
  ({{% bible val="Exodus 19:6" link="exo:19,6" lang="en" %}}); the church in Peter
  ({{% bible val="1 Peter 2:9" link="1pe:2,9" lang="en" %}}) and in Revelation's own opening
  ({{% bible val="Rev 1:5-6" link="rev:1,5-6" lang="en" %}}).
- **Temple** — the place of fellowship with God; the church is now that temple
  ({{% bible val="1 Corinthians 3:16-17" link="1co:3,16-17" lang="en" %}}), built on Jesus as its
  foundation ({{% bible val="Ephesians 2:20-22" link="eph:2,20-22" lang="en" %}}).

This is not a handful of coincidental echoes — it's a sustained, deliberate pattern across
multiple New Testament authors.

**The concrete tension in Revelation itself.** The seven churches weren't reading this in the
abstract. In Smyrna and Philadelphia specifically, local Jewish opponents actively attacked the
church for testifying about Jesus — which is why John calls them a "synagogue of Satan"
({{% bible val="Rev 2:9" link="rev:2,9" lang="en" %}},
{{% bible val="Rev 3:9" link="rev:3,9" lang="en" %}}). When those same opponents in Philadelphia
are later said to "bow down" before the church, the point isn't submission to church authority as
an institution — it's that they come to accept Jesus. The same dynamic surfaces in chapter 11,
where the place the two witnesses are killed is spiritually called "Sodom" and "Egypt" — a
pointed description of Jerusalem itself, since that's where Jesus, its rightful Lord, was
killed. Tellingly, the text avoids naming Jerusalem directly.

**Yet Revelation never turns its argument against ethnic Israel as such.** The 24 elders in
chapter 4 represent the 12 tribes and 12 apostles together — a single unified image, not two
competing symbols. Both the 12 tribes' names and the 12 apostles' names are engraved side by
side in the New Jerusalem's structure. Heaven itself, notably, is never pictured as some neutral,
placeless realm — it's pictured as the New Jerusalem, which is part of why it would be no
surprise at all if Jesus were to appear in the actual, physical city of Jerusalem at His return.

**Why the language is so sharp, then: it's a family dispute, not an ethnic rejection.** The
church can speak this pointedly about Jewish opponents precisely *because* it is part of Israel
and cannot cut off its own roots — even when some Jews are attacking it. This is the crucial
distinction the source material insists on: Revelation's harsh words about "the synagogue of
Satan" are never a statement about ethnicity. They're a statement about relationship to Jesus,
directed at specific opponents in specific cities, from within a family that still claims its own
Jewish inheritance as fully its own.

**A note on tone for this session specifically.** This is one of the more theologically freighted
topics in the whole introduction, with a long, sometimes painful history of misuse in the church.
Handle it the way the source material does: carefully, without triumphalism, and without letting
"the church inherits Israel's titles" slide into "therefore Israel has been replaced" — the
argument here is continuity and inclusion, not replacement.

## Discussion Questions

**Understanding the text:**
1. Pick two or three of the title-transfers above (beloved, bride, kingdom of priests, temple)
   and trace them from their Old Testament root to their New Testament application. What
   pattern do you notice in how consistently this happens?
2. Why does the source material insist that Revelation's "synagogue of Satan" language is about
   relationship to Jesus, not ethnicity? What in the text supports that reading?
3. What does it mean that the 24 elders represent the 12 tribes and 12 apostles as a single,
   unified image rather than two separate groups?

**So what for us:**
4. If the church now carries titles once given exclusively to Israel — beloved, bride, God's own
   treasured possession — how does that change the way you see your own church community on an
   ordinary Sunday?
5. This subject has a difficult history of being misused to justify contempt for Jewish people.
   How does framing this as "a family dispute, not a rejection" guard against that misuse — and
   where do you need to be careful in how you talk about this with others?
6. What would it look like practically for your group to hold together being "part of Israel"
   and having real, sometimes painful history with the Jewish people, the way Revelation itself
   holds those two realities together?

## Closing Prayer Prompt

Thank God for the staggering grace of being grafted, as Gentile believers, into the full
inheritance once given to Israel alone. Pray for humility and care in how your group talks about
this subject going forward — free of triumphalism, honest about the tension, and grounded in
gratitude rather than superiority.
