## An army you have to look at twice

After the sixth seal, before the seventh, John hears a number: 144,000 sealed — 12,000 from
each of the twelve tribes of Israel, counted like an army mustered for battle, echoing the
census of fighting men taken during the Exodus. An angel marks them for protection before the
coming trumpets, recalling Ezekiel's vision where God sealed the faithful before judgment fell
on Jerusalem (Ezekiel 9).

But then John does something Revelation loves to do: he tells you what he hears, and then
shows you something bigger. "Then I looked, and there before me was a great multitude that no
one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language, standing before the throne and
before the Lamb" (Revelation 7:9). The 144,000 soldiers John hears counted turn out, when he
actually looks, to be an uncountable crowd from every nation on earth, worshiping God. It's the
same reversal we already saw with the Lion of Judah, who turned out to be a slaughtered Lamb
(Revelation 5:5–6): God's power keeps arriving in a different shape than we expect.

This uncountable multitude is the church itself — sealed, safe, and worshiping even in the
middle of tribulation. Their leader tribe, tellingly, is Judah — Jesus' own tribe — placed
first. And their situation connects directly to the souls under the altar from the fifth seal,
crying "how long?" and given white robes (Revelation 6:9–11): the same people, now comforted,
already living in the tension of "the not yet" even while surrounded by trouble.

## What is the Great Tribulation, really?

"The Great Tribulation" is not a vague phrase — it's a specific term borrowed from Daniel:
"There will be a time of distress such as has not happened from the beginning of nations until
then. But at that time your people ... will be delivered" (Daniel 12:1). Jesus quotes this
directly (Matthew 24:21, Mark 13:19) in connection with the destruction of Jerusalem and the
suffering surrounding it.

Here's the key move worth making clearly: in Daniel, the great tribulation purified and
divided God's people from the unfaithful who fell away under pressure (Daniel 11:32–34,
12:10). And in the New Testament, this same tribulation is described as something the church
already lives inside — foretold plainly by Jesus (John 16:33), lived out in Paul's own mission
(Acts 14:22), described as a catalyst that produces hope, not despair (Romans 5:3–4), something
that cannot separate believers from Christ's love (Romans 8:35–36), and a warning given
directly to Timothy that "everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be
persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12).

So when did the Great Tribulation start, and when does it end? The answer Revelation gives is
larger than most popular teaching assumes: it spans the entire age between Jesus' first coming
and his second. Revelation 12 shows why — Satan, defeated and cast down at Jesus' first coming,
knows "he has only a short time" (12:12), and precisely because of that, he becomes more
vicious, not less, attacking first Israel and then the church across the whole church age. The
Great Tribulation, in other words, isn't a future seven-year countdown still waiting to start.
It's the name for the suffering the church has already been enduring — in the first century, in
every century since, and often still, right now, in communities across this region and around
the world facing real economic, social, or violent pressure for their faith.

## Sealed and safe in the middle of it

The comfort this session offers is not "you'll be taken out before it gets hard." It's the
opposite, and arguably better: you are sealed, marked, and kept — by God himself — in the very
middle of it. The 144,000/uncountable multitude aren't rescued from tribulation before it
starts; they come "out of the great tribulation" (7:14) having washed their robes in the Lamb's
blood, and are comforted by God with no more hunger, thirst, or tears (7:15–17). The promise is
presence and belonging through the hardship, not exemption from it.

## Questions for reflection

- Does it change anything for you to hear the Great Tribulation described as something the
  church has always been living inside, rather than a future event still to come?
- Where have you or your community already experienced the kind of pressure — economic,
  social, or worse — this passage describes, and where did you see God's comfort show up in the
  middle of it rather than as an escape from it?
- What does it mean to you that the "army" John hears counted turns out to be a worshiping
  multitude when he actually looks? How does that reshape what real spiritual strength looks
  like?

Whatever tribulation you are living through right now, this vision says the same thing said to
every generation before you: you are sealed, you are known by name, and you are already part of
a multitude no one could ever count — worshiping, together, before the throne.
