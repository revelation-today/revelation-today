## The answer before the questions

Here is something remarkable about how Revelation is built: before a single plague falls,
before the beast appears, before Babylon is even mentioned, John is shown a throne room. Two
whole chapters (Revelation 4–5) are given to nothing but worship — God enthroned in glory, the
Lamb who was slain found worthy to open the sealed scroll, every creature in heaven and on
earth crying out in praise. Everything that follows in the rest of the book — every seal,
trumpet, and bowl — flows out of this scene. It's not decoration before the "real" content
starts. It is the foundation everything else stands on.

Why does the book put worship first, before it addresses a single problem? Because the seven
churches John just finished writing to are struggling in exactly the ways worship answers:
wrong doctrine leading to wrong behavior, wrong self-evaluation leading to wrong actions, wrong
priorities leading to meaninglessness, hard circumstances tempting people to give up. In every
case, the cure is the same: know God as he actually is, and everything else reorders itself
around that knowing.

## Worship you already know how to do

Few places in the world need less convincing on this point than the church across Latin
America. Whether the room is a packed cathedral with incense rising and voices lifted for the
Sanctus, or a Pentecostal congregation with hands raised and the Spirit clearly, tangibly
moving — this region already knows, in its bones, that worship is not a warm-up act. It's
where you meet God, and meeting God changes everything else. That instinct is not a cultural
quirk to indulge before getting to the "serious" theology. It's precisely the response
Revelation itself argues is the answer to every crisis facing the church.

Notice a few details in the scene itself. John's first impression is God's overwhelming glory
— so intense it can't be looked at directly, echoing Moses, who asked to see God's glory and
was shown God's mercy instead (Exodus 33:18–20). That's worth sitting with: whatever judgments
follow later in Revelation, we're meant to read them through the lens of mercy, not menace.
Then there's the rainbow around the throne (Revelation 4:3) — the same sign God gave Noah as
an unconditional promise never to flood the earth again. Even the coming judgments, in other
words, are bounded, not unlimited. And there's the "sea of glass" before the throne (4:6) — the
sea, throughout Scripture, is where chaos and evil are pictured as living. Here it's frozen,
stilled, under God's feet. Whatever storms come later in the book, God is not threatened by
them, and neither, ultimately, are his people.

## One family at worship

The 24 elders surrounding the throne echo the 24 divisions of priests and worship leaders
King David established (1 Chronicles 24–25), but they also evoke the 12 tribes of Israel and
the 12 apostles together — Israel and the church pictured as one worshiping body, despite every
conflict between them elsewhere in Scripture. That matters here: whatever confessional
tradition shapes your worship — the Mass's ancient liturgy or a Pentecostal service's
Spirit-led freedom — this scene doesn't ask you to choose a side. It shows one throne room
where every legitimate expression of God's people gathers around the same worthy Lamb.

## Worship as the antidote

This session matters more than it might first appear for what's coming later in this series.
When we get to the harlot Babylon and the counterfeit security she offers — money, glamor,
influence dressed up as blessing — the answer will not primarily be a better argument. It will
be the same answer given here: a people who already know how to worship the true God with
their whole selves are far less likely to be seduced by a cheap substitute. Worship is not
just what we do when the sermon's over. It's the thing that reorders our loves so that
Babylon's offer stops looking attractive in the first place.

## Questions for reflection

- Think about your own experience of worship, whether liturgical or charismatic — when have
  you felt most aware that you were in God's presence, not just performing a routine?
- How does seeing God's judgments framed by mercy (the rainbow) and the sea of chaos stilled
  under his feet change how you feel about the hard chapters still ahead in Revelation?
- If worship really does reorder our priorities, what would need to change in your life for
  worship to shape your view of money, security, and status rather than the other way around?

Whatever else this series covers — beasts, bowls, a harlot, a New Jerusalem — remember where
it starts. Not with a threat, but with a throne room full of praise. That's the room every
other chapter of Revelation is preached from.
