## Not quite the popular picture

Ask most people to picture heaven and hell, and you'll usually hear something like this:
heaven is where good people go to be happy because they earned it; hell is where bad people go
to suffer because they deserve it. Revelation doesn't describe either one quite that way.

## Heaven as intimacy, not reward

Revelation paints heaven with a few consistent images: the comforted saints of chapter 7 and,
at far greater length, the New Jerusalem of chapter 21. Both converge on one central point —
heaven is defined by fellowship with God, not by earned comfort. That intimacy comes through
sharply once you notice the New Jerusalem is described as "the Bride of Christ" (21:2) — the
picture is nuptial closeness, not a reward ceremony.

The architecture makes the same point even more precisely. The New Jerusalem is shaped as a
cube (21:16) — and the only object shaped that way anywhere in the Old Testament is the Holy of
Holies (2 Chronicles 3:8), the innermost chamber of the temple where God's presence dwelt so
intensely that only the high priest could enter, and only once a year. Heaven, in other words,
isn't primarily a nice place. It's the place of maximum possible nearness to God — access to
his presence that used to be reserved for one man, once a year, now opened to everyone who
belongs to him.

## Total commitment, not a spectrum

The New Jerusalem is a cube; everything outside it is hell. There's no gradient, no partial
credit, no comfortable middle zone. That might sound harsh until you see what it's actually
naming: priority. If you are not fully given to something, something else has quietly taken
first place instead — and that's the real issue in any relationship, including this one. This
connects directly to the "third way" theme running through this whole series, from the very
first session on the Pax Romana onward: there was never a safe, uncommitted middle position
available. Heaven and hell make that same point at the largest possible scale.

## Hell as absence, not just punishment

So what is hell, then, if not primarily a torture chamber for bad behavior? Simply this: hell
is the place with no relationship with God. That description alone is enough to make it
terrible, once you notice how much rides on that relationship. Every good thing we treasure —
hope, joy, peace, purpose, forgiveness, even the ability to love well — has its actual source in
God, whether or not we notice or credit him for it day to day. We experience these gifts now
because this present life is the season in which God is actively courting us, patiently
pursuing relationship. That season ends. What's left, without God, is a downward spiral: no
forgiveness offered or received, bitterness with nowhere to go, hopelessness, ongoing fear —
not because God is inflicting torment out of anger, but because none of the things that make
life bearable have any other source but him.

Crucially: God does not force anyone into relationship with him. If someone does not want to be
fully his, in the end he honors that — but the result of that choice is a life without him, and
therefore without everything that flows from him. That is hell.

It's worth being honest, too, about how hell often gets used: as a threat, a scare tactic —
believe or else. Given everything above, that claim isn't technically false, but it misses the
point badly, and it borrows a method that belongs to the beast, not to the church. Fear and
manipulation are the devil's tools throughout Revelation (see the beast and the second beast's
coercive tactics). The church's actual calling is testimony — bearing faithful witness to what
is true and good, the way the two witnesses do later in Revelation — not manufacturing dread.

## Questions for reflection

- Does describing heaven as intimacy with God, rather than a reward for good behavior, change
  how you think about it, or how you talk about it with others?
- Where in your own life do you notice good things — peace, hope, the capacity to forgive — and
  do you trace them back to God as their source, or take them for granted as though they simply
  belong to you?
- Have you ever used the threat of hell, or had it used on you, mainly as a scare tactic? How
  does thinking of hell as "life without the source of everything good" change how you'd talk
  about it with someone you love?

Heaven is not a prize for good behavior, and hell is not primarily a punishment God inflicts out
of anger. Both are simply what's true when every good thing traces back to one source: with
him, or without him. There is no third option — and that's exactly why the invitation now
matters so much.
