## The real prosperity, finally revealed

Every session in this series has, in one way or another, been building toward this one. We've
watched a counterfeit gospel of security ride out disguised as a conquering hero (the first
horseman). We've watched a glamorous economic system called Babylon offer wealth and status in
exchange for faithfulness, only to collapse without warning in a single hour, abandoned by her
own allies. Now, at last, John shows us what real, lasting security actually looks like — and
it makes Babylon's counterfeit look exactly as cheap as it always was.

## Hearing one thing, seeing another

John hears about "the bride, the wife of the Lamb" (21:9) and then sees her as a dazzling city,
the New Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven (21:10). It's the same literary move Revelation
has used before — the Lion turns out to be a slaughtered Lamb (5:5–6), the counted army turns
out to be an uncountable worshiping multitude (7:4, 9) — and it signals the same thing every
time: pay attention, because the reality is bigger and stranger than the first image suggests.

## Everything Babylon promised, actually delivered

Set the New Jerusalem directly against Babylon and the contrast is deliberate and total.
Babylon is a woman, a harlot; the New Jerusalem is a woman, a bride — same shape, opposite
character. Babylon's glamor is built on exploitation and idolatry; the New Jerusalem's glory is
God's own presence, freely given. Babylon causes suffering; the New Jerusalem removes it
entirely — "no more death or mourning or crying or pain" (21:4), with every tear personally
wiped away by God himself. Babylon's wealth is extracted from others, even treating human
beings as merchandise; the New Jerusalem's riches are gift, not extraction — living water
"without cost" (21:6), freely offered to anyone thirsty. Babylon falls in a single hour and is
gone forever; the New Jerusalem is described in exhaustive, loving detail specifically because
it lasts. Whatever the harlot promised — security, wealth, status, permanence — the New
Jerusalem is where that promise is finally, truly kept, without the exploitation, without the
expiration date.

## The Holy of Holies, thrown open

The city is a cube (21:16) — deliberately recalling the Holy of Holies, the one place in the
old temple shaped that way, where God's presence dwelt so intensely only the high priest could
enter, only once a year, at real risk to his life. Now, in the New Jerusalem, that access is
thrown open to everyone who belongs to Christ. Nobody stands further from God than anyone else.
The old system of graduated access — priests closer, ordinary people farther out, foreigners
excluded entirely — is gone. Whatever confessional tradition you come from, and however that
access to God's presence has been understood in your own church life, this vision points to the
same destination: full, unmediated nearness to God, for everyone who is his.

## Every tribe, every nation, one city

The twelve gates recall the twelve tribes of Israel; the twelve foundations, the twelve
apostles. Israel and the church, pictured as one people, one city, one household — a note this
series has sounded before and sounds again here at the very end. And notice who else shows up:
"the nations will walk by its light, and the kings of the earth will bring their splendor into
it" (21:24) — an image lifted straight from Isaiah's vision of the nations streaming toward
Jerusalem (Isaiah 60), except here what they bring isn't extracted wealth paraded in triumph,
the way Babylon's kings once "grew rich from her excessive luxuries" (18:3). It's glory and
honor freely given back to God, from Abraham's ancient calling to be a blessing to all
nations, now finally, fully realized.

## The tree of life, and no more curse

The final image closes the whole story of Scripture: a river of life flowing from God's throne,
and the tree of life — last seen barred from Adam and Eve after Eden — now standing open, its
fruit and leaves available to everyone, "and there will be no more curse" (22:3). Every wound
this book has named — persecution, poverty, exile, injustice, death itself — finds its ending
here, not through a better economic system or a better empire, but through the direct,
unmediated presence of the God who made everything new.

## Questions for reflection

- Where in your own life have you looked for the security, wealth, or permanence the harlot
  promises, when the New Jerusalem was offering the real, lasting version all along?
- What does it mean to you personally that access to God's presence — once reserved for one
  priest, once a year — is now open to you, without restriction?
- This series began by asking who you actually credit for your security (the Pax Romana). Having
  walked through the whole book, how would you answer that question differently now?

This is where the whole book has been heading from its very first line: not away from the
world into some thin, spiritual escape, but toward a real city, filled with real people from
every nation, dwelling forever in the real presence of God. That is the prosperity Revelation
actually promises — free, permanent, and already guaranteed by the Lamb who was slain and lives.
