## A golden age, if you played along

Imagine someone offering you this deal: security, prosperity, cultural achievement, and law
and order, all guaranteed — on one condition. You have to publicly acknowledge who it's all
coming from, and show it.

That was the real offer on the table for the first readers of Revelation. It had a name: the
Pax Romana, the "Roman Peace." From about 27 BC to the late second century AD, the Roman
Empire enjoyed a long stretch of relative stability — few civil wars, real economic
prosperity (at least for many), and a flourishing of art and culture. Underneath it sat a
whole theology: the gods had chosen Rome, the emperor was their agent on earth, and every
blessing worth having — security, peace, prosperity, fertility — flowed through submission to
him. Therefore, the emperor deserved worship.

This wasn't background noise. It was on every coin, in every procession, at every public
meal, in the games, in the statues that filled every city square. Cities in Asia Minor — right
where the seven churches of Revelation stood — competed with each other to build temples to
the emperor, hoping to win his favor. And underneath the golden surface was the uncomfortable
truth: this "golden age" was purchased with violence and conquest, and it was only golden for
those willing to go along.

## The believer's dilemma

For Christians, this created a genuine, everyday problem. Jesus was their true king — not the
emperor. They trusted him, not the state, to provide for them. And they simply could not offer
the emperor the worship the whole system demanded.

That left three options: deny the faith outright and blend in; hold to the faith fully and
risk real loss — social exclusion, poverty, prison, even death; or find a middle path —
confess the emperor with your mouth while staying loyal to Jesus in your heart. Outward
conformity, inward faithfulness. Safe, convenient, and quietly corrosive.

John, the author of Revelation, chose the second option, and it cost him exactly what you'd
expect: exile on the island of Patmos, "your brother and partner in the suffering and kingdom
and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus" (Revelation 1:9). The book he wrote from that
exile is, in a real sense, counter-propaganda — an argument, image by image, that Jesus is the
true king, and that Rome's dazzling peace was never as golden as it looked.

## Our own Pax Romana

This is where the sermon stops being ancient history. Every generation gets its own version of
this exact question, dressed differently. Ask yourself: who do you actually credit for your
security? Where do you expect the answer to your deepest problems to come from — from God, or
from your bank account, your career, your health, your government, your nation? And when
holding that answer publicly would cost you something — socially, economically, with family —
would you still hold it?

Across Latin America, this question shows up in more than one costume. Sometimes it wears the
face of political power that asks for more loyalty than any human government deserves.
Sometimes — and this will come up again later in this series, especially around the harlot
Babylon material — it wears the face of a promise that God's blessing shows up mainly as
money, health, and visible success, so that "trusting God" quietly becomes indistinguishable
from trusting the next paycheck or the next breakthrough. None of that makes prosperity itself
evil, any more than Rome's peace and order were evil in themselves. The question Revelation
asks was never "is it good?" It was always: who gets the credit, and what does that credit
demand of you?

## The third way, unmasked

Revelation is remarkably honest about that "middle path." It never presents outward
conformity with inward faithfulness as a wise compromise. It exposes it for what it is:
half-heartedness. That's precisely the charge leveled later against the church in Laodicea —
neither hot nor cold, comfortable and self-satisfied, and much closer to disaster than it
realizes.

And notice what Revelation offers instead of a call to abandon the world or retreat into a
private, safe faith. Before anything else happens in the book — before any plague, any
judgment, any confrontation with the beast — John is shown a throne room, filled with
unceasing worship of God and the Lamb (Revelation 4–5). That is the book's actual answer to
every form of Pax Romana, ancient or modern: not withdrawal, but the question of who truly sits
on the throne of your life, answered out loud, together, in worship.

## Questions for reflection

- Where do you find yourself expecting security, provision, or your sense of worth to come
  from — and is it really from God?
- Where do you say something publicly that you don't quite believe inwardly, just to belong?
- If your convictions cost you something concrete this week — socially, financially, with
  family — would you still hold them? What would that look like in practice?

The Pax Romana collapsed centuries ago. The temptation it represented never did — it just
changed clothes. The good news Revelation offers is that the true King already reigns. He
doesn't ask for a performance. He invites us into real, whole-hearted worship — and he has
already won, not through force, but through self-giving love.
