## The most important session in this series

Of everything in this regional pack, this is the one session worth slowing down for and
giving the most room to. Revelation 17–18's vision of the harlot Babylon is, more than any
other passage in the book, a direct, pointed confrontation with a message many of you have
heard preached in good faith, in churches you love, by leaders who genuinely believe it: that
wealth, health, and visible success are the reliable proof of God's favor. This session
addresses that message head-on — not as an attack on anyone who has taught or believed it, but
as an invitation into something more durable than what it offers.

## Untangling a crowded chapter

Revelation 17 is crowded with characters: a beast, seven hills, ten kings, and a harlot riding
on all of it. Start with the harlot herself. She sits enthroned, dressed in purple, scarlet,
gold, and jewels — the picture of imperial wealth and glamor — with a cup in her hand "full of
abominations and the filth of her adulteries" (17:4), and a name on her forehead: "Babylon the
Great, the Mother of Prostitutes." The kings of the earth "committed adultery with her," and
she has made "the inhabitants of the earth intoxicated with the wine of her adulteries"
(17:2). She sits on a scarlet beast with seven heads and ten horns — the same beast from
chapter 13 — identifying her with imperial Rome and, by extension, with every system that
functions the same way. The seven heads are seven hills (a clear pointer to Rome, the
"city on seven hills," for John's first readers) and also seven kings; the ten horns are kings
who hand their power to the beast for "one hour." And the chapter's ending is the real shock:
the beast and the kings who once adored her suddenly "hate the prostitute. They will bring her
to ruin and leave her naked; they will eat her flesh and burn her with fire" (17:16).

## What she actually represents

Trace her back through the Old Testament and the picture sharpens. Jeremiah calls unfaithful
Judah a harlot with "a prostitute's forehead" (Jeremiah 3:3), seducing others into
sin (2:33) — yet in Revelation, the harlot is dressed like the Old Testament high priest,
in materials straight from the temple offering list (Exodus 25, 28) with "Holy to the Lord"
inverted into "Mother of Abominations" written on her forehead instead. She is what a former
relationship with God looks like once it has curdled into idolatry — a warning aimed first and
sharpest at God's own people, the way Ezekiel accused Jerusalem itself of acting as a harlot
(Ezekiel 16). Tyre and Nineveh get the same label elsewhere for exactly the same reason:
economic wealth built through exploitation, dressed up as blessing (Isaiah 23; Nahum 3).

So is Babylon simply the church gone bad? Not quite — she's a system in her own right, the
glamorous, moneyed, propaganda-soaked partner riding on top of raw political and military
power. The beast is brute force; the harlot is what makes that force look attractive, secure,
and worth trusting. Together they form the devil's whole counterfeit gospel: security bought
through fear of violence, wealth built by exploiting the poor and excluding anyone who won't
comply, a feeling of superiority, and the silencing of any inconvenient voice that names what's
really happening (see Revelation 13:4, 7, 10, 13–14, 16–17; 17:6). But the church is always at
risk of being pulled into her orbit — "so that you're forced to keep reflecting and stepping
out of it, day after day," as the text itself puts it (18:4), "because Babylon is everywhere."

## Naming the collision directly

This is where we need to be plain rather than vague. The prosperity gospel — the teaching that
faith reliably produces wealth and health, that financial giving functions as a kind of
spiritual transaction guaranteeing return, that a leader's visible wealth is itself evidence of
God's anointing — is, functionally, the harlot's offer wearing modern clothes. Not because
wanting provision, praying boldly for healing, or working hard and prospering are themselves
wrong; Scripture nowhere condemns those things. The harlot's danger is specifically the
trade she's built on: faithfulness to God exchanged for glamor, security, and gain — with the
cross, the possibility of real cost, and the call to costly love for the poor quietly dropped
out of the message.

Look again at what the harlot is actually selling and notice how precisely it maps onto that
trade: security purchased through fear rather than trust; wealth generated by treating people —
even, chillingly, "human beings sold as slaves" (18:13) — as commodities alongside cargo;
untouchable self-confidence ("I sit enthroned as queen... I will never mourn," 18:7) that has
no room left for repentance or humility; and glamor that photographs beautifully while resting,
underneath, on real human suffering. None of this makes prosperity, business success, or a
comfortable life inherently sinful — trade and wealth aren't condemned here. What Revelation is
asking is sharper and more personal: what spirit lies behind the wealth, and how do you respond
when it's threatened? Do you mourn right alongside everyone else when the economy crumbles
(18:10–17)? Would you support real injustice to protect your own financial interest? If so,
Revelation's own verdict is blunt: you're standing inside Babylon, "and we need to leave"
(18:4).

## The invitation underneath the warning

This is not a call to despise wealth, success, or a good and provided-for life — Scripture
never asks that. It's an invitation to a security that doesn't depend on any of those things
holding steady, because the harlot's security never does. Her own former allies — the kings and
the beast who once adored her — turn on her without warning and destroy her (17:16), a pattern
that keeps repeating in history: political and economic systems that promise permanent security
collapsing within a single generation, sometimes within a single year. If your sense of being
loved by God rests on your bank balance, your health, or your platform, you have built on
exactly the ground Revelation says gives way first. The real, unshakable security this book
actually promises — the New Jerusalem's presence of God, permanent and unearned — is where this
series will land in its closing sessions. That's the true prosperity being offered. This one is
the counterfeit.

## Questions for reflection

- Where have you heard teaching that quietly measured God's favor mainly by wealth, health, or
  visible success? How do you hold onto genuine hope for God's provision without falling into
  that trade?
- The harlot's list of goods includes human beings alongside cargo (18:13) — where do you see
  people treated as assets rather than image-bearers in systems you participate in?
- What would it look like, concretely, for you to "come out of her" (18:4) in an area of your
  life where security has quietly become more about money or status than about God?

Babylon looks glamorous right up until the hour she falls. The Lamb looked like nothing right
up until he was revealed as worthy of all worship. Choose your security carefully — one of
those two outlasts the other.
