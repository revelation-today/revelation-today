## Four riders, one puzzle

When the Lamb opens the first four seals, four horsemen ride out — white, red, black, and pale
— bringing conquest, war, famine, and death (Revelation 6:1–8). Three of the four are easy to
read: they bring exactly the destruction their color and description suggest. But the first
horseman has puzzled readers for centuries, because on the surface he looks like the hero of
the story.

## The case for a good rider — and why it falls apart

The first horseman rides a white horse (white is a positive color throughout Revelation), wears
a crown, and "rode out as a conqueror bent on conquest." Some readers have taken this as a
picture of Christ or the gospel going out to conquer the nations — after all, Jesus is also
described elsewhere as a rider on a white horse with many crowns (Revelation 19:11).

But look closer, and the case collapses. God strikes the bow from the hand of Gog and Magog,
the Bible's own picture of the end-time enemy (Ezekiel 39:3) — meaning a bow marks this rider
as hostile, not holy. The demonic locusts later in Revelation also wear crowns (9:7), so a crown
alone proves nothing. "Conquering" elsewhere in the book describes the beast conquering the
saints, not Christ's gospel advancing (Revelation 11:7, 13:7). And decisively: the four
horsemen function as a single unit, drawn from Zechariah's vision of four horses sent to patrol
the whole earth (Zechariah 1:8–15; 6:1–8) and from Ezekiel's four plagues — sword, famine, wild
beasts, and pestilence (Ezekiel 14:12–23). All four belong together. Either all four riders
serve God's purposes in the same way, or none of them do — and the last three are plainly not
heroic.

The resolution: the first horseman is evil pretending to be good — a counterfeit Christ.
That's exactly the pattern the passage is building. Chapter 5 has just revealed Jesus as the
only one worthy to open the scroll. In direct response, the devil's true nature is unveiled: he
shows up disguised as a conquering hero, offering victory and security — and seven verses later
leaves nothing but war, injustice, and death in his wake. The devil cannot deliver on his own
promises. That's why the next scene is the martyrs' cry, "How long, O Lord?" (6:9–11) — the
mask has already slipped.

## The fake gospel that matters most here

This is the single most important point of contact this whole series has with a movement many
of you have grown up in, sat under, or have family members deeply committed to: prosperity
teaching. The first horseman's whole strategy is to look exactly like triumphant faith — a
crown, a conquest, an unbroken record of victory — while quietly leaving out the cross,
leaving out cost, leaving out the slaughtered Lamb who was just revealed as the true King one
chapter earlier. He offers security and conquest without ever having been wounded for it. That
is not a historical curiosity about first-century Rome. It is a live description of any gospel,
ancient or modern, that promises unbroken victory, health, and wealth as the guaranteed sign of
God's favor — and quietly drops the part where following Jesus can mean real loss.

To be clear: this is not a claim that wanting a good life, praying for healing, or trusting God
for provision is wrong. Scripture is full of God's real concern for his people's bodies and
needs. The warning is narrower and more specific: watch for any gospel that promises the crown
without ever mentioning the cross — where "if your faith is strong enough" becomes the whole
explanation for suffering, and where a preacher's own visible wealth becomes proof of his
spiritual authority rather than a question worth asking. The devil's counterfeit isn't obvious
evil. It's a shinier version of the real thing, offered without the one part that actually costs
anything.

## What the other three riders confirm

The second horseman brings war — persecution of those who won't buy into the promises. The
third brings a strange, specific injustice: luxury goods (oil and wine) untouched while the
price of basic grain — a day's wage for a single day's food — becomes crushing for the poor
(Revelation 6:5–6). This is economic injustice dressed as normal commerce: those with means are
protected, those without are squeezed. The fourth horseman, Death, simply names the outcome of
the first three combined.

Read together, the horsemen sketch a whole system: deception first, then coercion of those who
resist, then economic pressure on the poor who can't comply, ending in death. That system
reappears later in Revelation as the beast and the harlot — this is the same playbook,
introduced here in miniature.

## Questions for reflection

- Where have you heard the gospel presented mainly as guaranteed victory, health, and wealth,
  with little mention of the cross or the possibility of real cost?
- How do you tell the difference between genuinely trusting God for provision and quietly
  measuring God's favor by your bank balance or your health?
- The third horseman's injustice protects luxury while crushing basic needs for the poor — where
  do you see that same pattern in your own economy or community today?

Whatever looks like an unbroken record of victory, offered without any mention of the cross,
deserves a second look. The real King already proved his worth — not with a crown that skipped
the wound, but with a wound that earned the crown.
