# Heaven and Hell: What Really Awaits Us?

## Opening: Two pictures worth setting aside

Popular imagination pictures heaven as a reward for good behavior — a pleasant place granted to people who scored well enough — and hell as a torture chamber for the wicked, a punishment matched in kind to the crime. Revelation doesn't actually describe either one this way, and it's worth setting both popular pictures aside before looking at what the text itself gives us. What we find instead is closer to something your own tradition has long taught, even if it's rarely put in quite these terms: heaven and hell are not first about reward and punishment at all. They are about the presence or absence of God himself.

## Heaven as intimacy, not scenery

Revelation paints heaven with two main images: the comfort of the saints gathered in chapter 7, and the New Jerusalem described at length in chapter 21. Both converge on the same single point — heaven is defined by fellowship with God, not by any of the incidental scenery around that fellowship. This intimacy comes through sharply once you notice that the New Jerusalem is described as the Bride of Christ (21:2) — not a place Christ visits, but a people united to him in the closest relationship Scripture has language for.

The imagery draws heavily on the temple, itself an echo of Eden, discussed earlier in this series. The New Jerusalem's shape makes the point unmistakably: it is a cube (21:16), and the only object in the entire Old Testament with that same shape is the Holy of Holies — the space so saturated with God's presence that only the high priest could enter, and only once a year. Heaven, then, is not a reward handed out separately from God. It is simply unrestricted access to the presence for which the Holy of Holies was only ever a temporary, limited approximation. This is precisely the trajectory your tradition names theosis: not distant approval from God, but actual, ever-deepening union with him.

## Hell as absence, not a matching punishment

If heaven is intimacy with God, hell is described with equal simplicity: it is the condition of no relationship with God at all. And this is what makes it so devastating — not primarily fire or torment as an inflicted penalty, but the total absence of the one source from which every good thing flows. Hope, joy, peace, purpose, forgiveness — even the things we assume we generate ourselves — all in fact originate in God. We experience them now because this present life is, in a real sense, the season of God's patient courtship of us. That season ends, and what remains, without God, is what the text calls a downward spiral: unforgiveness, bitterness, hopelessness, fear — not because God inflicts these things as punishment, but because they are what is left when the source of every good gift is refused.

God does not force fellowship on anyone. If someone does not want to be fully united with him, he lets that choice stand — but the consequence is a life without him, and therefore without everything that flows from him. That absence, not an arbitrary sentence, is what Revelation means by hell.

## An honest word about emphasis

Here is a place worth being candid about a real difference in tone, not a doctrinal disagreement to be argued away. The source material states its case about heaven and hell with more definiteness — cube-shaped city, clear either/or, hell explained fairly directly as the natural consequence of refused relationship — than Orthodox tradition typically prefers when speaking of the final state of souls. Your tradition's instinct here is not evasion; it is a genuine, long-held theological caution. The Church has always resisted turning eschatology into a fixed system with every detail settled — leaving real room, for instance, around questions like the nature and duration of the "aerial toll-houses," or how exactly divine energies are experienced differently by the saved and the lost, or the ultimate scope of God's mercy toward those outside visible communion with the Church. This caution is not a weaker faith; if anything it takes seriously that we are speaking of realities beyond what any of us has directly seen, and it declines to over-specify what Scripture itself leaves in some places genuinely open.

That said, the core claim this material makes — heaven as intimacy, hell as its absence, both flowing from the same reality of who God is rather than from two different divine dispositions — sits comfortably within, and arguably strengthens, the same tradition. You do not need to accept every detail of how the argument is framed here to receive its central insight.

## What this is not for

It's worth being direct about one more thing: hell is often used as a threat, a tool to frighten people into repentance — "believe, or else." Given everything above, that claim isn't strictly false, but it profoundly misses the point, and it borrows a method that belongs to the beast, not to the Church. Fear and manipulation are the beast's tools throughout Revelation; the Church's defining mark is instead her testimony — a patient, faithful witness borne out of love, not coercion. If this teaching is ever used simply to frighten rather than to invite toward union with God, something essential has gone wrong in how it's being handled.

## Closing

Heaven is not a prize handed out at a distance. It is the fullness of the union with God your tradition has always named theosis — the completion of the story that began in Eden and runs through the temple, through Christ's own body, to the New Jerusalem. Hell is simply what remains when that union is finally, fully refused. Neither is a system to master in every detail. Both are, finally, about whether we will be found in the presence we were always made for.
