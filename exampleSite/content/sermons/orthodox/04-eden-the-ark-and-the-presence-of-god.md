# Eden, the Ark, and the Presence of God

## Opening: The whole story in one line

If you had to summarize the entire Bible in a single sentence, you could do worse than this: God relentlessly pursues face-to-face fellowship with the people he made, despite every human attempt to keep him at arm's length. That is the story running from Eden to the tabernacle, to Jesus's own body, to the New Jerusalem — and it is, not coincidentally, the deepest logic behind your own tradition's theology of theosis: that the goal of human life is not merely to be forgiven, but to be united with God, to partake, as Saint Peter says, of the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). This session traces that one story across Scripture, and it will feel, by the end, less like new information and more like a homecoming.

## The garden was never meant to be lost

Genesis begins with God dwelling among his creatures, not at a distance. He walks with Adam and Eve, meets with them in the garden, gives them a sanctuary to tend as priests of creation. This is already a kind of temple — the first place where heaven and earth overlap without division. After the fall, humanity is exiled from that fellowship, and things spiral: Cain kills Abel, and even after God mercifully marks Cain for protection, Cain "looks for his own security instead and builds a city" (Genesis 4:16–17). His descendants keep building, generation after generation, their own architecture of safety apart from God, culminating in the arrogant tower at Babel. Every human city in Genesis, in other words, is a counterfeit temple — a substitute refuge built by people no longer welcome in the real one.

But God never abandons the goal. Even from Cain's line, "people begin again to call on the name of the Lord" (Genesis 4:26), and God chooses to make a name for Abraham exactly where Babel failed to make one for itself.

## God moves in with his people

When God brings Israel out of Egypt, he does something remarkable: rather than commanding his people to ascend to him, he comes down and pitches a tent among them, then later builds a house — the temple. This is not incidental. The temple is deliberately covered in imagery borrowed straight from Eden: the logic being that since humanity could no longer have fellowship with God in the garden, God would instead have fellowship with humanity in the temple. And crucially, this was designed from the beginning to include the nations, not only Israel.

At the innermost point of that structure stood the Holy of Holies — the one place on earth where God's manifest presence dwelt, entered only by the high priest, only once a year, on the Day of Atonement. Every other square inch of that sacred geography measured distance from God's presence. This is the architecture your own church building still echoes: the icon screen, the sanctuary, the sense of graduated holiness moving toward the altar. It is not an arbitrary custom. It is the temple's own logic, carried forward.

## Even this was not the end

Even that arrangement, though, was provisional. When Israel broke covenant, Ezekiel records something devastating: God's presence actually departs the temple (Ezekiel 10:18–19). And yet the same book that records that departure also promises the return — God's glory re-entering through the East Gate (Ezekiel 43:1–2), accompanied by a new and startling image: a river flowing from the altar itself, bringing life and abundance wherever it reaches (Ezekiel 47). Hold onto that image; it resurfaces, transformed, at the very end of the Bible.

## Jesus is the temple

Here is where the whole story turns. In the New Testament, Jesus does not merely visit the temple — he *is* the temple (John 2:19–22). When he died, the veil guarding the Holy of Holies tore in two, not as a small dramatic flourish but as the announcement that the barrier separating God's manifest presence from ordinary humanity has been permanently removed. What no ritual under the old covenant could finally accomplish, the incarnation and crucifixion accomplish outright: God himself, in flesh, has entered fully into union with humanity, and offers that same union to us.

This is precisely the ground on which your tradition's understanding of theosis stands. Union with God is not a late theological invention — it is the trajectory Scripture has been tracing since Genesis 3, when humanity was first put outside the garden. Christ reverses that exile from the inside, taking on our nature so that we might, in Saint Athanasius's famous phrase, become by grace what he is by nature.

## The church, and then the city

With Christ's death, Paul tells us, the temple becomes the church, since the church is Christ's own body (1 Corinthians 6:19). And in Revelation, this reaches its final form: the church appears as the Bride of Christ, who is also the New Jerusalem — the place, at long last, where fellowship between God and humanity is entirely undisturbed. Ezekiel's river returns, now flowing from the throne of God and the Lamb (Revelation 22:1–5), watering the tree of life that was lost at Eden's gate. The whole arc closes precisely where it opened, except now the barrier is gone for good.

## Closing

Every time you venerate an icon, you are not worshipping wood and paint — you are honoring the truth that matter itself can bear God's presence, because the Word became flesh and dwelt among us. Every time you approach the chalice, you are stepping, however briefly, into the Holy of Holies that used to bar all but one man, one day a year. This is not a doctrine bolted onto Revelation from outside. It is the very story Revelation was written to complete: from garden, to tent, to temple, to the Son himself, to the Bride adorned as a New Jerusalem — one continuous pursuit, ending not in information about God, but in union with him.
