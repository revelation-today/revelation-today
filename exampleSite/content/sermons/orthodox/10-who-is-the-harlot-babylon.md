# Who Is the Harlot Babylon?

## Opening: A question people keep answering too quickly

Ask around, and you'll hear the harlot Babylon confidently identified as the Pope, a particular world power, a global economic system, or even "the corrupted institutional church" broadly speaking. Revelation 17 is crowded enough — a beast, seven hills, ten kings, a harlot — that it rewards patient unpacking rather than a quick guess. And the identification the text itself supports is both more historically anchored and more searchingly relevant than any of those popular candidates.

## The scarlet beast and the seven hills

The harlot rides a beast with seven heads and ten horns (17:3), closely paralleling the first beast of chapter 13. The seven heads are explicitly interpreted for us as "seven hills" (17:9) — for John's first readers, an unmistakable reference to Rome, the famous city of seven hills, seat of the emperor who claimed titles like "Lord of lords" as though he were God's equal. This is not, whatever later imaginative readings have proposed, a coded prophecy about some future occupant of that city. It's a description of first-century imperial power, in the place it actually held court.

At the same time, the text also treats these seven heads as kings in succession, "five have fallen, one is, the other has not yet come" (17:10) — most plausibly a sequence of Roman emperors, with the point being urgency ("the time is short, stay alert") rather than a precise, permanently fixed count. The beast itself then appears as an eighth king who is somehow also one of the seven (17:11), a deliberate, mocking parody of God's own self-description as the one "who was, and is, and is to come" (1:8). Whatever invincibility Rome projected, the text insists: this is a false eternity, headed for destruction, not the real one.

## The harlot herself

The woman riding this beast is deliberately drawn as a dark mirror of two other women in the book: the woman of chapter 12 (Israel, or the Theotokos, discussed in an earlier session) and the Bride of chapters 19 and 21 (the Church, the New Jerusalem). Where the Bride is dressed in fine linen reflecting the righteous deeds of the saints, the harlot is dressed in identical splendor — purple, scarlet, gold, jewels — covering not righteousness but corruption. Where the Bride's security rests in God, the harlot's security rests in "the nations and kings" she has entangled herself with (17:15, 18). She is also drawn in close, damning comparison to Jezebel — the corrupting figure already condemned in the letter to Thyatira — sharing her seduction of God's people, her false glamour, her persecution of the faithful, and, ultimately, her sudden, complete downfall at the hands of the very powers she once seduced (17:16).

The deeper background comes from the prophets: Jeremiah's Judah, called a harlot for chasing other lovers with no shame (Jeremiah 2–4); Ezekiel's Jerusalem, pictured wearing the high priest's own sacred garments while playing the harlot (Ezekiel 16); Tyre and Nineveh, both judged as harlots for the same fusion of idolatry and commerce. The pattern across every one of these texts is consistent: "harlot" describes what a covenant relationship with God looks like once it has been traded away for wealth, security, or status gained through unfaithfulness. Babylon is not "the world" in some generic sense. She is specifically what happens when a people who once belonged to God sell that belonging for something else.

## The economic aspect, and a warning close to home

Revelation 18's list of Babylon's trade goods — twenty-eight items, a deliberately complete-sounding number — echoes almost exactly Ezekiel's earlier list of Tyre's luxury trade. The point in both cases is the same: an economic system that makes some people fabulously wealthy while resting on exploitation and idolatry, and that ties political power to commercial gain until the two are indistinguishable. The kings of the earth "committed adultery with her" and "grew rich from her excessive luxuries" (18:3, 9) — a phrase meaning something more than casual trade. It describes an entangled, compromising alliance, entered into willingly, for profit.

## Is Babylon the Church gone astray?

This question deserves an honest, careful answer, because it is easy either to dismiss it too quickly or to overstate it into an accusation. Babylon is not simply the visible Church in another costume — she is her own system, the counterpart to the Bride, not identical with any single institution. But the source material is direct about this much: the Church is always at risk of drifting into Babylon's territory, especially where wealth, status, or political entanglement start to feel like proof of God's favor rather than temptations to be examined. Since Babylon shares so much in common with Jezebel — a figure specifically condemned for corrupting leadership from within — this carries a pointed caution for church leadership in particular, though the call to vigilance belongs to every believer, not only those with authority.

This is precisely where the sharpest, most honest tension for many Orthodox communities lives — not as an accusation from outside, but as a mirror worth actually looking into. In places where the Church's institutional life has grown closely woven into national identity, state favor, or political power — where questioning a government's actions can feel indistinguishable from questioning the Church itself — this chapter's warning is not about someone else's empire. It is worth sitting with the question honestly: has the security of established status, state protection, or national prestige quietly become part of what we trust, alongside or instead of God himself? The text doesn't draw Babylon with sharp, easily externalized edges precisely so that this reflection stays urgent and personal, not settled once and applied only to others (18:4 — "come out of her, my people").

## Closing

Babylon is Rome, first and plainly, for the readers John was writing to. But she is also a recurring pattern rather than a single, closed historical event — any nation, system, or even church body that trades covenant faithfulness for wealth, security, and the world's approval risks stepping into her role. The call at the center of this chapter is not primarily to correctly identify Babylon somewhere out there. It is to keep asking, honestly and often, whether we ourselves have quietly moved into her territory.
