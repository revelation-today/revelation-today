# Laodicea: Neither Hot Nor Cold

## 1. The one church with nothing good said about it

Of all seven letters, Laodicea's is probably the most frequently preached, so many of us already associate it with being "lukewarm." But the details behind that image matter more than usual here, because Laodicea is the only one of the seven churches that receives no praise at all — not even a small commendation before the correction. That alone should make us slow down before we read it.

## 2. The city behind the letter

Laodicea sat at the junction of several major trade routes and was extraordinarily wealthy — something like the Swiss bank of Asia Minor. Its sheep-farming industry produced valuable black wool, and it was home to a well-known medical center famous for its eye ointments. This is a city that, by every worldly measure, had made it.

Jesus comes to this congregation as "the Amen, the faithful and true witness" — a deliberate contrast, since the church itself has become something of a counterfeit. Their core problem isn't a single obvious sin. It's a lack of self-awareness: they consider themselves to be doing well, when in fact they are failing in precisely the areas where they believe themselves strong (3:17).

## 3. The image of lukewarm water

Jesus approaches them like a merchant they would recognize, offering them — free of cost — the very things they believe they already possess in the finest available quality. Their assumption is that a respectable, comfortable life adds up to a healthy church: no glaring public sin, steady giving, regular religious observance.

Their real problem is described through the temperature of water. In that culture, water was valued in two useful states: hot, like the healing springs nearby, or cold, refreshing to drink. Laodicea's own water supply arrived lukewarm through an aqueduct, having lost its heat along the way — a local joke that Jesus turns into a spiritual diagnosis. The church has neither useful quality. It is lukewarm water, good for nothing but being spat out.

There are many ways to be a genuinely useful church — through evangelism, prayer, care for the poor, and more. Laodicea had settled into none of them, and had become just as useless as its own tepid water.

## 4. Handling this text carefully

The image Jesus uses next is unusually intense: he tells this self-satisfied, wealthy congregation that it is, in truth, "wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" (3:17) — and he offers to clothe their nakedness (3:18). In its original context, that language is a piece of pointed spiritual honesty aimed at waking a self-deceived congregation up before it's too late, not a public spectacle meant to humiliate.

It's worth pausing here, though, because exposed nakedness as an image of shame carries an unusually heavy weight in cultures where public shame is one of the most severe social experiences a person or family can undergo. If we dwell on that image itself — imagining public exposure, picturing the humiliation in vivid detail — we risk being overwhelmed by precisely the wrong thing, and missing what Jesus is actually diagnosing. His concern is not to shame Laodicea before spectators. His concern is self-deception: a church so convinced of its own success that it can no longer see its actual need for him.

So rather than dwelling on the image of exposure, sit with the simpler, sharper question underneath it: **do I actually know my own need, or have I mistaken comfort for health?** That is a question best asked quietly, alone, in your own heart before God — not something to be pressed onto others in a group setting, and not something we need to illustrate with more vivid shame-imagery than the text requires.

## 5. The invitation

Despite everything, Jesus does not walk away from this church. He calls them out of their complacency and knocks at the door — language that, in its Old Testament background, is not an altar call to unbelievers but the tender call of a lover to the one he loves (echoing Song of Songs 5:1–5). Even to the church with nothing praiseworthy said about it, Jesus says: I am still here, still knocking, still wanting in.

And the promise, if they listen, is extraordinary: they will sit with him on his own throne. Stop and consider what that means — in the throne-room vision of chapters 4 and 5, no one else sits; every other figure stands or falls prostrate in worship. Is that promise enough to shake a self-satisfied church out of the way it sees itself?

## 6. A different way to apply this letter

Given everything above, we're going to handle this letter's application differently than we might handle a passage that lends itself more naturally to open discussion. Rather than naming out loud where our own congregation or our own family might be "lukewarm" — which risks becoming exactly the kind of public exposure this session has already flagged as a real danger — take a few quiet minutes now with pen and paper, privately, just for yourself.

Consider writing down, only for your own eyes:

- One area of my life where I have quietly assumed I'm doing fine, without really examining it before God.
- One place where comfort, security, or a good reputation may have replaced real spiritual dependence on Jesus.
- One honest sentence I would want to say to Jesus right now, if no one else could hear it.

You don't need to share this with anyone. Fold it, keep it, pray over it this week. The point was never public exposure. It was always private honesty — the very thing Laodicea, for all its wealth, had lost.

## 7. Closing

Jesus's harshest words in Revelation are aimed not at the persecuted or the poor, but at the comfortable and self-assured. That's worth sitting with regardless of your circumstances. And his final word to this church, even after everything, is still an invitation: to sit with him where only the worthy Lamb sits. That door remains open to anyone honest enough to hear the knock.
