# The Unholy Trinity

## 1. A dragon and two beasts

Having failed twice to destroy Jesus — at his birth and in the wilderness — the dragon in Revelation 13 assembles two beasts to attack what's left of the woman's offspring: the church. One beast rises from the sea, one from the earth, and together they form a grotesque parody of God himself.

## 2. Real, first-century figures — not abstract symbols

It's tempting to read these beasts as either purely abstract "evil forces" with no historical anchor, or as a secret code for whatever current headline seems to fit. Neither is right. Revelation is a real letter to real first-century congregations, and its first readers would have recognized these figures immediately.

The dragon is easiest to identify: he is Satan himself, the power behind every human system of oppression, since "we do not wrestle against flesh and blood" (Ephesians 6:12). He doesn't hold power directly — he drives the human powers that do.

The first beast, rising from the sea, is the imperial power of Rome itself — appropriate, since ships and governors arriving from Rome all approached these coastal cities by sea. This beast borrows features from all four of Daniel's beasts at once (Daniel 7): the mouth of a lion, the feet of a bear, the appearance of a leopard, ten horns. Just as one emperor's death didn't end Rome's rule — a new emperor simply took the throne — the beast's fatal wound is healed, and the world marvels that it cannot be stopped.

The second beast, rising from the earth, represents the local elites, civic leaders, and provincial networks who benefited from Rome's favor and worked to enforce loyalty to it — building temples, running the imperial cult, making sure no one's private nonconformity cost the whole city its standing. This beast doesn't look overtly menacing. It looks like ordinary civic life: "just go with the flow."

## 3. The counterfeit trinity

Line the details up and a clear pattern emerges: the dragon gives all authority to the first beast, just as God gives all authority to Jesus. The first beast appears slain and is resurrected, just as Jesus was. Every knee bows to it, just as every knee will bow to Jesus. The second beast speaks with the authority of the first, exactly as the Holy Spirit speaks with Jesus' authority — and it works to make an image "come alive," a counterfeit of the Spirit's true work.

Satan, in other words, doesn't invent something entirely new. He counterfeits the real thing, point for point: a false father, a false son, a false spirit — offering worship, security, and belonging through fear and coercion rather than through love. And notice how much of this counterfeit trinity's appeal is genuinely attractive: unstoppable strength, universal acceptance, dramatic signs, an end to suffering, submission enforced rather than merely invited. These are desires most of us actually feel. The problem was never that the beast offers nothing real. The problem is what it costs, and how it gets there — manipulation and violence rather than love.

## 4. Not just history — a present system

Here is where this session has to be read as more than an ancient case study. The demand for outward compliance to a system claiming ultimate authority over your life is not confined to first-century Rome. In some contexts today, that system takes the shape of comprehensive state surveillance: registration requirements, social-credit-adjacent scoring systems that reward conformity and penalize nonconformity, informal neighborhood reporting networks that function very much like the "second beast" of civic pressure in Revelation 13 — ordinary people making sure no one's private faith costs the group its standing with the authorities.

The mark placed "on the hand or forehead" (13:16) has an Old Testament background: it recalls the sign meant to remind God's people, in hand and mind, of what he had done for them at the Exodus (Exodus 13:16). The beast's mark counterfeits this — a comprehensive, economic loyalty test: "no one can buy or sell" without it. Wherever a system — governmental, social, economic — makes ordinary participation in life contingent on visible, verifiable loyalty to something other than Christ, this is the same basic pattern Revelation 13 describes, wearing modern clothes.

## 5. What actually distinguishes the two trinities

If the counterfeit looks so similar to the real thing, how can anyone tell them apart? The text gives us the answer directly: the second beast "had two horns like a lamb, but it spoke like a dragon" (13:11). From the outside, appearances can deceive. The difference is in the voice — and Jesus says plainly that only his sheep recognize his voice (John 10:1–5). In Hebrew thought, hearing carries the sense of obeying, so the real test isn't which system looks more impressive or more secure. It's which voice you actually follow when it costs you something.

Whenever a church — or a believer — resorts to fear, coercion, or manipulation to get compliance, it has, in that moment, adopted the beast's method rather than Christ's, regardless of which side of any political line it happens to stand on. That's a sober warning worth sitting with in every direction, not only toward the state.

## 6. What to actually do

Revelation's own answer, given a chapter later, is simple and countercultural: stay faithful, refuse to compromise, and keep worshiping the true God rather than the counterfeit one (14:1–5). Chapter 13 itself calls this "the patient endurance and faith of the saints" (13:10) alongside wisdom to discern what's really happening (13:18). Not spectacle. Not force. Just sustained, costly faithfulness — precisely the quality many of you already practice, often without anyone outside your community ever seeing it.

## 7. Closing reflection

The beast's system offers unstoppable strength and total security in exchange for your worship. Revelation's answer is not to fight fire with fire, but to recognize the true voice and hold to it, whatever the visible cost.

- Where in your own context does "just go with the flow" compliance function like the second beast's quiet, respectable enforcement — the pressure that doesn't look evil at all?
- What would it look like, practically, to keep worshiping the true God even where a surveillance system or a social pressure demands visible, verifiable loyalty elsewhere?
- Where might you be tempted to use fear or coercion yourself — even for a cause you believe is good — and how does recognizing that as "the beast's method" change your approach?
