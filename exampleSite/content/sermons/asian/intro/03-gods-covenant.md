# God's Covenant: A Story of Amazing Grace

## 1. Family was everything

In the ancient world, belonging to a family was not a sentimental matter — it was survival itself: provision, protection, identity, standing in the community. Many of you already understand this instinctively, in a way modern Western individualism often doesn't: your family is not a private arrangement you can casually opt out of. It is the fabric that holds your life together.

So how did someone outside the right family get access to that safety net? Scripture recognizes four doors: birth, marriage, adoption, and covenant. Today we're looking at the last of these — covenant — because it is the door through which God brings his people in, and it reframes what family, loyalty, and belonging actually mean.

## 2. How covenants normally worked

Historically, ancient covenants came in two forms. Covenants between equals functioned like alliances between brothers — mutual support, shared standing. Covenants between the strong and the weak, however, worked more like a conquest treaty: the stronger party was "father" or "master," the weaker was "son" or "servant." The stronger nation had real authority over the weaker one, but — crucially — was also *obligated* to defend it, precisely because the weaker party was now treated as family. You don't abandon your own household.

A conquest covenant typically had five parts: a preamble naming the greater party's titles; a prologue recounting the greater party's past kindness; the demands placed on the weaker party; blessings for obedience and curses for rebellion; and witnesses called to guarantee the whole arrangement. The weaker party could not make competing covenants with anyone else — you can only have one father.

## 3. God flips the script

When God made his covenant with Abraham in Genesis 15, the sacrificial ceremony followed the expected form: animals cut in half, arranged in two rows, with the party under obligation expected to walk between the pieces as a graphic warning of what would happen if the covenant were broken. But here is the shock: it is not Abraham, the weaker party, who walks between the pieces. It is God himself, represented by fire, who passes through — taking the curse of covenant-breaking onto himself, in advance, on Abraham's behalf.

The same pattern appears, in full form, in God's covenant with Israel at Sinai: the preamble ("I am the Lord your God"), the prologue ("who brought you out of Egypt" — a recounting of grace already shown), the demands (beginning with the command to have no other god), the blessings and curses (Deuteronomy 28), and witnesses (heaven and earth themselves). And unlike so many human covenants designed to set the weaker party up for failure, God's terms were never impossible. Israel could have kept them. They simply chose not to — repeatedly.

## 4. The Father who remains merciful

Throughout this story, God calls himself Israel's Father (Exodus 3:6) — one who, even while bound by the covenant's own terms, remains committed to mercy. Scripture captures this in the rich word *hesed*: covenant loyalty, love, goodness, faithfulness, all at once. God does not merely tolerate his covenant family. He is bound to them, by his own choice, in a way that guarantees his ongoing care even when they fail him.

## 5. Why this matters here

This has direct weight for many of you who live inside cultures where family loyalty, filial duty, and honor toward parents and elders are not optional extras but the very grammar of a good life. Some of you may have entered a new spiritual family through faith in Christ at real cost to your standing in your birth family — perhaps outright rejection, perhaps a quieter, ongoing coldness. Revelation's God does not treat that cost lightly. The covenant language of Scripture takes family bonds with total seriousness — it doesn't dismiss the value of family, honor, and loyalty; it relocates their deepest fulfillment in the one Father who took the curse upon himself so that you would never be truly without a family again.

This also reframes what "belonging" to God actually means. It isn't a transaction, a membership card, or an outward compliance you perform under pressure — themes that will return when we look at the Pax Romana and the seven churches. It is being brought into a family bound by covenant, where the stronger party has already taken your place under the curse.

## 6. The ongoing requirement

God's covenant with Israel also came with a rhythm: it had to be read aloud again and again — at the king's coronation, every seven years to the whole community, always alongside a shared meal. Each party held a copy of the terms, both of which, remarkably, ended up stored together in the same temple. Covenant faithfulness was never meant to be private and forgettable. It was rehearsed, remembered, and lived out in community — again, a rhythm many of you already understand instinctively through communal worship, family devotion, and the discipline of returning again and again to God's word together.

## 7. Closing reflection

The God who made this covenant is the same God who, in Revelation, calls his church to costly faithfulness under pressure — and he does not ask for a loyalty he has not already proven himself. He walked between the pieces first. He took the curse that should have been yours.

A few questions to sit with:

- Where in your life do you still relate to God as a transaction — trying to earn or maintain your place — rather than resting in a covenant God has already secured on your behalf?
- If following Christ has cost you standing within your birth family, how does knowing God's own *hesed* — his covenant loyalty toward you — speak into that loss?
- What would it look like, practically, for your household or community to "read the covenant aloud" together — to keep rehearsing and remembering God's faithfulness rather than letting it grow distant?
