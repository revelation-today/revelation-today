# Babel and Balaam: Two Ancient Warnings

## 1. Two very different stories, one shared warning

The Tower of Babel and the story of Balaam are separated by centuries and read very differently on the surface — one is about a building project, the other about a foreign prophet and a talking donkey. But both stories, and the way Revelation later uses them, converge on the same warning: the deadliest threats to God's people rarely come through open attack. They come through the quiet appeal of belonging to something bigger than yourself, or through seduction into compromise.

## 2. Babel: building a name apart from God

After the flood, humanity gathers to build a great tower reaching toward the sky, for two stated reasons: to make a name for themselves, and to keep from being scattered (Genesis 11:1–4). God comes down to look — a deliberately mocking gesture, given how small their tower actually is from heaven's vantage point — and neither of their goals is achieved. They wanted to make a name for themselves; that never happens for them, but God goes on to make a name out of Abraham instead. They wanted to avoid being scattered; scattering is exactly what results when God confuses their language, a division only reversed later, at Pentecost, when the Spirit gives every nation the ability to hear the gospel in its own tongue.

Underneath the building project is something recognizable in every generation: the desire for a unified, self-sufficient identity, a name and a legacy built by human effort and human loyalty rather than received as a gift from God. This is the deep root of "Babylon" throughout Scripture — including in Revelation, which draws directly on this story: the same theme of pride, the same swift fall, and God's people again living as exiles inside a system not built for their good.

## 3. Balaam: attacked through seduction, not force

The story of Balaam looks entirely different on the surface. Israel, fresh off a string of military victories, alarms the king of Moab, who hires the prophet Balaam to curse them. God directly forbids it, and even when Balaam is eventually permitted to go, he can only bless Israel, not curse them (Numbers 22–24) — much to Balak's fury.

The story seems to end there. It doesn't. Immediately afterward, the Moabites and Midianites invite Israel to a feast, resulting in exactly the outcome cursing could never achieve: sexual immorality and idolatry, followed by a devastating plague among God's own people (Numbers 25). Later, we learn Balaam had engineered this all along (Numbers 31:16) — unable to curse Israel directly, he instead taught Balak how to seduce them into compromise, which accomplished what a direct attack never could.

Revelation names this explicitly: Jesus rebukes the church in Pergamon because "there are some among you who hold to the teaching of Balaam, who taught Balak to entice the Israelites to sin so that they ate food sacrificed to idols and committed sexual immorality" (Revelation 2:14). The lesson transfers directly: opposition and persecution, painful as they are, have never been the main danger to God's people. Seduction into quiet compromise is far more effective, and far harder to notice while it's happening.

## 4. Why this pairing matters here

Both stories are about the pull toward belonging — to a great human project, to a compromised but comfortable community, to a system that offers real, tangible benefits in exchange for a little accommodation. That pull is genuinely difficult to resist, because it rarely announces itself as betrayal. It presents itself as reasonable, even loving: honoring your ancestors, keeping your family whole, staying inside your community's good graces.

This brings us to a real and unresolved pastoral question many of you carry, particularly in East Asian and Confucian-influenced contexts: how does exclusive worship of the Lamb relate to practices of ancestor veneration and filial piety?

## 5. An open question, not a closed one

We want to be honest here rather than offer a tidy answer the text itself doesn't give. Revelation's core concern — exclusive worship of Jesus, refusal of any rival claim to ultimate loyalty — is unmistakable throughout the book. Nothing in this session backs away from that. But the specific question of how the fifth commandment's call to "honor your father and mother" relates to your culture's rites for deceased parents and ancestors is not a question Revelation, or Scripture generally, addresses directly. It simply isn't in the text.

That means this is genuinely a matter for careful, humble pastoral discernment — not a place for either a blanket condemnation of your family's traditions or a casual dismissal of the real theological questions involved. Consider some of the actual complexity involved: Is a given family practice, in its intention and in the mind of the one performing it, an act of worship directed at a deceased relative as a spiritual power — which Scripture's exclusive-worship demand would clearly rule out — or is it fundamentally an act of memory, respect, and family solidarity, expressed through a culturally inherited form? Different families, different regions, and even different individuals within the same extended family may genuinely mean different things by what looks, from the outside, like the same practice.

This is precisely the kind of question the story of Babel and the story of Balaam should make us humble about, in both directions. Babel warns us against building an identity — even a family identity, even an ancestral one — as something ultimately self-sufficient, apart from dependence on God. Balaam warns us that compromise rarely looks like open betrayal; it looks like an invited feast among people you already trust and love. Both stories should make us examine our own hearts honestly rather than rush to judge someone else's.

At the same time, neither story gives license to treat this question as settled or unimportant. If you are wrestling with exactly this tension in your own family, the honest posture Revelation calls for is not silence, and not a quick verdict borrowed from someone in a very different cultural setting who has never had to answer this question for themselves. It's careful conversation with pastors and trusted elders who know your specific family situation, real prayer for wisdom, and real honesty about where the line between honoring your parents and worshiping something other than the Lamb actually falls for you.

## 6. Closing reflection

Babel and Balaam both warn that the greatest danger to faithfulness rarely comes as a frontal assault. It comes disguised as belonging — to a great human project, to a beloved family tradition, to a community you have every natural reason to want to stay inside. That doesn't mean every inherited practice is a trap. It means every inherited practice, however dear, deserves honest examination in light of Jesus' exclusive claim on our worship.

- Where in your life is the pull toward "belonging" — to family, to community, to a shared cultural identity — strongest, and how do you examine that pull honestly before God?
- If you carry unresolved questions about ancestor rites or filial obligations in your own family, who is a trusted elder or pastor you could bring that question to directly, rather than resolving it alone?
- What is the difference, in your own specific family's practice, between honoring memory and offering worship — and are you willing to sit with that question rather than rush past it?
