# Living Under Pax Romana

## 1. An offer that sounds fair

Imagine someone makes you this offer: security, prosperity, cultural flourishing, law and order, a stable life without war on your doorstep. And all that's required in exchange is that you acknowledge who provides it — and that you show that acknowledgment openly, whenever asked.

That sounds almost reasonable. It was the exact offer the first readers of Revelation lived inside, and it had a name: the Pax Romana, the "Roman Peace." I want to suggest today that many of us already live inside a strikingly similar arrangement — the names have changed, but the offer, and the decision it forces, have not.

## 2. What the Pax Romana actually was

From around 27 BC into the late second century AD, the Roman Empire experienced a long stretch of relative stability: few civil wars, a strong and unified state, real prosperity for many, flourishing art and culture — alongside sporadic persecution of Christians under emperors like Nero and Domitian, even though the first truly empire-wide, systematic persecution didn't begin until Decius in 250 AD.

Underneath this stability sat a coherent, quasi-religious story: the gods had chosen Rome; the emperor was their agent on earth; every blessing — security, peace, justice, prosperity — flowed through him to everyone who submitted to his rule. Therefore, the emperor deserved worship, loyalty, and titles that belonged to a god.

This message was not tucked away in the background. It was everywhere — coins, statues, processions, public games, even toasts at shared meals. Cities across Asia Minor, exactly where the seven churches of Revelation stood, competed with each other to build temples to the emperor, hoping to win his favor or a break on taxes.

And underneath the golden surface was a harder truth: this "golden age" was purchased with violence, conquest, and forced compliance. It was golden mainly for those willing to submit.

## 3. The real dilemma for the first Christians

For Christians living inside this system, the pressure was not abstract. Jesus was their true king — not the emperor. They trusted him, not the state, for provision and protection. And they could not, in good conscience, offer the emperor the honor due to God alone.

Jesus himself had already made the terms unmistakable: "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and money" (Matthew 6:24) — and by extension, you cannot serve God and Caesar. That left three real options:

1. **Deny the faith outright** — to belong, to avoid trouble, to keep the family's standing intact.
2. **Live the faith fully and openly** — risking social exclusion, financial ruin, imprisonment, even death.
3. **Conform outwardly, stay faithful inwardly** — confess the emperor with your mouth while holding Jesus in your heart. Comfortable. And, Revelation insists, deeply compromised.

That third option has always been the most attractive, in every century, and it is one of the central concerns of Revelation from its opening page. John introduces himself as "your brother and partner in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus, who was on the island of Patmos because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus" (Revelation 1:9). He is writing from exile — a direct consequence of his refusal to play along. Revelation itself is his answer: it names who the true emperor really is, and shows that the impressive facade of the Pax Romana will not stand.

## 4. Not ancient history — this week's decision

Here is where this session has to depart from a purely historical retelling. For many reading this today, the exact same choice is not a museum exhibit. It is a live, sometimes weekly, decision.

Consider the shapes this takes right now:

- **The unregistered gathering.** Meeting in a home rather than a state-approved venue is, in some places, itself an act that carries risk — a modern version of refusing to burn incense to the emperor's image.
- **The form that asks your religion.** A background check, a school application, a government registration that quietly notes which faith you claim — and the small, constant calculation of what to write, and what it might cost you later.
- **The surveillance that is simply normal.** In some contexts, cameras, social-credit-adjacent systems, or informal neighborhood monitoring make "outward conformity" not a rare crisis moment but a permanent, ambient condition — the Pax Romana as daily atmosphere, exactly as it was for the seven churches.
- **The minority calculation.** In contexts where Christians are a small minority inside a Hindu-, Buddhist-, or Muslim-majority society, the pressure to blend in — to avoid the family shame of conversion, to keep quiet at a public festival, to let a difference pass unmentioned — is not occasional. It is the texture of ordinary life.

The Pax Romana's real question was never "is this good?" People might reasonably answer yes to peace, prosperity, and order. Its real question was: **who do you actually credit for this, and what does that require of you?** That question hasn't gone anywhere. It has only changed its clothing.

## 5. The frog in slowly warming water

You may know the old image of a frog dropped into boiling water — it leaps out immediately. But place it in cool water and heat it slowly, and it may not notice the danger until it's too late. Biologically, this isn't quite accurate, but as a picture it captures something real: the Pax Romana was not, for most believers, a single dramatic moment of testing. It was an atmosphere — constant, comfortable, unremarkable. That is precisely what made it so effective.

The same slow process operates today. No one wakes up and formally renounces Jesus. It happens by inches: an opinion quietly kept to yourself, a form filled out one careful way rather than an honest one, a decision to let your children's faith stay unspoken at school "just for now." Eventually someone may still be gathering, still praying — but the true ruler of their actual daily decisions has quietly shifted, without anyone ever "deciding" it.

## 6. What Revelation offers instead

What's remarkable about Revelation's answer is that it does not primarily call believers to withdraw from the world. It calls them to worship — clear-eyed, uncompromised worship. Before any plague or judgment appears in the book, John is shown a throne room in chapters 4 and 5, where God and the Lamb are worshiped without interruption. That is Revelation's answer to every Pax Romana, ancient or modern: not escape, but the settled question of who actually sits on the throne of your life.

And the book is honest that the "third way" — outward compliance, inward faith — is never presented as a workable, godly solution. It is unmasked for what it is: half-heartedness. That is exactly what Jesus later says to the church in Laodicea — neither hot nor cold.

## 7. Closing and application

The believers in Asia Minor had to decide who they truly served: the emperor, who promised security and prosperity, or the crucified and risen Jesus, who promised suffering first and life everlasting after. Many of you are asked, in smaller or larger ways, to answer that same question — this week, not someday.

A few questions to carry with you, asked plainly on purpose:

- Where do you genuinely expect the solution to your problems to come from — God, or your registration status, your standing with the authorities, your family's approval, your government's goodwill?
- Where do you say or sign something publicly that you don't fully believe inwardly, just to stay safe or to keep belonging?
- If your convictions cost you something concrete this year — a relationship, a status, your safety — would you still hold to them? What would that actually look like in practice, for you, by name?

The Pax Romana ended long ago. The temptation it represented has not — it has simply changed its name and its uniform. The good news of Revelation is that the true King is already seated on the throne. He does not ask for lip service. He invites you into whole, undivided worship — and that King has already won, not through force, but through faithful, costly love.
