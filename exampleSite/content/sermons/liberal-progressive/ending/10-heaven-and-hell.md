# Heaven and Hell: What Really Awaits Us?

## The session we've been building toward, and the one we can't resolve for you

If you've felt earlier sessions building toward something, this is it. Many in this room carry a real, thoughtful hope that God's love is wide enough, in the end, to hold everyone — that a God worth trusting couldn't finally slam a door on anyone made in God's image. Revelation's own ending, though, keeps a stark, binary shape: a New Jerusalem, and an outside; those written in the book of life, and those who are not. This session won't pretend that tension isn't real, and it won't manufacture a resolution the text itself doesn't hand us. What it can do is make sure we're hearing what the text actually says — which turns out to be different, on close reading, from the popular caricature of heaven as a reward for good behavior and hell as torture for bad behavior.

## What heaven is actually described as

Revelation gives us heaven through two main images: the comforted saints of chapter 7, and the far more developed picture of the New Jerusalem in chapter 21. Both converge on a single point, and it isn't primarily about location, comfort, or reward. It's about fellowship with God. Notice how the New Jerusalem is introduced — as "the Bride of Christ," language of intimacy and relationship, not real estate. Notice, too, that its architecture is drawn directly from the temple, and the temple is itself a reflection of Eden — the place where humanity once walked with God without obstruction. The city is described as a perfect cube, and the only object in the entire Old Testament with that exact shape is the Holy of Holies: the innermost sanctuary of the temple, so saturated with God's presence that only the high priest could enter it, and only once a year, at great personal risk.

Put simply: heaven, in Revelation's own terms, is not a prize handed out for accumulated merit. It is the maximum possible nearness to God — the innermost room of the temple, thrown open, with everyone inside it standing equally close to the presence that used to be reserved for one person, one day a year.

## What hell is actually described as

Read that way, hell isn't described primarily as active torture inflicted by an angry deity either. It's described, more simply and more soberingly, as the absence of relationship with God — "everything outside" the cube. And here is the argument worth taking seriously rather than dismissing too quickly: every good thing we treasure and often assume we can generate or sustain on our own — hope, joy, peace, purpose, forgiveness, the capacity to love well — has its actual source in God, whether we credit that source or not. We experience these goods now partly because we exist in a world where God is still actively present, still "courting" creation, in the text's own gentler image. But that season, the text insists, will not run forever. Eventually there will be only with-God or without-God, with no permanently available third option held open indefinitely.

Without God, what's left isn't a lake of fire imposed from outside as punishment for its own sake, so much as it is the natural, unavoidable trajectory of separation from the one source of everything worth having: an unraveling into what the source material names plainly as unforgiveness, bitterness, hopelessness, and fear — not a sentence handed down, but the shape a life takes when it's finally, fully cut off from what was sustaining it all along, often without full awareness that it was doing the sustaining.

## Why this reframing matters, without pretending it resolves everything

This reframing is genuinely useful, and it's worth taking it as far as it honestly goes: both heaven and hell, read this way, are the natural outcome of nearness to God or distance from God — not an arbitrary court sentence handed down by a judge choosing favorites. That takes real weight off the picture of an angry deity picking winners and losers on a whim, and puts the weight instead on the plain claim that intimacy with the actual source of every good thing is, definitionally, what makes a life good — and its absence is, definitionally, what makes a life not good.

But let's be honest about what this reframing does not do. It does not resolve the tension with universalist hope, because the passage still insists on the cube's edge: you are either all the way in or all the way out, with no stated third category and no explicit promise that everyone eventually crosses over. It would be dishonest to claim the text quietly teaches universal reconciliation if you just read it generously enough. It doesn't say that. Nor does it say the opposite with the kind of triumphalist certainty that lets anyone comfortably assume they know exactly who ends up on which side of that line, or take satisfaction in imagining who doesn't. What it offers is the logic of the boundary — nearness or distance, relationship or its absence — without settling, for us, whether or how wide that boundary might ultimately be drawn, or by whom.

## What the text refuses, which may matter as much as what it affirms

One thing is stated plainly enough to lean on: fear and manipulation, used to frighten people into compliance, are explicitly named elsewhere in this book as tools of the beast, not of the church and not of God. If hell has ever been wielded against you, or by you, as a threat designed mainly to produce fear and control obedience rather than to honestly describe what nearness or distance from God actually means, that use of the doctrine has more in common with Revelation's beast than with its Lamb. The church's own defining posture throughout this book is not threat. It's testimony — a patient, costly witness to what's actually true, offered rather than forced on anyone.

## Sitting with what we don't get to settle

This is, honestly, the hardest session in the series, and it should stay hard rather than being wrapped up neatly. We don't get a resolution to the universalism question here, because the text itself doesn't hand us one. What we do get is a reoriented picture of what's actually at stake: not an arbitrary judge assigning reward and punishment, but the real, weighty claim that closeness to the source of every good thing is what makes eternity good, and that this book, whatever else it says, refuses to let threat and fear be the church's tools for talking about it.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- How does hearing heaven described as "nearness to God" rather than "reward for good behavior" change how you imagine it?
- Where have you seen hell used as a threat, in a way that functioned more like manipulation than honest description? How did that shape your own resistance to the doctrine?
- The text won't resolve the universalism question for us. How do you sit faithfully with a text that draws a stark line without telling you, definitively, where or how permanently that line finally falls?
