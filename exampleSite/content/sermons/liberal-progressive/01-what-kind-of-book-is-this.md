# What Kind of Book Is This?

## Why we're starting here, and why we won't rush it

Most of us in this room have a default move when Revelation comes up: file it under "ancient apocalyptic literature," note that it was written to comfort a persecuted minority under Rome, and quietly conclude that whatever claim it made on its first readers, it doesn't make much of a claim on us. We've been trained — often well and rightly — to read historically, to ask what a text meant before asking what it means. That training is good. But somewhere in the process, a lot of us learned to treat "this is an apocalypse" as the end of the inquiry rather than the beginning of it. Genre becomes an escape hatch: once we've named the category, we've excused ourselves from the content.

This series is going to resist that move, starting now. Not because historical-critical reading is wrong — it's exactly right, and we're going to use it constantly — but because naming a genre correctly should make a text easier to hear accurately, not easier to set aside. So before we go anywhere else in Revelation, we need to get honest about what kind of book this actually is, because the answer is more demanding than "coping mechanism for the persecuted."

## Three genres, not one

Revelation is unusual because it isn't just one type of literature — it's three at once, layered on top of each other: a letter, a prophecy, and an apocalypse. Each one carries its own claim on the reader, and losing sight of any one of them distorts the whole.

**It is a letter.** It opens and closes like one — the formal greeting, the formal blessing — and it's explicitly addressed to seven real congregations in Asia Minor, each with a name and a specific situation. That matters more than it sounds like it should. If we assume the bulk of the book refers only to events in our own future, we've skipped past the fact that it was written to make sense to real people in the first and second centuries, wrestling with real, immediate problems. The letter form keeps us honest: this text had an original audience, and we read it best when we ask what it meant to them first.

**It is a prophecy.** Not primarily in the popular sense — a supernatural forecast of coming events — but in the Old Testament sense: a message grounded in covenant, calling people back to justice and mercy when they've drifted from it. Old Testament prophets did two things at once: they comforted the oppressed and they challenged the unjust — sometimes the same audience was doing both at different points. Revelation does exactly this. It isn't trying to satisfy our curiosity about the future; it's trying to change behavior now, using vivid, urgent imagery to provoke a response.

**It is an apocalypse.** This is the genre most of us are least equipped to read, because nothing else in our daily experience works quite like it. An apocalypse encourages people in crisis through scathing criticism of their oppressors, passionate calls to resist, and confidence in God's ultimate victory over evil — and it does this through dualism: the present age is genuinely evil, a better age is genuinely coming, and there is no neutral, uncommitted middle ground on offer. Lamb versus dragon. Jerusalem versus Babylon. No third option quietly available to those who'd rather not choose.

## The political cartoon test

Here's a way to hold the "symbolic, not literal" question without letting it collapse into "therefore not real." Think about a political cartoon — a dragon labeled with a country's initials, devouring a smaller figure labeled with another's. No one reading that cartoon thinks dragons exist, or that the artist is making a scientific claim. And no one reading it seriously doubts that it's making an urgent, specific, real claim about power and injustice happening right now. That's roughly how Revelation's first readers would have experienced its imagery: not confused about whether beasts are "literally" real, and not for a moment thinking the claim being made was anything less than dead serious.

The Greek itself backs this up. The book's opening line says God "made known" (*semaino*) what he showed — the same word used for symbolic visions in Daniel, and for John's Gospel's description of Jesus's death as a *sign* pointing to something larger than the bare physical fact. Revelation announces itself, in its very first sentence, as operating in symbol — not because symbols are less true than facts, but because some truths can only be carried by symbol.

## What this means for how we proceed

Putting these three genres together, Revelation is: a letter that had to make sense to real first- and second-century people; a prophecy meant to provoke a change in behavior right now, not merely someday; and an apocalypse that forces a decision by describing the world as a stark, uncompromising "either/or." What it is *not*: a coded forecast with no bearing on its first readers, or a straightforward newsreel of future events for us to decode.

That's a genuinely different proposal than either the "read it literally as a forecast" crowd or the "read it as safely ancient" crowd usually offers. It says: this book makes an urgent, present-tense claim on whoever reads it honestly, precisely because it refuses to resolve into either pure prediction or pure metaphor. The dualism is the point — not a primitive feature to be civilized away, but the very thing that will not let us stay neutral.

That should make us a little uncomfortable. Good. We're going to sit with that discomfort across this whole series rather than resolve it too quickly, because Revelation itself won't let us resolve it quickly. What it offers instead, session by session, is the chance to actually hear what this strange, demanding, urgent book has to say — not what we've assumed it says from a safe distance.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- Where have you, personally or in your faith community, used "it's ancient apocalyptic literature" as a way to stop listening to a text rather than a way to listen more carefully?
- Political cartoons work because they say something true and urgent through obvious exaggeration. What would it take to let Revelation's exaggerations do the same work for you?
- The apocalyptic genre insists there's no neutral ground. What's your gut reaction to that claim — and where does that reaction come from?
