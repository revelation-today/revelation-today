# A Different Christmas Story

## The story we know, and the one we don't

You already know the familiar Christmas story from Luke — shepherds, a manger, travelers following a star. It's gentle, and we've heard it enough times that it barely registers as strange anymore. Revelation 12 tells the same essential story — the birth of the Messiah into a hostile world — but strips away everything comfortable about it, and in doing so recovers something the familiar version has let us forget: that the incarnation was, from the very start, an act of cosmic resistance against a domination system determined to destroy it.

## The scene

"A great sign appeared in heaven: a woman clothed with the sun, with the moon under her feet and a crown of twelve stars on her head. She was pregnant and cried out in pain as she was about to give birth. Then another sign appeared in heaven: an enormous red dragon... [that] stood in front of the woman who was about to give birth, so that it might devour her child the moment he was born." The woman gives birth; the child — destined to "rule all the nations with an iron scepter" — is snatched up to God's throne before the dragon can reach him.

Strip away the cosmic staging and the emotional core is exactly what you'd expect from an actual birth story: a woman in the vulnerability of labor, facing an enormous, furious threat with only her exposure and the child's fragility on her side of the ledger. On the raw math of the scene, the woman has no chance and the child has no future. That the child survives at all — "snatched up to God and to his throne" — is the entire twist the passage exists to deliver.

## Who is who

The dragon is named outright a few verses later: "that ancient serpent called the Devil, or Satan" — language that reaches straight back to Eden. The child, described mainly through one detail — ruling with "an iron scepter" — is unmistakably tied to Psalm 2's messianic king and Isaiah's coming ruler; Jesus himself later claims that same iron scepter as his own in the letter to Thyatira. This is the story of his birth.

The harder identification is the woman. Three candidates present themselves — Mary, Eve, Israel — and it's worth working through why the text points to the third. Mary is the obvious first guess, but nothing in scripture itself links her to sun, moon, and stars; that association came later, from church tradition reading backward into the text, not from the text itself. Eve has a plausible claim, given her role in Genesis, but doesn't explain the specific detail of twelve stars. Israel fits best: scripture regularly personifies her as "daughter Zion," and the closest real parallel is Joseph's dream of the sun, moon, and eleven stars bowing to him — interpreted by his father as his parents and eleven brothers, with Joseph himself completing the twelve. That is essentially a picture of the twelve tribes of Israel.

So the woman is Israel, promised across the Old Testament to give birth to the Messiah — a promise finally fulfilled at a moment when Israel itself was under crushing Roman occupation, its repeated uprisings put down one after another. Into that specific pressure, at that specific historical moment, Jesus was born — rescued once already from Herod's attempt to kill him as an infant, tested again by the devil's temptation in the wilderness, and finally handing his own life over to death on a cross before being raised and enthroned. The passage compresses all of that — birth to ascension — into five verses, because its interest isn't biography. It's the shape of the victory.

## Cosmic resistance, told through vulnerability

This is where the passage becomes something genuinely different from the Christmas card version, and something this audience's instinct for resisting domination systems should recognize immediately. The birth of Jesus, told this way, is not a private, domestic, sentimental event. It's staged as an open confrontation between an empire of violence — the dragon, later revealed as the power behind Rome's throne — and the vulnerable, laboring body of an oppressed people giving birth to their long-promised deliverer. The dragon has every apparent advantage: size, position, ruthlessness, timing. The woman and child have none of the advantages that usually decide such contests. And yet the child is not devoured. He is caught up to the throne.

That is the whole shape of the gospel compressed into one image: the incarnation as an act of resistance, dressed not in armor but in the maximum possible vulnerability — an infant, and a mother mid-labor — against the machinery of empire, and it wins anyway.

## The pattern that runs through the rest of the book

Revelation never actually explains the mechanics of how the child overcomes; it simply asserts that he does, and then keeps returning to the same shape of victory throughout the rest of the book. The only two churches Jesus has no criticism for at all — Smyrna and Philadelphia — are also the two in the most obvious, humanly hopeless trouble. Laodicea, the most confident and comfortable of the seven, gets no praise whatsoever. The lion of Judah, announced with fanfare in chapter 5, turns out on closer look to be a slaughtered lamb. The two witnesses in chapter 11 appear utterly defeated, lying dead in the street, and are then vindicated in full public view. Meanwhile the dragon and the beast of chapter 13, who look for all the world like they hold total, unchallengeable power, are heading — the book insists — toward total collapse, taking everyone who trusted in them down with them.

The Christmas story in Revelation 12, in other words, isn't an isolated oddity. It's the template the rest of the book keeps repeating: apparent weakness overcoming apparent invincibility, not by matching force with force, but by simply, stubbornly, surviving and being vindicated. That's worth carrying into every harder chapter still ahead of us in this series.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- What does it change for you to hear the nativity told as an act of resistance against a domination system, rather than a purely domestic, sentimental scene?
- The woman here is Israel — a people under real, active political oppression at the moment their deliverer was born. Where do you see comparable vulnerability and hope held together today?
- Smyrna and Philadelphia, the two churches in the most obvious trouble, get no criticism at all; Laodicea, the most comfortable, gets no praise. What does that pairing suggest about where real faithfulness tends to show up?
