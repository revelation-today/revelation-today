# Who Is the Harlot Babylon?

## A question people have answered too many ways

Over the centuries, people have identified the Harlot Babylon of Revelation 17–18 as the papacy, as a specific modern nation, as global capitalism itself, as more or less anyone the reader already had a grievance against. The text supports none of those confident modern identifications directly, and chasing them tends to distract from what the passage is actually doing — which is naming, with real precision, how political power, economic wealth, and cultural seduction work together to produce a system that looks glamorous and turns out to be built on exploitation.

## Unpacking the scene

Chapter 17 is crowded: a great harlot, kings who "commit adultery" with her, a scarlet beast with seven heads and ten horns that she rides, ten additional kings who briefly share the beast's authority, and an ending where the very powers who once adored her turn and destroy her. Worked through carefully, the pieces resolve. The beast beneath her closely parallels the first beast of chapter 13 — the emperor, backed by Rome's military and political power. The seven heads are both the seven hills of Rome, immediately identifiable to any first-century reader, and a sequence of kings; the beast itself is described as one who "was, and is not, and is about to come" — a grotesque parody of God's own self-description as the one "who was, and is, and is to come." The ten horns are kings who briefly hand their power to the beast, representing the network of provincial and allied rulers propping up Roman authority at that moment in history.

The harlot herself is Rome — the city, its wealth, its culture, dressed to imitate the very thing it's really destroying: notice the deliberate visual echo between her and the true Bride of chapters 19 and 21. She's covered in jewels and fine cloth, just as the New Jerusalem will be; she's called a "city," just as the New Jerusalem is a city; she's a corrupted mirror image, right down to details drawn from the high priest's own garments in the Old Testament — meaning the text is picturing something that was meant to be holy and faithful, and has instead turned unfaithful, exactly the charge leveled at Israel itself by Jeremiah and Ezekiel when it played the harlot with foreign gods and foreign alliances.

## The economic aspect, taken seriously

This is where the critique lands most naturally for a justice-minded audience, and it deserves to be taken all the way seriously rather than softened. Chapter 18's extended lament lists Babylon's trade goods in detail — gold, silver, jewels, fine cloth, spices, livestock — and then, without any pause or apology, adds one more item to the list: "slaves, that is, human souls." The text places human beings on a commodity list alongside cargo, and does not blink at doing so. That's worth sitting with directly: what does it mean for any economic system, ancient or modern, to treat the people inside it as assets rather than as ends in themselves? The list of goods echoes the Old Testament's earlier lament over Tyre — another great trading city, whose wealth is called, without euphemism, her "harlot's wage." The pattern is consistent across scripture: the harlot label describes any relationship, religious or economic or both at once, where profit and power replace covenant faithfulness.

Look, too, at how the "kings of the earth" and "merchants of the earth" respond when Babylon falls: they don't mourn her cruelty, or the "human souls" bought and sold in her marketplaces. They mourn their own lost profits — the collapse of the trade routes that made them rich. That detail alone is a fairly precise indictment of any system whose real allegiance turns out, when tested, to be self-interest wearing the costume of loyalty.

## Rome, then and now — but not "just" a metaphor

It's worth being honest about how naturally this reading lands with this audience's existing instincts: identifying "Babylon" with unjust systems of empire, extraction, and economic exploitation is not a stretch we're imposing on the text — it's what the text itself, read in its first-century context, is doing. The temptation, though, is to stop there, satisfied with a purely structural critique aimed safely outward at "the system," without ever letting the passage's other, harder question land closer to home.

## The question the source material insists on

Because here's what the passage will not let us avoid: Babylon is never described with perfectly sharp, fixed edges, identifiable once and filed away. That vagueness looks like a weakness in the text until you notice it's deliberate — precisely so that every reader, in every generation, is forced to keep asking the same uncomfortable question about themselves rather than settling the matter once, safely, about someone else. Could the church itself become part of Babylon? Not simply "could an external, unjust system out there resemble Babylon" — but could our own congregations, our own economic choices, our own comfortable arrangements, have quietly moved inside her?

Ask concretely: do we mourn when the economy falters and our own standard of living drops, more than we mourn the injustice that produced our comfort in the first place? Do we quietly support policies or institutions that protect our own economic interests at real cost to others, without examining that cost too closely? If the honest answer is yes, the text's own conclusion is direct: we are standing inside Babylon, and the call is not analysis — it's "come out of her."

None of this makes trade, prosperity, or economic growth inherently corrupt; the passage doesn't argue that, and neither should we. The question was never whether wealth exists. It's what spirit stands behind it, and whether we're willing to name our own complicity as honestly as we name the ancient empire's.

## Why she falls — and why that's unsettling too

The harlot's own allies — the very kings who profited from her — are the ones who turn and destroy her, then stand at a distance mourning their own losses. Loyalty built purely on mutual advantage always eventually reveals itself as exactly that: mutual advantage, not commitment, dissolving the moment the advantage runs out. That's a warning as much as a judgment scene — anything built on shifting self-interest, rather than on God, is standing on ground that was never actually solid.

## Questions for reflection or discussion

- The harlot's trade list includes "human souls" alongside cargo. Where, in systems you participate in, are people treated as assets rather than as ends in themselves?
- The text refuses to give Babylon fixed, final edges, forcing every generation to keep asking whether it has moved inside her. Where might that question apply uncomfortably close to home, rather than safely to "the system out there"?
- What would it cost you, concretely, to "come out of" an arrangement you suspect is more Babylon than you'd like to admit?
