## Overview

But how can it work out that everyone gets it right?

This is where worship comes in. John gets to look into heaven, and he sees incredible things.

God is sitting there, and everyone around him is simply amazed - they bow down before him and lay their crowns down, which means: they want God to rule their lives. That's what worship is.

Maybe you've experienced God answering a prayer, or comforting you, or making you strong. You probably responded with thanks. After a while, you noticed that God, the one who gives all these good things, is good himself. That's called praise. And eventually you might say: if God is that good, I can trust him completely. That's worship.

And once you trust him completely, you naturally start living by what matters to God, and stop doing things that make him sad.

But why would that kind of faithfulness be enough to fix all the world's problems? Wouldn't it be much more important for all the bad people to simply disappear? People thought that 2000 years ago too, but the Bible says no.

We see that right here, as Jesus receives the right to open the scroll. He's announced as the great lion who can destroy everything - but he appears as a lamb, who kills no one.

God doesn't want to destroy bad people, even when they keep being mean - because honestly, haven't we all been that way sometimes? He wants to restore them and make them whole.

And here's the big surprise: out of the throne room, where God is being worshiped, come all the "plagues" (seals, trumpets, and bowls). That seems confusing at first.

But if we look more closely, we also see a rainbow in the throne room - a sign of God's goodness. So we can look forward to finding out what the plagues are really about.
