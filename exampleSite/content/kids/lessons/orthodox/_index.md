# Welcome

This series walks through the book of Revelation for an Orthodox parish — Eastern and Oriental alike — using the same underlying study material as the rest of the "Revelation Today" project, but read with the instincts your tradition already brings to Scripture.

That turns out to be a real advantage. Revelation is the most liturgical book in the New Testament: a throne room filled with unceasing worship, incense, elders, a slain-yet-standing Lamb, a fourfold chorus of living creatures. Western readers often have to be taught to notice this. You don't. Every Divine Liturgy already places you inside it — the iconostasis as the veil before the Holy of Holies, the Trisagion echoing the seraphim's unceasing cry, the great entrance evoking the procession before the throne. When Revelation 4 and 5 open, we are not asking you to imagine something foreign. We are asking you to recognize your own altar.

The patristic tradition is not treated here as one option among several modern theories — it is the starting point. Andrew of Caesarea's sixth-century commentary, the standard Orthodox reference on this book, shapes how these sessions handle symbol, number, and image. Where the source material argues a point from scratch, we've tried to show it was often already the Church's instinct: a symbolic reading of the beasts and bowls, an amillennial reading of the thousand years, a settled confidence that Christ has already won even while the age's suffering continues. And your tradition's apophatic caution — the refusal to over-define what remains mystery, especially around final judgment — is not a gap to be filled in. It is a form of humility this book itself models, since even John, given the vision directly, is repeatedly told to seal up what he sees rather than explain it fully.

Two things are worth naming honestly, in the spirit of self-examination rather than accusation. First, in places where Orthodoxy and national identity have grown tightly bound together, Revelation's warning against a state that asks for ultimate allegiance lands closer to home than it might feel comfortable admitting — worth sitting with rather than deflecting toward other people's empires. Second, for Coptic, Assyrian, and other Oriental Orthodox communities living as a pressured minority in the Middle East, and for any reader under real pressure for the name of Christ, this book may not need much explaining at all. John's exile, the martyrs under the altar crying "how long," the two witnesses killed and vindicated — these sessions are written with the awareness that some of you are not reading about someone else's history.

May this series be less an argument to win you over and more a returning home to a book your worship has been quietly rehearsing all along.
