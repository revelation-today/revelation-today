# The Woman, the Dragon, and the Baby King
### The Christmas story you've never heard told this way

---

## 1. Opening: Look at the icon

*(Bring an icon of the Nativity of Christ, pointing out the Theotokos, the cave, the star.)*

You know this icon well — it's the story of Jesus being born. But did you know the book of Revelation tells the Christmas story again, in a totally different, dramatic way? Instead of a quiet stable, it's told like a huge battle in the sky!

---

## 2. A woman, a dragon, and a baby

In Revelation 12, John sees a woman glowing like the sun, with the moon under her feet and twelve stars on her head, about to have a baby. Then a huge, scary red dragon appears, waiting right in front of her to eat the baby the moment he's born!

The baby is born... and instead of being caught by the dragon, he's snatched straight up to God's throne in heaven! The dragon completely fails.

---

## 3. Who is the dragon? Who is the baby?

The dragon is named clearly a few verses later: he's Satan, the devil. And the baby — the one who will "rule all nations" — that's Jesus.

---

## 4. Who is the woman?

This is a great question, and in the Orthodox Church, we can hold onto two beautiful answers at once! Some see the woman as Israel, God's people, who had been waiting a very, very long time for the Messiah to be born — kind of like the whole nation "gave birth" to Jesus. Others, especially in our own tradition, love to picture this woman as the Theotokos, the Mother of God, standing bravely as the world tried to threaten her newborn son.

Both pictures are beautiful, and they fit together: Mary is the truest daughter of Israel, the one person in whom all of God's people's hope and waiting came true.

---

## 5. Small and weak, but never actually in danger

Here's the lesson for us: a woman about to give birth is about as un-scary and unprotected as you can imagine. And yet the dragon completely fails to hurt the baby. This happens again and again in Revelation — the ones who look weakest turn out to be the safest, because God is holding them the whole time.

---

## 6. Memory verse

"She gave birth to a son... and her child was snatched up to God and to his throne." *(Revelation 12:5)*

---

## 7. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you that no matter how scary the dragon looked, he could never actually win against you. Thank you for the Theotokos who cared for you, and for your people who waited so long for you to come. Amen.

---

## Appendix: Ideas for preparation

**Opening idea:** Use a Nativity icon as the central visual anchor for the whole lesson — point out details (star, cave, animals, the Theotokos) before contrasting with Revelation 12's dramatic version.

**Craft idea:** Have kids draw the "two Christmas stories" side by side — the quiet manger scene and the woman-and-dragon scene — and connect them with an arrow.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Why do you think Revelation tells the Christmas story in such a dramatic, scary way?
2. What does it teach us that the dragon could never actually catch the baby?
3. Can you think of a time you felt small or scared, but God was still taking care of you?

**Song suggestions:**
- A Nativity hymn appropriate for children
- A simple song about God's protection
