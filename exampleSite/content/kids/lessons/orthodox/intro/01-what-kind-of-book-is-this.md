# A Book Like an Icon
### Getting ready to read the last book of the Bible

---

## 1. Opening: Look at the icon

*(Bring an icon of Christ Pantocrator, or any icon with a hand available in the church, and hold it up.)*

Look at this icon. Is this a photograph of Jesus, like a picture you'd take with a phone? No. Is it just a nice decoration that doesn't mean anything? Also no. An icon is a window — it shows us something true and real, even though it doesn't look exactly like a photograph would.

Today we're starting a whole series about the last book of the Bible: Revelation. And here's the first, most important thing to know about it: **Revelation is written like an icon.** It's full of pictures — a Lamb, a dragon, a scroll, horses of different colors — that are all true, even though they're not meant to be photographs of what will literally happen.

---

## 2. Why pictures instead of just facts?

Think about how much you already know from pictures at church. When you see an icon of Christ enthroned, you don't need someone to explain "this is a photograph." You already know: this icon is showing me something real about who Jesus is and where he reigns, using image instead of a list of facts.

The Bible does the same thing in Revelation. John, who wrote it, says God "showed" and "signified" these things to him (Revelation 1:1) — that second word is a special word the Bible uses for pictures that stand for something, not just plain reports. It's the same kind of word used when Daniel, another Bible prophet, saw a huge statue in a dream that stood for whole kingdoms crashing down.

---

## 3. Not a secret code to crack

Some people treat Revelation like a puzzle: they try to match every picture to something in the news, like it's a secret code about a specific president or a specific war. But that's not how John wrote it, and it's not how the Church has ever read it in worship. Even the wisest teachers of our Church, like Saint Andrew of Caesarea long ago, read Revelation as pictures that strengthen our faith and our worship — not as a puzzle to solve with a calculator.

---

## 4. It's okay not to know everything

Here's something wonderful about being Orthodox: we're comfortable saying "that part is a mystery, and that's okay." We don't need to explain every single detail of Revelation perfectly. We trust that some things are meant to stay a little bit hidden, the way parts of the Liturgy stay behind the icon screen — not because they're bad, but because they're holy, and holy things don't always get fully explained.

So in this whole series, when we come across something strange or hard to picture, it's fine to say: "I don't fully understand that detail, but I know what the whole book is about — Jesus wins, and he wants us to stay faithful to him."

---

## 5. For some of us, this book is not far away

For some Christians around the world today — including Orthodox Christians in the Middle East — following Jesus really can cost something, just like it did for the people John wrote to. If that's part of your family's story, this book isn't just an old history lesson for you. It's written for people exactly like you.

---

## 6. Memory verse

"The revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place." *(Revelation 1:1)*

---

## 7. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you for giving us a whole book about you, even if some of the pictures in it are hard to understand. Help us to trust you with the mysteries and to listen for what you want to teach us. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for preparation

**Opening idea:** Bring an icon (Christ Pantocrator or similar) to hold up at the start. If possible, let each child hold or point at one.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Give each child paper and have them draw a simple "symbol" for something true about God (a crown for King, a heart for love, a lamb for gentleness) — then discuss how a symbol can be true without being a photograph.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. What's your favorite icon in our church, and what does it show you about God?
2. Why do you think John used pictures instead of just a list of facts?
3. Is it okay to not understand every detail of a Bible passage? Why?

**Song suggestions:**
- A simple Trisagion ("Holy God, Holy Mighty...")
- Any children's song about Jesus as King
