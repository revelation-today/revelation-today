# When Waiting Gets Hard
### Finishing our series together

---

## 1. Opening: The really long wait

*(Ask: What's the longest you've ever had to wait for something? How did you feel by the end of the waiting?)*

We're finishing this whole series today by talking honestly about something real: sometimes, waiting for God to fix a hard thing takes a really long time — long enough that it can start to feel like giving up would be easier than holding on. Today we're going to see that Revelation talks about this directly.

---

## 2. It's okay to ask "how long"

Remember the people we heard about who asked God "How long... until you judge?" (Revelation 6:10)? They were already in heaven, with God, and they still asked that question. That tells us clearly: it is never wrong to ask God "how long" when something hard has lasted a really long time. God didn't scold them for asking. He answered them.

---

## 3. Little strength is still enough

Remember Philadelphia, who Jesus said had "little strength" but hadn't denied his name (Revelation 3:8)? Staying close to Jesus was never about being strong enough on your own. It was about not letting go of him, even in weakness, even when you're tired.

---

## 4. When it looks over, but it isn't

Remember the two witnesses, who looked totally defeated, until God brought them back to life right in front of everyone (Revelation 11:11)? Their story reminds us: what looks like the end, from the outside, isn't always the real end. God's timing and our own sense of "it's over" aren't always the same thing.

---

## 5. Being honest about the hard parts

Sometimes, when following Jesus is hard for a really long time, it can be tempting to hide that you're a Christian just to feel safer, or to stop telling people what you believe. That's a real and human thing to feel, and it's important to talk to trusted grown-ups about it rather than carrying it alone. Revelation doesn't say this feeling makes you a bad Christian. It says: stay close to Jesus, even in the tiredness, even in the waiting — and let the grown-ups who love you help carry it with you.

---

## 6. You are not carrying this alone

The people crying "how long" in Revelation weren't the only ones — there were others before them, and others would come after them, all crying the same prayer. That means whatever you or your family are waiting for, you are joined by many others, across many years, who have prayed this same honest prayer. You're not alone in it.

---

## 7. Ending where we began

We started this whole series with Jesus standing in the middle of his struggling churches, holding them in his hand. We end here, with the same promise: even when holding on gets hard, Jesus doesn't let go of you. Your story isn't finished yet, and neither is his work in it.

---

## 8. Memory verse

"Be faithful, even to the point of death, and I will give you life as your victor's crown." *(Revelation 2:10)*

---

## 9. Prayer

Dear Jesus, sometimes waiting for you to fix hard things feels like too much. Thank you that it's okay to tell you "how long." Thank you that you don't let go of us, even when we feel tired of holding on. Help us to keep trusting you, together with the people who love us. Amen.

---

## Appendix: Ideas for preparation

**Craft idea:** Revisit the hand-holding image from session 1. Have children hold hands again, and talk about how even when your arm gets tired, someone else can help hold on for you — just like Jesus, and the people around us, help us hold on.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Is there something you've been waiting a long time for God to fix? It's okay to share, or just think about it quietly.
2. Why do you think it's important to talk to a trusted grown-up if following Jesus ever feels really hard or scary?
3. What is one thing from this whole series you want to remember when things feel hard?

**Song suggestions:**
- A gentle song about God's faithfulness through hard times
- A joyful song to close the whole series, celebrating that Jesus wins in the end

**Leader note:** This is the closing session of the series. Consider taking extra time to let children (and leaders) share honestly, and close with unhurried group prayer rather than rushing to end on time.
