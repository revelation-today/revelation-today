# The Rider Who Only Pretends to Win
### A trick that looks like a win

## 1. A trick question

Imagine someone tells you: "Follow me, and you'll always win. You'll never lose, you'll never
be sad, you'll always have everything you want." Sounds amazing, right? Now — how could you
tell if that promise was actually true, or just a trick?

That's exactly the puzzle in today's story from Revelation.

## 2. Four riders on four horses

John sees four horses ride out, one after another: white, red, black, and a pale gray one. The
last three are easy to figure out — they bring fighting, hunger, and death. Not good news.

But the first one, on the white horse, looks different. He's got a crown. He rides out "to
conquer" — like a winner, like a hero. At first, he almost looks like he could be Jesus!

## 3. But look again

Here's the clue: all four riders belong together, like a team. If three of them are clearly
bad news, the first one probably is too — he's just wearing a disguise. He's dressed up like a
winner, like someone who has everything figured out and never loses. But he's not the real
King. He's a copy — a fake — pretending to be good.

## 4. Why this matters so much

This is a really important one, so let's slow down. Sometimes people might tell you: "If you
really believe in Jesus enough, you'll always have everything — always be healthy, always have
what you want, never have a hard day." That can sound just like the shiny white horse rider —
a promise of winning with nothing hard about it at all.

Here's the truth instead: Jesus loves us so much that he was willing to go through the hardest
thing of all — dying on the cross — to save us. That's what makes him different from the fake
rider. The fake rider promises an easy win with no cost at all. Jesus never promised that. He
promised something better: that he would be with us always, even when things are hard, and that
in the end, he wins for real — not a fake, empty kind of winning.

Real security doesn't come from money, or from things always going our way. It comes from
Jesus, who loves us enough to have gone through the hardest thing for us.

## 5. Wisdom to spot the difference

How do you tell the real thing from the fake? By asking: does this promise skip the hard part
that Jesus never skipped? Jesus never promised an easy road. He promised himself — always with
us, even on hard days.

## Our Refrain

Let's say it together:

**"Real security comes from Jesus, not stuff."**

## Activity

**Real or Fake Game:** Read simple statements aloud and have kids give a thumbs up (real) or
thumbs down (fake): "Jesus loves me even on hard days" (real). "If I believe hard enough I'll
never have a bad day" (fake). "Jesus is with me when things are difficult" (real). "Having lots
of stuff means God loves me more" (fake).

## Simple Prayer

*Have each child repeat after you, one phrase at a time:*

"Jesus, thank you for loving me enough to go through something hard for me. Help me trust you,
even on days that aren't easy. Amen."

## Note for the Teacher

This is one of the sessions worth extra care. Keep the correction warm and positive — "real
security comes from Jesus, not stuff" — rather than naming or criticizing any specific church,
preacher, or teaching a child might recognize from home. Many children here may have relatives
who hold prosperity teaching in good faith; the goal is to plant a deeper, truer picture of
security in Jesus, not to make a child feel their family is wrong. If children mention specific
promises they've heard (health, wealth, always winning), gently affirm the good desire behind
them (wanting good things, wanting God's blessing) while pointing to Jesus' own example of love
that included the cross.
