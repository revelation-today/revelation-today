# Welcome, Church of Latin America

Revelation was written for a people who worshiped with everything they had, who leaned on
each other like family because the world outside offered no safety net, and who could not
look at the suffering of the poor without believing God saw it too. That is not a foreign
world to the church across Latin America — in many ways, it is home ground. This regional
pack does not stand alone. It is a short set of notes meant to sit alongside whichever
confessional pack already fits your congregation — Catholic or Pentecostal/Charismatic, since
both traditions are deeply present across the region, often within the same extended family.
Use the confessional pack for the theological and liturgical framing, and use this pack for
the region-specific points of contact the wider material doesn't cover on its own.

## Where this book feels like home

Three things stand out immediately. First, few places in the world worship the way Latin
America worships — with the whole body, out loud, together, expecting God to actually show
up. Revelation's own answer to every crisis facing the seven churches is not a strategy or a
program; it is unceasing, wholehearted worship in the throne room of chapters 4 and 5. That
instinct — that worship itself is the answer, not just the warm-up before the real answer —
is already alive in your churches. Second, family and community are not add-ons to faith here;
they are the shape faith takes. Revelation assumes exactly that kind of community: believers
who stand together because standing alone was never really an option, in the first century or
now. Third, this region carries a long, hard-won memory of standing with the poor against
unjust power — a concern that runs straight through Scripture and straight through Revelation's
courtroom scenes against the empire that grew rich by crushing the weak. That instinct honors
something true in the text; it should be affirmed, not corrected.

## Where we'll think together

A few places call for extra care, and we name them honestly because they matter enough to get
right. The region's fastest-growing churches have often preached a gospel where wealth,
health, and visible success are the proof of God's favor — and Revelation's harlot Babylon is
written as a direct, pointed warning against exactly that trade: faithfulness exchanged for
glamor, security, and gain. We address this not as an attack on anyone taught this in good
faith, but as an invitation to a deeper, more durable security — the kind Revelation says can
only be found in Jesus, not in a portfolio or a platform. Second, this region's own history
with liberation theology gives it real eyes to see the empire in Revelation as an oppressor
worth resisting — a genuine strength — but it carries a real risk too: reducing the whole book
to a political program and losing the worship-first center everything else in the book flows
from. Third, like many places, this region has its own appetite for treating the beast and 666
as a literal prediction about a specific president, currency, or ID system just around the
corner — the same "newspaper exegesis" impulse that has misfired for two thousand years and is
worth naming plainly rather than indulging.

## What's inside

Twelve topics, drawn from the same master series used across the whole "Revelation Today"
project, each with adaptation notes for the region and a full adult session plus a matching
kids' session. Pair these with your confessional pack of choice, use them in order, or pull
individual sessions as needed for a class, small group, or preaching series.
