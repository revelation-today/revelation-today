# The Promise God Made With His Own Blood
### A story about the biggest promise ever made

## 1. Let's talk about family

Who is in your family? Not just your mom and dad — think about grandparents, aunts, uncles,
godparents, cousins, maybe neighbors who feel like family too. In a lot of our families, being
"family" means you help each other no matter what, right?

A long, long time ago, family meant even more than that — it meant safety. If you didn't belong
to a family, nobody had to protect you or feed you. So how could someone join a family they
weren't born into? The Bible says there were a few ways in: getting married in, being adopted
in, or... making a special kind of promise called a covenant.

## 2. A very serious kind of promise

Long ago, when two groups made this kind of serious promise (a covenant), they would do
something dramatic: they'd sacrifice an animal, cut it in half, and lay the two halves in a
row. Then someone would walk between the two halves. It meant: "If I break this promise, let
what happened to this animal happen to me."

That sounds scary! And that's exactly the point — a covenant was serious business.

## 3. God does something nobody expected

Here's the amazing part. When God made his covenant with a man named Abraham (Genesis 15), the
animals were cut in half just like always. But guess who walked between the pieces this time?

Not Abraham. **God himself** — shown as fire, passing through the middle.

That means God was saying: "If this covenant gets broken, let the punishment fall on me — not
on you."

And you know what? Abraham did break the promise later. But it wasn't Abraham's own son who
paid for it. Many years later, it was God's own Son, Jesus, who did.

## 4. A promise nobody could ever keep alone

God made this same kind of promise with the whole nation of Israel. He said, "I am the Lord
your God, who brought you out of Egypt" — reminding them of everything he'd already done for
them, before he even asked anything of them.

The most amazing part? God's rules were never impossible to keep. His people just kept choosing
not to keep them. And even then, God kept calling himself their Father — a Father who never
stopped loving them, even when they didn't deserve it. The Bible has a special word for that
kind of love: hesed — loyal love that doesn't give up.

## 5. What this means for us

That's still true for us today. God doesn't love us because we're perfect. He loves us like a
Father who already paid the price himself, so that we could belong to his family — for good.

## Our Refrain

Let's say it together:

**"God keeps his promises, even when we don't."**

## Activity

**Family Chain:** Give each child a strip of paper to write or draw their own family (or draw
stick figures). Tape the strips together into a big paper chain, and explain: God's family
works the same way — connected together, and nobody has to earn their place in it.

## Simple Prayer

*Have each child repeat after you, one phrase at a time:*

"God, thank you for loving me like a Father. Thank you for keeping your promise, even when I
mess up. Amen."

## Note for the Teacher

Keep the focus on God's faithfulness rather than the mechanics of ancient covenant-making — the
key idea for this age is simply: God made a serious promise, God kept it himself even when it
cost him everything, and that's how we became part of his family. There's no need to explain
Hittite treaty structures or the details of Genesis 15's ceremony beyond what's given above.
