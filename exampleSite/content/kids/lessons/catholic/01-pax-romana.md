# Pax Romana

### Who Is Really the Boss?

## Game: "You're the Best!"

Play a quick game. The rule: every time the leader raises a hand, everyone must jump up, clap,
and shout "You're the best!" Anyone who doesn't join in sits out a round.

Play it once or twice, then ask:
- How did that feel?
- Was that rule fair?
- What if you had to do that every single day — even on days you didn't think the leader was
  "the best"?

That silly game is a small taste of something the very first Christians dealt with for real.

## The Story: A Kingdom That Wanted Everyone to Cheer

About two thousand years ago, almost the entire known world belonged to one empire: Rome, ruled
by one man, the emperor. Those years were actually peaceful and prosperous for a long stretch —
historians call it the "Pax Romana," meaning "Roman Peace."

But there was a catch. The emperor's message was something like: "I give you safety, food, and a
good life. In return, everyone must show that I'm the greatest — almost like a god." His face was
on every coin. Huge statues of him stood in the cities. At festivals, people were expected to
toast him and praise him out loud. Whole cities competed to build him temples, hoping to win his
favor.

Christians had a problem. They already had a King — Jesus! They believed their food, safety, and
good life came from God, not Caesar. So they couldn't honestly cheer that the emperor was the
greatest.

That left them three choices: pretend to stop believing in Jesus; refuse completely, even though
that could mean real trouble; or quietly go along outside while still believing in Jesus in
secret. Revelation makes it clear: that third option is not okay with Jesus. He wants our
outsides and insides to match.

John, who wrote Revelation, chose to stand firm. Because he refused to go along, he was sent away
to a lonely island called Patmos. But even there, alone, God gave him an amazing vision: Jesus is
alive and truly reigns — far more than any emperor ever could. The book John wrote afterward was
meant to give other Christians courage to stay close to Jesus, no matter what.

## Application: Who Is Your "Caesar"?

Nobody today asks you to cheer for a statue. But the same question is still around, just wearing
different clothes: Who do you trust the most? Who do you most want to make happy?

Think about it:
- Is it more important that other kids think you're cool than that you do the right thing?
- Do you feel safer with your phone or game console than when you're talking with God?
- Do you go along with something you know is wrong, just so you fit in?

Compromise rarely shows up all at once — it sneaks in little by little, until we barely notice
who has quietly become the "boss" of our life. That's part of why going to Mass every Sunday
matters: it's a chance to say clearly, with our whole church family, that Jesus is really our
King.

## Memory Verse

"No one can serve two masters." — Matthew 6:24

## Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you for really being King — stronger than anything that scares us or anything
we want to look cool for. Help us stand for you honestly, even when it's hard. Show us if we're
secretly trusting something else more than we trust you. Amen.

## For Preparation

**Opening game:** The "You're the Best!" game above — talk afterward about how it felt.

**Craft idea:** Have each child draw a simple crown. On one side, they draw or write something
that sometimes acts like a "Caesar" in their life (a phone, wanting to be cool, video games). On
the other side, they write "Jesus." Talk about whose crown should really be biggest.

**Discussion questions:**
1. When is it hard for you to say "no" when others are doing something wrong?
2. What would you do if someone made fun of you for believing in Jesus?
3. What sometimes feels more important than Jesus to you, even if you don't want it to?

**Song suggestions:** A simple children's hymn about Jesus as King; a song about trust and
courage.
