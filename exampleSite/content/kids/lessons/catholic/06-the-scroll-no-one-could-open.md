# The Scroll No One Could Open

## Game/Hook: The Impossible Lock

Bring a box or envelope with a note taped shut with lots of tape, or a "lock" made of several
knots. Announce there's a super important message inside — but it's sealed shut, and no one in
the room is allowed to open it unless they're strong enough or important enough. Let a few kids
try (gently) to open it and fail. Then ask: "What if NO ONE could open it? What would we do?"

That's exactly the problem John had in heaven — except the stakes were even bigger.

## The Story: A Mystery With a Twist

In heaven, John saw something amazing: a scroll — like a rolled-up important letter — sealed shut
with seven seals. Back then, sealing something with seven seals meant it was super official, like
the most important document you could imagine. But there was a problem: whoever opened it would
get the power to rule the world — and no one could be found who was allowed to open it. Not
anyone in heaven, not anyone on earth, not anyone anywhere.

John was so sad about this that he started crying. Really crying. It seemed like the most
important message in the universe might never get opened.

Then someone told him good news: "Don't cry! Look — the Lion of Judah has won the battle! He can
open the scroll!"

A lion! John must have been so excited. He turned to look for a mighty, roaring lion...

...and instead he saw a Lamb. A Lamb that looked like it had once been hurt badly, but was alive
and standing strong.

Wait — a Lamb, not a Lion? Here's the twist: it's the same one. Jesus is both. He's the Lion —
strong enough to win any battle there ever was. And he's the Lamb — the one who gave up
everything, even his life, out of love for us. He didn't win by being the loudest or the
strongest fighter. He won by loving us all the way to the cross, and coming back alive. That's a
kind of winning nobody expected, and it's the best kind there is.

And the moment everyone saw the Lamb, the whole room burst into worship: "Worthy is the Lamb who
was slain to receive power and honor and glory!" Because he is worthy — because he is Jesus.

## What This Means for Us

Here's something cool: this story matches the shape of every single Mass. First, at Mass, we
listen to God's Word — the readings and the homily "open up" what God wants to say to us, just
like the scroll being opened lets its message go out into the world. Then, we come to the altar,
where Jesus, the Lamb, is really present with us. First the Word is opened. Then the Lamb is
here. That's Revelation 5, happening again every week at your parish.

Next time you hear the priest hold up the host and say "Behold the Lamb of God," and everyone
answers "Lamb of God, you take away the sins of the world" — remember: that's the whole heavenly
crowd, worshiping that same Lamb, and you get to say it too.

## Memory Verse

*"Do not weep; behold, the Lion of the tribe of Judah... has conquered."*
— Revelation 5:5

## Prayer

Jesus, thank you for being both the Lion and the Lamb — strong enough to win, and loving enough to
give everything for us. Thank you for opening God's Word to us and for being really present at
every Mass. Help us worship you the way all of heaven does. Amen.

## For Preparation

**Craft/activity ideas:**
- **Sealed scroll craft:** roll up a piece of paper with a simple message ("Jesus wins!") and let
  kids "seal" it with stickers or wax-seal stamps, then take turns "opening" it and reading the
  message aloud.
- **Lion-to-Lamb reveal:** draw or fold a picture so a lion is hidden and flips/unfolds to reveal
  a lamb underneath — a simple way to relive John's surprise.
- **Mass matching game:** make simple cards pairing "Scroll opened" with "Liturgy of the Word" and
  "Lamb present" with "Liturgy of the Eucharist," and have kids match them.

**Discussion questions:**
1. Why do you think John was so surprised to see a Lamb instead of a Lion?
2. How is Jesus both strong like a lion and gentle like a lamb at the same time?
3. What part of the Mass reminds you most of the scroll being opened? What part reminds you most
   of the Lamb being present?
