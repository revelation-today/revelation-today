# What Kind of Book Is This? Letter, Prophecy, Apocalypse

### A Letter, a Warning, and a Comic Book — All in One

## Game: Three Ways to Say Something Important

*(Bring, or describe: a letter in an envelope, a whistle, and a picture of a dragon or monster
like you'd see in a comic book.)*

Imagine I need to tell you something important. I could do it three different ways:
1. Write you a **letter**: "Dear friends, I want to tell you something..."
2. Blow a **whistle** and shout: "Watch out! Pay attention, something's happening!"
3. Show you a **comic-book picture** of a scary dragon threatening a town.

Very different, right? Here's the surprising thing: the book of Revelation does **all three at
the same time**. That's part of why it can feel confusing — but once we know what to look for,
it gets much easier.

## The Story: One Book, Three Jobs

**First, it's a letter.** Revelation begins and ends like a real letter, and John really did write
it to seven real churches, full of real people with real problems. What he wrote needed to make
sense to them, back then — not just to us, thousands of years later.

**Second, it's a warning, like the Old Testament prophets gave.** We might think a prophet just
predicts the future like a fortune-teller. But prophets mostly tell people honestly when they're
heading the wrong way, and call them back to God right now — like a friend who says, "Turn around
before this ends badly!" That's what Revelation does: it comforts people treated unfairly, and
wakes up people making bad choices.

**Third, it's like a comic book with a deeper message — an "apocalypse."** That means a book full
of big, wild pictures pointing to a deeper truth. Look at a scary dragon in a cartoon — does that
exact dragon really exist? No! But you instantly understand: something dangerous is at work.
That's how Revelation's pictures work too — the dragon, the beast with many heads, the Lamb. They
aren't photographs of the future. They send a message straight to your heart.

Pictures in the Bible can be both a real event *and* a symbol at once. Think of the Nile turning
to blood in Exodus — that really happened, and it also showed that Egypt's false gods had no real
power. Revelation's pictures work the same way: real, and pointing to something true, both at
once. There's even a clue in Revelation's very first verse — the Greek word John uses means
"showing through a symbol," the same word used back in Daniel, where God showed a king a vision
of a giant statue standing for whole kingdoms. John had a plainer word for "just telling the
news," but he chose the symbol-word instead.

Christians long ago read Revelation this same careful way, cautious about guessing exact dates or
matching pictures to one news event of their day. Instead they read it as a book about deep,
ongoing truths for the whole life of the Church — something our own Church family has practiced
for a very long time.

## Application: Whose Side Are You On?

Some people read Revelation only like a forecast for the far future. But if we forget it's first
a letter and a warning to real people, we miss the most important question it's asking us right
now: whose side are you on — today, in your regular, everyday life?

## Memory Verse

"The revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show to his servants what must soon take
place." — Revelation 1:1

## Prayer

Dear God, thank you for this exciting, mysterious book. Help us listen to what you want to tell
us today, not just read it like a puzzle about the future. Show us whose side we want to be on.
Amen.

## For Preparation

**Opening idea:** Bring a letter, a whistle, and a dragon/cartoon picture; let the children guess
what each has to do with Revelation.

**Craft idea:** Have each child draw a "comic symbol" for something good (a light, a lamb, a
shield) and something to avoid (a monster for lying or unkindness). Talk about what each picture
means.

**Discussion questions:**
1. Why do you think God didn't just write this book in a plain, ordinary way?
2. Which picture from Revelation have you heard of — the dragon, the Lamb, the golden city? What
   do you think it means?
3. What would you say to a friend who thinks Revelation is just a scary prediction?

**Song suggestions:** A song about God's Word; a song about courage and standing firm in faith.
