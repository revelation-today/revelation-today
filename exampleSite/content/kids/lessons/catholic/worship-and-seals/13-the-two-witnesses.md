# The Two Witnesses
### Brave friends who told the truth about Jesus

## 1. Starting game: "Tell the truth"

Play a quick round of "Two Truths and a Fib" — each kid says three things about themselves, two true and one made up, and the group guesses which is the fib. After a few rounds, ask: "Was it ever a little scary to tell the truth, even in a silly game? Why do you think it's sometimes hard to tell the truth about important things, especially when other people don't want to hear it?"

Say: "Today we're going to meet two brave friends in the book of Revelation whose whole job was telling the truth about Jesus — even when it made people furious. And even though it looked, for a little while, like the truth-tellers lost, God had the last word."

## 2. The story: Two witnesses, one big job

In Revelation 11, John sees two special messengers God sends to tell the world the truth about him. They wear scratchy, sad-looking clothes called sackcloth — the kind of clothes people wore back then when they wanted to show they were serious about turning back to God. These two messengers remind us of two heroes from the Old Testament: Moses, who did amazing signs in front of Pharaoh, and Elijah, who once called down fire from heaven to prove there is only one true God (you can read about that in 1 Kings 18). The two messengers in Revelation do things like that too — because their job is to show everyone that God is real and worth trusting, no matter what.

Here's an important detail: the Bible calls them "two" witnesses on purpose. Back then, one person telling a story wasn't considered enough proof — you needed two people agreeing, for it to really count (that's a rule from Deuteronomy 19:15). So God sends two, standing together, to make sure their testimony really counts.

For a long time, the two witnesses keep telling the truth, and nothing can stop them. But eventually, a scary enemy (the Bible calls it "the beast") manages to defeat them and they die. The people watching are so happy about it that they throw a party and send each other presents! They even leave the two witnesses' bodies lying in the street, refusing to bury them — the meanest way they knew to say "you lost."

But that is not the end of the story. After three and a half days, God breathes life back into the two witnesses. They stand back up. And then, in front of everyone who thought they'd won, God calls the two witnesses up to heaven. Everyone who was celebrating is left stunned. And here's the really wonderful part: instead of everyone staying mean, a huge number of people turn around and start giving glory to God instead (Revelation 11:13). Telling the truth, even when it cost everything, ended up changing hearts.

## 3. Why this matters: brave is never wasted

Here's the big point: the two witnesses didn't win by being the strongest or the loudest. They won by staying faithful to Jesus all the way to the end — even when it looked, for a little while, like the bad guys had won. God always had the last word.

This is exactly why the Catholic Church loves to remember martyrs — brave men and women throughout history who stayed loyal to Jesus even when it was hard, even when people made fun of them or hurt them for it. At every Mass, the priest says some of their names out loud, right in the middle of the most important prayer of all. Churches are named after them. We celebrate their feast days like a birthday — except it's the day they went to be with Jesus forever. The Church has never forgotten: being brave for Jesus is never wasted, even when it looks, for a moment, like it didn't work.

## 4. Talking it through

- Have you ever told the truth about Jesus, or stood up for what's right, even when it was hard or embarrassing?
- Why do you think God sent *two* witnesses instead of just one?
- Can you think of a saint or martyr whose story you know? What made them brave?

## 5. Memory verse

"Be faithful, even to the point of death, and I will give you the crown of life." *(Revelation 2:10)*

## 6. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you for the two witnesses who told the truth about you, even when it was scary. Thank you for all the brave saints and martyrs who loved you more than they loved being safe or popular. Help us be brave too, and help us remember that being brave for you is never wasted, even when it doesn't look like it right away. Amen.

## For preparation

**Craft/activity idea:** Give each child a small paper "candle" or lampstand cutout (Revelation calls the witnesses "lampstands") and have them write or draw one way they can "shine the truth" about Jesus this week, even if it's small — like being kind when others aren't, or telling a friend something true and good.

**Alternate activity:** Look up one child-friendly martyr story appropriate for the group's parish or diocese (a patron saint, a well-known martyr like St. Maximilian Kolbe or St. Agnes) and tell it briefly, connecting it to the two witnesses' pattern: brave, faithful, and honored by God even after apparent defeat.

**Discussion questions for leaders to prompt:**
1. What's the difference between being brave and just being loud or strong?
2. Why do you think the Church remembers martyrs' names during Mass?
3. What's one true thing about Jesus you could tell a friend this week?

**Song ideas:** Any simple worship song about courage, truth-telling, or the communion of saints — for example, a simple setting of "For All the Saints" or a child-friendly song about being God's light.
