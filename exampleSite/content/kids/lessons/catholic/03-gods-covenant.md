# God's Covenant: A Story of Amazing Grace
### Getting ready to talk about promises and the Mass

---

## 1. Opening: The pinky-promise game

*(Have kids pair up and make a silly pinky promise to each other — "I promise to save you the last cookie," "I promise to be your friend at recess." Then ask a few to share.)*

Promises are a big deal, right? A real promise means something — you're not supposed to break it. Today we're going to talk about the biggest, strongest kind of promise in the whole Bible. It's called a **covenant**. A covenant isn't just a promise — it's a promise so strong it makes two people (or two families, or a whole nation and God!) into one family.

And here's a surprise: you already do something every Sunday that's connected to this. Going to Mass and receiving Communion is like sitting down at a family meal that reminds us of God's biggest promise ever.

---

## 2. Long ago: promises sealed with a serious ceremony

In Bible times, when two groups made a covenant, they did something dramatic to show how serious it was. They would even walk between pieces of sacrificed animals as if to say, "This is what should happen to me if I ever break this promise!" That's how serious a covenant was.

Now here's the amazing part. When God made his covenant with Abraham (you can find it in Genesis 15), God did something nobody expected. Instead of Abraham walking through to show what would happen if *he* broke the promise, **God himself** — shown as fire — walked through alone. God, the stronger one, took the scary part that should have belonged to Abraham. That's a picture of grace: God taking on what we deserved, long before we even knew we needed it.

Later, God made a covenant with the whole people of Israel at Mount Sinai. He told them the rules (like the Ten Commandments), reminded them of everything he'd already done for them, and then — this is the important part for today — they sealed the promise by sharing a meal together on the mountain (Exodus 24). A meal made the promise real and celebrated.

---

## 3. Jesus and the Last Supper

At the Last Supper, Jesus took that same idea — a meal that seals a promise — and made it about himself. He held up the bread and said, "This is my body." He held up the cup and said, "This is my blood of the covenant." Then he said something every priest still says today: "Do this in remembrance of me."

That's exactly what happens at Mass! Every single time you go to Mass, the priest says those same words. It's not just remembering something from long ago like a history lesson — it's renewing God's biggest promise, right there, with you as part of the family meal. When you go up for Communion (or watch your family go, if you haven't had First Communion yet), you're standing in the same line as Abraham's fire, and Moses's mountaintop meal, and Jesus's Last Supper — all the way back to the very beginning.

And remember that fire that walked through for Abraham? At the cross, Jesus really did take the hard part that belonged to us, so that we could be part of God's family for good.

---

## 4. Living this out

- Next time you're at Mass, try thinking: "This is a family meal that renews God's promise to me."
- Is there a promise you've made that was hard to keep? How does it help to know God always keeps his promises to us, even when we mess up?

---

## 5. Memory verse

"This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many." *(Mark 14:24)*

---

## 6. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you for making a promise to us that you never break. Thank you for the Mass, where we get to remember your promise and be close to you. Help us to keep our promises too, and to remember that you already took care of the hard parts for us. Amen.

---

## Appendix: Ideas for preparation

**Opening idea:** The pinky-promise game above works well as a hook. You could also bring a small cup and plate to hold up when talking about the Last Supper and Mass.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Have kids draw or color a simple picture of an altar or a family table, and write one word on it that reminds them of a promise God has made (e.g., "always," "forever," "family").

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Why do you think God chose a meal, instead of just words, to seal his promises?
2. What part of the Mass reminds you the most that God keeps his promises?
3. How does it feel to know God took the "hard part" of the promise so we wouldn't have to?

**Song suggestions:**
- "One Bread, One Body"
- "Table of Plenty"
- A simple children's song about promises or God's faithfulness
