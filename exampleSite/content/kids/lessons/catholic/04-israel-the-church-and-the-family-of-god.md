# Israel, the Church, and the Family of God
### Getting ready to talk about one big family

---

## 1. Opening: The family tree game

*(Draw a simple tree on a whiteboard or poster with two big branches. Label one branch "The Twelve Tribes of Israel" and the other "The Twelve Apostles." Ask kids: do these look like two different families, or one family?)*

Today we're going to find out something really encouraging: in the Bible's last book, these two branches turn out to be leaves on the very same tree. The Church isn't a brand new family that replaced Israel — it's Israel's family, grown bigger, with room for everyone.

---

## 2. A little bit of history

A long time ago, in the Roman Empire, Jewish people were allowed to worship God without getting in trouble, as long as they stayed loyal to the emperor in other ways. At first, Romans thought Christians were just part of that same Jewish family. But Christians wouldn't worship the emperor like Rome wanted — and that got some Jewish communities worried they'd get blamed too. In a couple of cities, Smyrna and Philadelphia, some local Jewish people actually got Christians in trouble with the Roman government, because the Christians kept telling everyone that Jesus is Lord.

That's why, in Revelation, John uses a tough phrase — "synagogue of Satan" — about those specific opponents in those specific cities. It's important to know: this is about a particular argument in a particular place and time, not John saying something bad about Jewish people in general. In fact, when John says those same people would one day "bow down," he means he hopes they will come to believe in Jesus too — not that anyone has to bow down to a building or an institution.

---

## 3. One family, not two

Here's the good news that this session is really about: Revelation never turns against Israel. Not once. What Revelation cares about is whether someone loves and follows Jesus — not what family they were born into.

Look at what Revelation shows us about heaven:
- Twenty-four elders sit around God's throne — and they represent the twelve tribes of Israel AND the twelve apostles, together, as one group.
- In the New Jerusalem (that's heaven's city), the names of the twelve tribes of Israel and the names of the twelve apostles are carved right next to each other, on the same gates and foundations (Revelation 21).
- Heaven itself is even pictured as a city with a very Jewish name — Jerusalem! Not some plain, generic place.

That's why the Catholic Church has always taught — especially at a big meeting called the Second Vatican Council — that the Church is the continuation of Israel's story, the People of God made up of Jewish and Gentile believers together, all one family through Jesus.

---

## 4. Living this out

- When you disagree with someone — even a friend or a classmate — does the disagreement mean you're not family anymore? (Think about how this session shows an argument doesn't erase belonging.)
- How can we talk about people who believe differently than we do with kindness and respect, remembering we're all part of God's bigger family story?

---

## 5. Memory verse

"On the gates were written the names of the twelve tribes... and the wall of the city had twelve foundations, and on them were the twelve names of the twelve apostles." *(Revelation 21:12,14, adapted)*

---

## 6. Prayer

Dear God, thank you for making one big family out of so many different people. Help us to remember that we belong to the story of Israel and the story of the Church together. Help us to be kind, even when we disagree, because we're family. Amen.

---

## Appendix: Ideas for preparation

**Opening idea:** The family tree drawing above works well. You could also bring two lists of names (fictional "Tribe names" and "Apostle names") and have kids tape them side by side on a paper "gate," like the gates of the New Jerusalem.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Have kids design a simple paper "gate" or "wall" for the New Jerusalem, writing a tribe name on one side and an apostle name on the other, right next to each other.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Why do you think it matters that the tribes' names and the apostles' names are carved next to each other, not in separate places?
2. Have you ever had an argument with someone in your own family? Did the argument mean you weren't family anymore?
3. What's one way you can show love and respect to people who believe differently than you do?

**Song suggestions:**
- "We Are Called" (David Haas)
- "Gather Us In"
- A simple children's song about being God's family or God's people
