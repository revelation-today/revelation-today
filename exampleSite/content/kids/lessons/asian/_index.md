# Welcome

This series walks through the book of Revelation for churches across East, South, and Southeast Asia — house churches meeting quietly behind ordinary apartment doors, registered congregations navigating a watchful state, and minority congregations gathered as a small light inside a much larger Hindu, Buddhist, or Muslim community. It draws on the same underlying study material as the rest of the "Revelation Today" project, but it starts from your situation rather than treating it as an afterthought.

That matters, because for many readers of this pack, Revelation is not ancient history. The book's central dilemma — will you conform outwardly to a system that demands ultimate allegiance, or will you hold to Jesus even when that costs you something real — is not a first-century curiosity to be explained. It is a decision some of you are quietly making again this week: what to say if you're asked directly, what to put on a form, whether to register, whether to gather, whether to explain your absence from a family shrine. We want to name that plainly rather than talk around it. The book of Revelation was written by a pastor in exile, to real congregations facing a real emperor cult, and it was written to strengthen exactly this kind of costly, ordinary faithfulness.

Three things about this audience deserve to be said clearly, in gratitude rather than as mere background. First, your faithfulness is often costly in ways that comfortable Christians elsewhere rarely have to consider — whether that is the quiet risk of an unregistered gathering, the social cost of being the only believer in an extended family, or the professional cost of a background check that notes your religion. Revelation was written for exactly this kind of endurance, and it has real news for you: the letters to Smyrna and Philadelphia, the two poorest and most pressured of the seven churches, are the only two that receive no rebuke at all. Second, many of you carry an unusually deep communal and family faith — your discipleship is rarely a private, individual transaction, but something worked out inside a household, an extended family, and a tightly connected community of believers who may meet, pray, and suffer together. Third, your discipline and perseverance — showing up, staying faithful, continuing to gather, continuing to pray — over years of real pressure is not a lesser form of faith than dramatic testimony. It is exactly the "patient endurance" Revelation asks for and praises.

We also want to name, warmly and without judgment, a few real tensions this series will not pretend don't exist. Revelation's letters to the seven churches use direct, public language to name a congregation's specific failure — language that was written into a culture with its own sense of honor and shame, but one that can land very differently in cultures where public shame carries a uniquely severe social weight. So in the sessions on the seven letters and on Laodicea, we've deliberately shifted the emphasis toward private, personal reflection rather than public naming — a card you fill in alone, not a confession you're asked to share aloud. We also take seriously, as an open pastoral question rather than a closed one, how exclusive worship of the Lamb relates to the real, unresolved questions many of you carry about honoring parents and ancestors — the text itself doesn't hand us a tidy answer, and neither will we.

May this series affirm what your churches already know in your bones: that faithfulness under pressure is not a burden to survive but a form of reigning with Christ, right now, in the middle of the story.
