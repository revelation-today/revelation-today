# The Tower That Fell, and the Prophet Who Tried to Be Clever
### Two old warnings that still matter today

---

## 1. Warm-up: The tallest tower

Have you seen pictures of the tallest buildings in the world? People love building tall, impressive things — sometimes just to show off: "Look how great we are!" That's exactly what today's first story is about.

---

## 2. The Tower of Babel

Long ago, people decided to build a huge tower reaching to the sky. Their reason: "Let's make a name for ourselves, so we won't be scattered across the earth." They wanted to make themselves great — completely without God.

What happens? God "comes down" to look at the tower — almost like he's teasing them, because this huge tower they were so proud of looked tiny to him. He mixes up their languages, and the very thing they feared — being scattered across the earth — happens anyway.

**The result:** Everything they wanted to achieve fails. But later, God makes one man — Abraham — into a truly great name, without any tower at all.

---

## 3. Balaam — the prophet with the donkey

The second story is about a man named Balaam. An enemy king wanted Balaam to curse God's people, Israel. But God wouldn't allow it — and when Balaam tried anyway, even his own donkey refused to move, because it saw an angel of God with a drawn sword that Balaam himself couldn't see! In the end, Balaam could only bless Israel, not curse them at all.

---

## 4. The second try

But Balaam didn't give up. If a loud attack (a curse) didn't work, he tried something else: he suggested the enemy king attack Israel more gently but more dangerously — through a big party with foreign gods, where lots of Israelites joined in and turned away from God.

That's an important lesson: **sometimes the biggest danger isn't a loud attack from outside — it's a quiet invitation to "just go along with it."**

---

## 5. A question worth asking gently

In some families, there are old traditions about honoring parents and grandparents who have passed away — special meals, special days, special customs. The Bible doesn't give one simple answer that fits every family and every tradition, and this isn't a topic for us to decide for someone else's family. What we can say clearly is this: Jesus wants our whole heart, our whole trust, our whole worship — and that's worth talking about, honestly and gently, with your own parents or a trusted pastor, rather than figuring it out alone.

---

## 6. Why both stories matter

- Babel warns us: be careful about making yourself (or even your family) great without God.
- Balaam warns us: be careful when someone gently invites you to "just go along with it," even if it seems harmless.

Both stories remind us: God's people rarely fall because of a loud enemy. They fall much more often because of quiet compromise.

---

## 7. Memory verse

"Let the one who boasts, boast in the Lord." *(1 Corinthians 1:31)*

---

## 8. Prayer

Dear God, help us not to make ourselves great without you, and help us notice when we're being gently invited to go along with something wrong. If we have questions about family traditions, help us bring them honestly to you and to the grown-ups who love us. Amen.

---

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening idea:** Build a tall tower together out of blocks that then tips over — a picture of the Tower of Babel.

**Storytelling idea:** Tell the story of Balaam and the talking donkey vividly — kids love this story!

**Note for leaders:** Section 5 raises ancestor veneration and filial piety gently as an open family question, not a verdict. Be ready to listen more than you speak if a child brings up a specific family practice, and point questions back to parents/pastors for follow-up rather than resolving them in the moment.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. When have you wanted to show off how great you are? How did that turn out?
2. Which is harder to notice: a loud attack, or a gentle invitation to "just go along with it"?
3. Where are you sometimes gently invited to do something you already know isn't right?

**Song ideas:**
- A song about Balaam and the donkey, if known, or a similar storytelling song
- A song about humility and faithfulness to God
