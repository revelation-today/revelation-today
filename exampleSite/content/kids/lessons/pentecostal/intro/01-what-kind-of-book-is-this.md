# What Kind of Book Is This?
### Getting ready to read Revelation together

## 1. Starting game: Guess the picture

Show the kids a political cartoon or a simple symbolic drawing (a picture of a big, strong
animal squashed by something small, for example). Ask: "Do you think this animal is real? Is
the artist saying this really happened?" Kids will usually say no right away — they understand
instantly that a picture like this is making a point using symbols, not reporting news.

Say: "That's exactly the kind of picture-language we're going to find in the book of
Revelation, the last book of the Bible. It's full of amazing, wild pictures — dragons, beasts,
a Lamb who is actually a Lion. Today we're just going to learn how to read those pictures the
right way, before we dive into the story."

## 2. A letter, a warning, and a picture-book, all at once

Revelation is actually three kinds of writing mixed together:

- It's a **letter** — written to seven real churches, real people, a long time ago, who needed
  to read it and understand it.
- It's a **warning-message from God** (the Bible calls this "prophecy") — meant to comfort
  people who were having a hard time, and to help everyone choose to follow God more closely,
  right away, not just someday.
- It's a **picture-book of spiritual truth** (the Bible calls this "apocalypse") — full of big,
  dramatic symbols like dragons and beasts and lambs that aren't meant to be taken as a
  photograph, but as a picture pointing to something true underneath.

## 3. Why this matters

Some people read Revelation like a treasure map of the news — trying to match every dragon and
beast to something happening in the world today. But Revelation was written so the very first
people who read it could understand it and be encouraged by it — not so that only people living
thousands of years later could finally crack the code.

That means when we read about dragons and beasts in the next few weeks, we're going to ask:
"What true thing is this picture showing us?" — not "which country or which person does this
match today?"

## 4. An example

Think about the Lamb who is also called the Lion of Judah. Nobody expects an actual lamb the
size of a lion, or a lion made of wool. The picture is the point: something that looks weak
(a lamb) turns out to be the strongest, bravest one of all (a lion). That's exactly how
Revelation wants us to read — looking past the picture to the truth it's pointing at.

## 5. Why this is good news

Some of you might have heard grown-ups talk about the end times in ways that sound scary or
confusing — maybe from movies or books. Over the next weeks, we're going to look together at
what the Bible actually says, slowly and carefully, straight from the book itself. You don't
need to be afraid of Revelation. It was written to help God's people feel braver and closer to
Jesus, not more scared.

## 6. Memory verse

"The revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take
place." *(Revelation 1:1, adapted)*

## 7. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you for the book of Revelation. Help us read its pictures the right way, so
we can understand what you want to show us. Help us feel excited to learn about you, not
scared. Amen.

## Appendix: Preparation ideas

**Opening activity:** Bring a simple political cartoon or symbolic drawing (or draw one
yourself — e.g., a tiny lamb defeating a huge dragon) and ask the kids what it means before you
explain.

**Craft idea:** Have kids draw their own "lion who is really a lamb" picture, and talk about
how a picture can show something true even when it isn't a photograph.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Have you ever seen a cartoon or picture that used symbols to tell a true story?
2. Why do you think God chose to use pictures like dragons and lambs instead of just telling
   the story in plain words?
3. What's one question you have about the book of Revelation that you're hoping we answer this
   series?

**Song ideas:** Any simple worship song about God's Word being trustworthy and good.
