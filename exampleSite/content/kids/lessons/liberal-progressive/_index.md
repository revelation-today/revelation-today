# Welcome

If you come from a theologically progressive, mainline tradition, there's a good chance the book of Revelation has mostly functioned in your life as a book you politely avoid. Maybe you've heard it invoked more often by people you disagree with than by your own pulpit. Maybe the bowls of wrath, the lake of fire, and the stark line between the saved and the lost sit so awkwardly against everything you believe about God's expansive, inclusive love that it feels safer to let the book gather dust between Jude and the hymnal.

This series is built for you, and it isn't built to talk you out of your instincts. It's built on the conviction that this audience actually brings real strengths to reading Revelation — strengths this book needs. You already take seriously that God's concern reaches to the margins, to the poor, the persecuted, the economically exploited — and that is exactly who Revelation was written for and to. You're willing to do the work of historical-critical study, to ask what a text meant to its first hearers before asking what it means for us — and Revelation, read that way, turns out to be far less strange and far more urgent than its reputation suggests. And your commitment to justice for the oppressed maps almost exactly onto the book's own great theme: that the "wrath of the Lamb" is not a caprice but a long-overdue reckoning on behalf of real victims of real empires.

So this series won't ask you to stop being who you are theologically. It will ask you to actually open the book.

## Where this gets harder

Two honest challenges are worth naming up front, because avoiding them would be its own kind of dishonesty.

The first is a habit of reading around the hard two-thirds of the book — treating the seals, trumpets, bowls, beasts, and harlot as an embarrassing ancient relic to be explained away as "just" a coping mechanism for a persecuted minority, rather than a message that still has a claim on us. Apocalyptic is a genre, and genres have rules, but having rules is not the same as having no authority. We'll take the genre question seriously in session 1, and we'll keep returning to it, because skipping past discomfort isn't the same as resolving it.

The second is Laodicea — arguably the single most important mirror this whole series holds up. Laodicea was the wealthiest, most respectable, most self-satisfied of the seven churches, and it is the only one Jesus has nothing good to say about. Not because it was persecuted and failed under pressure, like some of the others, but because it never faced any pressure worth naming, and mistook comfort for faithfulness. That is worth sitting with honestly, as an invitation rather than an accusation, especially for those of us in traditions that are, by and large, comfortable and established rather than embattled and marginal.

Thirteen sessions follow — for adults and, in a shorter form, for kids — walking through Revelation's actual argument: worship first, judgment as justice, empire unmasked, and a hope stubborn enough to survive honest questions. Come with what you already believe about God. Bring your questions about wrath and judgment, too — they're taken seriously here, not talked past.
