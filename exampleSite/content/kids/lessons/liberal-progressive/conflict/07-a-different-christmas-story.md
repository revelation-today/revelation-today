# A Different Christmas Story

### Revelation's own version of the nativity

## 1. Getting started: the same story, different picture

You know the Christmas story — Mary, Joseph, the manger, the shepherds, the star. Did you know the Bible tells that same story one more time, in a totally different way, using a dragon instead of a stable? Let's look at it.

## 2. The scene

In Revelation 12, John sees a woman "clothed with the sun," with the moon under her feet and a crown of twelve stars, about to give birth. Facing her is a huge, terrifying red dragon, waiting to devour her baby the instant it's born.

If you only looked at this moment, it would seem hopeless: a woman in the middle of labor, completely exposed, against an enormous, powerful monster. No contest, right?

## 3. The surprise ending

But here's the twist: the baby is born, and instead of being devoured, he is "snatched up to God and to his throne." The dragon, with every advantage, loses. The woman and the baby, with no advantages at all, win.

## 4. Who is who

The dragon is later named directly: Satan, the same enemy from the Garden of Eden. The baby is Jesus — the passage tells us he'll "rule all the nations," which points straight back to promises about the coming Messiah. And the woman represents God's people, waiting a long, long time for their promised rescuer, at a moment when they were living under a powerful empire that could crush anyone who resisted it.

## 5. Why this matters

This is really the same Christmas story we already know — just told to show us something important: Jesus wasn't born into a safe, comfortable world. He was born right in the middle of danger, facing a real and powerful threat — and he still won, not through force, but just by surviving and being lifted up to God. That's a pattern that shows up again and again in the Bible: the one who looks weak turns out to win, and the one who looks unstoppable turns out to lose.

## 6. Memory verse

"She gave birth to a son... and her child was snatched up to God and to his throne." *(Revelation 12:5, shortened)*

## 7. Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you that you came into a hard, dangerous world and still won. Help us remember that you're stronger than anything that scares us, even when things look hopeless. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening idea:** Show the classic nativity picture side-by-side with an image of the woman-and-dragon scene from Revelation 12 (many family Bibles include an illustration) and ask what's the same and what's different.

**Craft idea:** Kids draw the "hopeless-looking" moment (woman and dragon) and then the "surprise win" moment (baby lifted to the throne) side by side.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Why do you think God chose to tell the Christmas story this second way, with a dragon instead of just a stable?
2. Can you think of another Bible story where someone who looked weak actually won?
3. When have you felt like the "dragon" in your own life looked way too big to beat?

**Song suggestions:**
- A Christmas song about Jesus as a rescuer, not just a baby
- A song about God's strength being bigger than any fear
