# The City That Looked Rich but Wasn't Really

### Who is the Harlot Babylon?

## 1. Getting started: the birthday party trap

Imagine a kid invites you to a huge birthday party, promising the best presents ever — but only if you help them be mean to another kid first. The party sounds amazing. The price to get in is doing something you know is wrong.

## 2. A dazzling, dangerous city

In Revelation 17 and 18, John sees a city called "Babylon," dressed up like a beautiful bride — gold, jewels, fine clothing. She looks glamorous and successful. But the more John looks, the more he sees what's actually underneath: a system built on cheating people, treating people unfairly, and forgetting God — all covered over by wealth and glitter.

## 3. What Babylon really trades in

The saddest detail: when the list of everything Babylon buys and sells is read out loud, right there among the gold and jewelry and spices is something else — people themselves, treated like cargo instead of like people who matter. That's the real problem with Babylon: not that she's rich, but that her riches come from treating people as things instead of as people God loves.

## 4. When Babylon falls

Eventually, Babylon collapses — and the very people who got rich alongside her don't grieve for the people who were hurt. They grieve their own lost profits. That tells us something important: anything built only on "what's in it for me" falls apart the moment it stops paying off.

## 5. Why this matters for us

It would be easy to just point at "Babylon" out there somewhere and feel glad we're not part of it. But the Bible's real question is closer to home: are there ways, even small ones, that we get used to treating people as less important than money, comfort, or success? Babylon isn't just an ancient city — it's a warning worth checking against our own choices.

## 6. Memory verse

"Come out of her, my people." *(Revelation 18:4, shortened)*

## 7. Prayer

Dear God, help us to notice when something looks glamorous on the outside but isn't treating people fairly underneath. Help us to care about people more than about looking successful. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening idea:** Use the birthday-party scenario from section 1 to spark discussion about tempting offers that cost something wrong.

**Craft idea:** Have kids draw a "glittery" picture on the outside of a folded paper, and something honest and simple on the inside — talk about how appearances can be different from what's really true.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Have you ever been tempted by something that looked good on the outside but wasn't really okay?
2. Why do you think it matters that Babylon's trade list includes people, not just goods?
3. What's one way you can make sure you're treating people as more important than money or success?

**Song suggestions:**
- A song about caring for others
- A song about choosing what's right over what looks impressive
