## A funny game

Let's play a quick game. The rule: whenever I raise my hand, everyone must stand up, clap, and shout "You are the greatest!" Anyone who doesn't join in has to sit out a round.

*(Play once or twice, then ask: How did that feel? Was the rule fair? What if you had to do that every single day — even on days you didn't believe I was "the greatest" at all?)*

That's a game. But a long time ago, real people had to play something just like it — for real, not for fun. That's what we're talking about today.

## Long ago: the emperor wants everyone to say yes

When the last book of the Bible — Revelation — was written, almost the whole known world belonged to one empire, ruled by one man: the emperor.

The emperor said: "I take care of everything. Safety. Enough food. Peace. A good life. In return, I ask only one thing: everyone must show that I am the greatest — almost like a god."

You could see it everywhere: his face on every coin, huge statues of him everywhere, and at every feast and celebration people had to raise a toast in his honor.

If you didn't join in, you could get into serious trouble.

## The problem: Christians already had a King

The Christians thought: "Wait a moment. We already have a King — Jesus! And our food, our safety, our good life come from God, not from the emperor." So they could not honestly shout, "The emperor is the greatest!"

It's a bit like if someone in your class suddenly announced: "From today, everyone laughs at the new kid, or you don't belong anymore." Some kids laugh along — not because they think it's funny, but because they want to belong. The first Christians faced that choice every single day: join in completely and pretend they didn't believe in Jesus anymore; say no completely, even though that could mean real trouble; or join in a little bit while secretly still believing in Jesus — showing one thing outside while their heart believed something else.

## John — someone who said no

The man who wrote Revelation was named John. He tells us himself: because he wouldn't join in, he was sent away to a lonely island called Patmos — far from home, all alone.

But right there, all by himself, John receives an enormous, encouraging picture from God: Jesus is alive. Jesus truly reigns — far more than any emperor ever could. The whole book John writes afterward is meant to give Christians courage to stay with Jesus, no matter what happens.

## And us today? Who is our "emperor"?

Nobody today makes us cheer for a statue of an emperor. But the same question is still here, just wearing new clothes: **Who do you trust the most? Who do you most want to make happy?**

A few things to think about: Is it more important that other kids think you're cool than that you do what's right? Do you feel safer with your phone in your hand than when you're talking with God? Do you go along with something you know is wrong, just so you belong?

These are our "emperors" today — things or people we trust more, or care about more, than Jesus, without even noticing it.

## The frog in warm water

Do you know the story about the frog in the water? Drop a frog into boiling water, and it jumps straight out. But warm the water up very, very slowly, and the frog doesn't notice the danger — until it's too late. *(This isn't quite true biologically, but it's a good picture.)*

The same thing can happen to us. Nobody forces us, all at once, to forget about Jesus. It happens little by little — a small excuse here, a little bit of going along there — until we don't even notice anymore who has really become the "boss" of our life.

## What helps us

Good news: we don't have to hide, and we don't have to go along with things just to belong. Jesus is already the true King — far stronger than any emperor, any trend, anything "everyone else" is doing. He invites us to belong to him honestly, to trust him, and to never hide him.

**Memory verse:** "No one can serve two masters." (Matthew 6:24)

## Prayer

Dear Jesus, thank you that you are truly King — much stronger than anything that scares us or anything we want to look cool for. Help us to stand for you honestly, even when it's hard. Show us when we're secretly trusting something else more than you. Amen.
