# The City That Promised Everything and Kept Nothing
### Who is the harlot Babylon?

## 1. Fool's gold

Show the kids a piece of gold-colored foil or glitter, or describe "fool's gold" (pyrite) —
something that looks valuable and shiny but isn't actually worth anything. Ask: how would
you find out something that looks valuable isn't really worth what it seems?

Today's story is about a city in Revelation that looks incredibly rich and glamorous — but
turns out to be exactly like fool's gold.

## 2. A woman dressed like a queen

In Revelation 17, John sees a woman dressed in purple and scarlet, covered in gold and
jewels, riding on a scary beast. She's called "Babylon," and kings and rulers from all
over the world are in love with her — her wealth, her glamour, her power.

For the very first readers of Revelation, this wasn't a mystery — they knew right away
this was a picture of Rome, the powerful city that ruled the world at that time. Rome
looked amazing on the outside: rich, powerful, in charge of everything. But something was
deeply wrong underneath.

## 3. What was wrong with her

The woman looks glamorous, but the Bible tells us she's actually done terrible things —
she's hurt God's faithful people to get her wealth and power (Revelation 17:6). All her
glitter and gold were built on hurting others, not on anything good or true.

## 4. Her allies turn on her

Here's the surprising twist: the very kings and rulers who loved Babylon and made her rich
suddenly turn against her and destroy her (Revelation 17:16). All her "friends" only
stuck around because of what she could give them — the moment that stopped being useful,
they abandoned her completely.

That's what happens to anything built on the wrong foundation — money, power, or fame
instead of truth and love. It might look strong for a while, but it can fall apart very
suddenly, and the people who seemed loyal can vanish overnight.

## 5. A warning for us too

Revelation actually warns God's own people about this: "Come out of her, my people"
(Revelation 18:4). That means: don't let yourself get tricked into trusting the same kind
of glittery, empty promises — wanting to be popular, having lots of stuff, or having power
over other people — more than you trust God.

## 6. What this means for us

It's easy to be dazzled by things that look amazing on the outside — like fool's gold. But
Revelation reminds us to ask: is this built on truth and love, or is it just shiny on the
outside? God's promises might not always sparkle the loudest, but they're the only ones
that never fall apart.

**Memory verse:** "Come out of her, my people, so that you will not share in her sins."
(Revelation 18:4)

## 7. Prayer

Dear God, help us not to be fooled by things that look amazing but aren't built on truth
or love. Help us to trust you, even when your promises don't look as shiny as the world's.
Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening activity:** The "fool's gold" object lesson above, or bring an obviously fake
piece of jewelry to compare with something plain but genuinely valuable.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Have kids draw two pictures: something that "looks amazing but
isn't really good for you," and something that "looks plain but is really good," and
discuss the difference.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Can you think of something that looks amazing but isn't actually good for you?
2. Why do you think Babylon's "friends" abandoned her so quickly?
3. What's one shiny-looking thing you sometimes trust more than you trust God?
