# The Rider Who Pretends to Be Good
### The four horsemen unmasked, and the wrath of the Lamb

## 1. A trick offer

Ask the kids: has anyone ever offered you something that sounded amazing at first, but
turned out to be a trick? (Like "I'll trade you my broken toy for your good one, and it's
totally fair!") How did you figure out it wasn't really a good deal?

Today's story is about four riders on horses — and the first one looks amazing at first,
but turns out to be a trick.

## 2. Four riders appear

In Revelation 6, Jesus opens four seals, and four riders come out, one at a time: a white
horse, a red horse, a black horse, and a pale, sickly-colored horse. The red, black, and
last horse clearly bring bad things — war, unfair prices for food, and death.

But the first rider, on the white horse, looks different. White is usually a "good" color
in the Bible. He wears a crown. He looks like a winner. A lot of people have wondered:
could this first rider actually be a good guy — even a picture of Jesus?

## 3. Unmasking the trick

Here's the clue that solves the mystery: all four horses are described together, as one
group, doing one job. And when you look closely, the "crown" this rider wears isn't unique
to him — later in Revelation, even scary, monster-like creatures wear crowns too. And the
"conquering" this rider does is the same word used later for the bad guys attacking God's
people.

So here's the twist: the first rider isn't Jesus at all. He's a fake, dressed up to look
like Jesus — a trick, just like the trick toy offer from before. He promises victory and
looks impressive, but he leaves war, unfairness, and death right behind him, exactly like
the other three.

## 4. Why this matters

This teaches us something really important: sometimes bad things dress up to look good at
first. They make big, shiny promises. But watch what happens after you follow them — do
they leave good things behind, or trouble? Jesus never tricks us or dresses up to look
like something he's not. He's honest about the cost of following him, and he never leaves
destruction behind.

## 5. The wrath of the Lamb

After all this trouble, people on earth get scared and say, "Hide us... from the wrath of
the Lamb!" (Revelation 6:16). That sounds surprising — Jesus is called "the Lamb" because
he's gentle and gave his life for us. But here's the thing: the people are scared because
they know they've done wrong, not because Jesus is suddenly mean or out of control. It's
like when you've done something wrong and you're scared to see your parents — not because
your parents are unfair, but because you know what you did.

Jesus is patient — the Bible says his anger builds up slowly, like water warming up, not
like a sudden explosion. He gives lots of chances to turn back to him before anything else
happens.

## 6. What this means for us

We don't need to be scared of Jesus if we're trusting and following him honestly. But we
do need to watch out for tricks that look good on the outside but leave trouble behind.

**Memory verse:** "No one can serve two masters." (Matthew 6:24)

## 7. Prayer

Dear Jesus, help us to notice when something looks good but is really a trick. Thank you
that you never trick us — you tell us the truth. Help us trust you, even when being honest
is harder than going along with the crowd. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening activity:** The "trick offer" discussion above, or a simple trading game where
one "deal" looks great but is secretly unfair — debrief afterward.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Draw two pictures side by side: something that looks good but
isn't (like candy that's actually bad for you) and something that's honestly good (like
vegetables, or a true friend). Talk about how to tell the difference.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Can you think of a time something looked good at first but caused trouble later?
2. Why do you think the first rider is dressed up to look like a good guy?
3. Why do people in this story feel scared, even though Jesus is patient and gentle?
