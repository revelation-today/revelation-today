# What Kind of Book Is Revelation, Anyway?
### Learning to read Revelation the right way

## 1. Guess the kind of book

Hold up a few different kinds of writing — a text message, a birthday card, a recipe, a
comic book. Ask the kids: how do you know how to read each one? Do you read a recipe the
same way you read a comic book?

*(Let them answer, then say:)* Right — the kind of writing tells you how to read it. A
recipe tells you exactly what to do, step by step. A comic book uses pictures and
sometimes exaggerates things to make a point. Revelation is a little bit like several
different kinds of writing mixed together, and today we'll learn what those are.

## 2. Revelation is a letter

First, Revelation is a letter — like a letter you might send to a friend. It was actually
written to seven real churches, in seven real towns, a long, long time ago. It starts like
a letter and ends like a letter. That means the very first people who read it had real
problems Revelation was talking to them about — problems like being scared, or being
tempted to do the wrong thing to fit in.

That doesn't mean it has nothing to say to us! It means we understand it best when we
remember: it was written to real people first, facing real problems, just like us.

## 3. Revelation is a prophecy

Second, Revelation is a prophecy. A lot of people think "prophecy" just means predicting
the future, like a fortune teller. But in the Bible, prophets weren't mainly fortune
tellers — they were people who reminded God's people what God had already told them, and
called them to actually live it out. A prophet's job was to say: "Here's what God wants —
are you doing it? If not, turn back to him." Revelation does exactly that: it reminds the
seven churches what God wants, and calls them to keep loving Jesus and doing what's right,
right now — not just someday far in the future.

## 4. Revelation is an "apocalypse"

Third — and this is the trickiest word — Revelation is an "apocalypse." That's a fancy
word, but here's what it means: it's a special kind of story that uses big, wild pictures
to show something true, without meaning those pictures literally.

Here's an example: imagine a newspaper cartoon showing one country as a giant dragon and
another as a little mouse. Nobody thinks a real dragon exists — but everyone instantly
understands what the cartoonist means. Revelation works kind of like that. When it talks
about scary beasts and dragons, it's not giving us a photograph of the future. It's using
big pictures to show us something true about good and evil, right now.

And here's the really important part: an apocalypse doesn't let you sit in the middle. It
asks you to pick a side — are you with God, or against him? There's no "sort of."

## 5. So how should we read it?

Putting it all together: Revelation was written to real people, a long time ago (that's
the letter part). It calls us to actually live differently right now, not just wonder
about someday (that's the prophecy part). And it uses big, wild pictures to show deep
truths, not photographs of the future (that's the apocalypse part).

That means Revelation isn't a secret code we have to crack to figure out exactly what will
happen next Tuesday. It's a book that wants to change how we live starting today.

## 6. A picture to remember

Think of Revelation like a stained-glass window in a big old church. From up close, it
just looks like a jumble of colored glass shapes. But step back, and you see it tells a
whole story — Jesus is at the center of it, and God wins in the end. Revelation is like
that: lots of strange pictures that, taken together, tell one clear story.

## 7. What helps us

The good news: we don't need to be scared of this strange book, or turn it into a puzzle
to solve. God gave it to help us live faithfully for Jesus every day — starting now.

**Memory verse:** "Blessed is the one who reads aloud the words of this prophecy, and
blessed are those who hear it and take to heart what is written in it." (Revelation 1:3)

## 8. Prayer

Dear God, thank you for giving us Revelation, even though it's sometimes a strange and
puzzling book. Help us not to be scared of it or turn it into a guessing game. Help us
understand its big pictures so we can love and follow Jesus better every day. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening activity:** Bring several types of writing (recipe, comic panel, birthday card,
text message) and let kids sort out how each should be "read."

**Craft/discussion idea:** Have kids draw their own simple "cartoon" using one picture to
represent something true (for example, a picture of a turtle to represent being slow, or
a lion to represent being brave). Talk about how a picture can be true without being
literal.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. Why does it help to know Revelation was first written as a letter to real churches?
2. Can you think of another example of a picture or cartoon that shows something true
   without being literally real?
3. What's one way you could "take to heart" something from Revelation this week?
