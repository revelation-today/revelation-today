# One Big Family
### Israel, the church, and the family of God

## 1. Adopted into the family

Ask the kids: has anyone ever joined a family by adoption, or knows a family that adopted
a child? Talk briefly about how an adopted child becomes a *real*, full member of the
family — not a second-class member.

That's a picture of something amazing the Bible tells us about: how everyone who trusts in
Jesus — no matter where they come from — gets adopted into God's family, the same family
God started with a man named Abraham a very long time ago.

## 2. God's family started small

A long time ago, God chose one family — Abraham's family, who became the nation of
Israel — to be his special people. He gave them special names: his "treasured possession,"
his "son," his "bride," a "kingdom of priests."

## 3. The family gets bigger

Here's the exciting part. When Jesus came, he opened the door for people from every
nation — not just Abraham's family by blood — to join God's family too. The apostle Paul
called this a "mystery" — not because it's spooky, but because it was such an amazing
surprise: people from every corner of the world, Jews and non-Jews together, now belong
to the very same family, with the very same promises.

That means all those special names God gave to Israel long ago — "treasured possession,"
"God's own children," "kingdom of priests" — now belong to the church too! Not instead of
Israel, but together with believing Israel, as one big family.

## 4. A family disagreement

Sometimes families argue, even when they still love each other. In the time Revelation was
written, some Jewish people were upset with Christians and even reported them to the
Roman authorities to get them in trouble. That's why Revelation says some tough things
about certain groups who were fighting against the church.

But here's the important part: Revelation isn't saying Jewish people are rejected by God.
It's more like a family argument — sharp words exchanged between people who are actually
part of the same family story, not two completely separate groups.

## 5. One city for everyone

At the very end of Revelation, we see a beautiful city called the New Jerusalem. It has
twelve gates, named after the twelve tribes of Israel, and twelve foundation stones, named
after the twelve apostles. Both sets of names, side by side, holding up the very same
city! That's a picture of the whole family — old and new, Israel and the church — brought
together as one people, home together forever.

## 6. What this means for us

If you trust in Jesus, you are part of this same big family — adopted in, not a visitor,
not a second-class member. You have the same promises Abraham had. You belong.

**Memory verse:** "So in Christ Jesus you are all children of God through faith."
(Galatians 3:26)

## 7. Prayer

Dear God, thank you for making us part of your family through Jesus. Thank you that we
are not visitors or outsiders, but truly your children. Help us to love everyone in your
big family, even when we disagree with each other. Amen.

## Appendix: Ideas for leaders

**Opening activity:** Ask kids to name people in their own family who joined it in
different ways (birth, marriage, adoption) — use this to introduce that God's family also
has different "doors in," and everyone who comes in through Jesus is fully family.

**Craft/discussion idea:** Draw a big city gate together and write the names of everyone
in the room on it, alongside "the twelve tribes" and "the twelve apostles," to show
everyone belongs to the same city/family.

**Questions for the kids:**
1. What does it mean that you're not just a "visitor" in God's family, but truly a child?
2. Why do you think the New Jerusalem has both the twelve tribes' names and the twelve
   apostles' names on it?
3. How can you show love toward someone in God's family that you sometimes disagree with?
