# Welcome, Friends in the Confessional Lutheran/Reformed Tradition

If you grew up on a catechism, you already have most of what you need to read Revelation
well. This series adapts the wider "Revelation Today" material for confessional
Lutheran and Reformed congregations — catechism-literate, creedal, comfortable with the
historical-critical method, and generally already amillennial in conviction, whether or
not that word ever came up in confirmation class. Of every adaptation in this project,
this is the one that changes the least. That's not a mark against it. It means this book
confirms what your tradition has been teaching for centuries, rather than asking you to
adopt something foreign to it.

## Why this needs so little translation

Three strengths this audience brings to the text are worth naming up front, because they
are exactly the tools Revelation rewards. First, you already think in covenant categories.
Reformed and Lutheran catechesis roots the whole Bible in the covenant God made with
Abraham and renewed at Sinai — a covenant of grace in which God himself bears the curse
his people deserved. Revelation assumes that same covenant structure at every turn: the
blessings and curses, the faithful remnant, the marriage between God and his people. You
will not need this vocabulary introduced to you; you will simply need to see it named
explicitly in the text, which this series does throughout. Second, you are comfortable
with genre-aware, historically grounded reading. The confessional tradition never asked
Scripture to be something it isn't, and Revelation asks its readers up front to notice
that it is letter, prophecy, and apocalypse all at once, addressed first to seven real
congregations in Asia Minor. That is not a concession to modern scholarship; it is simply
careful reading, the same kind your tradition has practiced since the Reformation.
Third, and most significantly: this series reads Revelation 20 as amillennial — the
thousand years as a symbolic description of the whole church age between Christ's first
and second comings, not a future golden age bolted onto the end of history. That is
precisely the reading confessed in the Augsburg Confession, Article XVII, which rejects
"Jewish opinions" of an earthly, thousand-year reign of the saints before the resurrection
of the dead. When this series discusses "the historic rejection of chiliasm," it is
naming your own confessional heritage, not correcting it.

## What's inside

Ten topics, each with a full adult session and a matching session for children, drawn from
the same underlying source material used across the whole "Revelation Today" project. Where
the material overlaps with confessional vocabulary — covenant theology, amillennialism, the
communion of saints gathered in worship — these sessions name that vocabulary explicitly, so
the material feels like it is speaking your theological language rather than translating
from someone else's tradition. Use the sessions in order as a short adult-education or
confirmation-adjacent series, or draw on individual sessions for a sermon, a Bible study, or
a family devotional evening.
