## When You Don't Know How Much Longer

You've probably had a season where you just wanted to know: how much longer does this last? Waiting for test results, waiting to hear back from a school, waiting for a friendship to either heal or finally end, waiting for a hard family situation to resolve. The not-knowing is often worse than the hard thing itself. It's disorienting to feel like you're stuck in a holding pattern with no end date.

Daniel and the Jewish exiles in Babylon lived that feeling for real, on a national scale. Their temple was destroyed, their city was rubble, and they were living as captives in a foreign empire, wondering if this was just permanent now. Then Daniel gets an answer — not a vague "hang in there," but something startlingly specific: God gives him an actual countdown.

## What's Actually Going On

Daniel had read the prophet Jeremiah's promise that Israel's exile would last seventy years, and as that time was drawing to a close, Daniel prayed — a long, honest prayer of repentance for his people. In response, he's given a vision about "seventy sevens" (Daniel 9:24-27), a phrase built on the idea of a "week" of years rather than days, tying back to Israel's sabbath rhythms.

Here's the big-picture point worth holding onto, because the exact math inside this vision has been debated by serious scholars for centuries, and it's easy to get lost arguing over which specific years line up with which specific decrees. What matters most for your faith isn't nailing every date. It's this: God told Daniel exactly what these seventy sevens were for. The vision lists concrete goals — putting an end to sin, atoning for wickedness, bringing in everlasting righteousness, anointing the most holy place. Those are enormous goals, the kind of thing no ordinary king or empire could accomplish. And the pattern the text lays out — a period leading up to "the Anointed One," followed by his being cut off, followed by a final period in which a covenant is confirmed and sacrifice is brought to an end — points forward to Jesus and what he accomplished on the cross far more fully than to any purely political event in Israel's history.

So even while a lot of the fine detail is genuinely contested — how the different segments of years connect, what starting date each part is measured from — the underlying message isn't contested at all: God was not making Israel wait in exile with no plan. He had already numbered the years, named the goal, and pointed forward to the person who would achieve it. That's worth sitting with before getting distracted by the calculations.

## The 3.5-Year Pattern

There's a related number that shows up again and again in Daniel and then gets picked up throughout Revelation: three and a half years, sometimes expressed as 1,260 days, sometimes as 42 months, sometimes as "a time, times, and half a time." Do the math with a 30-day month and they all land on the same length. Revelation uses this number for the two witnesses prophesying, for the woman (representing God's people) being protected in the wilderness, and for the outer court of the temple being trampled by outsiders.

This number has deep roots. It echoes the three-and-a-half-year drought that the prophet Elijah announced in his showdown with the prophets of Baal — a period where it looked like the enemy had the upper hand, but God was quietly still in control the entire time. Daniel's vision of the "seventy sevens" itself splits into halves built on this same 3.5-year rhythm. The exact reason Revelation switches between counting in days, months, and "times" for what seems to be the same length of time isn't fully settled — it's a real, open question, not a case where every detail lines up neatly. But the consistent pattern across Daniel and Revelation is this: it's a way of describing the entire stretch of time between Jesus' first coming and his return — a period that can feel like the world is under hostile control, while God's people are actually being protected and are still, quietly, on the winning side.

## Where You're Already Strong

Teens are often quick to notice when someone claims to have all the answers but is actually just guessing — you can smell overconfidence, whether it's a teacher who won't admit uncertainty or a preacher who claims to have cracked the exact end-times calendar. That skepticism is healthy. Daniel's vision itself invites humility: even Daniel, who received it directly, didn't fully understand everything he was shown. Being willing to say "some of this is genuinely unclear, and that's fine" isn't weak faith — it's honest faith, and you're often better at admitting that than adults are.

## Invitation to Grow

The flip side of that healthy skepticism is the temptation to let uncertainty about the details turn into doubt about the whole thing — to think, "if scholars can't agree on the dates, maybe none of it means anything." That's a trap worth naming. You can hold two things at once: real humility about contested details, and real confidence in the big picture God was communicating — that he had a plan running even during exile, that Jesus fulfills it, and that the waiting you're in right now isn't outside God's timetable either.

## Discussion Questions

- What's something in your life right now where the "not knowing how long" feels harder than the actual hard thing?
- Why might God have given Daniel specific numbers instead of just a vague promise that things would get better eventually?
- Does it bother you that scholars still debate the exact math of the seventy weeks? Why or why not?
- If the 3.5-year pattern describes the whole stretch between Jesus' first and second coming, what does that suggest about how you should read your own "waiting" seasons?

## Closing Prayer

God, thank you that you were counting down toward Jesus even during Israel's darkest years in exile. When we're in seasons of waiting and don't know how long they'll last, remind us that you're not absent or improvising — you have a plan, and you always have. Help us hold our questions honestly while trusting you fully. Amen.
