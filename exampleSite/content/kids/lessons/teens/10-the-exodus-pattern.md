## What are you actually trusting to hold you together?

Think about what you'd panic over losing. Your phone. Your friend group. Your grades. Your spot on the team. A certain follower count. These things aren't evil — but notice how much weight you've quietly put on them. Somewhere along the way, they became more than nice things you have. They became things you're depending on to feel like you're okay.

That's not a new problem. It's the exact problem God goes after, one system at a time, in the story of the Exodus.

## A nation built on fake security

By the time Moses shows up, Egypt is the most powerful civilization around — and it has convinced itself that its security comes from a whole stack of gods, each one supposedly in charge of a different piece of life: a god for the Nile's flooding, a god for the sun, a god for childbirth, gods represented in the sacred animals all over the land. Pharaoh sat at the top of it, treated as a god's own representative on earth, the guarantee that everything would keep working.

The Israelites, meanwhile, are enslaved — and after Moses first asks Pharaoh to let them go, things get worse, not better: Pharaoh piles more work on them, and the people lose all hope that anything will change. Then Moses performs a miraculous sign in front of Pharaoh, and Pharaoh still isn't impressed. Only once nobody — not Pharaoh, not even the Israelites themselves — expects anything more from Moses does God actually get to work.

What follows is nine plagues, then a tenth. And they are not random disasters. Look at the pattern: the first three plagues cause inconvenience, the next three cause real destruction, and the final three bring outright fear. Each one lands on something Egypt trusted to keep itself secure. The Nile — Egypt's entire water and food supply, tied to its own religious significance — turns to blood. Egypt's magician-priests can copy the first couple of plagues with their own secret arts, which is exactly the point: it looks, for a moment, like Egypt's power can match God's. But by the third plague they can't reproduce it anymore, and they say something remarkable out loud: "this is the finger of God." Even Pharaoh's own religious experts admit they're outmatched. By the sixth plague, they're so afflicted themselves they can't even show up anymore.

The plagues keep escalating — livestock essential to the economy and the military, boils that discredit the very purification rituals the priests relied on, hail devastating enough that Pharaoh, for one brief moment, admits "I have sinned" — and then hardens his own heart again the instant the pressure lifts. Locusts finish off whatever the hail spared, while Pharaoh's own officials beg him to just let Israel go. And then darkness falls over Egypt — a direct hit against the sun, worshiped as one of Egypt's most important gods — bringing the entire nation to a standstill.

It's worth pausing on that hardening of Pharaoh's heart, because it can sound unfair: does God force Pharaoh to resist and then punish him for it? Look closer at the actual text, though, and something more honest emerges. The same Hebrew words used for God hardening Pharaoh's heart are used, just as often, for Pharaoh hardening his own heart. God isn't forcing Pharaoh into evil he'd otherwise avoid — he's giving Pharaoh the strength to keep standing rather than collapsing the moment he sees God's power, which lets Pharaoh's real character show itself again and again, in his own choices. The hail moment proves it: Pharaoh admits guilt with his own mouth, then chooses, on his own, to go back on it the second the danger passes.

By the tenth plague, God targets Egypt's future itself — its firstborn, the heirs who would carry the nation forward — because Pharaoh had tried to destroy Israel's future first. Through the Passover, protection is offered to anyone willing to trust God, Israelite or not, and by the time Israel actually leaves, many Egyptians go with them.

Here's the big point underneath every plague: this was never really a fight between Moses and Pharaoh. It was God, methodically and specifically, showing that every single thing Egypt trusted for security — its river, its sun, its gods, its animals, its king — could not actually protect anyone. Only God could.

## Where you're already strong

You're probably better than most adults at spotting a system that's rigged to make you feel like you need it. You can smell it when an app is designed to make you anxious if you're not checking it, or when a friend group's "acceptance" always comes with strings attached, or when an influencer's whole brand depends on you feeling like you're missing out without their product. That instinct — refusing to fully hand your sense of security over to something that hasn't earned it — is precisely the instinct God was building in Israel through every plague. He wasn't just punishing Egypt. He was dismantling, piece by piece, every reason Israel might have had to think Egypt (or its gods) could give them what only God actually gives.

## The harder question

So what's your Egypt? What's the thing that, if it disappeared tomorrow — the phone, the group chat, the grades, the relationship, the image you've built online — would make you feel like your whole world had collapsed? That's worth naming honestly, because it's usually not something dramatic or obviously sinful. Egypt wasn't "evil" to the Egyptians. It was just everything. That's exactly what made it dangerous.

God didn't dismantle Egypt's gods because Israel asked him to. He did it because he wanted them to walk out of slavery actually free — not still secretly convinced that Pharaoh's world was the safe one and God was the risk. The same offer is in front of you: let God show you which of your securities are actually solid, and which ones only look that way because you've never had to test them.

## Talk about it

- What's one thing in your life you'd panic about losing — and does that reaction tell you anything about what you're really trusting?
- Why do you think Pharaoh kept hardening his heart even after seeing plague after plague fail to protect Egypt? Have you ever done something similar — kept going back to something you already knew wasn't working?
- Does it change how you read this story to know it's really about dismantling false securities rather than just "miracles happening to prove a point"?
- What would it look like, practically, for God to "plague" one of your own false securities this year?

## Prayer

God, show me honestly what I'm actually depending on to feel safe and okay — even the things that don't look dangerous. Thank you for being patient enough to prove, piece by piece, that you're the only secure thing there is. Give me the courage to let go of my own Egypt before you have to tear it down for me. Amen.
