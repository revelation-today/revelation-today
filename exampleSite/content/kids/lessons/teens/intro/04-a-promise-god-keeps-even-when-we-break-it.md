## The deal that's rigged against you

You've probably already met a version of this: the terms and conditions nobody reads, the group project where one person does the work and everyone gets the grade, the "friendship" that only works as long as you keep being useful. Some deals look fair on the surface but are quietly built so one side can't actually win.

Maybe you've wondered, honestly, whether your relationship with God is one of those deals. Follow all these rules, and maybe, if you're good enough, consistent enough, you'll be loved and accepted. It can start to feel like a contract stacked against you from the start — one bad week, one bad decision, and you've blown it.

Here's what's actually going on, and it's very different from that picture.

## How ancient covenants really worked

In the ancient world, belonging to a family meant everything — provision, protection, survival itself. If you weren't born into the right family, there were still ways in: marriage, adoption, or covenant. A covenant between a stronger nation and a weaker one worked like this: the stronger party (called "father" or "master") took on the weaker party (called "son" or "servant") as if they were family. The stronger nation had real authority over the weaker one — but in exchange, it was *obligated* to protect and fight for them, the same way you'd defend your own family. That's exactly what happened when the Gibeonites tricked Joshua into a covenant with them: once the covenant existed, Joshua had to defend them against attack, even though he didn't like them or trust how they'd gotten the deal in the first place.

These covenants had a real ceremony behind them, and it was intense: animals were cut in half and laid out in two rows, and the weaker party walked between the pieces. The message was blunt — this is what happens to you if you break your word.

## God rewrites the deal

In Genesis 15, God makes exactly this kind of covenant with Abraham — same animals, same two rows. But then something happens that breaks the entire pattern: it's not Abraham, the weaker party, who walks between the pieces. It's God. A flame representing God's own presence passes through, taking on the curse himself. So later, when Abraham breaks the covenant's terms — and he does, more than once — the weight of that curse doesn't fall on Abraham's family. It falls, ultimately, on God's own Son.

Let that sit for a second. In the normal version of this deal, the weak party takes all the risk. God flipped it. He took the risk onto himself, before Abraham had done anything to earn it.

The covenant God later made with the whole nation of Israel at Sinai followed the standard five-part format every ancient treaty used: an introduction identifying who God is ("I am the Lord your God"), a reminder of what he'd already done for them ("who brought you out of Egypt"), the actual terms ("have no other gods before me" and what follows from it), the consequences of keeping or breaking those terms, and witnesses called to hold it in place — in this case, heaven and earth themselves. This wasn't unusual as a format. What was unusual was the content.

Here's the detail worth sitting with: unlike a lot of real-world treaties designed to set the weaker side up to fail, God's terms to Israel were never impossible. His people could have kept them. They simply chose not to. And even then — even after Israel broke the terms again and again — God kept describing himself as their Father, a word that in Hebrew comes paired with *hesed*: covenant loyalty, a stubborn, committed love that doesn't quit just because the other side did something to deserve it quitting.

## Where you're already strong

Teens tend to have sharp radar for anything that smells like a rigged system — a rule that only benefits the person making it, a "chance" that was never really a chance. That instinct is worth trusting here, because it's the right question to bring to this story: was this deal actually fair? And the honest answer, once you look closely, is yes — more than fair. God didn't just offer achievable terms. He absorbed the cost of failure himself, before anyone had failed. Your suspicion of rigged systems is accurate elsewhere in life. It's worth applying the same sharp scrutiny here and noticing that this one holds up.

## The invitation

Here's where it gets personal: if the terms were genuinely fair and God even covered the cost of failure in advance, then the shame-spiral so many people feel — "I keep messing up, so God must be done with me" — isn't actually the conclusion this story supports. It's worth being honest about where you're tempted to think of your relationship with God as a performance you have to keep winning instead of a relationship someone already fought to keep intact. That doesn't mean your choices don't matter — they clearly did to Israel, and they do to you. It means the foundation under your standing with God was never "have you been good enough this week." It was something God secured before you were in a position to secure anything yourself.

## Talk about it

- Have you ever been in a "deal" — a friendship, a team, a job — that turned out to be rigged against you from the start? What did that feel like?
- Why do you think God chose to take on the risk himself in his covenant with Abraham instead of requiring Abraham to prove himself first?
- Does knowing that God's terms were "never impossible" change how you think about times you feel like you're failing him?
- What's the difference between a relationship built on performance and one built on loyalty (hesed)? Which one describes how you actually relate to God right now?

## Prayer

God, thank you that you didn't set up an impossible deal and wait to watch me fail it. Thank you for taking the risk and the cost onto yourself before I ever had the chance to earn anything. Help me stop treating my relationship with you like a performance review, and help me actually trust your loyalty even on the weeks I don't feel like I deserve it. Amen.
