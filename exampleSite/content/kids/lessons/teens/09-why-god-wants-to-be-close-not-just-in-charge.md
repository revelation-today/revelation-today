## The question underneath the question

If someone asked you "what does God actually want from you," what would you say? Obedience? Good behavior? To not mess up too badly before Sunday?

Most people, if they're honest, picture God mainly as management — someone in charge, checking whether you're following the rules. That picture isn't crazy; God really is in charge of everything. But it misses something the Bible cares about a lot more than you'd expect: God doesn't just want to rule you. He wants to be near you. Those are not the same thing, and the difference changes what your faith is actually about.

You can obey a boss you never see. You can't be close to one. The whole story of the Bible, from its first page to its last, is about which one God is actually going for.

## The story you didn't know was one story

It starts in Eden. God doesn't just create people and leave them a rulebook — he walks with them, meets with them, gives them work to do together. That's the original design: unbroken closeness, no performance required.

Then it breaks. Adam and Eve hide, and the garden is lost. But watch what God does next — because it's not "give up on closeness and settle for control." It's the opposite. God keeps pursuing nearness, generation after generation, even as people keep trying to build their own substitute security instead (Cain builds a city; his descendants build a civilization around it; eventually a whole crowd builds a tower at Babel trying to "make a name for themselves" without God). Every one of those projects is really the same move: trying to secure your own life without needing God close by.

God's response isn't to zap the project and walk away. He calls Abraham, and centuries later, when God rescues Israel from Egypt, he does something remarkable: he moves in with them. First in a tent — the tabernacle — and later in the temple in Jerusalem. That building is covered in imagery that deliberately echoes the garden of Eden, because it's making the same statement: since people couldn't have God's presence in the garden anymore, God would bring his presence to them instead, in a house built for exactly that.

There was a catch, though. Deep inside that temple was a room called the Holy of Holies — the place where God's presence actually dwelt. Only one person, the High Priest, could enter it, and only once a year. Closeness to God was real, but it was heavily restricted. The nearer you wanted to get, the more barriers stood in the way.

And even that arrangement didn't last. When Israel broke trust with God repeatedly, the prophet Ezekiel describes something devastating: God's presence actually leaves the temple. But here's the part worth sitting with — that same book doesn't end there. Ezekiel later describes God's presence returning, and adds a strange, beautiful image: a river flowing out from the altar, growing as it goes, making everything it touches come alive. That image isn't finished in Ezekiel. It shows up again at the very end of Revelation, where a river of life flows from God's throne through the New Jerusalem — the place where God finally lives with his people with nothing in between them anymore. We'll get to that city in much more depth later on. For now, just notice the pattern: God keeps promising to close the distance completely, and keeps making good on it.

The turning point arrives in Jesus. John's Gospel makes a striking claim: Jesus himself is the temple — God's presence, in a person, walking around. When he died, the curtain guarding the Holy of Holies tore in two. That wasn't decoration. It was an announcement: the barrier is gone. And Paul tells the Corinthian church that they — together, as a community — are now God's temple, a place where God's Spirit actually lives. Not a building anymore. People.

## Where you're already strong

Teens are usually sharper than adults about spotting the difference between someone who's technically "in charge of you" and someone who actually wants to know you. You can tell instantly when an adult is managing you versus present with you — when a coach is just running drills versus actually invested in you as a person, when a parent is enforcing rules versus actually paying attention. That instinct — refusing to settle for a relationship that's all authority and no presence — is exactly the instinct this whole story is built to satisfy. You're right to want more than rule-following from the people in your life. It turns out you're right to want more than that from God too, and the wild claim of Christianity is that you can actually have it.

## The harder question

Here's where it gets personal: if God has gone to this much trouble across the entire Bible to be close to you — not just to run your life from a distance — does your actual relationship with him feel like that? Or does it mostly feel like checking boxes: pray sometimes, don't do the obviously bad stuff, show up when you're supposed to?

It's worth being honest about which one you're actually living. A faith that's only about rules eventually feels hollow, because rules don't know you back. But a faith built on the idea that God tore down every barrier to be near you — that changes what prayer is for, what church is for, what "being good" is even about. It stops being performance for a distant boss and starts being time with someone who moved heaven and earth, literally, to not be far from you.

## Talk about it

- Before today, if you had to describe God in one sentence, would "in charge" or "close" have come first? Why do you think that is?
- Does it change anything to think of church, at its best, as "God's people" rather than "a building where God visits"?
- What's the difference between obeying someone and actually wanting to be near them? Have you experienced both with the same person?
- If Jesus tearing the curtain means the barrier between you and God is already gone, what's still making you feel distant from him?

## Prayer

God, thank you that you never settled for being far away and in charge — you kept moving toward us, all the way to the cross, just to be close. Forgive me for the times I've treated you like a rulebook instead of a relationship. Help me actually want your presence, not just your permission. Amen.
