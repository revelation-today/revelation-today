## When the Winning Side Turns Out to Be Fake

You've probably watched someone build a following online by promising something huge — a lifestyle, a shortcut to success, a cause worth dying for — and it looked unstoppable right up until it wasn't. Maybe you've seen it happen to a friend: they got pulled into a group, a relationship, an ideology that promised belonging and power, and it took a while before anyone noticed the wreckage it left behind. The scary part isn't that the deception was obviously evil from the start. It's that it looked like a win.

Revelation 6 opens with exactly that kind of scene, and it's more unsettling than it first appears.

## What's Actually Going On

John sees four horsemen released one at a time, and the first one is the strange one. He rides a white horse, holds a bow, wears a crown, and rides out "as a conqueror bent on conquest." White and crowns and conquest sound like good things in Revelation — Jesus rides a white horse later in the book, wears many crowns, and is called a conqueror too. So is this rider Jesus, or a picture of the gospel going out into the world before hard times come?

Look closer and the case falls apart. Bows in the Bible are also linked to deception — the very first bow mentioned in Scripture belongs to Jacob's scheme to steal a blessing through disguise. Crowns aren't unique to Jesus in this book; demonic forces wear them too. And "conquering" elsewhere in Revelation describes the beast overpowering God's people, not Jesus rescuing them. Most tellingly, Jesus's own teaching about the end times — in Mark, Matthew, and Luke — opens every single time with the same warning, in the same position: "Watch out that no one deceives you." Only after that warning does he mention wars, then famine. That's the exact order of the first three horsemen. The first rider isn't Jesus. He's the imitation of Jesus — dressed to look like victory, sent out first specifically to deceive.

The second horseman brings war. The third is stranger: he holds a pair of scales, and a voice cries out pricing — a day's wages for a small ration of wheat, three times that for barley, while the oil and wine are protected from damage. That's not a description of a general famine. Luxury goods stay untouched and even climb in value, while the price of survival food becomes crushing for the poor. It's an economy rigged to protect the comfortable while ordinary people can't afford to eat. That voice isn't God ordering the suffering — it's a cry of protest against the injustice. The fourth horseman, Death, simply sums up where all of this ends.

Put together: a fake gospel that promises victory, backed by force against anyone who won't buy in, propped up by an economy that punishes the poor to protect the rich — and it all collects into death. That's not a prophecy about some distant future event only. It's a description of how deception, power, and greed have always worked together, then and now.

Then comes the sixth seal: the sky goes dark, the world shakes, and everyone — kings, generals, the rich, everyone — hides in caves begging the mountains to fall on them and hide them from "the wrath of the Lamb." Notice what's actually being shown here: not God swinging wildly in anger, but people terrified because they finally see what they've built and who they've hurt. The Greek word for wrath here describes something that builds slowly over a long time, not a short fuse. It's the same instinct Adam and Eve had — hiding from God after they knew they'd done wrong. The passage never actually shows God's reaction. It shows human guilt catching up with itself.

## Where You're Already Strong

You've probably got a sharper radar than most adults give you credit for when it comes to spotting a fake. You can usually tell when an influencer's confidence is a performance, when a "movement" someone's hyping is actually just a scheme, when the person promising easy answers is selling something. That instinct — to ask "wait, what's actually happening here, underneath the packaging?" — is precisely the skill this passage is asking you to practice on a bigger scale.

## Invitation to Grow

Here's the harder question this passage puts to you: the wrath of God can feel like a difficult, even embarrassing thing to defend to your friends. But that discomfort is partly because you've grown up in one of the most protected, comfortable corners of human history. Most people who have ever lived have known war, famine, slavery, or persecution up close. When you hear "God's wrath," try picturing it less like a temper and more like this: a good parent who has watched, for a long time, someone hurting the people they love — and who eventually acts, not out of a short fuse, but because real harm to real victims eventually has to be answered. Growing in this means learning to see wrath as part of God's justice for the exploited, not a scary add-on to his character.

## Discussion Questions

- Have you ever seen something online or in a friend group that looked like a win at first — popularity, confidence, a cause — but turned out to be manipulative once you looked closer? What tipped you off?
- Why do you think Revelation shows the counterfeit-Jesus rider first, before showing the destruction he leads to? What does that say about how deception usually works?
- Does thinking about wrath as "slow-building justice" instead of "sudden anger" change how you feel about it? Why or why not?
- Who in the world right now is in the position of the third horseman's victims — people priced out of what they need while luxury stays protected? What would it look like for you to care about that now?

## Closing Prayer

God, give us eyes to see through what only looks like victory, and the patience to wait for your justice instead of demanding it work on our timeline. Thank you that you see the suffering of people the world ignores. Keep us from hiding from you in guilt, and instead draw us honestly into your presence. Amen.
