## Where Do You Actually Belong?

Think about the last time you walked into a room and immediately tried to figure out if you belonged there. Maybe it was a new school, a youth group retreat where you didn't know anyone, or a family gathering on your mom's side where you feel like the outsider. Humans are wired to ask: "Am I actually part of this, or am I just tolerated here?"

Now here's a strange question that the book of Revelation forces you to ask: are you part of Israel?

Your first instinct is probably "no, obviously not — I'm not Jewish." Fair. But Revelation keeps using the language of Israel — tribes, temple, priests, the twelve — to describe the church, meaning you, if you follow Jesus. That's not a metaphor tossed off casually. It's the backbone of how the whole book thinks about who God's people are. So it's worth actually understanding instead of skimming past.

## What's Actually Going On

Revelation was written into a real, awkward situation. Jews were a protected, respected religious group in the Roman Empire — they'd been around forever, and Rome basically let them do their thing as long as they stayed loyal to the emperor. Christians got no such deal. They wouldn't worship the emperor, and worse, from Rome's perspective, they kept telling people to follow a Jewish man named Jesus instead. That put Jews under suspicion by association, which is part of why some Jewish communities pushed back hard against Christians — turning them in to Roman authorities was a way of saying, "Don't lump us in with them."

That tension shows up directly in Revelation. In two of the letters to the churches, local Jewish communities are described as attacking Christians for witnessing about Jesus — language sharp enough that John calls them "a synagogue of Satan." Later, in chapter 11, the city where God's two witnesses are killed gets described — without even using the name "Jerusalem" — as spiritually equivalent to Sodom and Egypt, because that's where Jesus, their Lord, was killed.

That's some genuinely harsh language. So here's the part that matters for you: none of it means Revelation is arguing that the church replaces Israel, or that Jewish people are erased from God's story. What the book is actually doing throughout is asking one question of everyone — Jew or Gentile — namely, what's your relationship to Jesus? That's the line that matters, not ethnicity.

And this is where it gets personal. Revelation pictures the twenty-four elders around God's throne as representing the twelve tribes of Israel and the twelve apostles together — not one replacing the other, side by side. In the New Jerusalem, the names of the twelve tribes and the twelve apostles are engraved right next to each other, permanently. Heaven itself isn't shown as some placeless, generic "spiritual realm" — it's pictured as a city, the New Jerusalem, tied to real history and a real people.

The point: the church is part of Israel. Not a replacement. Not a separate plan B. If you belong to Jesus, you've been grafted into a story that started with Abraham, and you didn't earn your way in — you were included. That's why Revelation can't just write off ethnic Israel, even when some Jews in that specific moment were hostile to the church. You don't cut off the root you grew out of.

## Where You're Already Strong

Teens have an unusually sharp radar for fake belonging — you can smell it when a school, a club, or even a church is putting on a show of inclusion that doesn't actually include anyone. You notice when people say "everyone's welcome here" but really mean "everyone who already fits our mold." That instinct is worth protecting, because it's basically the same instinct Revelation is training: real belonging isn't about surface categories, it's about an actual relationship. You already know the difference between being tolerated and being claimed as family. Trust that radar. It's accurate.

## Invitation to Grow

Here's the harder question this raises: if belonging to God's people was never about the "right" ethnicity, background, or pedigree, then it was never about being the right kind of person at your school either. A lot of you spend enormous energy trying to earn a seat at some table — the popular group, the "spiritual enough" kids at church, the ones who seem to have it figured out. Revelation's picture of Israel and the church says something different: you don't get grafted in by performing well enough. You get grafted in because Jesus claimed you. That should loosen the grip that peer approval has on you. You don't need to audition for belonging you were already given.

## Discussion Questions

- Have you ever felt like you were "included" somewhere but not really wanted? What was the difference between that and real belonging?
- Why do you think it matters that Revelation puts the twelve tribes and the twelve apostles side by side instead of one replacing the other?
- Where in your life are you currently trying to earn belonging that you should instead just be accepting as a gift?
- Is there a group — at school, online, even at church — where you've decided someone doesn't "count" the way you do? What would it look like to see them the way Revelation frames Jew and Gentile together?

## Closing Prayer

God, thank you that we don't have to earn our way into your family — you grafted us in through Jesus. Help us stop performing for belonging we already have, and help us see the people around us the way you see them. Give us the confidence to rest in being truly yours. Amen.
