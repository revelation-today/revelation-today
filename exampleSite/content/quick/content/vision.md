Revelation opens by telling us exactly what kind of book it is: an urgent message from Jesus, sent through John, meant to be obeyed now - not a puzzle to be filed away for a future generation to decode. "The time is near" is said twice, at the very start and the very end of the book. That framing matters for everything that follows.

Then comes the vision itself: John, exiled on Patmos for refusing to bow to anyone but Jesus, sees the risen Christ standing among seven golden lampstands, holding seven stars, with eyes like fire and a voice like rushing water. John falls down as if dead - and Jesus' first words are "Don't be afraid." He is the one who died and is alive forever, holding the keys of death itself.

The lampstands and stars are then explained directly: they are the seven churches and their "angels" (messengers) - meaning this vision is really about the church's own identity and calling. The image is simple and repeats a single idea twice: the church is meant to shine light into a dark world, the way a lampstand does - but only by staying lit from a source outside itself, the way a lampstand needs oil and stars only shine because they're held in place. Dependence, not self-sufficiency, is the whole point.

Everything Jesus says to the seven churches in the next two chapters will measure them against this one vision: are they actually being this kind of light, in this kind of dependence?
