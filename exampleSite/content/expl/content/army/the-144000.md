What is this mysterious number in Revelation all about? Who are the 144,000, and what is the larger context around them?

## The sealing

<a name="9366"></a>
After the sixth seal, there's an interlude that already connects forward to the trumpets — even before the seventh seal itself has happened.

An angel is called to seal the servants of God before the trumpets begin. {{% bible val="This reminds of the sealing of the righteous in the prophet Ezekiel, where the angel was to mark all the people who mourned over the unrighteousness that had taken place. After they were all sealed, another angel came and struck down those without a seal, starting at the temple of God." link="ezk:9" lang="en" %}}

This scene invites an application beyond protection: a call not to compromise with what God sees as evil. That protection will only become active during the {{% int_link val="fifth trumpet" link="/expl/content/trumpets/the-trumpets-in-revelation" %}}, when people become so desperate that they want to die.

## The army of God

<a name="2cd4"></a>
Next, the 144,000 are introduced, described as 12,000 from each of twelve tribes — though the list is not the usual one: it drops Dan, lists Levi (normally excluded from land-based tribal lists), and uses "Joseph" alongside a separately named "Manasseh" instead of naming Ephraim. There are actually 13 tribes to draw from in total, since the list counts not only the 12 sons of Jacob but also the two sons of Joseph, whom Jacob adopted, in place of Joseph himself — which is why Old Testament tribal lists vary from one another.

What's especially interesting is which tribe leads them: Judah, the tribe Jesus comes from. On one widely held reading (including Beale's, followed here), the church is now understood as part of Israel, so this description of Israel can be read as describing the church too — though other interpreters (see {{% int_link val="the dispensationalist alternative" link="/expl/topics/others/dispensionalism-and-its-critic#40c6" %}}) take the 144,000 as referring specifically to ethnic/believing Israel, distinct from the innumerable multitude described later in the chapter.

The way the counting is presented is also worth noticing — it echoes the {{% bible val="counting of the men ready for military service during the Exodus" link="num:1,2-3" lang="en" %}}. Here, the same people who are sealed are presented as an army.

But there's a twist here, just as in {{% int_link val="chapter 5" link="/expl/content/worship/the-book-with-the-seven-seals" %}}, where the {{% bible val="lion of Judah is identified as a lamb being slaughtered" link="rev:5,5-6" lang="en" %}}. The same pattern plays out again: {{% bible val="the 144,000 counted as soldiers is what John hears" link="rev:7,4" lang="en" %}}, but {{% bible val="what John sees is an uncountable number from all nations who worship God" link="rev:7,9" lang="en" %}}.

There's also a connection to the {{% bible val="fifth seal" link="rev:6,9-11" lang="en" %}}, where the souls under the altar cry out and receive white robes, just like these people here. They appear to be the same group: they come out of the {{% int_link val="Great Tribulation" link="/expl/content/army/the-end-time-and-the-great-tribulation" %}} and {{% bible val="are now comforted by God" link="rev:7,15-17" lang="en" %}}, living out the tension of the "already and the not yet." We'll look at this more closely in chapter 14, when the 144,000 appear again.
