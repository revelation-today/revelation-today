Chapter 14 of the book of Revelation is one of the most puzzling chapters in the Bible — at first glance it can feel disconnected from everything around it. Read against what comes before and after, though, it turns out to be a hinge: a chapter that answers the previous one and sets up the one to come. It deserves a closer look.

## The 144,000

<a name="6c9d"></a>
The chapter opens by picking up two earlier threads: the {{% int_link val="Lamb standing from chapter 5" link="/expl/content/worship/the-book-with-the-seven-seals" %}}, found worthy to receive the scroll, and {{% int_link val="chapter 7, where the 144,000 are mentioned" link="/expl/content/army/the-144000" %}}. Now those same 144,000 are gathered before the throne — the mention of Mount Zion in verse 1 may well be pointing to the heavenly Jerusalem.

The 144,000 carry the name of the Lamb and of God on their foreheads, marking their identity and whose they are. This is a different angle on the same sealing described back in chapter 7. They sing a new song, likely in the same key as {{% bible val="chapter 5" link="rev:5,9-10" lang="en" %}}, where the Lamb's worship is likewise carried outward by angels to the whole of creation.

This scene comes right on the heels of the dragon and the two beasts at the height of their power: conquering the saints, forcing worship, deceiving the whole earth, marking it as their own. Given all that pressure, it would be easy to expect God's people to be crushed. Instead they are still standing, with a song of praise and victory on their lips — proof that, for all his bluster, the devil is the real loser in the story so far:

- {{% bible val="He fails to destroy the child" link="rev:12,1-6" lang="en" %}}.
- {{% bible val="He loses his position in heaven" link="rev:12,7-12" lang="en" %}}.
- {{% bible val="He fails to destroy the woman" link="rev:12,13-17" lang="en" %}}.
- {{% bible val="His persecution and suppression through the two beasts" link="rev:13" lang="en" %}} turns into a {{% int_link val="disaster" link="/expl/content/beasts/666-the-number-of-the-beast" %}} for him.
- {{% int_link val="And his victory over the saints backfires" link="/expl/content/witnesses/the-two-witnesses" %}}.

Not only do the 144,000 outlast the devil's assault — they fulfill the {{% int_link val="destiny of the little scroll" link="/expl/content/scroll/the-little-scroll" %}}. What is their secret? The text spells it out directly:

"{{% bible val="These are those who did not defile themselves with women, for they remained virgins. They follow the Lamb wherever he goes. They were purchased from among mankind and offered as first fruits to God and the Lamb. No lie was found in their mouths; they are blameless." link="rev:14,4-5" lang="en" %}}"

Each phrase in that description carries weight. They are called virgins — which sounds strange at first, but doesn't refer to marital status; it points to their fidelity to Christ, to whom nothing is more precious. They follow the Lamb, meaning they don't compromise: their loyalty and trust are total. They "have been purchased," which means they know they were bought at a high price. And no lie was found in their mouths, because they were faithful witnesses just like Jesus, {{% bible val="who had no deceit in his mouth" link="1pe:2,22" lang="en" %}}. In short, they lived out their witness in the footsteps of Jesus without compromise, enduring hardship and even martyrdom.

## The first three angels

<a name="6911"></a>
The first three angels reflect the same tension the chapter has been building: the 144,000 stand faithful, resisting the beast and testifying to the nations who come to Christ, while others give in to the beast's stress and deception.

The first angel comes and proclaims the eternal gospel:

"{{% bible val="he had the eternal gospel to proclaim to those who live on the earth — to every nation, tribe, language and people. He said in a loud voice, ‘Fear God and give him glory, because the hour of his judgment has come. Worship him who made the heavens, the earth, the sea and the springs of water.’" link="rev:14,6-7" lang="en" %}}"

"Eternal gospel" is a striking term, and it traces back to Psalm 96: "[Proclaim His salvation from day to day](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/psalms/96-2.htm)." The Hebrew verb behind "proclaim" there is "[basar](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/1319.htm)" — announce good news — and the Greek Old Testament renders it with the euangelizō word-group, the verb form of evangelion, gospel. And the phrase "from day to day" can carry the sense of "eternally," similar to the idiomatic "day after day" of {{% bible val="Proverbs" link="pro:8,30" lang="en" %}}.

Psalm 96 goes on to proclaim this good news to all {{% bible val="nations and peoples" link="psa:96,3" lang="en" %}}, and the content of that {{% bible val="message runs through the rest of this short psalm" link="psa:96,7-10" lang="en" %}} as well. It's worth noting that the psalm opens with "Sing to the Lord a new song" — the very description used earlier for the 144,000. Its argument throughout is that God is king, and all nations are invited to worship him.

The second angel announces the fall of Babylon, while the third brings a stern warning of judgment for anyone who gives in to the beast. Together they mirror the {{% bible val="prophesying in sackcloth" link="rev:11,3" lang="en" %}} and the {{% bible val="warning judgments" link="rev:11,5" lang="en" %}} already delivered by the two witnesses.

That raises the obvious question: will the nations actually listen? We get a strong hint from the story of the witnesses: {{% int_link val="many do turn to God" link="/expl/content/witnesses/the-two-witnesses" %}}.

## The two harvests

<a name="833c"></a>
After the three angels finish speaking, four more figures appear: three more angels and one like a son of man, each tied to a harvest — one of grain, one of grapes. What do these two harvests mean? Are both of them pictures of judgment?

Both are drawn from Joel: "{{% bible val="Swing the sickle, for the (grain) harvest is ripe. Come, trample the grapes, for the wine press is full and the vats overflow — so great is their wickedness!" link="jol:3,13" lang="en" %}}"

In Joel, both images point to judgment, especially the wine press — which Revelation names outright as {{% bible val="the wine press of the wrath of God" link="rev:14,19" lang="en" %}}, an echo of {{% bible val="the day of vengeance in Isaiah" link="isa:63,1-6" lang="en" %}}. It also reflects the {{% bible val="fall of Babylon announced just a few verses earlier" link="rev:14,8" lang="en" %}}. Because both harvest images in Revelation are drawn from that same single verse in Joel, many readers — including Beale, cited above — take the grain harvest the same way as the wine press: as another picture of judgment, in step with Jesus' own end-of-age harvest language in Matthew 13, where the angels gather both wheat and weeds "at the end of the age."

There is, even so, a case for reading the grain harvest differently, though it should be held as a proposal rather than a settled conclusion. Three details set it apart. First, the 144,000 were introduced earlier in this chapter as the first fruits, and first fruits were {{% bible val="normally taken from grain" link="lev:23,9-14" lang="en" %}} (alongside a lamb offering) and served as a {{% bible val="pledge of the full harvest still to come" link="1co:15,20-23" lang="en" %}} — so if the grain harvested here is meant to recall the 144,000, redeemed from every nation (on the church-as-part-of-Israel reading discussed at {{% int_link val="the church and Israel" link="/expl/topics/others/dispensionalism-and-its-critic#40c6" %}}), they would stand as a first fruit of all the nations. A first fruit could technically also {{% bible val="come from grapes" link="exo:22,29" lang="en" %}}, but that would make for a strange reading here — are the martyrs really meant to be the first fruit of God's vengeance? Second, while the wine press involves both the gathering and the crushing of the grapes, the grain harvest as narrated stops at the reaping; what comes after grain is gathered — {{% bible val="threshing" link="jer:51,33" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="burning the chaff" link="mat:3,12" lang="en" %}}, or being {{% bible val="blown away" link="psa:1,4" lang="en" %}} — is simply not mentioned, and elsewhere in Scripture {{% bible val="reaping can also picture the ingathering of people, as when Jesus says the fields are ripe for harvest" link="jhn:4,35" lang="en" %}}. Third, the grain harvest is carried out by "one like the Son of Man seated on a cloud wearing a crown" — language that reaches back to {{% bible val="Daniel, where he receives the eternal kingdom with all the nations worshiping him" link="dan:7,13-14" lang="en" %}}.

None of this is decisive on its own. Nothing in the grain-harvest scene itself explicitly ties the grain back to the 144,000 of verse 4, and royal, Daniel-shaped imagery doesn't automatically mean a positive outcome elsewhere in Revelation: the glorified Christ, described in similarly kingly terms, is the one who later {{% bible val="treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God Almighty" link="rev:19,15" lang="en" %}} himself. So the two harvests may lay out two options for the nations — judgment or salvation — but readers should know this is one reading among others; many careful interpreters take both harvests as judgment, differing only in whose. The story of the two witnesses ended with the majority giving God the glory — but here, for a moment, the outcome is left open.

## The song of Moses

<a name="5102"></a>
The next scene draws us into a new exodus, signaled by the sea of glass — an echo of the parting of the Red Sea — and by the song of Moses and the song of the Lamb sung together:

"[Great and marvelous are your deeds, Lord God Almighty. Just and true are your ways, King of the nations. Who will not fear you, Lord, and bring glory to your name? For you alone are holy. All nations will come and worship before you, for your righteous acts have been revealed.](https://www.bibleserver.com/NIV/Revelation15%3A3-4)"

{{% bible val="The original song of Moses describes the praise that followed the miracle at the Red Sea" link="exo:15,1-19" lang="en" %}}, but the words sung here are quite different from what Moses actually said — so the label "song of Moses" is doing more than just naming a source; it's drawing a deliberate parallel.

That parallel runs deeper than Exodus itself. The Exodus story is taken up again in {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:11,15-16" lang="en" %}} and reworked as {{% bible val="a new Exodus" link="isa:12" lang="en" %}} across two songs that also {{% bible val="echo" link="isa:12,4" lang="en" %}} {{% bible val="Psalm 105" link="psa:105,1" lang="en" %}}, which likewise recalls the Exodus. The thread connecting all of these is that the nations, too, are affected by God's action at the Red Sea.

Look closer still at the line "Who will not fear you, Lord, and bring glory to your name? ... King of the nations," and it links to {{% bible val="Jeremiah, where the same question is asked of the King of the nations" link="jer:10,7" lang="en" %}} as well as Psalms {{% bible val="86" link="psa:86,8-10" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="98" link="psa:98,1-2" lang="en" %}}. Taken together, these threads weave the content of the song of Moses and the Lamb into a single argument:

- Who is like you, O Lord, among the gods?
- He shows this by working miracles as acts of deliverance.
- In doing so, he demonstrates that he is the one true God.
- And this brings all nations to worship him.

The song, in other words, is a proclamation that all nations will come to God and worship him — accomplished through his judgment of their enemies. Its celebration isn't only about the martyrs' own vindication; it's equally about the praise that God's actions have drawn out of the nations.

That sets up what comes next: with the bowls of wrath, we watch God deal with the nations that refuse to worship him and instead stay loyal to the dragon and the beasts.
