Once we know who the harlot is, a few open questions remain, and the Bible answers them too: What is her behavior, her "tactic"? How should we respond? And what ultimately causes her to fall?

## The character of the harlot

<a name="b96c"></a>
The earlier articles on the harlot showed that she reflects the economic system, and drew the parallel to Jezebel, where the same connection between idolatry and unrighteousness showed up.

The harlot sits on the {{% bible val="second beast of chapter 13" link="rev:13" lang="en" %}}, which represents military and political power. She covers the cruelty of the beast's brute force with the allure of wealth, and she makes rulers drunk so they'll do her bidding. Like the second beast, she works through perception — she controls money, and with it, she can both build up and destroy.

The list of goods and the mourning song over her both draw on the earlier song about Tyre: the king of {{% bible val="Tyre is described with the same kind of stones" link="ezk:28,13" lang="en" %}} as the {{% bible val="New Jerusalem" link="rev:21,19-20" lang="en" %}}, and the {{% bible val="stones in Tyre are linked there to righteousness" link="ezk:28,12-18" lang="en" %}}.

Looking at the {{% bible val="list of her goods" link="rev:18,12-13" lang="en" %}}, notice that people — souls — are listed right alongside every other commodity. It's worth pausing on that: what does it mean for a company to treat its employees as assets in the same sense?

{{% bible val="She sees herself as untouchable" link="rev:18,7" lang="en" %}} and beyond destruction — which may bring to mind government bailouts of banks.

None of this makes economic growth, or the research behind it, inherently bad. Trade and the exchange of goods and money are necessary. The question is what spirit lies behind it, and how we respond to it.

Do we mourn along with everyone else {{% bible val="when the economy crumbles" link="rev:18,10" lang="en" %}} and our standard of living drops? {{% bible val="When jobs move to other countries" link="rev:18,17" lang="en" %}}? Do we support military interventions to protect our business interests? If the answer is yes, we're standing inside Babylon — {{% bible val="and we need to leave" link="rev:18,4" lang="en" %}}.

## The mystery of her fall

<a name="0a44"></a>
The end of Babylon is genuinely fascinating. She {{% bible val="sits on the beast" link="rev:17,3" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="controls the kings" link="rev:17,2" lang="en" %}}, yet {{% bible val="they are the ones who destroy her" link="rev:17,16" lang="en" %}} — and then {{% bible val="weep over her afterward" link="rev:18,9" lang="en" %}}. Why would they turn on their own source of power?

{{% bible val="The simplest answer is that God put it into their hearts" link="rev:17,17" lang="en" %}}, but there's more going on underneath that:

- People change their loyalties — today's friend is tomorrow's enemy — but God does not change. Babylon relied on the support of the rulers, and that support was always going to prove deceptive, because in the end everyone in this world looks out for their own advantage first.
- This fulfills a pattern already set in {{% bible val="Daniel" link="dan:7,21-22" lang="en" %}}, where evil defeats the saints only for God to hand them the victory anyway — a theme that recurs throughout Revelation, in the witnesses and in the millennial kingdom alike. Right at the moment evil looks like it's winning, and shows its true face, God turns the tables.
- The beast and the kings attack the saints, but Jesus defeats them; he intervenes precisely when the Church comes under attack.
- The harlot looks like the bride from the outside — that resemblance is deliberate, part of the same deception carried out by the second beast with its two horns like a lamb. So this may be a case of Satan falling for his own deception: the kings want to destroy the saints, {{% bible val="for obvious reasons" link="rev:11,6-10" lang="en" %}}, but mistake the harlot for the saints and destroy her instead. How could such confusion happen? Consider that [major parts of Western society, and its successful economy worldwide, are built on Christian foundations and values](https://www.pdfdrive.com/the-book-that-made-your-world-how-the-bible-created-the-soul-of-western-civilization-e200370906.html) — the lines between the two can blur more than we'd expect.

## The judgment

<a name="3d3b"></a>
Her judgment matches her actions point for point:

- She considered herself untouchable, so she falls within a single hour — about as short a time as the text can picture.
- She will be judged {{% bible val="with the same measure" link="rev:18,6" lang="en" %}} that {{% bible val="she inflicted on others" link="rev:18,22-24" lang="en" %}} ([the "double measure" is simply another way of saying "equivalent"](https://meredithkline.com/klines-works/articles-and-essays/double-trouble/)).
- She will be consumed by fire — the same judgment prescribed when a priest's daughter committed adultery — a strong warning to stay away from Babylon and resist its pull.
