Revelation 8–9 read like modern disaster headlines, but John isn't describing anything new — he's replaying {{% int_link val="the plagues in Egypt" link="/expl/bible/exodus/the-plagues-in-egypt" %}} for a new empire and a new Pharaoh.

The trumpets are the second of the book's three seven-part cycles (seals, trumpets, bowls). Like the seals and bowls, they aren't a strict chronological sequence tacked onto the seals — they recap and intensify the same events from a different angle, which is why the same closing signs (thunder, lightning, earthquake) and the same paired judgments (trumpet six and bowl six, trumpet seven and bowl seven) keep echoing each other. This recapitulation reading is the same framework laid out more fully in {{% int_link val="premillennialism, postmillennialism, and amillennialism compared" link="/expl/content/1000y/pre-post-and-amillennialism" %}}, in contrast to readings that treat the seals, trumpets, and bowls as one strict, sequential timetable. Their opening scene sets the tone for everything that follows: it starts not with an angel, but with a prayer meeting.

## The origin of the trumpets

<a name="5cb5"></a>
{{% bible val="“When he opened the seventh seal, there was silence in heaven for about half an hour. 2 And I saw the seven angels who stand before God, and seven trumpets were given to them. 3 Another angel, who had a golden censer, came and stood at the altar. He was given much incense to offer, with the prayers of all God’s people, on the golden altar in front of the throne. 4 The smoke of the incense, together with the prayers of God’s people, went up before God from the angel’s hand. 5 Then the angel took the censer, filled it with fire from the altar, and hurled it on the earth; and there came peals of thunder, rumblings, flashes of lightning and an earthquake. 6 Then the seven angels who had the seven trumpets prepared to sound them.”" link="rev:8,1-6" lang="en" %}}

Every cycle in the book closes the same way: thunder, lightning, and an earthquake, each round louder than the last. So verse 5 is doing two jobs at once — it's the end of the seals and the start of the trumpets in the same breath.

Look closely at the order of events. In verse 2, the angels already hold their trumpets, but nothing happens yet — they're waiting for a signal. That signal, in verses 3–5, is prayer. If you're wondering what the incense represents: {{% bible val="It is the prayer of the saints" link="rev:5,8" lang="en" %}}. Notice, too, who carries it to the altar: a strong angel — because the prayers themselves, offered by ordinary, often stumbling people, were never going to be strong enough on their own to set this in motion.

Sit with that for a moment: it's our prayers that set the trumpets blowing.

That leaves two hard questions the text doesn't resolve for you. First, if the prayers of the saints carry enough weight to trigger something on this scale — weight the text puts alongside the cross and God's own justice in the arrival of his kingdom — that should change how seriously you take your own praying. Second, and harder to sit with: do you actually want your prayers answered the way they're answered here?

## The first four trumpets

<a name="8718"></a>
The {{% int_link val="plagues in Egypt" link="/expl/bible/exodus/the-plagues-in-egypt" %}} exposed Pharaoh and his gods as frauds — powerless to protect anyone, with nothing real to offer. The trumpets do the same work on us. They strip away, one layer at a time, whatever we quietly trust instead of God, echoing {{% bible val="the letter to the Hebrews" link="heb:12,26-28" lang="en" %}}: everything that can be shaken will be shaken, so that what can't be shaken remains. The people living through it are meant to testify, by how they respond, that God's kingdom is a security worth having.

{{% bible val="The first trumpet" link="rev:8,7" lang="en" %}} burns up a third of the earth and a third of the trees, and strips every blade of green grass — not just a third — gutting the food supply, echoing {{% bible val="the plague of hail" link="exo:9,22-25" lang="en" %}}, which likewise struck down everything growing in the field.

{{% bible val="The second trumpet" link="rev:8,8-9" lang="en" %}} kills a third of sea life and wrecks a third of the ships: food and trade both gone. The detail about ships is a pointed jab at Babylon, {{% bible val="which grew rich through shipping" link="rev:18,19" lang="en" %}}. Even the trumpet's own image of {{% bible val="a mountain, ablaze, hurled into the sea" link="rev:8,8" lang="en" %}} points the same direction, since biblical imagery uses a mountain to picture a kingdom — John is shown {{% bible val="the New Jerusalem from a great, high mountain" link="rev:21,10" lang="en" %}}, and Babylon itself is called {{% bible val="a destroying mountain" link="jer:51,25" lang="en" %}}. Revelation later pictures Babylon's fall the same way but sunk rather than burned: {{% bible val="a great millstone hurled into the sea, never to be found again" link="rev:18,21" lang="en" %}}, echoing how {{% bible val="Jeremiah's scroll against Babylon was tied to a stone and sunk in the Euphrates" link="jer:51,63-64" lang="en" %}}. The plague itself recalls the {{% bible val="first plague of Egypt" link="exo:7,21" lang="en" %}}, water turned to blood.

{{% bible val="The third trumpet" link="rev:8,10-11" lang="en" %}} poisons a third of the earth's fresh water, again reaching back to {{% bible val="that same first Egyptian plague" link="exo:7,21" lang="en" %}}.

{{% bible val="The fourth trumpet" link="rev:8,12-13" lang="en" %}} darkens a third of the sun, moon, and stars — orientation, day and night themselves. It echoes the {{% bible val="ninth plague in Egypt" link="exo:10,22-23" lang="en" %}}, a darkness so total it drew a hard, visible line between Israel and Egypt.

Why a third — with grass the one exception, wiped out entirely — and never everything else? Because these trumpets fall on God's creation — including, by the sixth trumpet, humanity itself — a creation already under sin's power. The fraction matches one already used earlier in the book: {{% int_link val="a third of the stars — often read as the angels who fell with Satan" link="/expl/content/jesus/a-different-christmas-story" %}}, so a third of creation now falls with it.

## The fifth and sixth trumpet

<a name="403f"></a>
The next two trumpets change targets completely. Where the first four stripped away what people rely on to live, these two come after life itself — and this time the source is explicitly demonic.

{{% bible val="The fifth trumpet" link="rev:9,1-12" lang="en" %}} opens with a star that has already fallen, opening the abyss. The verb is the same one used elsewhere for {{% bible val="Satan falling from heaven" link="luk:10,18" lang="en" %}}, for {{% bible val="demons" link="rev:12,4" lang="en" %}}, and for {{% bible val="Satan and his demons together" link="rev:12,9-10" lang="en" %}} — which points toward a demonic figure rather than an angel simply sent down on an errand. (The office itself doesn't settle it: later in the book, {{% bible val="an angel comes down from heaven holding the key to the abyss" link="rev:20,1-3" lang="en" %}} in order to bind Satan, and no one doubts he's a good angel. It's the "fallen" language, not the key, that points this way.) Whoever it is, the locusts he releases answer to a king, and Revelation names him: {{% bible val="the angel of the abyss, Abaddon in Hebrew, Apollyon — 'Destroyer' — in Greek" link="rev:9,11" lang="en" %}}. "Apollyon" sits close enough to "Apollo" — the god the emperor Domitian modeled himself on, whose symbol included the locust — that the name likely lands as a jab at Rome's throne as much as at hell's.

That figure releases locusts that torment people so badly they {{% bible val="long to die and can't" link="rev:9,6" lang="en" %}} — and yet {{% bible val="even the locusts stay under God's command" link="rev:9,5" lang="en" %}}, the same way {{% bible val="Satan needed permission before he could touch Job" link="job:2,4-6" lang="en" %}}. {{% bible val="God's own people are sealed and off-limits to the locusts entirely" link="rev:9,4" lang="en" %}}. This plague has an Egyptian ancestor too: it's the one that finally {{% bible val="broke Pharaoh into admitting he had sinned" link="exo:10,13-17" lang="en" %}} and had {{% bible val="his own officials pleading with him to submit to God" link="exo:10,7" lang="en" %}}.

{{% bible val="The sixth trumpet" link="rev:9,13-21" lang="en" %}} escalates again, aimed squarely at human life. Four {{% bible val="angels are released at the Euphrates" link="rev:9,14" lang="en" %}} — [the river where Babylon stood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylon) — for this appointed moment, closely mirroring {{% bible val="the sixth bowl" link="rev:16,12-14" lang="en" %}}, which gathers the nations for {{% int_link val="Armageddon" link="/expl/content/bowls/the-key-to-armageddon" %}}. The army numbers 200 million — 2×10,000×10,000. In everyday Greek, "myriad" (10,000) was the largest single number word in common use; stacking myriads on myriads, as this verse does, was exactly how you reached for a number so large it was effectively uncountable — which is the point being pictured.

And after all of that, {{% bible val="the people still did not repent" link="rev:9,20-21" lang="en" %}}. That's the point the chapter won't let you miss: judgment was never the only goal of the plagues — they were meant to give people good reason to turn to God. Instead, people clung tighter to the very idols that can't help them at all. It's the same pattern that resurfaces at the {{% bible val="fourth and fifth bowls, where people again refuse to repent and curse God" link="rev:16,8-11" lang="en" %}} instead of turning to him — the same pattern already on display earlier in the book, where {{% bible val="people worship the dragon and the beast" link="rev:13,4" lang="en" %}} rather than their Maker.

## The seventh trumpet, and what comes first

{{% bible val="The seventh trumpet" link="rev:11,15-19" lang="en" %}} doesn't sound until several chapters later, and when it finally does, it brings God's kingdom in full — the mirror image of the {{% bible val="seventh bowl, where the devil's kingdom is torn down" link="rev:16,17-20" lang="en" %}}. But what happens in the gap before it sounds is worth pausing on.

Since hardship alone never turned anyone's heart back to God, God tries something different: he calls for {{% bible val="the testimony of two witnesses" link="rev:11,3-12" lang="en" %}}, grounded {{% bible val="in worship" link="rev:11,1-2" lang="en" %}} rather than disaster. The witnesses are killed for it — and yet {{% bible val="a tenth of the city collapses and seven thousand people die, while the rest are overwhelmed and give glory to the God of heaven" link="rev:11,13" lang="en" %}}.

Stop and notice how striking that is: after nine trumpet-plagues that produced no repentance at all, watching two witnesses die finally provokes a response — even if the text only lets us measure it in a city's casualties, not a clean percentage of hearts. {{% bible val="The word translated 'terrified' here" link="rev:11,13" lang="en" %}} is [a rare one in the New Testament](https://biblehub.com/greek/1719.htm), and elsewhere it describes something stronger than plain fright — a being-overwhelmed that can tip either way:

- the {{% bible val="women at the empty tomb" link="luk:24,5" lang="en" %}}, overwhelmed into worship,
- the disciples {{% bible val="the moment the risen Jesus first stood among them" link="luk:24,36-37" lang="en" %}}, overwhelmed into thinking they'd seen a ghost,
- {{% bible val="Cornelius, seeing the angel who told him his prayers had been heard" link="act:10,4" lang="en" %}}, overwhelmed into asking what to do,
- and the Roman commander {{% bible val="Felix, hearing what the gospel actually demanded" link="act:24,25" lang="en" %}}, overwhelmed into telling Paul to stop talking — and no closer to repentance for it.

So the word itself doesn't decide the question; being "overwhelmed" can lead to worship or to stalling. What tips it in verse 13 is what the survivors do next: they {{% bible val="give glory to the God of heaven" link="rev:11,13" lang="en" %}} — the very phrase Joshua once used to press a guilty Achan toward confession (Joshua 7:19), and the response Revelation itself calls for elsewhere. Whether that counts as full repentance or a Pharaoh-style admission wrung out by terror, the text doesn't spell out. What it does make unmistakable is that this is the first time in the whole trumpet cycle anyone responds to God at all — which is itself the turning point the chapter is building toward.
