The {{% bible val="letter to the church of Laodicea" link="rev:3,14-22" lang="en" %}} is the most frequently preached letter in Revelation, so most of us already know about the problem of being lukewarm. But do we really know the context behind that image? It matters here more than usual, since Laodicea is the *only* church that receives no praise at all — not even a little.

## The historical background

<a name="a3f2"></a>
Laodicea sat at the crossroads of several trade routes and was an extremely wealthy city:

- it functioned as something like the Swiss bank of Asia Minor,
- its sheep farming produced valuable black wool,
- and it had a well-known medical center famous for its eye ointments.

## Jesus' view

<a name="6bbf"></a>
Jesus comes to this church as the "True Witness" and the "Amen," a pointed contrast, since Laodicea itself is a counterfeit church. Their core problem is a lack of self-awareness: they consider themselves God's gift to the world, when in reality they are {{% bible val="failing exactly in the very ways they think they are strong" link="rev:3,17" lang="en" %}}.

## The problem

<a name="d6b2"></a>
Jesus approaches them as a merchant — a figure they can relate to — offering them, free of charge, the very goods they believe they already possess in the best available version. They assume that living a respectable life is enough to make a great church: no glaring sins, faithful tithing, regular Bible reading, and so on.

Their real problem is that they are lukewarm, and the image is drawn straight from the temperature of water. In that culture, water was valued in two states: hot, useful for healing purposes like a hot spring, or cold, refreshing to drink. Laodicea's own water supply, notably, arrived lukewarm through an aqueduct, having lost its heat along the way — a local joke Jesus turns into a spiritual diagnosis. The church has neither of the useful qualities: only lukewarm water that gets spat back out the moment it's tasted.

There are many ways to be an effective church — evangelism, prayer, healing, ministering to the poor, and more — each with its own real purpose. The Laodicean congregation had settled into none of them, and had become just as useless as their own lukewarm water.

## The solution

Jesus calls them out of their complacency and knocks at their door. This is not an altar call aimed at unbelievers — it is {{% bible val="the call of the lover to the beloved" link="sng:5,1-5" lang="en" %}}.

And if they listen, the promise is astonishing: they will *sit* with him on the throne, in the throne room of God itself. Stop and consider what that means — no one else sits in that throne room; everyone else stands. Is that promise enough to shake Laodicea out of the way it sees itself?
