Once you've wrestled with the details of Revelation 20 itself, a bigger question is usually waiting right behind it: which overall system does this chapter fit into? Historically, Christians have answered that question in three main ways, named for where they place Christ's second coming relative to the 1,000 years: premillennialism (before), postmillennialism (after), and amillennialism (the number describes something other than a future golden age). None of the three is a modern invention — all three have been argued, in some form, since the early centuries of the church.

A few questions are worth holding in mind as you weigh them, since they expose what's really driving the disagreement:

- Does the 1,000 years describe a future, still-unfulfilled era, or a present reality already underway?
- Does the world get better before Jesus returns, worse, or does it stay a mixture of wheat and weeds right up to the end?
- Is the "reign" of {{% bible val="Revelation 20:4" link="rev:20,4" lang="en" %}} something the saints do on earth, in heaven, or both?
- How much weight should Old Testament promises to Israel carry once they're picked up again in the New Testament — see the earlier discussion of {{% int_link val="Israel and the church" link="/expl/background/israel/the-church-is-part-of-israel" %}}?

## Premillennialism

<a name="9c2a"></a>
Jesus returns first, defeats the beast and false prophet, and then reigns bodily on earth for a literal 1,000 years before Satan is released for one final rebellion and the new creation arrives. It comes in two main varieties — historic premillennialism, where the church goes through the tribulation, and dispensational premillennialism, which adds a {{% int_link val="pre-tribulation rapture" link="/expl/topics/others/the-rapture" %}} and a separate future for ethnic Israel.

**In favor:**

- It takes the narrative sequence of chapters 19 and 20 at face value, without needing to argue that "and I saw" reaches back to describe an earlier event from a new angle.
- It gives a concrete, this-worldly home to prophecies like {{% bible val="Isaiah 65" link="isa:65,18-25" lang="en" %}}, which describe long life and ordinary work but not the total absence of death — details that don't sound like the present age, but don't sound like the final new creation either.
- It fits a plain reading of {{% bible val="Zechariah 14" link="zec:14,4" lang="en" %}}, where the Lord's feet stand on the Mount of Olives at his coming, pictured as a visible, earthly event.
- It preserves a future, distinct role for ethnic Israel, in line with a plain reading of the Old Testament's land and nation promises.

**Against:**

- It has to read the single "and" that opens chapter 20 as strictly temporal, against the pattern found everywhere else in the book, laid out in the {{% int_link val="previous article" link="/expl/content/1000y/the-thousand-year-kingdom" %}}.
- It requires the battle of chapter 19 and the battle of Gog and Magog in chapter 20 to be two separate end-time battles, even though both closely echo the one final battle described in {{% bible val="Ezekiel 38" link="ezk:38" lang="en" %}}–{{% bible val="39" link="ezk:39" lang="en" %}}.
- Isaiah 65 is introduced by {{% bible val="'new heavens and a new earth'" link="isa:65,17" lang="en" %}} — the same language Revelation later uses for the truly final state — which cuts against reading it as an intermediate kingdom rather than the new creation itself.
- It still needs an explanation for why people {{% bible val="die during the millennium" link="isa:65,20" lang="en" %}} despite living under Christ's direct, visible rule.
- The dispensational variety in particular depends on separating "Israel" and "the church" into two peoples with two destinies, which sits uneasily next to passages that call the church heir to Israel's promises.

## Postmillennialism

<a name="47e1"></a>
The 1,000 years, taken loosely rather than as a literal count, describes an age of gradually increasing gospel success, in which the nations are progressively won over through preaching, discipleship, and Christian influence in culture — before Jesus returns to a world that has, in large part, already been Christianized.

**In favor:**

- It takes seriously the Great Commission's expectation that {{% bible val="all nations" link="mat:28,18-20" lang="en" %}} will be discipled, not just individuals scattered among them.
- It fits the growth parables well, where the kingdom starts small and gradually fills the whole garden: {{% bible val="the mustard seed and the leaven" link="mat:13,31-33" lang="en" %}}.
- It reads {{% bible val="Psalm 110:1" link="psa:110,1" lang="en" %}} — Christ reigning "until his enemies are made a footstool" — as a reign that visibly advances toward victory rather than one that stays contested right up to the end.
- It offers real motivation for cultural and social engagement, since believers are working toward an outcome, not just holding the line until an exit.

**Against:**

- It sits awkwardly next to Revelation's own picture of history, where evil doesn't shrink but escalates — the beast makes war and, {{% bible val="for a time, conquers the saints" link="rev:13,7" lang="en" %}}, rather than being gradually out-preached.
- Jesus's own parables cut both ways: alongside the mustard seed sits the {{% bible val="wheat and the weeds, growing together until the harvest" link="mat:13,24-30" lang="en" %}} — a picture of an age that stays mixed, not one that resolves into increasing Christian dominance before the end.
- Church history hasn't obviously trended toward a gospel-saturated global culture the way the theory anticipates, which has made this the least common of the three views since the upheavals of the 20th century.
- It reads the 1,000 years symbolically as a long, gradually-won era, while still wanting the final Gog and Magog rebellion read as one sudden, literal event — a combination that can look like picking whichever reading fits best case by case.

## Amillennialism

<a name="7d18"></a>
The 1,000 years symbolically describes the whole church age, from Christ's first coming to his second — the period we are living in right now — during which Satan is restrained from deceiving the nations the way he once did, and believers already reign with Christ in a real but not yet fully visible sense. This is the reading the {{% int_link val="previous article" link="/expl/content/1000y/the-thousand-year-kingdom" %}} argues for in detail.

**In favor:**

- It matches the visionary use of "and I saw" found everywhere else in Revelation, rather than treating this one instance as an exception.
- It doesn't need two separate final battles — the single, climactic battle echoed in Ezekiel 38–39, chapter 19, and chapter 20 can simply be the same event described from different angles, matching the book's broader habit of recapitulation.
- It fits the New Testament's repeated claim that the kingdom, and Satan's defeat, are already underway: {{% bible val="the kingdom of God has already come upon you" link="mat:12,28" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="rescued... and brought into the kingdom" link="col:1,13" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="I saw Satan fall like lightning" link="luk:10,18" lang="en" %}}.
- It takes Isaiah 65's own framing at face value, letting the passage describe the New Jerusalem rather than requiring an in-between stage the text itself never mentions.
- It fits the wheat and the weeds growing together, since the church age is precisely a mixed age, not a golden one — there's no need to explain away either the growth or the ongoing evil.

**Against:**

- It reads "1,000 years" as a large round number for an already long and continuing period, which some see as special pleading compared to Revelation's more precise numbers elsewhere, like the {{% bible val="42 months" link="rev:13,5" lang="en" %}}.
- It has to take the "binding" of Satan in {{% bible val="the opening of chapter 20" link="rev:20,1-3" lang="en" %}} as a real but partial restriction — unable to deceive the nations the way he once did — rather than the total removal the plain words "bound" and "sealed" seem to suggest. Critics ask why, if Satan is bound, {{% bible val="he still prowls like a roaring lion, looking for someone to devour" link="1pe:5,8" lang="en" %}}.
- It softens or spiritualizes some Old Testament promises — long life, agricultural abundance, a personally present king in Jerusalem — that other readers think were meant more concretely.
- It leaves less room for a triumphant, visible vindication of the saints within history — the reign of {{% bible val="Revelation 20:4" link="rev:20,4" lang="en" %}} ends up largely invisible to onlookers, which can feel like an anticlimax next to the very physical, visible reign premillennialism expects.

## So which one?

<a name="b3f5"></a>
The {{% int_link val="previous article" link="/expl/content/1000y/the-thousand-year-kingdom" %}} on this site argues for the third option, mainly because of how strongly Revelation 20 echoes chapter 12's account of Satan's fall, and because of the visionary use of "and" documented throughout the book. But it's worth working through your own answers to the questions above before settling on a label — much of the disagreement between these three views comes down to different judgments about how literally to read specific numbers, and how much weight Old Testament promises should carry once they're picked up again in the New Testament. {{% int_link val="Bock, Three Views on the Millennium and Beyond" link="/about/ressources/index.html#bock_millennium" %}} is a useful place to see the strongest form of each argument side by side, made by scholars who actually hold each position, rather than a caricature of it.

A few closing questions worth sitting with:

- Which of the three pictures of "1,000 years" makes the best sense of Revelation's other numbers — the {{% bible val="144,000" link="rev:7,4" lang="en" %}}, the {{% bible val="42 months" link="rev:13,5" lang="en" %}}, the {{% bible val="1,260 days" link="rev:12,6" lang="en" %}} — are those usually literal head-counts and calendars, or symbols?
- If you hold to premillennialism, what do you make of the people in {{% bible val="Isaiah 65:20" link="isa:65,20" lang="en" %}} who still die?
- If you hold to postmillennialism, what do you make of {{% bible val="the beast's temporary victory over the saints" link="rev:13,7" lang="en" %}}?
- If you hold to amillennialism, why does {{% bible val="Peter still describe Satan as a roaring lion" link="1pe:5,8" lang="en" %}} seeking someone to devour, if he's already bound?
