The thousand-year kingdom of Revelation 20 has spawned more competing theories than almost any other passage in the book. Rather than starting from a theory, let's start from the text itself and its context, and see what a fresh look turns up.

A few questions are worth holding in mind from the outset, since they expose assumptions most readers bring to the passage without noticing:

- [Who will reign over whom, and where?](https://www.bibleserver.com/NIV/Revelation20%3A4)
- Why not just a new heaven and a new earth — why would the text even need a millennium in between, if the alternative is just everyone sitting on clouds playing harps?
- Where else in the Bible does the concept of a 1000-year kingdom appear, outside this {{% bible val="one verse" link="rev:20,4" lang="en" %}}?
- What kind of {{% bible val="reigning" link="mat:20,20-28" lang="en" %}} are we even assuming happens during those 1000 years?

One piece of context shapes everything that follows: the 1000-year kingdom sits inside a larger chiasm, a structure that organizes material symmetrically rather than in strict chronological sequence. That means an earlier theme can resurface from a different angle rather than describing a genuinely new, later event — a possibility worth keeping open as we work through the chapter.

## A little word with big consequences

<a name="ed7b"></a>
[The 1,000-year episode begins with a simple word: "and"](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/revelation/20-1.htm) — and how you take that one word turns out to carry a great deal of weight.

"And I saw" could be read temporally: after the events just narrated ({{% bible val="the final battle" link="rev:19,11-21" lang="en" %}}), the devil is bound for a thousand years, and everything that follows happens after that battle in sequence. Or "and I saw" could be read visionally: John simply receives a new vision at this point, one that shows the devil bound for a thousand years, without any claim about where that vision falls in time relative to what came before.

To decide between the two, it helps to see how "and" functions elsewhere in the book:

- Most of the time, "and" simply links one vision or block of material to the next — it's a visionary connector, not a timestamp.
- When "and" does carry a temporal sense, that sense usually comes from the surrounding paragraph, not from the word itself; the word is used the same way whether the events are simultaneous or sequential, and it's the wider context that settles which.
- A temporal "and" is the exception rather than the rule — it happens only 4 times out of 35 uses in the book ({{% bible val="the beast and the false prophet were captured right after the battle was lost" link="rev:19,20" lang="en" %}}; [all the people were killed](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/revelation/19-21.htm); [then the birds fed on the corpses](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/revelation/19-21.htm); and perhaps {{% bible val="Jesus fought the battle after the wedding" link="rev:19,14" lang="en" %}}).
- When "and" introduces the ascent or descent of an angel, it marks a new vision — sometimes with a temporal delay, as {{% bible val="with the angel with the little scroll" link="rev:10,1" lang="en" %}}, and sometimes as a flashback, as with the {{% bible val="angel with the seals of the living God" link="rev:7,2" lang="en" %}} or {{% bible val="the judgment of Babylon" link="rev:18,1" lang="en" %}}, which was already {{% bible val="described in the previous chapter" link="rev:17,16" lang="en" %}}. Most commentators who read the "and" in chapter 20 temporally still read the equivalent "and" in these other chapters visionally — an inconsistency worth noticing.

## Two final battles?

<a name="1767"></a>
There are strong parallels between three battle scenes: {{% bible val="Jesus's battle 'before' the 1000-year kingdom" link="rev:19,11-21" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="the battle of Gog and Magog after the 1000-year kingdom" link="rev:19,7-10" lang="en" %}}, and the two visions of Gog and Magog in Ezekiel, in chapters {{% bible val="38" link="ezk:38" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="39" link="ezk:39" lang="en" %}}. All three share the same shape: a huge gathering for war, an "end times battle" fought by a massive army, and God's overwhelming, final victory.

If those parallels mean what they seem to mean, then Jesus's battle in chapter 19 and the battle with Gog and Magog in chapter 20 are the same event seen twice, which would mean the 1,000-year kingdom actually precedes the events of chapter 19 in time.

But could these be two different battles instead? A few concerns push back against treating them as identical:

- Chapter 19 describes destruction by {{% bible val="sword" link="rev:19,15" lang="en" %}}, chapter 20 by {{% bible val="fire" link="rev:20,9" lang="en" %}} — different weapons, which might suggest different battles. But the same pattern shows up within Ezekiel's own two visions, where both weapons appear in both: the {{% bible val="sword" link="ezk:38,21" lang="en" %}} and the {{% bible val="fire" link="ezk:38,22" lang="en" %}} in chapter 38, and the {{% bible val="sword" link="ezk:39,17-21" lang="en" %}} and the {{% bible val="fire" link="ezk:39,6" lang="en" %}} in chapter 39. So the change of weapon doesn't obviously prove two separate events.
- Chapters 19 and 20 could describe different degrees of fulfillment, but both use language that closely tracks the end-time battles described in {{% bible val="Ezekiel" link="ezk:38" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="Zechariah" link="zec:12" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="Zephaniah" link="zep:3" lang="en" %}} — and in all three of those, the battle described is final, not one of several stages.
- Chapter 19 describes {{% bible val="human armies" link="rev:19,19" lang="en" %}} while chapter 20 describes {{% bible val="demonic armies" link="rev:20,8" lang="en" %}} — but where does the text actually say the armies in chapter 20 are purely demonic? Read both passages again carefully alongside {{% bible val="the sixth bowl" link="rev:16,14" lang="en" %}}: in both chapters, Satan is leading human armies, so the supposed distinction may not hold.
- Could the order of events in Daniel {{% bible val="chapter 7" link="dan:7" lang="en" %}} support multiple battles, on the grounds that the devil is defeated only after the reign of the saints? Daniel's vision is highly repetitive, though: the reign of the saints appears in verses {{% bible val="8" link="dan:7,8" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="13–14" link="dan:7,14" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="19–22" link="dan:7,19-22" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="23–25" link="dan:7,23-25" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="27" link="dan:7,27" lang="en" %}}, while the judgment of the beast appears in verses {{% bible val="9–11" link="dan:7,9-11" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="26" link="dan:7,26" lang="en" %}} — the same events revisited, not a strict timeline. And notably, the {{% bible val="opening of the book" link="dan:7,10" lang="en" %}} comes before everything else, which complicates any attempt to read the sequence as strictly chronological.

## Further arguments for one battle

<a name="ba8d"></a>
The sequence of the bowls themselves points the same direction. During the sixth bowl, the armies are gathered at {{% int_link val="Armageddon which refers to a mountain" link="/expl/content/bowls/the-key-to-armageddon" %}}, so the battle is still ahead of the narrative at that point. It's only announced as won in the seventh bowl, which is followed by a detailed account of the harlot Babylon's defeat and, as its counterpart, the wedding of the bride; the battle itself is then narrated in detail in {{% bible val="chapter 19" link="rev:19,17-21" lang="en" %}}. And since the {{% bible val="beginning of the bowls explicitly calls them the final plagues" link="rev:15,1" lang="en" %}} — meaning they culminate in the {{% bible val="destruction of the devil's kingdom" link="rev:16,18-20" lang="en" %}}, which is exactly what happens in {{% bible val="chapter 19" link="rev:19,17-21" lang="en" %}} — any battle narrated after that point is best read as a recapitulation of chapter 19, not a second, later event.

## The binding of Satan

<a name="e7fd"></a>
The {{% bible val="beginning of chapter 20" link="rev:20,1-3" lang="en" %}} describes the binding of Satan, and the scene has a strong connection to {{% bible val="chapter 12" link="rev:12" lang="en" %}}, which describes Satan's fall as a result of Jesus's death. The parallels between the two chapters run deep:

- Chapters {{% bible val="12" link="rev:12,1" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="20" link="rev:20,1" lang="en" %}} both open with a heavenly scene.
- Chapter 12 has a {{% bible val="battle of angels against Satan and his accomplices" link="rev:12,7-8" lang="en" %}}; chapter 20 has {{% bible val="what looks like a similar battle of angels against Satan" link="rev:20,2" lang="en" %}}.
- As a result, Satan is thrown down — to the {{% bible val="earth" link="rev:12,9" lang="en" %}} in chapter 12, into the {{% bible val="abyss" link="rev:20,3" lang="en" %}} in chapter 20.
- He's identified by the same elaborate title in both places: "{{% bible val="that ancient snake called the devil, or Satan" link="rev:12,9" lang="en" %}}" and "{{% bible val="dragon, that ancient snake, who is the devil, or Satan" link="rev:20,2" lang="en" %}}".
- Both chapters {{% bible val="12" link="rev:12,9" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="20" link="rev:20,3" lang="en" %}} mention that he deceives the world, and in both, the action taken against him puts an end to that deception.
- In chapter 12 Satan knows he {{% bible val="has only a short time" link="rev:12,12" lang="en" %}}; in chapter 20 he {{% bible val="is released for a short time" link="rev:20,3" lang="en" %}}.

## But aren't there also differences?

There are a few genuine differences worth weighing. In chapter 12 Satan is thrown to the {{% bible val="earth" link="rev:12,9" lang="en" %}}; in chapter 20 he's thrown into an abyss. But in both cases an angel comes down from heaven to throw him down, and the {{% bible val="abyss" link="rev:20,3" lang="en" %}} may itself be located on earth, or refer to the {{% bible val="place of death, which can be on earth" link="rev:6,8" lang="en" %}} — so the distinction may be smaller than it first appears.

It's also true that {{% bible val="deception increases after Satan is cast out in chapter 12" link="rev:13" lang="en" %}}, while {{% bible val="it is stopped in chapter 20" link="rev:20,3" lang="en" %}}. But this contrast is less convincing than it looks: the deception increases in chapter 13 because the mystery of the faith has just been revealed, and the church, unable to be deceived by it, stands as a witness against it — which is a different dynamic than a simple increase or decrease in Satan's power. Also, the binding described in chapter 20 doesn't necessarily mean Satan is completely immobilized; the same language elsewhere describes having one's authority restricted rather than being rendered powerless, as with the sealed stone in {{% bible val="Daniel" link="dan:6,17" lang="en" %}} or {{% bible val="Matthew" link="mat:27,66" lang="en" %}}.

Taken together, these chapters read less like two contradictory timelines and more like two texts describing the same event from different angles and complementing each other — a pattern common elsewhere in the Bible.

{{% bible val="The binding of Satan" link="rev:20,2" lang="en" %}} is also described elsewhere as something {{% bible val="Jesus already accomplished at his first coming" link="mrk:3,27" lang="en" %}}. And yet {{% bible val="he (the man of lawlessness) is released shortly before Jesus's second coming, when he attacks the church" link="2th:2,6-12" lang="en" %}} — both things are true at once, on this reading.

The phrase "man of lawlessness," or {{% bible val="the lawless one" link="2th:2,8" lang="en" %}}, is used in the Old Testament in the {{% bible val="Psalms" link="psa:89,22" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:57,3-4" lang="en" %}} as a personification of evil. The same passage in 2 Thessalonians also says he exalts himself above God, a detail that reaches back to {{% bible val="Daniel" link="dan:11,36" lang="en" %}}. Put together, this figure is Satan, and the one who restrains him in Revelation's story is the angel.

## What about the prophecies?

But shouldn't there be many Old Testament prophecies that can only be fulfilled in the 1,000-year kingdom? You probably have in mind something like this passage from Isaiah 65:

“{{% bible val="But be glad and rejoice for ever in what I will create, for I will create Jerusalem to be a delight and its people a joy. I will rejoice over Jerusalem and take delight in my people; the sound of weeping and of crying will be heard in it no more. 'Never again will there be in it an infant who lives but a few days, or an old man who does not live out his years; the one who dies at a hundred will be thought a mere child; the one who fails to reach a hundred will be considered accursed. They will build houses and dwell in them; they will plant vineyards and eat their fruit. No longer will they build houses and others live in them, or plant and others eat. For as the days of a tree, so will be the days of my people; my chosen ones will long enjoy the work of their hands. They will not labor in vain, nor will they bear children doomed to misfortune; for they will be a people blessed by the Lord, they and their descendants with them. Before they call I will answer; while they are still speaking I will hear. The wolf and the lamb will feed together, and the lion will eat straw like the ox, and dust will be the serpent’s food. They will neither harm nor destroy on all my holy mountain,' says the Lord." link="isa:65,18-25" lang="en" %}}”

Since these promises haven't yet been fulfilled, and don't look like they will be on this earth as we know it, the natural conclusion is that they must be fulfilled in a coming 1,000-year kingdom. But look at the verse just before this passage: {{% bible val="Behold, I create new heavens and a new earth. The former things shall not be remembered, nor shall they come to mind." link="isa:65,17" lang="en" %}}

That clearly refers to the new heaven and earth described in Revelation 21 — which means the prophecy Isaiah gives us is explicitly framed as describing that new creation, not some intermediate stage before it. That raises a few questions worth sitting with:
- What picture do you have of the New Heaven and Earth, or the New Jerusalem?
- Which of the prophecies cannot be fulfilled in the New Jerusalem and why?
- Where in the Bible was the “pre-New Heaven and Earth” announced? What is the spiritual reason?
- Do you think the Millennium will be better than the New Heaven and Earth? If so, in what way? If not, why should people wait 1000 years and not receive the real thing immediately?

## What is the story line?

If chapter 20 isn't describing a separate future era, what is its storyline actually tracking? Revelation's account here roughly follows the shape of the prophet Ezekiel's vision, laid out in four movements:
- {{% bible val="37/1–14" link="ezk:37,1-14" lang="en" %}}: the resurrection of the people of God by the Holy Spirit, which corresponds to the {{% bible val="resurrection" link="rev:20,4" lang="en" %}} in chapter 20 — pointing to Pentecost.
- {{% bible val="37/15–28" link="ezk:37,15-28" lang="en" %}}: the Messianic kingdom, corresponding to the reign of the saints in {{% bible val="chapter 20" link="rev:20,4-6" lang="en" %}} — the time of the church's witness between Jesus's first and second coming.
- {{% bible val="38–39" link="ezk:38" lang="en" %}}: the {{% bible val="battle of Gog and Magog" link="rev:20,7-10" lang="en" %}}, corresponding to the second coming of Jesus.
- {{% bible val="40–48" link="ezk:40" lang="en" %}}: the final vision of a new temple and New Jerusalem, corresponding to {{% bible val="chapter 21" link="rev:21" lang="en" %}}.

## What's about the second death and the first resurrection?

“{{% bible val="I saw thrones on which were seated those who had been given authority to judge. And I saw the souls of those who had been beheaded because of their testimony about Jesus and because of the word of God. They had not worshiped the beast or its image and had not received its mark on their foreheads or their hands. They came to life and reigned with Christ for a thousand years. (The rest of the dead did not come to life until the thousand years were ended.) This is the first resurrection. Blessed and holy are those who share in the first resurrection. The second death has no power over them, but they will be priests of God and of Christ and will reign with him for a thousand years." link="rev:20,4-6" lang="en" %}}”

This is a rather puzzling text that hasn't been looked at closely yet. We've just concluded it describes the time between Jesus's first and second coming, but read at face value, the sequence of events sounds odd: people are beheaded because of their testimony, they exist for a time only as souls, and then they come back to life and reign.

There's another oddity that most translations quietly smooth over. Literally, the text begins, "[And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them and judgment was given to them](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/revelation/20-4.htm)." But look back for an antecedent to "they" and "them," and there isn't a clear one — the closest candidate is actually the devil, mentioned two verses earlier. On top of that, every verb in the passage sits in the same tense (all [aorist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aorist)), so there's no grammatical marker of temporal sequence between these actions at all.

The identity of "they" is only clarified afterward: "those who did not worship the beast and did not receive the mark." This pattern — naming a group before explaining who they are — is called [hysteron-proteron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hysteron_proteron) ("the last before the first"), telling the story in reverse order to put emphasis on the first part. It shows up a few other times in Revelation too:
- {{% bible val="hold onto it and repent" link="rev:3,3" lang="en" %}},
- {{% bible val="I am rich, I have acquired wealth" link="rev:3,17" lang="en" %}},
- {{% bible val="turn your stomach sour, but it is sweet in your mouth" link="rev:10,9" lang="en" %}}.

Read backwards, in the order the events actually happened, the passage makes more sense: they reign because they did not worship the beast, did not take his mark, and were beheaded for it.

## Will we be beheaded?

That raises an obvious question: why does the text specify {{% bible val="beheaded" link="rev:20,4" lang="en" %}}? Does that mean only people who were literally beheaded get to reign?

No — the {{% bible val="parallel with the souls under the altar" link="rev:6,9" lang="en" %}} shows that this language refers to all killed witnesses, not a literal, narrow category. The specific detail of beheading is itself another of the book's parodies: in the Roman Empire, only people of high rank were executed by beheading, which means that by dying for the faith, ordinary believers are dying as kings.

Does that mean only people who actually die for their faith get to rule? Not quite — the book describes several different kinds of witnesses and different kinds of suffering, not just martyrdom:
- {{% bible val="John in exile" link="rev:1,9" lang="en" %}},
- {{% bible val="overcomers who did not love their life (did not consider it that precious)" link="rev:12,10" lang="en" %}},
- those {{% bible val="faithful in the face of death" link="rev:2,10" lang="en" %}},
- those {{% bible val="faithful to Jesus" link="rev:14,1-5" lang="en" %}},
- and {{% bible val="all of us, called kings and priests" link="rev:1,6" lang="en" %}} who {{% bible val="rule" link="rev:5,9-10" lang="en" %}}.

So the reign belongs to anyone loyal to God, whether that loyalty costs them their life or something smaller, such as {{% bible val="economic or social pressure" link="rev:13,15-17" lang="en" %}}.

## So what is the second death?

“{{% bible val="Blessed and holy are those who share in the first resurrection. The second death has no power over them, but they will be priests of God and of Christ and will reign with him for a thousand years." link="rev:20,6" lang="en" %}}”

At physical birth, we receive our "first life" in the old creation. When {{% bible val="we are born again, we receive a 'second life'" link="jhn:3" lang="en" %}}, which is what this passage calls the first resurrection. The second death, by contrast, is simply never having that second life — remaining spiritually dead toward God.
