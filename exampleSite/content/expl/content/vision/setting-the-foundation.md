The book of Revelation begins with a few statements that are easy to skim past, yet they are key to understanding everything that follows. Let's look at them with fresh eyes.

## The Revelation of Jesus

<a name="33b5"></a>
“{{% bible val="The revelation from Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place. He made it known by sending his angel to his servant John." link="rev:1,1" lang="en" %}}”

The book opens intensely, as the "Revelation of Jesus Christ" (Apokalypsis Iesou Christou in Greek), "which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place." (Who this John was, and when he wrote, are both genuinely debated questions — see {{% int_link val="who wrote Revelation, and when" link="/expl/topics/others/who-wrote-revelation-and-when" %}} for a short overview and why it matters for how some later articles read the text.)

The word "apocalypse" probably triggers certain images in your mind — but that's not what it means here. Even the accurate, literal translation, "Revelation of Jesus," still leaves questions open, because {{% int_link val="apocalypse is a literary genre that describes how to read this book" link="/expl/background/literature/the-book-of-revelation-how-to-read-it" %}}: it's a book that provokes and demands action, not a code to be cracked.

Through all of this, we shouldn't forget that the book is about Jesus — he is the center of it. There are two ways to translate "of Jesus Christ":

- God gave the revelation to Jesus Christ, He is the one who gives the revelation, or
- Jesus is the content of the revelation, the revelation is a revelation about Jesus.

The Greek supports both readings, and that may well be the intention: the revelation comes from Jesus, but its purpose is to reveal more of Jesus. The "servants" are the believers John is writing to — they are the ones this book is addressed to.

The phrase "what must soon take place" is also deliberately chosen. It refers back to the {{% int_link val="vision of Daniel 2" link="/expl/bible/daniel/the-four-kingdoms-in-daniel" %}}, which describes the end of the world's kingdoms and the arrival of God's reign. The difference is that for Daniel this was still future, while for John it has already begun to happen.

{{% bible val="The next verse" link="rev:1,2" lang="en" %}} reinforces this by pointing to the testimony of Jesus. {{% int_link val="We will see that this is a central theme throughout the book" link="/appl/topics/power/the-power-of-testimony" %}}: Jesus doesn't just talk about it, he is a living testimony — and so should we be.

The verse after that makes the urgency even clearer: "Blessed is the one who reads aloud the words of this prophecy, and blessed are those who hear it and take to heart what is written in it, because the time is near."

- We should study this — in those days the book was read aloud, because not everyone had a copy — but more importantly, we should apply it.
- The time is near. There isn't much time; we should act now. Nothing has changed about that in the last 2,000 years.
- It is a prophecy: an urgent reminder to the Church to act.

"The time is near" doesn't mean the end can happen at any random moment; it's an exaggerated expression of imminence, the same way {{% bible val="Mark" link="mrk:1,15" lang="en" %}} uses [the same phrase](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/mark/1-15.htm) to describe how the kingdom of God has already come.

## The base

<a name="65e2"></a>
Now comes a passage that reads much like the opening of a New Testament letter, where the writer typically lays the foundation for what follows. This is no different.

"{{% bible val="John, To the seven churches in the province of Asia: Grace and peace to you from him who is, and who was, and who is to come, and from the seven spirits before his throne," link="rev:1,4" lang="en" %}}"

- Grace and peace are given to the churches because they will need them — they cannot meet the challenges ahead on their own.
- Jesus is the eternal one, "who is, who was, and who is to come" (verses 4 and 8). He is the Ruler of time, the Almighty. The same truth is expressed in "Alpha and Omega," the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet: he is the beginning and the end, and he does not change.
- The phrase "seven spirits before his throne" sounds a little strange at first. {{% bible val="In the prophet Zechariah, the lamps are identified with the Holy Spirit" link="zec:4,6" lang="en" %}}, and these lamps likely correspond to the {{% bible val="seven lampstands that identify the churches" link="rev:1,20" lang="en" %}}. The phrase "before the throne" is picked up again in {{% bible val="chapter 4" link="rev:4,5" lang="en" %}}: the Holy Spirit comes from the throne of God to carry out his plan.

"{{% bible val="and from Jesus Christ, who is the faithful witness, the firstborn from the dead, and the ruler of the kings of the earth. To him who loves us and has freed us from our sins by his blood," link="rev:1,5" lang="en" %}}"

- Here is the theme of Jesus as the faithful witness again — it recurs throughout the book because it is the key to everything. The pairing of "faithful witness," "firstborn," and "ruler of kings" is drawn from {{% bible val="Psalm 89" link="psa:89,19-32" lang="en" %}}, describing the son of David.
- "Firstborn from the dead" is an encouragement to stay faithful even to death, since death is not the end.
- "He loves us and has freed us from our sins" is a reminder that Jesus is for us.

"{{% bible val="and has made us to be a kingdom and priests to serve his God and Father — to him be glory and power for ever and ever! Amen." link="rev:1,6" lang="en" %}}"

- He has already made us kings and priests. We are kings now, but we still need to live it out — not by ruling the way the world rules.

## Coming with the clouds

<a name="e267"></a>
"{{% bible val="‘Look, he is coming with the clouds,’ and ‘every eye will see him, even those who pierced him’; and all peoples on earth ‘will mourn because of him.’ So shall it be! Amen." link="rev:1,7" lang="en" %}}"

This is a quote from Zechariah:

"{{% bible val="And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication. They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son. On that day the weeping in Jerusalem will be as great as the weeping of Hadad Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo." link="zec:12,10-11" lang="en" %}}"

Here, though, it is extended to the whole earth rather than just Israel. This mourning is compared to the {{% bible val="mourning for their king Josiah" link="2ch:35,22-25" lang="en" %}}.

The first part of the passage in Revelation comes from Daniel:

"{{% bible val="In my vision at night I looked, and there before me was one like a son of man, coming with the clouds of heaven. He approached the Ancient of Days and was led into his presence. He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all nations and peoples of every language worshipped him." link="dan:7,13-14" lang="en" %}}"

A similar combined quote appears in {{% bible val="Matthew" link="mat:24,30" lang="en" %}}, where {{% bible val="Jesus speaks of the end times" link="mat:24,3" lang="en" %}}. And "all the peoples on earth" echoes {{% bible val="Abram's promise that through him all nations will be blessed" link="gen:12,3" lang="en" %}}.

Put these passages together and a picture emerges:

- There is a deep emotional response — mourning for a beloved king.
- It has to do with the death of Jesus, the Son of Man.
- The outcome is that all nations will worship him, just as promised to Abram.
- But the text leaves open how the nations react ("beating their breasts for his sake") — some out of repentance for their savior, others out of fear of judgment.

So the passage isn't describing the coming wrath of Jesus against the specific people who killed him (the nations, after all, weren't really involved in that). It's a call to repentance and salvation for the nations.

## Conclusion

<a name="8d7d"></a>
The time is near, and the Church should act. Because Jesus has already accomplished everything, the Church can step forward with confidence and reach the nations.
