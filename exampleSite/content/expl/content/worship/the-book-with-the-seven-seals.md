Do you know the idiom "that's a book with seven seals," meaning a great mystery? The phrase actually originates in this chapter — though it doesn't mean quite what people assume.

## The book with the seven seals

<a name="92c0"></a>
In the throne room, the place of perfect worship, {{% bible val="a scroll with seven seals appears and draws all attention" link="rev:5,1" lang="en" %}}. But what is this seven-sealed scroll?

In the ancient world, certain documents were written by an official and sealed by seven witnesses. A summary of the contents was written on the outside, to make sure the scroll couldn't be opened by accident, because opening it meant its contents became legally valid — activated. The contents of such a scroll could be a will or a new law.

So when the voice asks "who is worthy to open the scroll," it really means "who is able to put the contents of that scroll into effect?" It isn't entirely clear at this point what the scroll refers to concretely — you could call it the prophets, the Old Testament, or simply God's plan — but one thing is clear: whoever opens it will rule the world. {{% bible val="Since no one turns out to be able to open it, John is understandably in deep distress" link="rev:5,2-4" lang="en" %}}.

{{% bible val="But there is one who can open it: the Lion of Judah. That's what John hears — but what he sees is a slaughtered lamb. The two are aspects of the same reality: the apparently helpless lamb is the victorious, invincible lion" link="rev:5,5-7" lang="en" %}}. It is the {{% int_link val="mystery of a hero" link="/appl/topics/hero/a-real-hero" %}} — a challenging image, since every apocalyptic hope pictures a strong ruler who overthrows the forces of evil by sheer power.

Another surprising element is the worship of the Lamb. The previous chapter showed everything revolving around the throne; here, everything centers on Jesus — he is worshipped, and God allows it. That itself is telling: it shows Jesus is truly God, because only God may rightly be worshipped.

## The seven seals

<a name="4f8c"></a>
When Jesus opens the seals, he makes sure {{% int_link val="that the devil's plans are exposed" link="/expl/content/seals/the-mystery-of-the-four-horse-men" %}}. Satan wants to rule the world, and the first four seals show exactly what he intends to bring about on the earth.

{{% bible val="Since the saints are not excluded from hardship (we will see why during the trumpets), they suffer and ask when this will end. Their request is granted (they receive white linen garments). From there on the vindication of the saints and the answer to their prayers is a common theme in the book." link="rev:6,9-11" lang="en" %}}

{{% bible val="The main reaction is Jesus' great judgment" link="rev:6,12-17" lang="en" %}}. This is truly the {{% int_link val="Day of the Lord" link="/expl/background/israel/the-day-of-the-lord" %}} — the Last Judgment, where everyone is held accountable and no one can hide.

After the interruption of the {{% int_link val="144,000" link="/expl/content/army/the-144000" %}}, the seventh seal is opened, and it brings a half hour of silence. Silence carries its own meaning in the Bible, and in Jewish literature there's a strong connection between a time of silence in heaven and the moment God hears the prayers of the saints. This continues the idea from the {{% bible val="fifth seal, where the prayers of the saints come into focus" link="rev:6,9-11" lang="en" %}}. Even though the prayer of the saints is mentioned only {{% bible val="twice" link="rev:8,2-5" lang="en" %}}, it connects the themes of the seals and the trumpets and plays a central role in both.
