What will heaven be like, and what will hell be like? Revelation doesn't answer either question in exhaustive detail, but it gives real insight into both.

## The structure of the chapter

<a name="aecd"></a>
Chapter 21 opens with the same literary move used earlier in the book, where the {{% bible val="lion" link="rev:5,5" lang="en" %}} turns out to be the {{% bible val="lamb" link="rev:5,6" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="the army of God" link="rev:7,4-8" lang="en" %}} turns out to be {{% bible val="worshipers from every nation, coming out of the Great Tribulation" link="rev:7,9-17" lang="en" %}}: John hears one thing and sees another.

Here, too, John {{% bible val="hears about the bride of God" link="rev:21,9" lang="en" %}} but then {{% bible val="sees her as the New Jerusalem" link="rev:21,10" lang="en" %}}. The mismatch is the point — a woman and a city are obviously not the same thing — and it signals that this is a symbolic description, not a literal one, while still insisting on its Jewish roots.

The passage can be structured as follows:

21:1–8: Introduction to the New Jerusalem, which summarizes the passage that follows (similar to {{% bible val="Isa. 60" link="isa:60,1-20" lang="en" %}}), with each verse anticipating a later section:

- 21:2 -&gt; 21:9–11
- 21:3 -&gt; 21:22–24; 22:3
- 21:6 -&gt; 22:1
- 21:8 -&gt; 21:27a

21:9–22:5: The New Jerusalem in detail:

- 21:9–14: first impression of the city
- 21:15–17: measurements of the city
- 21:18–21: material of the city
- 21:22–27: internal features of the city
- 22:1–5: symbols of God's presence

## Introduction of the New Jerusalem

<a name="f50f"></a>

### Everything is new

<a name="e5bc"></a>
“{{% bible val="Then I saw ‘a new heaven and a new earth,’ for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea." link="rev:21,1" lang="en" %}}”

"New" here isn't just describing something that recently came into existence — it describes a quality. The first heaven and earth were {{% bible val="temporary, but the new ones are permanent" link="isa:66,22" lang="en" %}}, and in them {{% bible val="many of the Old Testament promises reach fulfillment" link="isa:65,17-25" lang="en" %}}.

### No more sea

<a name="a258"></a>
The verse also says there {{% bible val="will be no more sea" link="rev:21,1" lang="en" %}}, which matters because of what the sea represents throughout Scripture. It is the place where evil originates, as in {{% bible val="Revelation" link="rev:13,1" lang="en" %}} itself, or in the sea monsters {{% bible val="Leviathan" link="psa:74,13-14" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="Rahab" link="isa:51,9-10" lang="en" %}}, or in evil kings personified as sea monsters, like {{% bible val="Pharaoh" link="ezk:32,2" lang="en" %}}, or in the {{% bible val="sea itself functioning as an evil medium" link="psa:93,3-4" lang="en" %}}. It is also {{% bible val="the place of the dead" link="rev:20,13" lang="en" %}}, alongside Hades.

So if there is no more sea, there is no longer any place where evil can dwell and oppress the faithful — indeed, the {{% bible val="sea is cast outside the city" link="rev:20,14-15" lang="en" %}} along with everything else that doesn't belong there.

### Jerusalem the Holy city

<a name="c358"></a>
“{{% bible val="I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband." link="rev:21,2" lang="en" %}}”

This picture has deep roots in Isaiah, where Jerusalem is the {{% bible val="beautifully dressed city" link="isa:52,1-10" lang="en" %}}, an image that {{% bible val="is developed further" link="isa:61,10" lang="en" %}} until she {{% bible val="receives a new name" link="isa:62,1-2" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="enters an intimate relationship with God, who rejoices over her" link="isa:62,3-5" lang="en" %}}.

The New Testament picks up the same thought:

- {{% bible val="God has prepared a city for the faithful" link="heb:11,13-16" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="we have a citizenship in heaven" link="php:3,20" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="there is a heavenly Jerusalem" link="heb:12,22" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="Jerusalem is free" link="gal:4,26-31" lang="en" %}}

There's also an interesting parallel within Revelation itself, between verse 2 and {{% bible val="verses 10 and 11" link="rev:21,10-11" lang="en" %}}: verse 2 has the new Jerusalem "coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband," while verses 10–11 have the "Holy City, Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God," where "it shone with the glory of God." Lining the two up shows that the bride's beautiful dress is the glory of God — and {{% bible val="God's glory is his mercy and compassion" link="exo:33,17-23" lang="en" %}}, which the church is likewise called to reflect.

### God lives with the people

<a name="93e0"></a>
“{{% bible val="And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, ‘Look! God’s dwelling-place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God." link="rev:21,3" lang="en" %}}”

This loud voice already appeared earlier, at the {{% bible val="judgment of the kingdom of Satan" link="rev:16,17" lang="en" %}} and the {{% bible val="judgment of Babel" link="rev:19,5" lang="en" %}}.

That God lives with his people is a {{% bible val="promise in the Pentateuch" link="lev:26,11-12" lang="en" %}}, picked up in {{% bible val="Ezekiel" link="ezk:37,27" lang="en" %}}, when he {{% bible val="returns to the temple" link="ezk:43,7" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="even including the nations" link="zec:2,10-11" lang="en" %}}. This is also a topic in the {{% bible val="New Testament" link="2co:6,16" lang="en" %}} and in Revelation.

### Wipe away the tears

<a name="cbf4"></a>
“{{% bible val="He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." link="rev:21,4" lang="en" %}}”

This is another promise from {{% bible val="Isaiah, where tears and death flee" link="isa:25,8" lang="en" %}}, along with {{% bible val="crying" link="isa:35,10" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="sighing" link="isa:51,11" lang="en" %}}. The {{% bible val="old order will be gone, along with all its mourning" link="isa:65,17-20" lang="en" %}}, and we {{% bible val="are meant to read this vision by looking forward, not back" link="isa:43,18" lang="en" %}}.

### All things new

<a name="ad18"></a>
“{{% bible val="He who was seated on the throne said, ‘I am making everything new!’ Then he said, ‘Write this down, for these words are trustworthy and true.’" link="rev:21,5" lang="en" %}}”

The promise that {{% bible val="God makes all things new" link="isa:43,18-19" lang="en" %}} is fulfilled in {{% bible val="Jesus, who began the new creation with us" link="2co:5,17" lang="en" %}}.

### Living water

<a name="bac3"></a>
“{{% bible val="He said to me: ‘It is done. I am the Alpha and the Omega, the Beginning and the End. To the thirsty I will give water without cost from the spring of the water of life." link="rev:21,6" lang="en" %}}”

This fulfills {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:49,10" lang="en" %}}, where God freely gives water to drink. The term "[without cost](https://biblehub.com/greek/1432.htm)" recurs throughout the New Testament — in {{% bible val="John" link="jhn:15,25" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="Matthew" link="mat:10,8" lang="en" %}}, the epistles to the {{% bible val="Corinthians" link="2co:11,7" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="Galatians" link="gal:2,21" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="Romans" link="rom:3,24" lang="en" %}}, and the {{% bible val="Thessalonians" link="2th:3,8" lang="en" %}} — and is very often paired with the idea of a gift given for free.

The living water has its source in {{% bible val="God" link="jer:2,13" lang="en" %}}, who {{% bible val="gives abundantly to drink" link="psa:36,8-9" lang="en" %}}, even to the point of {{% bible val="overflowing" link="jol:3,18" lang="en" %}}. It is, of course, fulfilled in Jesus, both with the {{% bible val="woman at the well" link="jhn:4,10" lang="en" %}} and as {{% bible val="a promise to all who believe in him" link="jhn:7,38" lang="en" %}}.

### The overcomer

<a name="e8f9"></a>
“{{% bible val="Those who are victorious will inherit all this, and I will be their God and they will be my children." link="rev:21,7" lang="en" %}}”

{{% bible val="Isaiah links God's covenant with the water given to drink" link="isa:55,1-3" lang="en" %}}, tying this verse back to the same well of imagery.

The promises to the churches that overcome in chapters 2 and 3 all find their fulfillment here:

- {{% bible val="Ephesus" link="rev:2,7" lang="en" %}} is promised to eat {{% bible val="from the tree of life" link="rev:22,2" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="Philadelphia" link="rev:3,12" lang="en" %}} is promised to be a {{% bible val="pillar in the new temple" link="rev:21,22-24" lang="en" %}}, to be {{% bible val="part of the New Jerusalem" link="rev:21,2" lang="en" %}}, and to have {{% bible val="God's name written on them" link="rev:22,4" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="Sardes" link="rev:3,5" lang="en" %}} is promised to be {{% bible val="in the book of life" link="rev:21,27" lang="en" %}} and to wear {{% bible val="white clothes" link="rev:21,2" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="Pergamon" link="rev:2,17" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="Thyatira" link="rev:2,28" lang="en" %}} are promised a {{% bible val="light stone" link="rev:21,11" lang="en" %}} ({{% bible val="or stones" link="rev:21,18-21" lang="en" %}})
- {{% bible val="Thyatira" link="rev:2,26-27" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="Laodicea" link="rev:3,21" lang="en" %}} are promised to {{% bible val="rule with Jesus" link="rev:22,5" lang="en" %}}
- {{% bible val="Smyrna" link="rev:2,11" lang="en" %}} is promised to be {{% bible val="excluded from the second death" link="rev:21,7-8" lang="en" %}}

### Ready for the second death

<a name="0819"></a>
“{{% bible val="But the cowardly, the unbelieving, the vile, the murderers, the sexually immoral, those who practise magic arts, the idolaters and all liars — they will be consigned to the fiery lake of burning sulphur. This is the second death.’" link="rev:21,8" lang="en" %}}”

This list of the condemned is the direct counterpart to the list of {{% bible val="overcomers" link="rev:21,7" lang="en" %}} just given.

[Cowardly](https://biblehub.com/greek/1169.htm) people are the ones {{% bible val="who don't act out of fear" link="mat:8,26" lang="en" %}} — the counterpart of those who {{% bible val="have peace" link="jhn:14,27-29" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="trust in God" link="jhn:14,1" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="know that God never leaves them" link="jhn:16,31-33" lang="en" %}}. "Murderers, the sexually immoral, those who practise magic arts, and idolaters" can describe unbelievers, but they can just as easily describe people inside the church who compromise, as happened in {{% bible val="Pergamon" link="rev:2,14" lang="en" %}} and {{% bible val="Thyatira" link="rev:2,20-21" lang="en" %}}. All of these sins, in fact, trace back to idolatry: murder, as in the way idolaters {{% bible val="sacrifice children" link="psa:106,36-38" lang="en" %}} or {{% bible val="kill believers" link="rev:13,15" lang="en" %}}; sexual immorality, linked to temple prostitution; and practising magic arts, which {{% bible val="Scripture directly links with idolatry" link="deu:18,9-11" lang="en" %}}, as with {{% bible val="Jezebel" link="2ki:9,22" lang="en" %}}.

Liars, finally, have betrayed the faith — {{% bible val="they call themselves apostles" link="rev:2,2" lang="en" %}} but {{% bible val="act in Satan's way" link="rev:3,9" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="bring false testimony" link="act:6,13" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="disobey Jesus' word" link="1jn:2,4" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="deny that Jesus is Lord" link="1jn:2,22" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="hate their brothers or sisters" link="1jn:4,20" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="don't believe we have eternal life in Jesus" link="1jn:5,10" lang="en" %}}. The list is repeated {{% bible val="at the very end" link="rev:22,15" lang="en" %}} of the book and in a {{% bible val="shorter version at the end of this chapter" link="rev:21,27" lang="en" %}}, and in every case it ends with "liar," because it is {{% bible val="essential that our words and deeds are consistent" link="tit:1,16" lang="en" %}}. After all, the {{% bible val="ultimate liar is Satan" link="jhn:8,44" lang="en" %}} himself, and this is one place we want to be as distinguished from him as possible.

## Details of the New Jerusalem

<a name="8e75"></a>

### {{% bible val="First look" link="rev:21,9-14" lang="en" %}}

<a name="946d"></a>
The New Jerusalem is the counterpart to Babel when we look at the {{% int_link val="larger context" link="/expl/content/1000y/the-thousand-year-kingdom" %}}. The {{% bible val="New Jerusalem represents a woman (the bride)" link="rev:21,2" lang="en" %}} just as Babel represents one (the harlot), which is also reflected in {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:62,1-5" lang="en" %}}.

- Babel is the economic system distributed with the power of the ruler, and immorality is done with idolatry (unfaithfulness), but God's people are faithful to him
- Babel causes suffering while Jerusalem removes suffering
- Babel destroys itself and Jerusalem stays forever
- Babel gets rich by exploitation and persecution, but Jerusalem is rich as God's gift

The twelve gates refer to the twelve tribes, and the twelve foundation stones to the twelve apostles. The {{% bible val="twelve-times-two reflects the eternal worship in heaven" link="rev:4,4" lang="en" %}}, but it is also a reminder to seek reconciliation between Jews and non-Jews, a {{% int_link val="conflict described throughout the book" link="/expl/background/israel/israel-and-the-church" %}}.

There's something strange in the arrangement, though: the apostles form the foundation of the tribes — the church as the foundation for Israel. That makes sense once you see that {{% bible val="Jesus is the fulfillment of Israel, worked out through the church" link="eph:2,20" lang="en" %}}. It fits, too, that Judah is {{% bible val="listed first among the tribes elsewhere in the book, reflecting Jesus" link="rev:7,4-8" lang="en" %}}.

### {{% bible val="The measurement of the city" link="rev:21,15-17" lang="en" %}}

<a name="5b7b"></a>
The measuring of the city draws on {{% bible val="Ezekiel" link="ezk:40,3-5" lang="en" %}}, where measuring signals {{% bible val="security" link="deu:3,5" lang="en" %}} against {{% bible val="destructive forces" link="rev:21,27" lang="en" %}} — a deliberate contrast with the old Jerusalem, whose walls were destroyed. In Ezekiel, the temple itself is {{% bible val="measured as a square" link="ezk:45,2-3" lang="en" %}}, while the {{% bible val="city around it has a different shape, with the temple at its center" link="ezk:48,8-13" lang="en" %}}. In Zechariah, God measures the {{% bible val="city" link="zec:2,2" lang="en" %}} so that {{% bible val="people find refuge" link="zec:2,6" lang="en" %}}, since {{% bible val="God has again shown mercy to Jerusalem" link="zec:1,16" lang="en" %}}. Much of the language in Revelation 21 is drawn from these temple descriptions, especially from the last nine chapters of Ezekiel — yet {{% bible val="there will be no temple" link="rev:21,22" lang="en" %}} in the New Jerusalem at all.

## Why a cube

When you look further, you find the city has a strange shape: it is a {{% bible val="cube" link="rev:21,16" lang="en" %}}. The only object in the Old Testament with this shape is {{% bible val="the Holy of Holies" link="2ch:3,8" lang="en" %}}. Why revisit that shape here? Because the Holy of Holies pointed to a real limitation in the old temple: God dwelled there, and the farther you stood from it, the farther you were from him. Now, in the New Jerusalem, everyone stands equally close to God. More than that: all {{% bible val="the unbelievers are outside the city" link="rev:22,14-15" lang="en" %}}, meaning outside the Holy of Holies altogether — so once again, you are either completely in or completely out.

One more detail is worth noting: the size of the city is roughly the size of the Hellenistic world — the Roman Empire — at that time, meaning there is room enough for everyone willing to enter.

### {{% bible val="The material of the city" link="rev:21,18-21" lang="en" %}}

<a name="73fd"></a>
The material recalls the {{% bible val="restoration of Jerusalem after her suffering" link="isa:54" lang="en" %}}: the {{% bible val="gold reflects God's glory" link="1ki:6,20-22" lang="en" %}}, as does {{% bible val="the jasper among the stones" link="rev:21,11" lang="en" %}}.

The twelve stones reflect the {{% bible val="breastpiece of the high priest" link="exo:28,17-20" lang="en" %}}, which {{% bible val="held the lots used to ask for God's will" link="exo:28,30" lang="en" %}} and was itself {{% bible val="square" link="exo:28,16" lang="en" %}}. Each {{% bible val="stone represented one tribe of Israel" link="exo:28,21" lang="en" %}}, and here {{% bible val="the twelve stones represent the twelve apostles" link="rev:21,14" lang="en" %}} instead — fitting, since we too are called {{% bible val="living stones in God's house" link="1pe:2,5" lang="en" %}}. It's worth noting, too, that the {{% bible val="temple of Solomon was itself built with four rows of stones" link="1ki:7,9-12" lang="en" %}}, echoing the same {{% bible val="breastpiece" link="exo:28,17" lang="en" %}} pattern.

One translation note matters here: our English versions typically read "the first foundation was decorated with jasper," but a [better translation is "the first foundation *is* jasper"](https://biblehub.com/interlinear/revelation/21-19.htm) — the foundation isn't merely ornamented with the stone, it is made of it.

The mention of the {{% bible val="street in the New Jerusalem" link="rev:21,21" lang="en" %}} recalls {{% bible val="the two witnesses, whose dead bodies were once exposed" link="rev:11,8" lang="en" %}} in a street — except now that same street stands in their honor. Our former shame will be replaced by eternal glory.

### {{% bible val="The internal features" link="rev:21,22-27" lang="en" %}}

<a name="bf9a"></a>
The statement {{% bible val="that the city has no need of sun or moon" link="rev:21,23" lang="en" %}} draws on {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:60,19" lang="en" %}}, where the line "your God will be your glory" is here replaced by "the Lamb is its lamp" — so Jesus is God's [glory, or beauty](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/strongs_8597.htm). This also fulfills {{% bible val="Ezekiel 43:2 and 5" link="ezk:43,2-5" lang="en" %}}.

## The nations and their gifts

The {{% bible val="statement that the nations will bring their splendor, glory, and honor into the city" link="rev:21,24-26" lang="en" %}} is easier to understand alongside {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:60,3-11" lang="en" %}}, which uses the same phrases but speaks of wealth where Revelation speaks of glory and honor.

The parallel is close enough to set side by side. Isaiah: “{{% bible val="Nations will come to your light, and kings to the brightness of your dawn. …, to you the riches of the nations will come" link="isa:60,3-5" lang="en" %}}.” Revelation: “{{% bible val="The nations will walk by its light, and the kings of the earth will bring their splendour into it" link="rev:21,24" lang="en" %}}.” And again, Isaiah: “{{% bible val="Your gates will always stand open, they will never be shut, day or night, so that people may bring you the wealth of the nations — their kings led in triumphal procession." link="isa:60,11" lang="en" %}}” Revelation: “{{% bible val="On no day will its gates ever be shut, for there will be no night there. 26 The glory and honour of the nations will be brought into it." link="rev:21,25-26" lang="en" %}}”

In Isaiah, the nations bring their wealth to acknowledge that God is with Israel and {{% bible val="will therefore no longer suffer alongside the others" link="isa:60,12" lang="en" %}} — they come bearing the good news of God's salvation. A later passage, “{{% bible val="I will extend peace to her like a river, and the wealth of nations like a flooding stream" link="isa:66,12" lang="en" %}},” likewise links the nations' gift to peace with Israel. In both texts, the glory, honor, and splendor described are not an attribute the nations already possess but something they give to God — not {{% bible val="the kind of glory and riches they once drew from Babel" link="rev:18,3" lang="en" %}}. Instead, {{% bible val="they bring themselves to serve God" link="rev:22,3-5" lang="en" %}}.

But who are these kings, and will all the nations be saved? It seems some of them repent and give God the glory, but not all do — the {{% bible val="same passage in Isaiah we've been following describes both good and bad kings" link="isa:60,1-14" lang="en" %}}, a pattern {{% bible val="found elsewhere too" link="isa:19,17-18" lang="en" %}}. This is, in fact, Israel's calling reaching its fulfillment: it began with {{% bible val="Abraham, called to be a blessing to all nations" link="gen:12,2-3" lang="en" %}}, passed to Israel at the Exodus, and was finally fulfilled in Jesus, who brought the nations into Israel — that is, into the church. So {{% bible val="the nations are now coming" link="rev:21,24-27" lang="en" %}}, though not all of them: only those who believe in Christ, since the {{% bible val="closing verse of the chapter still excludes unbelieving nations" link="rev:21,27" lang="en" %}}.

The {{% bible val="gates aren't open for exchange with an outside world" link="rev:21,25" lang="en" %}} — there is no such world left to trade with — but to display the result of what has already happened and the security that comes with it: there will be no night. And while the faithful may come in, {{% bible val="the unfaithful cannot" link="rev:21,27" lang="en" %}}, which is reflected in two {{% bible val="related passages" link="isa:52,1" lang="en" %}} of {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:35,8" lang="en" %}}. There, {{% bible val="pools of water appear just before" link="isa:35,7" lang="en" %}} the same statement about who may and may not enter; here in Revelation, the water instead follows it.

### {{% bible val="Symbols of God's presence" link="rev:22,1-5" lang="en" %}}

<a name="38e5"></a>
This closing picture draws on several Old Testament rivers: the {{% bible val="river flowing from the altar" link="ezk:47,1-9" lang="en" %}}, the {{% bible val="river that flows when Jerusalem is rebuilt" link="zec:14,8" lang="en" %}}, and the {{% bible val="water from the house of the Lord watering the trees" link="jol:3,18" lang="en" %}}. It also echoes the {{% bible val="creation story, where the rivers are likewise associated with precious stones" link="gen:2,10-12" lang="en" %}}, just as they are in {{% bible val="Revelation" link="rev:21,19-20" lang="en" %}}.

The source of it all is the throne of God and the Lamb, and water throughout Scripture is closely associated with the Holy Spirit: {{% bible val="when God gives us a new heart" link="ezk:36,25-27" lang="en" %}}, when we are {{% bible val="born of water and Spirit" link="jhn:3,5" lang="en" %}}, in {{% bible val="living water and worship in the Spirit" link="jhn:4,10-24" lang="en" %}}, and in {{% bible val="water and Spirit as joint witnesses" link="1jn:5,7-8" lang="en" %}}.

Finally, the circle closes back to the beginning, to creation and the fall: {{% bible val="the Tree of Life returns, and everyone has access to it" link="rev:22,2" lang="en" %}}. All evil is gone, and we are free to focus entirely on serving God.

The {{% bible val="name on the forehead" link="rev:22,4" lang="en" %}} was {{% bible val="originally worn on the priest's forehead" link="exo:28,36-38" lang="en" %}}, and here it shows {{% bible val="that God's own character, once imprinted on the priest, is now imprinted on us" link="1jn:3,2" lang="en" %}}.
