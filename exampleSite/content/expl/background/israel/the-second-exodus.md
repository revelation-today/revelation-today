There was an exodus from Egypt. But when Israel failed to keep the law, they went into exile — filled, all the while, with promises of their return. When the return finally came, though, they saw that nothing had really changed. The conclusion the prophets and later Jewish thought drew from this: Israel was still in exile, still waiting for a new exodus. That new exodus is what arrives with Jesus.

## The exodus in Mark

<a name="70f4"></a>
The opening lines of Mark's gospel are dense with Exodus echoes:

- {{% bible val="I will send my messenger" link="mrk:1,2" lang="en" %}} parallels both the {{% bible val="leading angel in the Exodus" link="exo:23,20" lang="en" %}} and the {{% bible val="messenger for the Day of the Lord" link="mal:3,1" lang="en" %}} — in both cases, a figure whose arrival warns of judgment for disobedience.
- {{% bible val="A voice of one calling in the wilderness, “Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him.”" link="mrk:1,3" lang="en" %}} picks up the prophecy of {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:40,3" lang="en" %}}, itself a message of restoration.
- {{% bible val="“And so John the Baptist appeared in the wilderness, preaching a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins." link="mrk:1,4" lang="en" %}}” is the {{% bible val="fulfillment of Elijah, preparing the new Exodus before the coming of the Day of the Lord" link="mal:4,5" lang="en" %}}.
- {{% bible val="“Just as Jesus was coming up out of the water, he saw heaven being torn open and the Spirit descending on him like a dove." link="mrk:1,10" lang="en" %}}” refers back to {{% bible val="Isaiah" link="isa:63,11-19" lang="en" %}}.
- {{% bible val="“And a voice came from heaven: ‘You are my Son, whom I love; with you I am well pleased.’" link="mrk:1,11" lang="en" %}}” echoes the language used for the faithful servant of the Lord in Isaiah.
- {{% bible val="“After John was put in prison, Jesus went into Galilee, proclaiming the good news of God. ‘The time has come,’ he said. ‘The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news!’" link="mrk:1,14-15" lang="en" %}}” is the final {{% bible val="announcement of a new king" link="isa:52,7" lang="en" %}}.

Taken together, these allusions show that the structure of Mark's gospel mirrors the structure of Isaiah 40–66:

## The new Exodus in Luke

<a name="1f7d"></a>
The key to the Gospel of Luke is “{{% bible val="As it is written in the book of the words of the prophet Isaiah: ‘A voice of one calling in the wilderness, “Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him. Every valley shall be filled in, every mountain and hill made low. The crooked roads shall become straight, the rough ways smooth. And all people will see God’s salvation.” ’" link="luk:3,4-6" lang="en" %}}” — {{% bible val="the same reference used at the beginning of Mark" link="isa:40,2-3" lang="en" %}}.

"The way," in both gospels, is the Exodus: it is God's way of restoring Israel, and it runs through the church.

When {{% bible val="Jesus read from Isaiah, he stated its fulfillment" link="luk:4,16-30" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="that was the sign of the second Exodus" link="isa:61,1-2" lang="en" %}}. Fittingly, then, the Gospel of {{% bible val="Luke ends with the fulfillment of the Scripture" link="luk:24,44-49" lang="en" %}} that {{% bible val="refers to the restoration of Israel and the inclusion of the Gentiles in Isaiah" link="isa:49,6" lang="en" %}}.

## The new Exodus in Acts

<a name="a809"></a>
Luke returns to this same language of "the way" six times in the book of Acts:

- {{% bible val="during the conversion of Saul" link="act:9,2" lang="en" %}},
- {{% bible val="in Ephesus" link="act:19,9" lang="en" %}}, in fact {{% bible val="twice" link="act:19,23" lang="en" %}},
- {{% bible val="in Paul’s speech" link="act:22,4" lang="en" %}} to the crowd in Jerusalem,
- and {{% bible val="in his speech to Felix" link="act:24,14" lang="en" %}}, also {{% bible val="twice" link="act:24,22" lang="en" %}}.

Alongside that, Acts develops six main messages that carry forward the Exodus theme:

- the restoration of the tribes of Israel by the 12 apostles,
- {{% bible val="Pentecost including Jews from all nations" link="act:2,5" lang="en" %}}, pointing to the restoration of the Jews from exile,
- the {{% bible val="coming of the Holy Spirit" link="act:2" lang="en" %}},
- the {{% bible val="restoration of the kingdom of David" link="act:15,13-18" lang="en" %}},
- the {{% bible val="inclusion of the excluded among the people of God" link="act:8,28-38" lang="en" %}},
- and Israel's initial repentance — at {{% bible val="Pentecost" link="act:2,41-47" lang="en" %}}, in the {{% bible val="early church" link="act:5,14" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="in the choosing of Stephen" link="act:6,1-7" lang="en" %}}, and in the {{% bible val="church at Antioch" link="act:11,24" lang="en" %}} — until, finally, the {{% bible val="book ends with" link="act:28,26-27" lang="en" %}} the very words where {{% bible val="Isaiah begins" link="isa:6,9-10" lang="en" %}}: Israel's rejection of the message.

A related theme running through Acts is "the word of God," which advances after the {{% bible val="choosing of Stephen" link="act:6,7" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="after the death of Herod" link="act:12,24" lang="en" %}}, and during {{% bible val="Paul’s time in Ephesus" link="act:19,20" lang="en" %}}. This echoes key passages in Isaiah on the same theme: {{% bible val="how can God be compared" link="isa:40,18-24" lang="en" %}} to anything, he {{% bible val="is more reliable than idols" link="isa:41,4-10" lang="en" %}}, the {{% bible val="vanity of idols" link="isa:44,9-20" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="the idols of Babylon" link="isa:46,1-13" lang="en" %}}.

Taken together, this comparison highlights a real tension between Acts and Isaiah: God is shown to be sovereign over the idols, yet Israel keeps clinging to them anyway. That tension comes to a head especially in {{% bible val="Acts 17" link="act:17" lang="en" %}}, where the Jews' resistance to Paul is likely rooted in this same attachment to idols.

Judgment is announced with particular force whenever these idols, or the people behind them, claim divinity for themselves — as in the cases of {{% bible val="Simon" link="act:8,4-24" lang="en" %}}, {{% bible val="Herod" link="act:12,20-23" lang="en" %}}, and {{% bible val="Elymas" link="act:13,10-11" lang="en" %}}.

## Summary

<a name="f43c"></a>
The new Exodus for which Israel had been waiting so long finally happens — and it is tied inseparably to Jesus. What remains open is whether Israel and the Gentiles will choose God, or keep clinging to their idols instead; the book of Acts treats that as no small question.

Read this way, the Gospel and Acts trace a single, ongoing tension: the restoration of the Israel that believes in Christ, running alongside the salvation of the Gentiles who are being drawn in together with them.
