The book of Revelation seems disorganized at first glance — one catastrophe after another, and then the cycle starts all over again. But look closer, and an artistic structure and deliberate craftsmanship emerge.

## The Challenge

<a name="8179"></a>
The book is full of symbols and references to the rest of the Bible, all in service of reminding us of our purpose as the church.

It opens with what God has done and what he expects of the church in response: to be light, and to depend on him.

From there, the {{% int_link val="churches are judged" link="/expl/content/letters/the-letters-to-the-seven-churches" %}}, and the result is sobering. Only two of the seven churches come away blameless, and both are placed in unimportant positions within the list. Two others are outright disasters, bookending the sequence at its beginning and end, and right in the middle sits a church so troubled that Jesus singles it out as a warning example for the rest.

## The Answer

<a name="b85e"></a>
Against that challenge, God gives an anchor for our lives: the place of eternal, {{% int_link val="unchanging worship" link="/appl/topics/power/worship" %}} before the unchanging, eternal God.

Everything that follows — the seals, the trumpets, and the bowls — flows out from that place, each series ending with its own sequence of thunder, lightning, and earthquakes, echoing what already exists in the throne room. Together, the three series reveal different aspects of how God's kingdom will come to pass.

Each of the three series also carries its own interruption: the seals and trumpets each pause for a deepening of the theme, while the trumpets and bowls each attach an appendix that develops it further. The bowls' appendix runs all the way to the final chapter, the New Jerusalem itself.

## The Seals

<a name="8ec8"></a>
The first series is the {{% int_link val="seals" link="/expl/content/worship/the-book-with-the-seven-seals" %}}. They express what Jesus accomplished on the cross and its consequences: exposing the devil and judging the world for its alliance with him, while also taking into account the prayers of the saints.

This series is interrupted in chapter 7 by God {{% int_link val="gathering his army" link="/expl/content/army/the-144000" %}}, and it closes with the seventh seal, which doubles as the introduction to the sounding of the trumpets.

## The Trumpets

<a name="c56a"></a>
The {{% int_link val="trumpets" link="/expl/content/trumpets/the-trumpets-in-revelation" %}} answer our prayers, but with a surprising twist. We might expect our prayers to bring comfort and peace; instead, the opposite happens. The trumpets strip away provision and security, and eventually life itself, so that everyone comes to realize that God alone is the true source of provision and security.

But people refuse to accept that lesson, so John is called {{% int_link val="once more to prophesy and to bear witness" link="/expl/content/scroll/the-little-scroll" %}}. Before the seventh trumpet sounds, the mystery of the temple is unveiled, and the {{% int_link val="two witnesses are revealed" link="/expl/content/witnesses/the-two-witnesses" %}}.

After that, {{% int_link val="the story of Jesus's victory over the devil as the true witness is shown" link="/expl/content/jesus/a-different-christmas-story" %}}, followed in turn by {{% int_link val="the presentation of the devil and his minions as a failed deception" link="/expl/content/beasts/the-nature-of-the-beast-in-the-book-of-revelation" %}}.

The whole sequence concludes with the presentation of God's faithful army — the same army we already met in chapter 7 — receiving their reward, alongside the judgment of everyone loyal to the devil and their own, very different "reward."

## The Bowls

<a name="d15a"></a>
The last major section turns to {{% int_link val="God's relentless passion for mankind to see his kingdom come" link="/expl/content/bowls/the-bowls-of-wrath" %}}. In the bowls, we watch God do battle against every remaining obstacle standing in the way of mankind's salvation, ending with the destruction of the devil's kingdom.

The appendix attached to this section goes into considerable further detail:

- God's main target is the mysterious {{% int_link val="harlot named Babel" link="/expl/content/harlot/who-is-the-harlot-babylon-part-1" %}}.
- Once the harlot is judged, the marriage of the Bride of Christ — the church — can finally take place.
- The destruction of the devil's kingdom is reported twice, in chapters 19 and 20, with the millennial kingdom sandwiched between the two accounts.
- Finally, as a deliberate counterpart to the harlot, the Bride is presented in full glory.

The book then closes with a series of final warnings, driving home just how serious all of this is.
