Revelation is a letter, a prophecy, and an apocalypse all at once — which means it was written to be read as a real message to real people, meant to provoke a change now, not a puzzle to decode about events far removed from your own life. Prophecy's goal was never simply predicting the future; it was changing the present so that the announced future didn't have to happen, the way Jonah's warning to Nineveh worked.

That's a very different way to hold this book than treating it as a code to crack. A code just needs to be solved. A letter needs to be obeyed.

There's a related skill worth building alongside that: learning to ask, image by image, whether something is meant literally or symbolically, instead of defaulting to one rule for the whole book. A dragon with seven heads was never meant to be photographed; a real historical Rome, a real persecuted church, and a real future return of Christ were. Serious readers disagree about which parts of Revelation land where — a first-century letter, a still-unfolding pattern, a future event, a timeless picture of the conflict between good and evil — and rather than forcing a single answer, it's worth holding all four questions at once for any given passage, since the book itself keeps refusing to sit still in just one of them.

- Have you mostly been reading Revelation (or prophecy in general) as a puzzle to solve, or as a letter addressed to you personally, asking something of you right now?
- If a specific passage in Revelation is meant to provoke a change in you today, which passage comes to mind first — and what change is it actually asking for?
- Take one image that's stuck with you from Revelation. Have you been reading it as a literal snapshot, a timeless symbol, both, or have you not actually stopped to ask which it is?
