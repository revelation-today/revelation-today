Every story we tell children ends with "happily ever after" and we never ask what that actually means. We just assume it means the hard part is over. Revelation ends with a happily-ever-after too - but if you slow down and actually look at it, it isn't answering the question you expected. It isn't mainly telling you what you'll get. It's telling you who you'll finally, fully be with.

## The sea is gone

{{% bible val="Then I saw 'a new heaven and a new earth,' for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea." link="rev:21,1" lang="en" %}} That detail about the sea is easy to skip past, but through this whole book the sea has been where the monsters came from - the beast climbed out of it in chapter 13, and it's the place the dead are given up from in chapter 20. So when John says there's no more sea, he's not describing geography. He's telling you there is no longer *anywhere left* for evil to come from. Not a place it's been defeated. A place that has stopped existing.

## A wedding, not a real-estate listing

{{% bible val="I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband." link="rev:21,2" lang="en" %}} And then the line the whole book has been building toward: {{% bible val="Look! God's dwelling-place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God." link="rev:21,3" lang="en" %}}

Notice what this is not. It's not "you get to go live in a nice place." It's a wedding. All the way back in a garden, in the very first pages of this story, one man and one woman walked with God in the cool of the day, and then that stopped. Every single thing that has happened since - the whole book of Revelation included - has been the long road back to that closeness. This is the wedding day.

{{% bible val="He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death, or mourning, or crying, or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." link="rev:21,4" lang="en" %}} Every category of grief you have ever carried, named specifically and ended specifically. Not because the memories are erased, but because the one wiping the tear away is the reason it's finally safe to stop crying.

{{% bible val="He who was seated on the throne said, 'I am making everything new!'" link="rev:21,5" lang="en" %}} Not *I am making new things* to replace the old ones. He is renewing this creation, this world, the actual dirt under your feet - not throwing it away for a spiritual substitute.

## A room only one man was ever allowed to enter

Here's the detail worth sitting with the longest. When John measures the New Jerusalem, it comes out as a perfect cube. Look through the entire Old Testament and there is exactly one other object that shape: the Holy of Holies, the innermost room of the temple, the one place on earth so filled with God's presence that only the high priest could enter it - and even he only once a year, and only with blood in his hands in case he didn't survive the encounter.

That is the shape of your address in eternity. Not the outer courts. Not "as close as ordinary people were allowed to get." The room reserved for the one person, one day a year, trembling. That's where you live now, and you don't need a priest to go in for you anymore, because the whole city is built to that measurement.

And that is exactly why there is no middle ground at the edge of it. Outside the walls is described in the same breath as everything the book has spent twenty chapters warning against. Not because God is unwilling to have you close - the entire city is engineered for closeness - but because you cannot be dragged into a wedding you never said yes to. Which side of that wall you're on isn't decided that day. It's being decided this week, in what you're saying yes to right now.

## Every promise, called in

Remember the seven letters back in chapters 2 and 3 - each one ending with a promise "to the one who is victorious"? Ephesus was promised the tree of life. Sardis was promised white clothes and a name kept in the book of life. Philadelphia was promised to become a pillar, never to leave again. Smyrna was promised to be untouched by the second death. Every one of those promises gets paid out here, by name, in these two chapters. This isn't a new ending tacked onto the story - it's every check the book has written, cashed at once.

And the last image is the oldest one there is: {{% bible val="Then the angel showed me the river of the water of life, as clear as crystal, flowing from the throne of God and of the Lamb... On each side of the river stood the tree of life." link="rev:22,1-2" lang="en" %}} A river, and a tree, in a garden, and God walking among his people. Genesis 3 slammed a door. Revelation 22 is that same door, standing open, with the flaming sword gone and nothing left to keep you out except your own unwillingness to walk through it.

## So what now?

That's the actual question this whole book has been asking, from the very first chapter's "grace and peace to those who need it," to the seven letters, to every seal and trumpet and bowl in between: not *are you curious about the future*, but *whose wedding are you actually dressed for*. The invitation has been open the entire time. The city has already been measured to fit you.

### Reflection

- Why do you want to go to heaven - closeness with God, or the absence of everything you don't like about now? What difference would it make if it were only the first one?
- Would you want to spend eternity with God even if none of the other benefits were there (no more suffering, sickness, loss)? If that's a hard yes, what does that reveal?
- The New Jerusalem is shaped like the one room on earth only the high priest could enter. What would it change this week to actually believe that's your address now?
- Every promise from the seven letters gets fulfilled in this passage. Which one of those promises do you personally need to hear fulfilled today?
- How does this vision of the ending change the way you live on an ordinary Tuesday?
