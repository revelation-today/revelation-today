If I asked you who really rules the world right now, what would you say? Politicians? Whoever controls the markets, or the headlines, or the algorithm? Two thousand years ago the answer looked just as obvious. Rome ruled the world, and everyone knew it - you could see Caesar's face on every coin.

Then an old man, exiled to a prison island for refusing to say otherwise, wrote a letter that said something different. Not *someday*. Not *once we win*. Already. And it's addressed to you.

## Grace and peace for people who need it

{{% bible val="John, to the seven churches in the province of Asia: Grace and peace to you from him who is, and who was, and who is to come, and from the seven spirits before his throne" link="rev:1,4" lang="en" %}}.

Notice what's given here before anything is asked of them: grace and peace. Not a pep talk. Not a to-do list. Because John already knows what these churches are up against, and he knows they cannot meet it on their own strength. That's still true of us. Whatever this week is asking of you, you were handed grace and peace before you were handed a single instruction.

And who is this from? "Him who is, and who was, and who is to come" - the one who rules time itself, not just this news cycle or this election or this diagnosis. Whatever is coming, he is already there waiting for you.

## A king who bled for you, and made you one too

{{% bible val="and from Jesus Christ, who is the faithful witness, the firstborn from the dead, and the ruler of the kings of the earth. To him who loves us and has freed us from our sins by his blood, and has made us to be a kingdom and priests to serve his God and Father - to him be glory and power for ever and ever! Amen." link="rev:1,5-6" lang="en" %}}

Read that last part again: *has made us*. Not *will make us*, not *might make us if we're faithful enough*. It's already done. You are, right now, a king or a queen, a priest before God. That's not a metaphor to feel encouraged by on a hard day - it's your actual job description.

But look at how this King got his throne. Not by conquest. By bleeding for people who didn't deserve it. If that's how he rules, that's how he's inviting you to rule too - not by getting louder or more powerful than everyone around you, but by loving people the way he loved you first.

## Every eye will see him

{{% bible val="Look, he is coming with the clouds, and every eye will see him, even those who pierced him; and all peoples on earth will mourn because of him. So shall it be! Amen." link="rev:1,7" lang="en" %}}

This line is stitched together from Daniel and Zechariah - the Son of Man arriving in glory, and Israel finally looking on the one they pierced and weeping over him like a father weeping for his only son. Except here it's not just Israel anymore. It's every people, every nation. The mourning could go two ways: grief that turns into repentance, or grief that's just fear of what's coming. The text leaves it open on purpose - because that's still the open question every time someone hears about Jesus for the first time, and it's the question your life is supposed to help answer well.

## The vision itself

Then John turns around, and what he sees is almost too much to describe: seven lampstands burning gold, and standing among them someone with eyes like fire, feet like bronze pulled glowing from a furnace, a voice like rushing water, a face too bright to look at, a sword coming from his mouth. John doesn't stay standing. He goes down like a dead man.

And the first thing this overwhelming figure does is put a hand on him and say: {{% bible val="Do not be afraid. I am the First and the Last. I am the Living One; I was dead, and now look, I am alive for ever and ever! And I hold the keys of death and Hades." link="rev:1,17-18" lang="en" %}}

That's the pattern worth sitting with. The vision is terrifying - and the very first words out of it are *don't be afraid*. Whatever in your life feels too big or too strange or too far gone, this is the same voice saying the same thing to you.

## Lampstands and stars: what you're actually here for

Finally Jesus explains the picture: {{% bible val="the seven stars are the angels of the seven churches, and the seven lampstands are the seven churches" link="rev:1,20" lang="en" %}}. Two images, and they share two things in common.

First: they're both sources of light. Stars for direction in a dark sky, a lampstand as the only light in a room with no windows. Second, and this is the part we'd rather skip: neither one shines on its own power. A star is nothing until it's set in the sky; a lampstand is just metal until there's oil in it. Every ounce of light either one gives off, it borrowed.

That's the whole job description for the church, restated: be light for the world - and do it entirely leaning on someone else's strength. Not more programs, more numbers, more visibility on your own steam. Just this: shine, because you're depending on Jesus, not because you've figured out how to shine yourself.

So here's the question this whole vision leaves you standing in front of: is your church - are you - actually a witness that would convince someone watching? Not impress them. Convince them that Jesus is worth trusting with their life the way you've trusted him with yours.

### Reflection

- This vision defines the church's purpose as being light while depending on Jesus. Which half of that do you find harder?
- What would it look like for your church to measure success the way Jesus does here, rather than by numbers or activity?
- Stars only shine while held in Jesus' hand. Where in your life have you tried to shine under your own strength instead?
- "Do not be afraid" comes right after the most overwhelming moment in the whole vision. What is it in your life right now that needs to hear those words?
- You have already been made a king or priest before God - not someday, now. What would change this week if you actually lived like that were true?
