Imagine you finally get to ask God the question that's been burning in you for years: "Why don't you do something?" Why don't you stop the dictator, heal the diagnosis, end the war, fix the marriage? You brace yourself for an answer about timing, or free will, or some grand strategy you're not wise enough to understand. Instead, John gets pulled through a door into heaven, and here is what he finds God doing about the mess of the world: God is sitting on a throne, being worshiped. That's it. That's the plan of action. And if you're honest, that might feel like the most unsatisfying answer possible — until you understand what's actually happening in that room.

## The door was already open

<a name="8bf5"></a>
We're in {{% bible val="chapter 4" link="rev:4" lang="en" %}} now, right after seven letters to seven churches that are, frankly, a mess. Congregations losing their first love. Congregations tolerating false teaching. Congregations so lukewarm Jesus says he wants to spit them out. If you were writing this book, this is where you'd send in the cavalry. Instead, John hears a voice like a trumpet say "come up here," and he's shown a throne room. Not a battle plan. A throne room.

Here's the thing: this isn't a new scene bolted onto the letters. Look closely and you'll find the throne room speaking the same language as the letters — the white clothing promised to the overcomers in Sardis and Laodicea shows up on the twenty-four elders. The crowns promised to Smyrna and Philadelphia are already on their heads. The open door promised to Philadelphia is the door John just walked through. Every struggling church was being shown, in advance, exactly where their promises come from. The throne room isn't an escape from the churches' problems. It's the source code underneath them.

## What worship actually is

<a name="0b7d"></a>
So why does God's answer to human suffering look like a worship service? Because worship isn't a hobby for religious people, and it isn't a mood. Worship is the act of seeing your life from God's vantage point instead of your own — of putting your trust in him above every other thing you're tempted to trust, and putting your life in his hands to lead wherever he wants. It's rooted in prayer, where you actually experience God answering you, which builds trust in his character, which builds a longing to become like him. You always imitate what you worship. That's where your transformation begins — not in willpower, in worship.

And this reshapes everything about how you survive hard things. Look back at what was actually wrong in those seven churches: wrong doctrine leading to wrong behavior in Pergamum and Thyatira, wrong self-assessment leading to wrong action in Sardis and Laodicea, wrong priorities draining Ephesus of meaning, brutal circumstances tempting Smyrna and Philadelphia to give up. For every single one of those, worship — not a program, not better leadership, not more effort — is the answer. The better you know God, the less false teaching can grab you. The more you worship him as he actually is, the more clearly you see yourself. When God is first, everything else falls into its right place. When your trust is in him, you are not easily shaken, because you're standing on something that cannot be shaken.

## Mercy, not just majesty

<a name="91eb"></a>
Now notice what John actually sees in that room, because the details are not decoration. He can't describe God directly — the glory is too much, the way Moses once asked to see God's glory and instead was shown God's mercy passing by. That's not a consolation prize. That IS what God's glory looks like up close: mercy. Then there's a rainbow around the throne — the very sign God gave Noah as a promise never to unleash unrestrained judgment on the earth again. And there's a sea of glass, the ancient symbol of congealed chaos and evil, now frozen solid and harmless before God's feet, while his people stand safely near him in the midst of it.

Hold onto that, because everything that is about to happen in this book — every seal, every trumpet, every bowl — comes out of this room. Not out of some other, angrier throne somewhere else. The same throne surrounded by a mercy-rainbow, over a defeated sea, radiating the glory of a God whose true nature is compassion. Whatever judgment is coming, it is dispensed from here.

## The Lion you hear, the Lamb you see

<a name="fb16"></a>
Then in {{% bible val="chapter 5" link="rev:5" lang="en" %}} the real tension of the whole book shows up: a scroll sealed seven times over, and a voice crying out, "Who is worthy to open it?" This scroll is like an ancient will or decree — sealed by witnesses, and whoever breaks the seals doesn't just read the contents, they set them in motion. Whoever opens this scroll rules the world. And {{% bible val="no one is found worthy — no one in heaven, on earth, or under the earth. John begins to weep bitterly" link="rev:5,2-4" lang="en" %}}. If no one can open it, there is no plan, no rescue, no future.

Then the announcement: {{% bible val="the Lion of the tribe of Judah has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll" link="rev:5,5" lang="en" %}}. Finally — the hero. The strong one. The one who crushes his enemies. Every apocalyptic hope you've ever had is about to be satisfied. And then John turns to look, and {{% bible val="what he sees is a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing at the center of the throne" link="rev:5,6" lang="en" %}}. The Lion you were told about and the Lamb you actually see are the same person. Not two options. One victory, won not by overpowering anyone, but by being slaughtered.

This is the mystery this whole book keeps circling back to: {{% int_link val="the real hero" link="/appl/topics/hero/a-real-hero" %}} does not look like the hero you were promised. And then heaven does something staggering — the worship that had been aimed entirely at God now turns, without a flicker of protest from God, and centers on this Lamb. Only God is worthy of worship. If Jesus receives it and heaven approves, there's only one conclusion: this slaughtered Lamb is God.

That is the answer to everything that's wrong in Ephesus, Pergamum, Sardis, and your own life this week. Not "God will finally act like the strongman you wanted." God has already acted — through weakness, through sacrifice, through a throne room where mercy sits at the center and evil lies frozen underfoot. Your task this week isn't to go fix what only he can fix. It's to walk back through that open door and worship, and let that reordering of your loves reorder everything else.

### Reflection

<a name="a821"></a>
- Why do you worship God?
- Can you see God's goodness in this world? Where?
- Do you see worship as the answer to the problems of our time, or as just something religious people do?
- John expected a Lion and saw a Lamb. What "lion" are you still waiting for God to send, when he may already have given you the Lamb?
- How has worship changed you? What has it changed in how you see your circumstances?
- How do you worship? What does worship look like in your daily life, beyond a Sunday service?
