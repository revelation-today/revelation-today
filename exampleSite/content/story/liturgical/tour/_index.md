Here is the story of Revelation, start to finish, told the way the whole Church has always half-known it: as a liturgy. No footnotes, no jargon - just the story, and the worship at its center, which you may recognize.

# How everything started

In the beginning, God knelt in the dust, shaped a man with his own hands, and breathed his own breath into him. Adam woke into a garden humming with life, set there to tend it as a priest tends a sanctuary - the first temple, God walking among his own creation, receiving the praise first given to it.

One thing alone was not good: Adam was alone. So God built Eve out of Adam's own side, and for one shining moment, all of creation stood as it was meant to stand - a single act of worship, offered back to the God who made it.

It did not last. Adam desired the gift more than the Giver. That one small turning cracked the world in two, and the sanctuary was left standing empty.

Driven from the garden, Adam's children built sanctuaries of their own devising - cities, towers, kingdoms - offered to gods that could not hear them. But God had not abandoned his purpose of communion with them. He turned to Abraham, and called him to entrust himself entirely to a promise - even, in the end, the son he had waited a lifetime to receive.

Out of Abraham, God raised up a nation, Israel, and gave it what Adam had lost: a tabernacle, then a temple, incense rising morning and evening, a curtain guarding the Holy of Holies. Worship, restored - but only in shadow, only from a distance.

# The promise

Israel carried the promise, and the pattern too: would this people love the gifts more than the Giver, as Adam had? They did, until the covenant lay in ruins, the temple was destroyed, and the people were driven out, exiled just as Adam had once been.

But God was not finished. He promised a Redeemer - one who would build a kingdom, and a worship, that would never fall, a door standing open to anyone willing to walk through it.

# New beginnings ({{% bible val="Chapter 1" link="rev:1" lang="en" %}})

Picture the scene: an old man alone on a rocky island, exiled for refusing to bow before anyone but Christ. It is the Lord's Day - kept even in banishment - when a voice behind him breaks like a trumpet, and everything changes.

He turns, and the vision overtakes him: seven lampstands burning gold in the air - recalling the very lampstand of Israel's sanctuary - and standing among them, one impossible to look away from. Eyes like fire, feet like bronze glowing from the furnace, a voice like the roar of many waters, a face like the sun at full strength. Seven stars rest in his right hand; a sword issues from his mouth. John falls before him like a dead man - the posture this whole book keeps returning to, the posture any true encounter with this glory demands.

A hand rests on his shoulder, and words undo the fear before it can form: "Do not be afraid. I am the First and the Last, the Living One. I died - and behold, I am alive forever. And I hold the keys of Death and of Hades."

The truth breaks like sunrise: the kingdom long awaited has arrived, and every believer - king and priest alike - is given a share in Christ's own priesthood. This vision reveals what the Church exists to be - a lampstand, kept alight not by her own strength but sustained by the One who stands among the lamps. So: is she found ready?

# A grand disaster ({{% bible val="Chapter 2-3" link="rev:2-3" lang="en" %}})

Seven churches, seven letters, seven mirrors - each measured by whether her lamp burns true.

Ephesus labors to exhaustion, sound in doctrine, faithful in duty - and has quietly let love slip through her fingers. Better, says the Lord, that this lampstand be removed than to go on so. Smyrna, poor and despised, is one of only two churches to receive nothing but commendation. Pergamum holds her ground in the shadow of Satan's throne, yet false teaching has crept in unopposed. In Thyatira, the corruption begins at the top. Sardis looks alive from without; within, she is already dead, persuaded she can manage without God. Philadelphia is the other church commended without reservation - small, depleted, faithful still.

Then there is Laodicea: lukewarm, blind to her own poverty, the only church to receive no praise at all. And yet even at her door, the Lord does not depart. He knocks, and still desires to enter and dine with her - the first hint of the wedding supper still to come.

# The throne room ({{% bible val="Chapter 4-5" link="rev:4-5" lang="en" %}})

It falls to every believer to rise and prepare. But how does one prepare for something of this magnitude?

The Lord's answer is not a strategy. It is a liturgy. A door stands open in heaven, and John is caught up into the place where every creature and every crown finds its true position before God - the room from which every act of worship on earth has always taken its pattern.

Around the throne, four living creatures cry without ceasing: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty, who was, and is, and is to come." This is the same acclamation still sung at the heart of the Mass and the Divine Liturgy - the Sanctus, carried unbroken from that room into this one. Twenty-four elders cast down their crowns before the throne, for worship costs something: it means laying down every crown of one's own.

Then a scroll appears, sealed with seven seals, and no one is found worthy to open it - until an elder says: weep no more, for the Lion of Judah has conquered. John looks for a Lion. He sees a Lamb, standing as though slain, and every creature bows to worship him: "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain." Golden bowls of incense are lifted, "which are the prayers of the saints" - the same incense still rising from the censer at every Vespers and every solemn Mass. Once a soul has passed through that door and knelt before that Lamb, there is no return to who one was before.

# Evil awakes - joining the army ({{% bible val="Chapter 6-7" link="rev:6-7" lang="en" %}})

The seals break, one by one, each release loosing something new upon the earth.

First, a white horse gallops forth - a rider crowned, already bent on conquest. It is counterfeit, borrowing the color of the true King's horse to sell a lie. Behind him comes a blood-red horse, and peace is stripped from the earth; a black horse, its rider holding scales for a day's wages against a day's bread; and a horse the color of ash, whose rider's name is Death. Conquest gives way to war, war to famine, famine to death.

Then the fifth seal uncovers not armies but an altar - and beneath it, souls slain for their testimony, crying: how long, O Sovereign Lord, until you judge and avenge our blood? They are given white robes and told to rest a little longer. Even beneath the altar, the posture is worship, not despair.

The sky itself answers: sun black, moon as blood, stars falling like unripe figs, the heavens rolled back like a scroll. The world drowns in what it has brought on itself, and the Church stands amid the wreckage, crying out still.

But before matters worsen: God's own are sealed, claimed as unmistakably his - a multitude no one can number, robed in white, crying: "Salvation belongs to our God, who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb." This is the army of God, arrayed not for battle but for liturgy, fighting with no sword but ceaseless worship. Whatever follows, whose side is chosen is already settled.

# The first strike ({{% bible val="Chapter 8-9" link="rev:8-9" lang="en" %}})

Now it is the Church's turn to shake the world - not with weapons, but the way the Lord always worked: through what looks like weakness. An angel offers much incense with the prayers of the saints on the golden altar before the throne - the same offering still rising wherever an altar is censed today. Its smoke goes up before God; then fire from that altar is hurled down upon the earth, and the trumpets sound.

Food fails. Water turns bitter. Trade collapses. Surely now humanity will look up and ask what is real. It does not. Even as their idols turn against them, stripping away every shred of hope, people cling tighter rather than let go. Suffering alone has never yet been sufficient to change a human heart.

# Reality check ({{% bible val="Chapter 10" link="rev:10" lang="en" %}})

The first strike has failed. Hardship can crack open almost anything - except a heart set on the wrong altar.

So a small scroll appears, and John is told to eat it, sweet upon the tongue and bitter afterward. This is not another disaster. It is a revelation: something true about God himself, about to accomplish what judgment alone could not.

# The great conflict ({{% bible val="Chapter 11-13" link="rev:11-13" lang="en" %}})

Step past the veil, and both worlds appear as they truly are. The temple is measured now - the place where loyalty either shows itself in worship, or does not.

Two witnesses testify with everything they possess, and the world puts them to death, then holds festival over their bodies. For three and a half days, it appears total defeat. Then they rise - precisely the pattern the Church herself is called to live: a victory that walks through suffering and stands on the far side.

Now the same story is drawn back to reveal the frame around it - a very different nativity, played out across the sky. A woman clothed with the sun, twelve stars crowning her head, cries out in labor, while an enormous red dragon waits to devour her child the instant he is born.

The child is born regardless and is caught up to God's own throne before the dragon's jaws can close. War breaks out in heaven, and the dragon is defeated, cast down, overcome by the blood of the Lamb and the plain testimony of those who did not cling to their own lives. Enraged, and running out of time, he turns his fury on the woman and all who belong to her.

Take courage nonetheless: overcoming the dragon was never the Church's task. Christ already accomplished it in heaven - he tried to kill Christ, and failed; tried to keep his place in heaven, and was cast out; tried to destroy Israel, and failed again.

So he reaches for new instruments - two beasts, one ruling by force, one deceiving by wonders: a grotesque parody of the Father, the Son, and the Spirit, close enough to deceive nearly everyone. Yet the counterfeit is already fracturing, because the One it imitates has already triumphed.

# Victory ({{% bible val="Chapter 14-16" link="rev:14-16" lang="en" %}})

Christ already won this at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory ever since - now the enemy's forces scatter like smoke. On a sea of glass mingled with fire, the redeemed stand and sing the song of Moses and of the Lamb. Their only qualification was fidelity kept to the end.

Not all are given to sing. Those who chose safety and sided with the beast now face a far graver cost: two sickles sweep across the earth, one harvest gathered in, one given to judgment. Truth ceases to be theoretical.

Then God's righteous zeal pours out, bowl upon bowl, until the kingdom built on falsehood has nowhere left to stand. The armies of the earth muster for one last stand, at a place whose name has become synonymous with ruin: Armageddon. They come certain of victory. They are already defeated. They simply do not yet know it.

# The final unmasking ({{% bible val="Chapter 17-19" link="rev:17-19" lang="en" %}})

One deception still waits to be unveiled before the story can close - and unveiling it discloses what this has always been about: not merely the defeat of evil, but God binding himself to his people forever, as in a marriage.

Here is the deception: a woman, resplendent, adorned in jewels that rightly belonged to the bride, offering prosperity to any who drink from her cup. Draw back the finery, and the beast stands beneath it, teeth bared. She is no bride. She is a harlot - and many even within the Church have fallen for the performance. Her final act appears flawless. It is not: the very powers the adversary sends to destroy the Church turn and tear the harlot apart instead, his own weapon used against him. The way is finally clear to finish him for good.

At this, heaven breaks into liturgy: a great multitude cries "Alleluia! Salvation and glory and power belong to our God" - the very word still sung at every Easter Vigil - and a voice announces what the whole story has led toward: "The marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready." Blessed are those invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb - the same invitation still extended at every altar rail.

# A new beginning ({{% bible val="Chapter 20-22" link="rev:20-22" lang="en" %}})

It is accomplished, even where it went unseen. All along, while the Church appeared to be losing, she was reigning - not with the kind of power anyone was watching for. Now comes the last reckoning: a final judgment, deciding, once for all, who spends eternity with God, and who has chosen otherwise.

And then: a wedding. A city descends from heaven, arrayed like a bride, and a voice from the throne declares what the whole story has waited to hear: "Behold, the dwelling of God is with humanity. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. Death shall be no more, neither mourning nor crying nor pain, for the former things have passed away."

The city itself nearly exceeds description: walls of jasper, streets of gold like glass, twelve gates each carved from a single pearl. No temple stands anywhere within it, for the Lord God and the Lamb are its temple. Neither sun nor moon is needed: the glory of God is its light, the Lamb its lamp, and its gates are never shut, for no night remains to close them against.

Through its center flows a river, clear as crystal, with a tree on either bank bearing fruit every month, its leaves for the healing of every nation that has ever suffered. The curse - the one that began in a different garden - is undone forever. God's own will see his face, bear his name, and reign with him without end. There the eternal liturgy is fulfilled at last: no longer a shadow glimpsed through incense and candlelight, but the throne room itself, entered fully, forever - the Sanctus sung not by a congregation gathered for an hour, but by a Bride united with her Bridegroom for good, no distance left to close.

And now, having heard the story - go, and keep the feast.
