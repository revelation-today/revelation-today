The first strike has failed. Hardship can crack open almost anything - except a heart set on the wrong altar.

Now a mighty angel descends, wrapped in cloud, a rainbow over his head, his face like the sun and his legs like pillars of fire - the same rainbow that once circled the throne itself, now brought down to earth. He sets one foot on the sea and one on the land, and cries out with a voice like a lion roaring, and seven thunders answer. John is about to write down what they said, but is told instead to seal it up - a reminder that not every mystery of the liturgy is given to be explained; some are only given to be received.

Then the angel holds out a little scroll, opened, and tells John to take it and eat it. It will be sweet as honey in his mouth, but bitter in his stomach once swallowed. John does exactly this - takes the word of God bodily into himself, the way a communicant takes the Host, sweetness first upon the tongue, and only afterward the weight of what has truly been received settling into the whole of a life.

This is not another disaster added to the ones already loosed. It is a revelation in the fullest sense: something true about God himself, offered to be tasted and digested rather than merely observed, about to accomplish through testimony what judgment alone could never accomplish. John is told he must prophesy again, about many peoples and nations and languages and kings - the scroll consumed is not the end of his commission but its renewal, sending him back out to bear witness.

The lesson for every reader is the same one every altar teaches: the word of God is not meant only to be heard, but eaten - taken in until it becomes part of the one who receives it, sweet in the promise, costly in the living out. What looked, moments before, like unrelenting judgment now turns toward testimony, toward two witnesses about to stand and speak in the very next scene.
