John is given a measuring rod and told to measure the temple of God and the altar, and those who worship there - but not the outer court, which is given over to the nations to trample. Step past the veil, and both worlds appear as they truly are. The temple is not a place one earns entry into by merit; it is the place where loyalty either shows itself in worship, or does not.

Two witnesses are given power to prophesy, clothed in sackcloth, standing as the two olive trees and two lampstands before the Lord of the earth - lampstands again, the same image that opened the whole vision, now embodied in flesh and blood. They testify with everything they possess, fire proceeding from their mouths to consume their enemies, until at last the beast that rises from the abyss makes war on them, conquers them, and kills them. Their bodies lie unburied in the street of the great city, and the peoples of the earth refuse them burial, and instead hold festival over the corpses, exchanging gifts, rejoicing that these two tormentors are silenced at last.

For three and a half days, it looks like total defeat - the same interval, broken and incomplete, that runs through so much of this book, always falling short of the fullness God alone holds.

Then the breath of life from God enters them, and they stand on their feet, and great fear falls on those who see them. A voice from heaven says, "Come up here," and they ascend to heaven in a cloud, in full sight of their enemies - a resurrection witnessed rather than merely reported.

This is exactly the pattern the Church herself is called to live: not a victory that bypasses suffering, but one that walks straight through it and stands upright on the far side, in full view of a watching world. What looks, for three and a half days, like the world's final word over faithful witness is never permitted to be the last word. The tomb is never the end of the liturgy; it is only ever the middle of it.
