It falls to every believer to rise and prepare for what is coming. But how does one prepare for something of this magnitude?

The Lord's answer is not a strategy. It is a liturgy. A door stands open in heaven, and John is caught up through it into the room where every creature and every crown finds its true position before God - the very room from which every act of true worship on earth has always taken its pattern, the sanctuary that every altar on earth is only a copy and a shadow of.

Around the throne, four living creatures cry without ceasing, day and night: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty, who was, and is, and is to come." This is the same acclamation still sung at the heart of the Mass and the Divine Liturgy, the Sanctus lifted at every altar where bread and wine are offered - the very words, the very cry, carried unbroken from that throne room into this one. Twenty-four elders fall down before the One seated there and cast their crowns before the throne, for worship costs something: it means laying down every crown of one's own, holding nothing back.

Then a scroll appears, sealed with seven seals, in the right hand of the One on the throne, and no one anywhere is found worthy to open it - until an elder says: weep no longer, for the Lion of the tribe of Judah has conquered. John looks for a lion. He sees a Lamb, standing as though slain, and at the sight every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth falls down to worship him: "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and might and honor and glory and blessing." Golden bowls of incense are lifted in the hands of the elders - "which are the prayers of the saints," John is told plainly - the same incense that still rises from the censer at every Vespers, every solemn Mass, carrying the prayers of the faithful upward exactly as it did before that throne.

He is the hero of the whole story: worthy, when no one else was found worthy, to open what needed opening and to rule what needed ruling. Once a soul has passed through that open door and knelt before that Lamb, there is no return to who one was before.
