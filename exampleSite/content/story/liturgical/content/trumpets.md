Silence falls in heaven for about half an hour - the same hush that still settles over a sanctuary at the elevation, the same stillness kept before the Blessed Sacrament - and into that silence, an angel steps forward with a golden censer, and much incense is given him, to offer with the prayers of all the saints upon the golden altar before the throne. The smoke of the incense rises before God, mingled with those prayers, exactly as it still rises today from every censer swung before an altar at Vespers, at the Divine Liturgy, at Benediction. Then the same angel takes that same censer, fills it with fire from the altar, and hurls it down upon the earth, and thunder and lightning and an earthquake answer.

Now it is the Church's turn to shake the world - not with weapons, but the way the Lord himself always worked: through what looks like weakness. The trumpets sound, one after another, and the earth trembles in answer to the Church's own prayer, carried up as incense and returned as judgment.

Food fails. Water turns bitter. Trade collapses. Security vanishes. Surely now humanity will look up and ask what is real, and turn at last toward the altar it has forsaken.

It does not. Even as their idols turn against them - even as those same idols strip away every last shred of hope, until death itself looks like relief - people cling tighter rather than let go, still bowing before gods of gold and silver and stone that can neither see nor hear nor walk. Suffering alone has never yet been sufficient to change a human heart or turn it back toward true worship. Repentance is not manufactured by hardship; it is a gift received, or refused, before the throne.
