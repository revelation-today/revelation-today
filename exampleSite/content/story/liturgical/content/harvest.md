John looks, and there stands the Lamb on Mount Zion, and with him the hundred and forty-four thousand who bear his name and his Father's name written on their foreheads - the true seal, set against the counterfeit mark just given by the beast. From the throne comes a sound like many waters, like loud thunder, and like harpists playing their harps, and they sing a new song before the throne, before the four living creatures and the elders - a song no one else can learn, for it belongs only to those redeemed from the earth. These are the ones who follow the Lamb wherever he goes, kept undefiled, the firstfruits offered to God and to the Lamb.

Christ has already won this at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory ever since - and now the enemy's forces begin to scatter like smoke. An angel flies overhead with an eternal gospel to proclaim to every nation and tribe and tongue and people, crying with a loud voice: "Fear God and give him glory... and worship him who made heaven and earth, the sea and the springs of water." Worship remains, even here, the dividing line running through the whole of the story. Another voice follows, announcing that Babylon the great has fallen, and a third warns of the fate awaiting any who worship the beast and his image and receive his mark - to be tormented with fire and sulfur in the presence of the holy angels and the Lamb. Here is the call for the endurance of the saints, those who keep the commandments of God and their faith in Jesus.

Not everyone gathered into that harvest is bound for the same end. Two harvests unfold: one Son of Man, seated on a white cloud, swings a sickle and reaps the earth's ripe grain. Then another angel swings a sickle of his own, gathering the earth's grapes into the great winepress of the wrath of God.

Two sickles sweep across the earth - one harvest gathered in for the wedding feast, one given over to judgment. Truth ceases to be theoretical.
