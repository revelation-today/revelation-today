Picture the scene: an old man, alone on a rocky island in the middle of the sea, exiled for refusing to bow before anyone but Christ. It is the Lord's Day - the same day kept holy in every parish and every parish church since - and John, even in banishment, keeps it as best he can. Then a voice behind him breaks like a trumpet, and everything changes.

He turns, and the vision overtakes him entirely: seven lampstands burning gold in the air, recalling the very lampstand that once stood in Israel's sanctuary, and standing among them, one impossible to describe and impossible to look away from. Eyes like fire. Feet like bronze glowing from the furnace. A voice like the roar of many waters, a face too radiant to behold directly, as though one were to stare into the sun. Seven stars rest in his right hand; a sword, sharp on both edges, issues from his mouth.

John falls before him like a dead man - the same posture the whole of Scripture asks of anyone who truly meets this glory, the posture still taken at the threshold of every sanctuary where the Lord is truly present.

Then a hand rests on his shoulder, and words undo the fear before it can finish forming: "Do not be afraid. I am the First and the Last, the Living One. I died - and behold, I am alive forever. And I hold the keys of Death and of Hades."

The truth breaks like sunrise: the kingdom long awaited has already arrived, and every believer - king and priest together - has been given a share in Christ's own priesthood, the very priesthood exercised at every altar where his sacrifice is proclaimed.

Before anything else may be said, one thing must be: this vision reveals what the Church exists to be - a lampstand kept alight not by her own strength, but sustained by the One who stands among the lamps. Nothing more is required of her; nothing less will do.

So: is she found ready, her lamp still burning, when he comes to walk among the lampstands again?
