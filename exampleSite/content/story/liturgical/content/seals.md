The seals break, one by one, each release loosing something new upon the earth - and even here, in the unsealing of catastrophe, the scene remains the throne room: nothing is loosed except what the Lamb himself unseals.

First, a white horse gallops forth - a rider armed with a bow, crowned, already bent on conquest. For a moment it resembles victory. It is counterfeit: Christ is not the only claimant to human worship, and this rider borrows the color of the true King's horse to sell a lie. Strip the costume away, and violence alone remains. Behind the white horse comes a blood-red one, and peace is stripped from the earth like a scab, neighbor turning on neighbor. Behind the red horse, a black one, its rider holding a pair of scales - a day's wages for a day's bread, while oil and wine are kept safe for those who can still afford them. Behind the black horse, one the color of ash, and its rider's name is Death, with the grave riding close behind.

Conquest gives way to war, war to famine, famine to death.

Then the fifth seal breaks, and what it uncovers is not an army but an altar - the same altar beneath which incense and sacrifice have burned throughout the whole vision - and beneath it, the souls of those slain for their testimony, crying with one voice: how long, O Sovereign Lord, holy and true, until you judge the earth and avenge our blood? Each is given a white robe, the same robe worn by every soul who stands redeemed before the throne, and told to rest a little longer. Even here, beneath the altar, the posture remains worship, not despair - a martyrdom offered up rather than merely endured.

The sky itself answers next: the sun turns black as sackcloth, the moon becomes as blood, the stars fall like unripe figs shaken loose in a gale, and the heavens are rolled back like a scroll. The world already drowns in what it has brought upon itself, and the Church stands amid the wreckage, crying out still - not abandoning the liturgy even here, but carrying it, like incense, straight into the ruin.
