Before the bowls are ever poured, John sees another sight in heaven: those who conquered the beast and its image and the number of its name, standing beside a sea of glass mingled with fire, holding harps of God, and singing the song of Moses, the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb - two songs become one, the whole of salvation history folded into a single hymn: "Great and amazing are your deeds, O Lord God the Almighty! Just and true are your ways, O King of the nations! Who will not fear you, O Lord, and glorify your name? For you alone are holy." Every nation will come and worship before God, for his righteous acts have been revealed.

Then the temple of the tent of witness in heaven is opened, and out of it come seven angels bearing seven golden bowls full of the wrath of God, and the temple is filled with smoke from the glory of God and from his power, so that no one can enter it until the seven plagues are finished - the sanctuary itself given over, for a time, to judgment rather than intercession.

Now God's righteous zeal pours out as something long withheld and finally released, bowl after bowl, wave after wave, until the kingdom built on falsehood has nowhere left to stand. Every bowl lands exactly where it is aimed: on false worship, on cruelty shown toward God's people, on hearts too hardened to break even now. The angel of the waters declares it just: those who shed the blood of saints and prophets have been given blood to drink, for they deserve it. And the altar itself answers: "Yes, Lord God the Almighty, true and just are your judgments" - the altar, which has carried the martyrs' prayers since the fifth seal, now vindicating them at last.

The armies of the earth gather for one last stand, at a place whose name has become a byword for disaster: Armageddon. They come certain of victory, gathered by demonic spirits performing signs, in one final grotesque liturgy raised against the true one.

They are already finished. They simply do not yet know it.
