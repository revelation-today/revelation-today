One deception still waits to be unveiled before the story can close - and unveiling it discloses what this has always been about: not merely the defeat of evil, but God binding himself to his people forever, as in a marriage.

Here is the deception: a woman, resplendent, seated on a scarlet beast, draped in purple and scarlet, adorned in gold and jewels and pearls that rightly belonged to the bride, holding a golden cup full of abominations, offering intoxication to any who will drink from it. She is named on her forehead: Babylon the great, mother of harlots and of earth's abominations. Draw back the finery, though, and the beast stands beneath it, teeth bared. She is no bride. She is a harlot, drunk on the blood of the saints and the martyrs of Jesus - and many even within the visible Church have been seduced by the performance, mistaking her splendor for glory.

Her final act appears flawless. It is not. The very powers the adversary sends to destroy the Church turn instead and tear the harlot apart, make her desolate and naked, devour her flesh, and burn her up with fire - his own weapon, turned against him. Heaven rejoices over her fall: "Fallen, fallen is Babylon the great," and a voice cries, "Come out of her, my people, lest you take part in her sins."

The way is finally clear to finish him for good.

At this, heaven itself breaks into liturgy: a great multitude cries "Alleluia! Salvation and glory and power belong to our God" - the very word still sung at every Easter Vigil and every Paschal liturgy, unbroken from that hour to this - and the twenty-four elders and four living creatures fall down and worship God, saying, "Amen. Alleluia!" A voice from the throne calls all his servants to praise him, and a great multitude answers like the roar of many waters and mighty peals of thunder: "Alleluia! For the Lord our God the Almighty reigns. Let us rejoice and be glad and give him the glory, for the marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready." Blessed are those invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb - the same invitation extended, still, at every altar rail, in the words that echo it even now: "Blessed are those called to the supper of the Lamb."
