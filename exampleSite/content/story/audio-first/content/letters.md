Seven churches. Seven letters. Seven mirrors, each one held up to a very different congregation.

Ephesus works itself to exhaustion. Every doctrine is correct. Every duty is done. But somewhere along the way, love has slipped through her fingers. Without love, all that labor amounts to nothing. Better, Jesus says, that this church did not exist at all than to go on like this.

Smyrna could not be more different. She is poor. She is hated. She is squeezed on every side by a hostile city. And she is one of only two churches Jesus has nothing but praise for.

Pergamum stands her ground in the shadow of Satan's own throne. Some die for it. And yet, inside her walls, false teaching has slipped in unchallenged, quietly leading people away.

In Thyatira, the rot starts at the top. The leaders themselves have gone astray.

Sardis looks alive from the outside. Underneath, she is already dead. She believes she can manage just fine without God — and she is failing at nearly everything because of it.

Philadelphia is the other church Jesus praises without reservation. She is small. She is worn down. She has nothing left in the bank. And still, she is faithful.

Then there is Laodicea. Lukewarm. Self-satisfied. Blind to how poor she really is. She is the only church that receives no praise at all. And yet even here, Jesus does not walk away. He knocks. He warns. He still wants in.
