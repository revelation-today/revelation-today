And then: a wedding. A city comes down out of heaven, dressed like a bride on her wedding day, and a voice from the throne says what everyone has been waiting the whole story to hear: "Look - God's home is now among his people. He will live with them. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death, no more mourning, no more crying, no more pain, because all of that has already passed away."

The city itself is almost too much to describe: walls of jasper, streets of gold so pure it looks like glass, foundations set with every color of jewel, twelve gates and each one carved from a single pearl. There is no temple anywhere in it, because God himself, and the Lamb, are the temple now - the access to God that used to belong to one priest, on one day, in one room, now the shared and permanent condition of everyone who belongs. There is no sun and no moon, because they are simply not needed anymore: the glory of God is the light, the Lamb is the lamp, and the gates never close because there is no more night left to shut them against.

Notice, too, who is named as walking through those open gates: the kings of the earth, bringing their glory and honor into the city. Not erased. Not punished forever for every crown they once wore. Brought home - their splendor, at last, no longer built on anyone's back, no longer extracted from anyone's suffering, simply given, freely, to the city where it always belonged. Even the language of nations and kings, so often a shorthand in this book for empire and its violence, gets folded here into something reconciled rather than merely destroyed.

Through the middle of it runs a river, clear as crystal, flowing straight from the throne of God, with a tree on either bank bearing fruit every month, and leaves for healing every nation that ever bled. Not just the nations that were faithful. Every nation that ever bled - which is to say, every nation, since none of them escaped this book's story without wounds of some kind, given or received. The curse - the one that started all the way back in a different garden - is gone for good. God's people will see his face at last, wear his name, and reign with him forever.

The bride and the groom, together at last, for good. God himself moves in with his people - no more distance left to close.

And now that you know the story - go and live like you believe it: that the powerless are not forgotten, that oppression does not get the last word, and that the justice this whole book has been aching for, since the very first cry from under the altar, is not a fantasy but a promise already being kept.
