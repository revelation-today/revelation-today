Before it gets any worse, something happens: God's people are sealed. Marked. Claimed, in the middle of the storm, as unmistakably his.

Notice the timing. The sealing does not happen after the horsemen have finished riding, as a reward for having survived them - it happens in the middle of the wreckage, while conquest and war and famine and death are still doing their work. Belonging to God has never been a promise of exemption from the world's violence. It is a promise that the violence does not get to define who you ultimately are, or whose you ultimately are.

This is the army of God - though it fights like no army you have ever seen. A hundred and forty-four thousand, sealed from every tribe, a number too neat and too complete to be a headcount and too clearly meant, instead, as a claim: nobody who belongs to God is missed, overlooked, or left off the list, however anonymous or powerless they were in the eyes of the systems that ran the world around them. No swords, no borrowed violence, just relentless worship and a loyalty that outlasts everything the enemy can throw at it. That is a strange way to describe an army, and it is meant to be. This army does not conscript, does not conquer territory, and does not answer violence with more of the same. Its only weapon is a refusal to stop worshiping the Lamb instead of the beast, no matter what that refusal costs.

Then the scene widens, and the sealed hundred and forty-four thousand turn out to be only the beginning: a multitude no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people, and language, standing before the throne in white robes, palm branches in hand - the international, uncountable company of everyone who ever refused to let empire have the last claim on their allegiance. They have come, John is told, out of the great tribulation, and God will wipe every tear from their eyes.

Whatever comes next, one thing is already settled: you know whose side you are on. And side-taking here is not tribal or nationalistic - it cuts across every tribe and tongue the old world used to divide people, gathering them instead around one thing they share: they would not bow, and now they will never thirst or hunger or weep again.
