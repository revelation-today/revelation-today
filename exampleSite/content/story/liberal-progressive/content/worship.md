So it falls to you - to all of us - to get up and get ready for what is coming. But how do you prepare for something this big?

Jesus says: look up. The answer is not a strategy - it is a throne room. Heaven opens, and there it is: the place where everyone and everything finds its true position before God. The door stands open, always.

Do not mistake worship for an easy fix, though. It costs something. It means laying your own crown down and giving God every part of your life, without exception. Twenty-four elders do exactly that, and four strange living creatures cry "holy" without pause, and the whole scene is designed to relativize every other throne a reader might be tempted to bow toward - Rome's included. Worship, in this vision, is not a private devotional feeling. It is a rival claim on your ultimate loyalty, made in a world that has already asked for that loyalty and calls it civic duty.

And in that room, one figure stands out from all the rest: a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain. Everyone in the throne room has been waiting for someone worthy to open the scroll that will set history moving toward its resolution - a lion is announced, the fiercest image the tradition has - and what walks forward instead is a slaughtered animal, still bearing its wound, somehow standing.

That substitution is the whole gospel in miniature, and it is worth pausing on rather than rushing past. Power, here, does not look like the power Rome trained its subjects to recognize and fear. It looks like a victim who did not stay a victim. He is the hero of this whole story - worthy, when no one else was found worthy, to open what needed opening and rule what needed ruling - precisely because he absorbed violence rather than dealt it, and came out the other side alive.

Once you follow him through that door, there is no going back to who you were before. The throne room does not just describe a hierarchy above the world's hierarchies. It reorders what counts as strength at all - and every hard chapter that follows, every plague and battle and judgment, has to be read back through this Lamb, standing, or it will be misread entirely.
