Step past the curtain, and you see both worlds as they truly are. The temple is not a place you earn your way into - it is where your loyalty either shows itself, or does not. John is told to measure it, but only the inner sanctuary; the outer court is handed over to be trampled for forty-two months. Even the sacred can be occupied. That is simply the world the church lives in, and this vision does not pretend otherwise.

Two witnesses testify with everything they have - dressed in the rough clothing of mourners, doing exactly what the prophets before them did: naming what is wrong, refusing to be silenced, absorbing the world's hostility rather than returning it. And the world kills them for it, then throws a party over the bodies, refusing them even burial, broadcasting the footage, so to speak, to every nation, tribe, and language. For three and a half days, it looks like total defeat, and worse than defeat - it looks like proof that speaking truth to power simply gets you killed and mocked, with nothing to show for it.

Then they stand up. Breath enters them again in full view of their killers, and a voice from heaven calls them home, and the same people who celebrated their deaths are terrified. That is exactly the pattern the Church herself is asked to live: not a victory that skips over suffering, engineered to avoid the cost altogether, but one that walks straight through it and comes out the other side standing.

It matters that this reversal is public, staged in the same square where the bodies had been left on display. Empire's power depends on making resistance look pointless - on making sure everyone watching learns the lesson that opposition ends in a corpse and a crowd's laughter. This vision insists that the lesson is a lie, even when, for three and a half days, it looks completely true. The witnesses do not win by escaping death. They win by not staying dead, and by doing it in front of the very people who thought they had settled the matter for good. Whatever it costs to tell the truth in a world arranged against it, this chapter's whole argument is that the cost is not the end of the story - it is, at most, three and a half days of it.
