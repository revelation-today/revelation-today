Jesus already won it at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory ever since - and now the enemy's forces scatter like smoke. On a sea of glass and fire, the faithful sing, harps in hand, the song of Moses and the song of the Lamb together - the ancient song of people who watched an empire's army drown behind them, and the new song of people who watched an empire's violence exhaust itself against the innocent. Their only qualification was staying loyal, not achieving anything spectacular.

Not everyone gets to sing. The ones who played it safe and sided with the beast to avoid the cost now face a far heavier one. An angel flies overhead announcing an eternal gospel to every nation, tribe, language, and people - one last invitation, extended as widely and as publicly as the beast's own propaganda ever was - before announcing, in the same breath, that Babylon has fallen.

Two sickles swing across the earth. One harvest gathers in grain, ready and ripe; one harvest gathers grapes, thrown into the great winepress of God's wrath, trodden until blood flows out - vivid, disturbing imagery, deliberately drawn from the prophets' own oldest picture of a nation's violence finally coming due. This is the moment truth stops being theoretical. Every choice this book has tracked - worship the Lamb or worship the beast, side with the persecuted or side with the persecutor, hold the tension of a costly loyalty or trade it for an easier compromise - resolves here into an actual harvest, an actual reckoning, with actual consequences that can no longer be deferred or debated.

It would misread this scene badly to treat the winepress as God suddenly discovering a taste for violence he had, until now, restrained. The wrath poured out here is the same wrath that began under the altar's cry for justice, the same wrath the fifth seal's murdered saints have been waiting for since chapter six. What looks, out of context, like an arbitrary and brutal image is, read in sequence, the answer finally arriving to a question this book has been holding open since its earliest pages: how long, O Lord, until the blood of the faithful is avenged? The harvest is that answer, delivered at last, to people who have been waiting a very long time to hear it.
