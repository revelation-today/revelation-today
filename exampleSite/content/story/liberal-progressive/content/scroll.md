The first strike has failed. Hard times can crack open just about anything - except a heart set on the wrong thing. The trumpets have done their worst, and the world, if anything, has hardened rather than softened. It is worth admitting plainly what that means: judgment, on its own, has a ceiling. It can expose what is wrong. It cannot, by itself, make anyone love what is right.

So a scroll appears, carried by an angel wrapped in cloud, a rainbow over his head, one foot on the sea and one on the land - claiming, quietly, the whole world as his. John is told to eat it, and something in it will change everything, though it will take time to digest.

This is not another disaster. It is a revelation: something true about God himself, sweet in the mouth and hard to swallow, about to break through where judgment alone could not. Sweet, because it is the assurance that the story is still moving toward the ending it was always headed for - that the delay is not abandonment. Hard to swallow, because what comes next asks something costly of John and of the church he represents: not another display of divine power, but a summons to keep speaking the truth to nations and kings, in a world that has already shown it would rather harden than repent.

That is the quiet turn this short chapter makes, easy to miss between larger scenes. The book does not now pivot to a bigger, more devastating round of plagues in the hope that more suffering will finally succeed where less suffering failed. It pivots to testimony - to witnesses who will say what is true, at personal cost, and trust that truth-telling, sustained over time, can do something raw catastrophe cannot: reach the part of a person that mere hardship never touches. The next chapter will show exactly what that testimony costs, and exactly how the world responds to it. For now, it is enough to notice that the story's center of gravity has shifted from wrath poured out to witness given - and that the second is, if anything, the harder assignment.
