This is Revelation told as one continuous story - the same events, the same images, the same hard chapters as any other telling - but read through the lens the book itself supplies: a persecuted community's cry for justice, not a spectator's taste for violence. Where the plagues fall and the armies gather, this version pauses just long enough to ask what the text is actually naming - empire's own violence, turned back on itself; exploitation, finally priced correctly - before the story moves on.

Nothing here is softened and nothing is added that isn't already implicit in the text. The wrath stays. The judgment stays. What changes is the frame: this is a story about the powerless being vindicated, not a God losing his temper at the world he made.

**Best for:** readers who find the justice and liberation themes in Revelation compelling, but who need the hard chapters read aloud in a voice that takes both the suffering and the reckoning seriously.

## Production notes

At the hardest moments - the four horsemen, the trumpet plagues, the bowls, the beast's violence, the harlot's fall - this version weaves a brief interpretive aside directly into the narrative voice, never as a footnote or a disclaimer set apart from the story. The aside names what the master narrative already implies: that the wrath described here is, in its own words, "a relentless, compassionate refusal to let the world's rebellion go unanswered," not an arbitrary tantrum, and that Revelation's violence is consistently the violence empire has already inflicted, now shown for what it is and finally exhausting itself. No event, claim, or theological commitment in the master narrative has been removed or reversed - only framed, inline, for a reader who needs to hear the "why" in the same breath as the "what."
