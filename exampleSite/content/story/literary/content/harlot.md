One deception still waits to be unmasked before the story can end, and unmasking it reveals what this was always about: not simply defeating evil, but God binding himself to his people forever, like a marriage - the oldest metaphor there is for the oldest covenant there is, now paid off in full.

Here is the deception: a woman seated on many waters, dressed in purple and scarlet, glittering with gold and jewels and pearls, a golden cup in her hand full of abominations and the impurities of her fornication, and on her forehead a name written, a mystery: Babylon the great, mother of prostitutes and of earth's abominations - the same charge once brought against a city called a whoring woman, decked in embroidered linen and oil and honey that were never hers to begin with, trading on gifts she had turned into wages. Kings have committed fornication with her; the merchants of the earth have grown rich from the power of her luxury.

Pull the purple back, though, and there is the beast underneath, seven heads and ten horns, and she rides it as though she rode a servant rather than a monster she cannot control. She is no bride. She is a harlot, drunk not with wine but with the blood of the saints and the blood of the martyrs of Jesus - and plenty inside the Church itself have fallen for the act, the way a people were warned, again and again, not to go whoring after gods that were never gods.

Her final performance looks flawless: I sit as a queen, she says, I am no widow, I will never see grief - the very words once put in the mouth of a virgin daughter of Babylon just before her fall, sit in the dust, uncover your legs, your nakedness will be exposed. It happens exactly that way. The ten horns and the beast that carried her turn instead and hate the harlot, and make her desolate and naked, and devour her flesh and burn her up with fire - the devil's own weapon, turned at last against its master, exactly the way one power has always eventually devoured the last one it used.

Then a marriage supper is announced, the way a feast on a mountain was once promised for all peoples, rich food and well-aged wine, and death itself swallowed up forever. Blessed are those invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb.

The road is finally clear to finish him for good.
