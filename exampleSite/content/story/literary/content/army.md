Before it gets any worse, something happens: four angels hold back four winds at the four corners of the earth, and nothing is permitted to move until the servants of God are sealed.

The seal falls on the forehead, the way a mark was once drawn on the foreheads of those who sighed and groaned over the wrongs done in a doomed city, so that the ones sent to destroy would pass by everyone bearing it and touch no one else. It is the same logic that once painted blood across a doorframe on a night when a destroyer walked a whole land street by street: the judgment does not stop, but it does not fall where the mark is. A hundred and forty-four thousand are named, sealed, tribe by tribe, the old twelve called out in full roll call, as though a scattered people were being counted back into being one people again.

Then the number breaks open into something no census could hold: a multitude no one can number, out of every nation and tribe and people and tongue, standing before the throne and before the Lamb, robed in white, palm branches in their hands, crying out with a loud voice - salvation belongs to our God - the way branches were once cut and carried and a whole people camped for seven days remembering a wilderness they had walked through and survived. These are the ones who have come out of the great tribulation, and they have washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb - stained white, cleansed by the very thing that should have ruined them.

This is the army of God, though it fights like no army the earth has ever fielded. No swords are issued, no borrowed violence, no counterfeit crown to chase. What outlasts everything the enemy can throw at it is worship and nothing else: they will hunger no more, neither thirst any more, for the Lamb will be their shepherd and will guide them to springs of the water of life, and God will wipe away every tear from their eyes - the last line of an older promise about a highway through a desert and a God who feeds his flock like a shepherd, now spoken not about the road home but about the home itself.

Whatever comes next, one thing is already settled: you know whose you are, and whose mark you carry.
