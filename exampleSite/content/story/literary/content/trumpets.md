Now it is the Church's turn to shake the world - not with weapons, but the way it was always done: through what looks like weakness. An angel takes a censer, fills it with fire from the altar, and prayer rises with the smoke of incense from under the hands of the saints, and the trumpets are handed out, and the earth begins to tremble the way it once trembled at the base of a smoking mountain.

Hail and fire mixed with blood fall on the earth, the way hail once fell mixed with fire on a land that would not let a people go. A mountain of flame is thrown into the sea, and the sea turns to blood, and every ship in it dies, the way a river once turned to blood and every fish in it died and the water could not be drunk. A star falls burning onto the rivers and the springs, and its name is Wormwood, and the waters turn bitter, the way water once was bitter at a place named for its bitterness before a tree was thrown in to sweeten it - no tree is thrown in this time. A third of the sun is struck, a third of the moon, a third of the stars, and darkness falls that can almost be felt with the hand, the way darkness once could be felt for three days over a whole land while light stayed where God's own people were.

Surely now people will finally look up and ask what is real.

They do not. Smoke rises from the shaft of the abyss like the smoke of a great furnace, and out of the smoke come locusts - not the kind that once stripped a land bare in a single day, but locusts like horses armored for battle, crowned, faced like men, haired like women, teeth like lions, a scorpion's sting in the tail, given power to torment but not to kill, for five months, anyone who does not carry God's seal on the forehead. Four angels are loosed at the great river, kept ready for this very hour, and cavalry beyond counting comes breathing fire and sulfur and smoke.

Even as their idols turn on them - the gold, the silver, the bronze, the stone, the wood that can neither see nor hear nor walk - even as those idols strip away every shred of hope until death starts to look like relief, people cling tighter, not looser. Suffering alone, however total, has never yet been enough to change a human heart bent on the wrong thing.
