Seven churches, seven letters, seven oracles, each one opening the way the old oracles opened - I know, I know, I know - as though the one walking among the lampstands were reading a covenant lawsuit against a bride he still calls by name.

Ephesus has worked itself past exhaustion - sound in every doctrine, tireless in every duty - and has let go, without quite noticing, of the love it had at first. Without it the labor is chaff blown off a threshing floor. Better, he says, that this lamp had never been lit than go on guttering like this; but to the one who conquers, he will give fruit from the tree that once stood in the middle of a garden, guarded and lost.

Smyrna is poor, slandered by a synagogue that calls itself something it is not, and she is one of only two churches to hear nothing from him but praise. Be faithful unto death, and I will give you the crown of life - hidden manna, kept in a jar before the testimony for a hungrier generation, and a white stone with a new name, the way a whole people once crossed into a land and were called by a name they had not carried before.

Pergamum keeps its ground in the very seat where the accuser is enthroned. Some die there for it, and still a teaching has slipped in unopposed, food offered to idols, a prophet who taught a king to set a stumbling block before his own people at Peor long ago and is teaching it again. In Thyatira the rot begins at the top: a prophetess, self-styled, leading the flock the way a queen once led a king's people into worship that was not worship.

Sardis has a name for being alive. Underneath she is dry bones in a valley, certain she can manage without breath from anywhere else, and failing at nearly everything for the certainty of it. Philadelphia is the other church without a single reproof - small, spent, and given a door no one can shut, a key of David that opens what no one shuts and shuts what no one opens.

Then Laodicea: neither cold nor hot, rich in her own accounting and destitute in his, blind and calling it sight, the only church that hears no commendation at all. Even here he has not walked away. He stands at the door and knocks, counsels her to buy gold refined in the fire, and asks only to come in and eat with her, as at the oldest table there ever was.
