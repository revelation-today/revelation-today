Jesus already won it at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory ever since. Now the Lamb stands on Mount Zion, and with him a hundred and forty-four thousand who have his name and his Father's name written on their foreheads, singing a song no one else can learn, the sound of many waters and of loud thunder and of harpists playing on their harps - the same sound that once filled a temple until the priests could not stand to minister.

An angel flies overhead with an eternal gospel to proclaim, and behind him another cries: Babylon is fallen, is fallen, the great city, the way a taunt song was once sung over a fallen king of Babylon at the edge of the pit: how you are fallen. A third angel warns of the cup of God's wrath, poured full strength into the cup of his anger - the same cup a whole line of nations was once made to drink from, staggering and vomiting, one after another, starting from the city called by God's own name.

Then a voice from heaven: blessed are the dead who die in the Lord from now on. Yes, says the Spirit, they will rest from their labors, for their deeds follow them - rest, the way the seventh day was always meant to be rest, and never was, until now.

One like a son of man sits on a white cloud, a golden crown on his head, a sharp sickle in his hand, and an angel calls out: put in your sickle and reap, for the hour to reap has come, for the harvest of the earth is fully ripe - the very words once sung over grapes crushed for wine and grain gathered into barns, put now in the mouth of an angel instead of a farmer.

Not everyone gathered in this harvest is gathered for joy. A second angel swings a sharp sickle of his own and gathers the clusters from the vine of the earth, and throws them into the great winepress of the wrath of God, and the winepress is trodden outside the city, and blood flows from the press as high as a horse's bridle - the same image that once described one figure coming from Edom, garments stained red, treading the winepress alone, with no one from the peoples to help him, because in his fury he trampled them, and their lifeblood spattered his clothing.

Truth stops being theoretical. The only qualification, for those still singing, was refusing to let go.
