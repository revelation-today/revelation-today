Here is the story of Revelation, start to finish, in the register it was written for those who already carry the older scriptures by heart. No footnotes. Just the story.

# In the beginning

In the beginning the deep lay formless, darkness on its face, until breath moved over the waters and light tore free from dark at a word. God knelt in the dust, shaped a man with his own hands, breathed into him the breath of life, and set him in a garden eastward, dominion over all that breathed. One thing alone was not good: the man was alone. God built the woman from his rib, and for one hour they stood unashamed.

It did not last. Every tree was theirs, save one, and that was the one they wanted. He reached for what had been withheld, and the one who had walked with God in the cool of the day now hid among the trees. Iniquity was found even in a guardian once blameless there; now the man was driven out by cherubim and a sword turning every way, the ground cursed for his sake, thorn where there had been fruit.

His children built towers reaching at heaven in the wreckage. God called one man to a land he would only be shown, and it was counted to him as righteousness - even on a mountain, knife raised over the promised son, until a ram stood caught in the thicket.

Out of him grew a nation, and Israel carried the promise like fire in a jar of clay - and drifted as Adam had drifted, carried at last to a land whose tongue they could not understand, exiles cast out of exile. But God does not close his hand for good: a shoot would rise from a stump long thought dead, government on a single shoulder, of his peace no end.

# New beginnings ({{% bible val="Chapter 1" link="rev:1" lang="en" %}})

An old man alone on a rocky island, banished for a testimony he would not take back - until a voice cracks behind him like a trumpet and nothing is ordinary again.

He turns: seven lamps of gold burning in the air, and among them one whose hair is white as wool, white as snow, eyes like flaming torches, feet like bronze glowing in the furnace, a voice like many waters, a sword from his mouth, his face the sun in its strength.

John falls as a dead man falls. Then a hand, and a voice that unmakes the fear before it forms: "Do not be afraid. I am the first and the last, the living one. I died, and see - I am alive forever, and I hold the keys of Death and of Sheol."

The kingdom promised has arrived, and you have been made a kingdom, priests to his God. This vision goes to the marrow of what the Church exists for - to burn gold as those lampstands, kept alight by the one who stands among them. So: is she ready?

# A grand disaster ({{% bible val="Chapter 2-3" link="rev:2-3" lang="en" %}})

Seven churches, seven letters, seven mirrors.

Ephesus has worked itself past exhaustion and let go, without noticing, of the love it had at first. Better, says the one who walks among the lampstands, that this lamp had never been lit than go on guttering like this.

Smyrna is poor, slandered, pressed by a synagogue that calls itself something it is not - one of only two churches to hear nothing from him but praise: be faithful unto death, and I will give you the crown of life.

Pergamum keeps its ground where the accuser is enthroned; some die for it, and still a teaching has slipped in unopposed. In Thyatira the rot begins at the top, a prophetess leading them into what looks like worship and is something else.

Sardis has a name for being alive; underneath she is a whitened tomb. Philadelphia is the other church without a single reproof - small, spent, and it is enough; a door stands open before her that no one can shut.

Then Laodicea: neither cold nor hot, rich in her own accounting and destitute in his, blind and calling it sight. Even here he has not turned away - he knocks, asking only to come in and eat with her.

# Guidance is ahead ({{% bible val="Chapter 4-5" link="rev:4-5" lang="en" %}})

How does anyone make ready for what is coming? Look up: a door standing open in heaven, a throne, and around it everything finding, at last, its true place.

Do not mistake this room for comfort. Twenty-four elders lay their own crowns down before it. Four living creatures ring the throne, thick with eyes, never resting, and before it a sea of glass like crystal. In the middle stands a Lamb, as though slaughtered, standing though slain - worthy, when none could be found worthy, to break what needs breaking. Follow him through that door and there is no way back.

# Evil awakes - you need to join the army ({{% bible val="Chapter 6-7" link="rev:6-7" lang="en" %}})

The seals break one after another, and each lets something off its chain.

First, a white horse, a rider crowned for conquest - a counterfeit, borrowing the true King's color to sell the lie beneath it. Then red, and peace torn off the earth like a scab. Then black, its rider weighing out a day's wage for a day's bread. Then ash, its rider's name Death, Sheol at his heel. Conquest becomes war, war famine, famine the grave.

Then a seal breaks on an altar, and beneath it the souls of the slaughtered cry with one voice that reaches back to the first blood spilled on the ground - how long, Master, before you avenge our blood? They are given white robes, told to rest a little longer.

The sky answers: sun black as sackcloth, moon like blood, stars falling as a fig tree drops its late figs in a great wind, the sky rolling back like a scroll wound shut.

But first: the servants of God are sealed on their foreheads, marked as those over whom no destroyer has any claim, the way a mark once turned a plague aside from a doorway. This is the army of God - a multitude no one can number, robes washed white, worship instead of weapons. You know whose you are.

# The first strike ({{% bible val="Chapter 8-9" link="rev:8-9" lang="en" %}})

Now it is the Church's turn to shake the world, not with iron but the way he always moved - through what looks like weakness. Prayer rises like incense, trumpets sound, and the earth trembles.

Hail and fire mixed with blood burn a third of the earth, a third of the sea to blood, a third of the sun struck dark, a third of the moon, a third of the stars. A star falls onto the rivers, its name Wormwood, and the waters turn bitter, and darkness falls as darkness once could be felt with the hand.

Surely now someone asks what is real. No one does. Locusts rise from the smoke of the abyss like horses armored for battle, teeth like lions, tormenting whoever does not carry God's seal. Still the rest do not repent of the work of their own hands - idols of gold and stone that can neither see nor hear nor walk. Even with dying starting to look like relief, the human heart holds on tighter, not looser.

# Reality check ({{% bible val="Chapter 10" link="rev:10" lang="en" %}})

The first strike has failed. Hard seasons crack open almost anything in a man - except a heart already given to the wrong thing.

A mighty angel comes down wrapped in cloud, a rainbow over his head, legs like pillars of fire, a little scroll open in his hand. John is told to eat it: sweet as honey in the mouth, bitter once it is down. Not one more disaster - a revelation of who God actually is.

# The big clash ({{% bible val="Chapter 11-13" link="rev:11-13" lang="en" %}})

A reed like a measuring rod is handed over: measure the temple and those who worship there - it was never a place you earn passage into; it is where loyalty declares itself, or does not.

Two witnesses prophesy in sackcloth twelve hundred and sixty days, two olive trees, two lampstands before the Lord of the whole earth, power to shut the sky, to turn water to blood, to strike the earth with every plague. When their testimony is finished, the beast from the abyss kills them, their bodies unburied while the peoples of the earth celebrate. Three and a half days it looks like the end. Then breath from God enters them, and a voice says, Come up here, and they rise in a cloud, their enemies watching - not a victory that skips the grave, but one that walks through it and stands again.

The same story is told a second time, pulled back to its frame: a woman clothed with the sun, moon underfoot, a crown of twelve stars, crying out in labor. A great red dragon, seven heads, ten horns, its tail sweeping a third of the stars down to earth, waits to devour the child at birth. She bears a son who will shepherd every nation with a rod of iron, and before the dragon's mouth can close, the child is caught up to God's throne. War breaks out in heaven, and the dragon is thrown down - that ancient serpent, deceiver of the whole world - beaten by the blood of the Lamb and the testimony of those who loved not their own lives even to death. Furious, knowing his time is short, he turns on the rest of her offspring.

Take heart: bringing the dragon down was never yours; it was finished before it reached you. He struck at the child and missed, fought for his place and lost it, set himself against a nation carried once on eagles' wings, and failed there too.

So he reaches for two beasts to fight in his place - one from the sea, crowned with blasphemy, shaped like every beast that has climbed from that water to devour a people; one from the earth, gentle-horned as a lamb, speaking like the dragon. A grotesque liturgy, close enough to fool nearly everyone - but the seams show. It conquers only by the sword, dazzles only by sign, and builds nothing that will outlast the one it copies, already won for good.

# Victory ({{% bible val="Chapter 14-16" link="rev:14-16" lang="en" %}})

He won it at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory since, and now the enemy's gathered strength scatters like smoke off a dying fire. On a sea of glass mixed with fire the redeemed sing the song of Moses and the song of the Lamb - their only qualification was refusing to let go.

Not everyone stands on that shore. Those who took the mark to spare themselves the cost meet a heavier one. Two sickles pass over the ripened earth: one gathers grain into the barn, one gathers grape-clusters into the winepress of God's fury, trodden until blood flows from the press.

Then wrath, held back so long it seems almost gentle by comparison, is poured out - bowl after bowl landing where it is aimed: on false worship, on the throne of the beast, on hearts too hard to break, on the great river dried to make a road for kings from the sunrise. Frog-spirits gather the kings of the earth to the place called, in that tongue, Armageddon. They come certain of the outcome. They are already finished. They do not yet know it.

# The ultimate battle ({{% bible val="Chapter 17-19" link="rev:17-19" lang="en" %}})

One last deception waits to be stripped bare, showing what this was always about - not simply the defeat of evil, but a marriage: God binding himself, for good, to a people of his own.

A woman seated on many waters, dressed in purple and scarlet, glittering with gold and pearls never rightly hers, a golden cup in her hand full of the filth of her fornications, on her forehead a name, a mystery: Babylon the great, mother of every harlotry the earth has practiced. Pull the purple back, and there is the scarlet beast underneath, and she rides it the way a rider believes she rides a servant.

Her final act looks flawless, a queen forever, no widow's grief to fear. It is not. The ten horns and the beast that carried her turn on her instead, strip her and burn her with fire - the very weapon sharpened for the saints, unsheathed against its own mistress. Kings who fattened themselves on her weep from a distance; merchants weep because no one buys their cargo anymore. In a single hour it is undone.

Heaven cries Hallelujah, for his judgments are true and just, and the road stands open, at last, to finish him for good.

# A new beginning ({{% bible val="Chapter 20-22" link="rev:20-22" lang="en" %}})

It is finished, even in the seasons no one saw it happening. All the while it looked as though the Church only suffered, she reigned. Now the ancient enemy is loosed once more, gathering his armies, numerous as sand along the shore - Gog and Magog, from the four corners of the earth - and fire falls from heaven and ends it.

Then the great white throne, earth and sky fleeing his face with no place left to be found. Books are opened, and another book, the book of life. Death and Sheol give up their dead, and are themselves thrown into the fire, the second death. Whoever's name is not found written in the book of life is thrown in with them.

And then: a wedding. A holy city, new Jerusalem, comes down out of heaven, dressed like a bride adorned for her husband, and a great voice says what the whole story has waited to say: Behold, the dwelling of God is with mankind, and he will dwell with them, and be their God, and wipe away every tear; death will be no more, neither mourning nor crying nor pain, for the former things have gone.

Almost more than can be told: a wall of jasper, the city pure gold, clear as glass, its foundations set with every jewel there is, twelve gates and each a single pearl. No temple in it, for the Lord God and the Lamb are its temple; no need of sun or moon, for the glory of God gives it light, and its gates will never be shut, for there is no night there.

Through the middle runs a river of the water of life, clear as crystal, flowing from the throne, and on either side the tree of life, bearing twelve kinds of fruit, its leaves for the healing of the nations. No more curse - the one first spoken over dust and thorns in another garden is undone at last. His servants will see his face, wear his name, and reign forever.

Bride and bridegroom, together now for good. Go and live as though you believed it.
