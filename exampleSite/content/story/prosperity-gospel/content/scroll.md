The first strike has failed. Hard times can crack open just about anything - houses, harvests, whole economies - except a heart set on the wrong thing. Judgment alone was never going to be the thing that turned the world around, and the book pauses here to say so plainly before moving on.

A mighty angel comes down wrapped in a cloud, a rainbow over his head, his face like the sun, his legs like pillars of fire, holding a small scroll open in his hand. He plants one foot on the sea and one on the land and shouts, and seven thunders answer him - a whole message John is told not to write down. Not everything about how this ends is meant to be handed out in advance.

Then a voice from heaven tells John to take the scroll and eat it. He does, and it happens exactly as promised: sweet as honey in his mouth, and bitter in his stomach once it is down. This is not another disaster arriving. It is a revelation - something true about God himself, sweet to receive and hard to carry, about to break through into the story where judgment alone could not.

Notice the order of things. Not comfort now, tears later. Not tears now, comfort later, arriving on schedule like a payout. Both at once, held together in the same swallowed scroll - because that has always been the actual shape of the good news, not the edited version that promises the sweetness without ever mentioning the weight. John is told he must prophesy again, about many peoples and nations and languages and kings. The word does not stop just because it costs something to carry. It goes out anyway, sweet and bitter both, exactly as it was given to him.
