And then: a wedding. A city comes down out of heaven, dressed like a bride on her wedding day - the real bride, at last, standing in the place where the harlot once postured and preened - and a voice from the throne says what everyone has been waiting the whole story to hear: "Look - God's home is now among his people. He will live with them. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death, no more mourning, no more crying, no more pain, because all of that has already passed away."

The city itself is almost too much to describe: walls of jasper, streets of gold so pure it looks like glass, foundations set with every color of jewel, twelve gates each carved from a single pearl. Every luxury the harlot ever wore as a costume is here in its rightful owner's hands at last, and the difference could not be plainer: she rented her glamor with other people's blood; this city is simply given, free, to people who never earned a single stone of it. There is no temple anywhere in it, because God himself, and the Lamb, are the temple now. There is no sun and no moon, because the glory of God is the light, and the gates never close - no locks, no walls against thieves, nothing left worth stealing because nothing here can be taken away.

Through the middle of it runs a river, clear as crystal, flowing straight from the throne of God, with a tree on either bank bearing fruit every month, and leaves for healing every nation that ever bled. The curse - the one that started all the way back in a different garden, when the first man wanted the gift more than the Giver - is gone for good. God's people will see his face at last, wear his name, and reign with him forever.

This, finally, is what the whole book has been pointing toward: not a fortune that has to be defended, not a formula that has to be worked correctly, not a crown some counterfeit rider hands out for a price that always comes due later. Real security was never a transaction to get right. It is a Person, keeping a promise he made before the world began, to people who arrived with nothing to offer him but their loyalty. "To the thirsty I will give from the spring of the water of life without payment," the voice from the throne says - without payment, after everything this story has shown about what counterfeit blessing always ends up costing. The gift, at last, matched to the Giver, because it was always him, all along.

The bride and the groom, together at last, for good. God himself moves in with his people - no more distance left to close.

And now that you know the story - go and live like you believe it.
