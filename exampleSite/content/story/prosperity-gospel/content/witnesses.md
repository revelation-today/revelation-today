Step past the curtain, and you see both worlds as they truly are. John is given a measuring rod and told to measure the temple and the altar and those who worship there - but not the outer court, which is given over to the nations to trample. The temple was never a place you earn your way into by keeping the right side of a ledger. It is where your loyalty either shows itself, or does not.

Two witnesses are given power to testify, dressed in sackcloth, for twelve hundred and sixty days. They speak with everything they have, fire coming from their mouths against anyone who tries to harm them, and the beast that rises from the abyss makes war on them anyway and conquers them and kills them. Their bodies lie in the street of the great city, and the people of the earth throw a party over the corpses, sending each other gifts, because these two had tormented everyone's comfortable arrangements just by telling the truth. For three and a half days, it looks like total defeat - the kind of ending the world always assumes is coming for anyone who refuses to play along with its version of security.

Then breath from God enters them, and they stand up on their feet, and great fear falls on everyone watching. A loud voice from heaven calls them up: "Come up here." And they go up to heaven in a cloud, while their enemies watch it happen.

That is exactly the pattern the Church herself is asked to live: not a victory that skips over suffering, not a formula that guarantees the sackcloth years never come, but one that walks straight through them and comes out the other side standing, in full view of everyone who was certain it was over. The seventh trumpet sounds right afterward, and heaven declares what has actually been true the whole time: "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ." It was always going to end this way. It simply had to be walked through first.
