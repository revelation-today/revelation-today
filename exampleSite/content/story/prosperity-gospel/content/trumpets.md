Now it is the Church's turn to shake the world - not with weapons, but the way Jesus always did: through what looks like weakness. The prayers of God's people rise like incense before the throne, and then the same angel throws fire from that altar down onto the earth, and the trumpets sound, and the earth itself begins to tremble.

Food fails. Water turns bitter enough to kill. Trade collapses. Every fallback plan people built instead of trusting God - every stockpile, every backup account, every alternative source of security - comes apart in the same season. Surely now, with the whole structure shaking, people will finally look up and ask what is real, and who is actually worth trusting with a life.

They do not. Even as their idols turn on them - even as those same idols strip away every last shred of hope, until death starts to look like relief and people search for it and cannot find it - people cling tighter, not looser. John says it plainly: the rest of mankind who were not killed by these plagues still did not repent of the works of their hands, did not stop worshiping demons and idols of gold and silver and bronze and stone and wood, objects that cannot see or hear or walk. They kept bowing to what could not save them, in the middle of watching it fail to save them.

That is the hard lesson buried in these two chapters, and it is worth sitting with rather than rushing past: suffering alone has never been enough to change a human heart. Hardship can strip away every false security a person has built, and still not touch the deeper attachment underneath it - the belief that some object, some formula, some system out there can be managed into giving us what only God was ever able to give. The trumpets are not proof that judgment fixes people. They are proof that only grace ever really does.
