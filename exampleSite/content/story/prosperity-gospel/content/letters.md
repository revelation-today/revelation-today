Seven churches, seven letters, seven mirrors held up to seven very different congregations.

Ephesus works itself to exhaustion - correct in every doctrine, faithful in every duty - and has quietly let love slip through its fingers. Without it, all the labor amounts to nothing. Better, Jesus says, that this church did not exist at all than to go on like this.

Smyrna could not be more different. Poor - genuinely, materially poor - hated, squeezed on every side by a hostile city. And she is one of only two churches Jesus has nothing but praise for. "I know your affliction and your poverty," he tells her, "but you are rich." No promise here that the hardship will lift. Just the plain declaration that her poverty and her wealth are two entirely different ledgers, and only one of them matters in the end.

Pergamum stands its ground in the shadow of Satan's own throne. Some die for it. And yet inside the walls, false teaching has slipped in unchallenged, quietly leading people away - the same seduction, in miniature, that later chapters will show in full.

In Thyatira, the rot starts at the top: the leadership itself has gone astray, tolerating a teacher who promises depths of blessing that cost nothing at all.

Sardis looks alive from the outside. Underneath, she is already dead - convinced she can manage just fine without God, and failing at nearly everything because of it. A reputation for life is not the same thing as life.

Philadelphia is the other church Jesus praises without reservation - small, worn down, with nothing left in the bank, and still faithful. She has "little strength," he says, and he opens a door for her anyway that no one can shut.

Then there is Laodicea: lukewarm, self-satisfied, and - this is the telling detail - rich, comfortable, in need of nothing, or so she believes. "You do not realize," Jesus tells her, "how wretched, pitiful, poor, blind, and naked you are." Notice the pattern across all seven letters: the two churches with nothing in the bank are the two he calls rich. The one church that feels entirely secure is the only one he says nothing good about at all. And yet even here, at rock bottom, he does not walk away. He knocks. He warns. He still wants in - offering gold refined in fire, which is worth more precisely because it cannot be bought at face value.
