One deception still waits to be unmasked before the story can end - and it is the most seductive one in the entire book, because it does not arrive looking like a monster. It arrives looking like a bride.

An angel carries John away to see "the judgment of the great prostitute who sits on many waters, with whom the kings of the earth have committed sexual immorality, and with whose wine the earth's inhabitants have become drunk." He sees a woman sitting on a scarlet beast, covered with blasphemous names, seven heads and ten horns beneath her. She is dressed in purple and scarlet, glittering with gold and jewels and pearls, and she holds a golden cup in her hand - full, John says, "of the abominations and impurities of her sexual immorality." Written on her forehead: "Babylon the great, mother of prostitutes and of earth's abominations." She calls herself blessed, favored, secure - and for a long time, kings and merchants alike have believed her.

Pull back the dress, though, and there is the beast underneath, teeth and all. She is no bride. She is a harlot - draped in jewels that should have belonged to the true bride, promising prosperity to anyone willing to drink from her cup, and plenty inside the Church itself have fallen for the act, mistaking her glamor for blessing because it arrived dressed so convincingly like one. Chapter eighteen reads out her merchandise like an invoice nobody wanted to see in full: gold, silver, jewels, pearls, fine linen, purple, silk, scarlet, citron wood, ivory, bronze, iron, marble, cinnamon, spice, incense, myrrh, frankincense, wine, oil, fine flour, wheat, cattle, sheep, horses, chariots - and then, at the very end of the list, almost too horrifying to read past, "slaves, that is, human souls." Her wealth was never separate from her cruelty. It was assembled out of it, line by line.

Her final performance looks flawless. It is not. The very powers the devil sends to destroy the Church - the beast and the ten kings who once shared her bed - turn instead and tear the harlot apart, strip her naked, devour her flesh, burn her up with fire: his own weapon, used against him, exactly as God had put it into their hearts to do. The merchants who grew rich supplying her stand far off, weeping and mourning - not over her, in the end, but over their own losses, "for no one buys their cargo anymore." That was always the shape of the love inside that arrangement: it never survived past the moment the money stopped moving.

The road is finally clear to finish him for good. And immediately, before judgment even settles, heaven does not go quiet - it throws a wedding. A great multitude cries out, "Hallelujah! For the Lord our God the Almighty reigns... let us rejoice and exult and give him the glory, for the marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready." Where the harlot dazzled without loyalty and dressed borrowed glory over violence, the real Bride is given fine linen, bright and pure, and told plainly what it is made of: "the righteous deeds of the saints." Nothing purchased. Nothing rented. Everything actually earned by the one who died for her, and freely given.
