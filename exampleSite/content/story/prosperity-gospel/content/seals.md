The seals break, one by one, and each one lets something loose.

First a white horse gallops out - a rider with a bow, handed a crown, already bent on conquest. Sit with this one a moment, because the whole rest of the book keeps circling back to him. Look at what he is riding: a white horse, the very color of the true King's own mount later in the story. Look at what he is wearing: a crown, not a helmet. Look at what he is doing: winning. For one heartbeat, before you know any better, this looks exactly like the good news everyone was hoping for - a conqueror who hands out victory and provision to anyone who follows him, without asking anyone to carry a cross first.

That is precisely the point. Jesus is not the only one who wants your allegiance, and this rider is a counterfeit, deliberately borrowing the true King's own colors to sell a counterfeit peace. He is not offered instead of security. He is offered as security - and that is what makes him dangerous.

Strip away the costume, and violence is all that is left underneath. Behind the white horse comes a blood-red one, and peace is torn off the earth like a scab - people turning on people with drawn swords. Behind the red horse comes a black one, its rider holding a pair of scales: a day's wages for a day's bread, while oil and wine stay safe for those who can still afford them - comfort rationed out to whoever already has enough to buy it. And behind the black horse comes a horse the color of ash, and its rider's name is Death, with the grave riding at his heels.

Conquest gives way to war, war to famine, and famine to death. That is the full invoice behind the first rider's promise, and it only ever gets presented after the sale, once it is too late to return the goods.

Then the seal breaks that reveals not armies but an altar - and under it, the souls of those killed for staying loyal to the real King instead of the counterfeit one, crying out with one voice: how long, O Lord, holy and true, until you judge the earth and avenge our blood? They are the ones who refused exactly the kind of safety the white horse was selling, and it cost them everything the world can see. They are told to wait, just a little longer - not because they were wrong to ask, but because the answer is already coming.

The sky itself answers next: the sun goes black, the moon turns to blood, the stars fall like fruit shaken from a tree, and the sky peels back like a scroll being rolled up. Every last prop the world ever leaned on for its sense of safety - sun, moon, stars, the sky overhead - shakes loose at exactly the same moment. The world is already drowning in what it has brought on itself, and the Church stands in the middle of the wreckage, crying out too, waiting for the same God who sealed the martyrs' cry to answer hers.
