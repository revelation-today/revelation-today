It is done, even if you never saw it happening. All along, while it looked like the Church was losing - the martyrs under the altar, the two witnesses lying dead in the street, the saints refused the right to simply buy bread because they would not wear the beast's mark - she was ruling with Christ the entire time, just not with the kind of power anyone was watching for. John sees thrones, and those given authority to judge, and "the souls of those who had been beheaded for their testimony to Jesus and for the word of God, and who had not worshiped the beast or its image and had not received its mark." They come to life and reign with Christ a thousand years. This is their reward, and notice again what it is not measured by: not by what they managed to keep, but by what they refused to trade away.

The dragon, meanwhile, is bound - thrown into the abyss and sealed there, no longer free to deceive the nations. Even his releasing, at the very end of the thousand years, changes nothing about the outcome. He gathers Gog and Magog for one final war, "numerous as the sand of the sea," certain that sheer numbers still count for something. Fire comes down from heaven and consumes them, and the devil is thrown into the lake of fire, joining the beast and the false prophet, "and they will be tormented day and night forever and ever." Every counterfeit throne this book has shown - the white horse's crown, the beast's stolen authority, the harlot's borrowed jewels - ends in exactly the same place: nowhere.

Now comes the last reckoning: the dead, great and small, standing before the great white throne, and books opened, and the dead judged "by what was written in the books, according to what they had done." But one more book is opened alongside them - the book of life - and it is that book, not the ledger of anyone's deeds, that decides who spends eternity with God. Death and Hades themselves are thrown into the lake of fire; even death does not get to keep what it thought it had claimed.

This is the last word on every counterfeit security the book has confronted: it is decided, once and for all, who has trusted the Lamb, and who has spent a lifetime trusting something else instead.
