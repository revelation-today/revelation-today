Jesus already won it at the cross, and the Church has kept faith with that victory ever since - and now the enemy's forces scatter like smoke. On Mount Zion stands the Lamb, and with him the hundred and forty-four thousand who bear his name and his Father's name on their foreheads, singing a song no one else can learn. On a sea of glass and fire, the faithful sing. Their only qualification was staying loyal - not staying comfortable, not staying prosperous by any measure the world would recognize, just staying loyal.

An angel flies through the sky with an eternal gospel to proclaim, calling every nation to fear God and give him glory. A second angel announces that Babylon is fallen - the counterfeit city, the one whose whole economy ran on false worship, already collapsing before the story even shows it in full. A third angel gives the plainest warning in the whole book: anyone who worships the beast and receives its mark will drink the wine of God's wrath, undiluted.

Not everyone gets to sing. The ones who played it safe and sided with the beast to avoid the cost - who found a mark more convenient than a cross - now face a far heavier one. "Here is a call for the endurance of the saints," John writes, right in the middle of the warning, "those who keep the commandments of God and their faith in Jesus." Then a voice from heaven pronounces a blessing on those who die in the Lord from now on: "Blessed indeed, that they may rest from their labors, for their deeds follow them."

Two sickles swing across the earth. One is Jesus himself, seated on a cloud, gathering in a harvest that is ready. One is an angel, cutting clusters from the vine of the earth and throwing them into the great winepress of God's wrath. One harvest gathers in. One harvest is for judgment. Truth stops being theoretical here - the gap between the two harvests is exactly the gap between trusting the Lamb and trusting the beast, and by this point in the story, everyone has already chosen which field they are standing in.
