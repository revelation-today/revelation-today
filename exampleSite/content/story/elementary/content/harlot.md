One last trick needs to be exposed before this adventure can end.

John sees a woman riding on the scarlet beast, dressed in purple and gold, glittering with jewels that should have belonged to someone else entirely. She calls herself glorious. She promises comfort and riches to anyone who drinks from her golden cup. Plenty of people — even some who thought they were on the right team — have fallen completely for the act.

But look closer, and the disguise slips. Underneath the jewels and the perfume is the beast itself, teeth and all. She's not a bride. She's a fake — Babylon the Great, dressed up to look like everything good, hiding everything that's actually rotten.

Her big performance looks perfect right up until it isn't. In one of the story's sharpest twists, the very powers the dragon sent out to attack God's people suddenly turn around and tear the fake queen apart instead — his own weapon, used against him, right when he least expects it.

With her gone, the road is finally clear. Heaven's armies ride out behind a rider on a white horse — the real King this time, not a copycat — and the beast and the false prophet are thrown down for good.

The dragon has one more scheme left to lose.
