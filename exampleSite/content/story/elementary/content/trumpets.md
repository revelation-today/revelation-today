Now it's the Church's turn to shake things up — and they don't do it with weapons. They do it the way Jesus always worked: through what looks like weakness. Prayers rise up to heaven like curls of incense smoke, and then, one after another, seven trumpets sound.

Each blast lands like a hammer. Hail and fire fall, and a third of the trees burn. A mountain of fire crashes into the sea, and a third of the creatures in it die. A star called Wormwood falls from the sky and turns a third of the rivers bitter. The sun, moon, and stars go dark for a third of the day.

Surely, after all that, people will finally stop and ask what's really true?

They don't. Even when the very things they trusted — money, power, fake gods — turn around and hurt them, even when hope gets harder and harder to find, people grip their idols tighter instead of letting go. Locusts swarm up like something out of a nightmare, and an army so vast it can't be counted marches across the earth. Still, most hearts stay shut tight.

It's a hard truth this adventure has to tell: pain and disaster alone have never been enough to change a stubborn heart. Something else is going to be needed.
