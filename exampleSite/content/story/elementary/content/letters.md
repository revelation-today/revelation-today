Jesus had seven small, scattered groups of friends spread across the land — seven churches, like seven teams facing seven very different kinds of trouble. He wrote each one a letter, and he didn't hold back the truth, because that's what you do for people you actually love.

Ephesus worked harder than anyone. They got every rule right. But somewhere along the way, they'd stopped loving people — and Jesus told them that without love, none of it counted.

Smyrna had nothing. No money, no respect, enemies on every side. And Jesus told them they were doing great — one of only two teams he had zero complaints about.

Pergamum stood firm in the scariest place imaginable, right under the enemy's nose. Some of them died for it. But sneaky lies had slipped in through the back door, and nobody had noticed.

Thyatira's own leaders had gone wrong, leading good people the wrong way.

Sardis looked amazing from the outside — busy, respected, alive. Underneath, they were basically asleep, coasting on their own strength instead of God's.

Philadelphia was small and worn out and broke — and Jesus loved them for it. The other team with zero complaints.

And then there was Laodicea: comfortable, satisfied, sure they had everything they needed — and blind to how much trouble they were actually in. They're the only team Jesus doesn't praise at all.

But even to them, he doesn't walk away. He knocks on the door. He still wants in.

Seven letters. Seven honest report cards. One coach who refuses to give up on any of his teams.
