One deception still waits to be unmasked right in front of you before the story can end — and unmasking it reveals what this was always about: not simply defeating evil, but God binding himself to his people forever, like a marriage.

Here is the deception, right in front of you: a woman, dazzling, seated on a scarlet beast, draped in jewels and purple and gold that should have belonged to the bride, a golden cup in her hand, promising prosperity to anyone who will drink from it. Her name is written across her forehead: Babylon the Great, the mother of prostitutes. Watch the crowd around you lean toward her, drawn in, thirsty for what she is pouring.

Watch the dress pulled back now — and there is the beast underneath, teeth and all. She is no bride. She is a harlot, and plenty inside the Church itself, people standing right beside you, have fallen for the act.

Her final performance looks flawless, right up until it isn't. Watch the very powers the devil sends to destroy the Church — the beast and its ten horns — turn instead and tear the harlot apart in front of you, stripping her, devouring her flesh, burning her with fire. His own weapon, used against him, right in front of your eyes.

Heaven erupts in front of you — a roar like a great multitude, like rushing waters, like peals of thunder: "Hallelujah! For the Lord our God Almighty reigns." And then the marriage supper of the Lamb is announced, and you are told: blessed are those invited.

The road is finally clear to finish him for good. Watch the sky open one last time, a rider on a white horse, faithful and true, robe dipped in blood, the armies of heaven following. The beast and the false prophet are seized and thrown into the lake of fire. It is over, right in front of you.
