Now the same story unfolds again in front of you, pulled back this time to reveal the frame around it — a very different Christmas story, played out across the sky itself, right over your head. A woman appears above you, clothed with the sun, the moon under her feet, twelve stars circling her head like a crown. She is pregnant, and she is crying out in the pain of labor, and you feel the tension of it in your own chest.

Then the sky tears open again, and something far worse appears: an enormous red dragon, seven heads crowned, ten horns, a tail vast enough to sweep a third of the stars out of heaven and hurl them down past you to the earth. He plants himself directly in front of the woman, waiting. The instant the child is born, he means to swallow him whole — you can see his jaws, already open.

The child is born anyway — a son who will rule every nation with an iron scepter — and before the dragon's jaws can close, watch the boy snatched up, safe, to God's own throne, out of reach forever.

War breaks out in heaven right over your head: Michael and his angels against the dragon and his — and the dragon loses. There is no place for him there anymore. He is hurled down, past you, to the earth, and a voice rings out loud enough to shake you: the accuser is thrown down at last, beaten by the blood of the Lamb and the plain testimony of people who refused to save their own lives to escape him.

Furious, running out of time, the dragon turns on the woman — and when she slips out of his reach into the wilderness, he turns his rage on everyone who belongs to her instead. On you.

Take heart anyway: defeating the dragon was never your job. Jesus already did that, in heaven, before it ever reached you. Yours is simply to keep drawing people toward him — even while the dragon, knowing exactly how little time he has left, rages harder than ever right now, all around you.
