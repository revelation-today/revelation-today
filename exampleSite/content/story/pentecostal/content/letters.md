Seven churches rise in front of you, one after another, and seven letters land like mirrors held up to your own face. Feel each one land.

Ephesus is working itself to exhaustion right in front of you — correct in every doctrine, faithful in every duty — and has quietly let love slip through its fingers without even noticing it was going. Watch all that labor amount to nothing without it. Better, Jesus says, that this church did not exist at all than to go on like this.

Smyrna could not be more different. Poor, hated, squeezed on every side by a hostile city pressing in — and she is one of only two churches Jesus has nothing but praise for. Feel the weight of that praise land on the poorest room in the building.

Pergamum stands its ground right where you can see it, in the shadow of Satan's own throne. Some die for it, right in front of you, refusing to bend. And yet inside the walls, false teaching has slipped in unchallenged, quietly leading people away while no one was watching the door.

In Thyatira, watch the rot start at the top: the leadership itself has gone astray, and it spreads downward from there.

Sardis looks alive from where you stand — busy, respectable, humming along. Underneath, she is already dead, convinced she can manage just fine without God, and failing at nearly everything because of it.

Philadelphia is the other church Jesus praises without reservation — small, worn down, nothing left in the bank, and still faithful, right there in front of you, holding on with both hands.

Then Laodicea: lukewarm, self-satisfied, blind to how poor she really is — the only church that receives no praise at all. And yet even here, watch Jesus not walk away. He knocks. He warns. He still wants in. Can you hear it — the knock, right now, on your own door?
